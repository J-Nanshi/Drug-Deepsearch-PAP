## 1. Drug Summary

Amivantamab (amivantamab‑vmjw; Rybrevant) is a fully human, low‑fucose IgG1 bispecific antibody targeting the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and mesenchymal–epithelial transition (MET) receptor, developed for EGFR‑ and/or MET‑driven non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).[1–5,9–11,13,14] It was the first targeted agent specifically approved for adult patients with locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC harboring EGFR exon 20 insertion mutations whose disease has progressed on or after platinum‑based chemotherapy, based on the single‑arm phase I/II CHRYSALIS trial (NCT02609776), which demonstrated clinically meaningful response rates and durability in this molecular subset.[4,5,9] In 2024, the U.S. FDA converted the post‑platinum indication from accelerated to traditional approval and expanded use to first‑line therapy in combination with carboplatin and pemetrexed for locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC with EGFR exon 20 insertion mutations, supported by improved progression‑free survival in the randomized phase III PAPILLON trial (NCT04538664).[11,13,14]

Mechanistically, amivantamab binds the extracellular domains of EGFR and MET, blocks ligand (EGF and HGF) binding, inhibits ligand‑induced receptor phosphorylation, and downregulates EGFR/MET expression and signaling in NSCLC models, including EGFR exon 20 insertion variants.[1,4,5,7] Its Fc‑engineered backbone enhances immune effector functions, mediating monocyte/macrophage‑dependent trogocytosis that depletes EGFR/MET from tumor cells and antibody‑dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), with MET engagement further augmenting ADCC against EGFR‑mutant NSCLC cells.[6,7] Clinically, activity has been observed across diverse EGFR exon 20 insertion variants and in broader EGFR‑mutant contexts under investigation (e.g., combinations with chemotherapy or EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors), but current approved lung cancer indications are confined to EGFR exon 20 insertion–mutated NSCLC in post‑platinum monotherapy and first‑line chemo‑combination settings.[4,5,11,13,14]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Amivantamab is a fully human, low‑fucose IgG1 bispecific antibody that simultaneously targets EGFR domain III and the MET Sema domain, blocking ligand binding (EGF, TGF‑α, amphiregulin, HGF) and thereby inhibiting downstream EGFR/MET signaling in NSCLC cells.[1,5] Ligand blockade together with antibody binding triggers internalization and degradation of EGFR and MET, leading to receptor downmodulation and suppression of oncogenic signaling in tumors driven by EGFR mutations (including exon 20 insertions) or MET activation.[1,5]

Its afucosylated Fc enhances binding to FcγRIII and promotes potent Fc‑mediated effector functions, including NK‑cell–mediated ADCC and monocyte/macrophage‑mediated trogocytosis that strips EGFR/MET from tumor cell membranes.[1,3,4] MET engagement increases formation of EGFR–MET–amivantamab trimers and augments ADCC at low antibody concentrations, particularly in NSCLC with common EGFR mutations.[1] These combined signaling and immune‑effector mechanisms are especially relevant in EGFR exon 20 insertion NSCLC, which is relatively TKI‑refractory, and in EGFR‑mutant disease with MET‑driven or broader EGFR‑TKI resistance, where dual EGFR/MET targeting can help overcome bypass signaling.[5]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

1. **EGFR signaling (ErbB network)** – Amivantamab binds EGFR, blocks ligand-induced activation, and downregulates EGFR-driven PI3K–AKT and MAPK signaling, which are central survival and proliferation pathways in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC and key drivers of sensitivity to EGFR‑directed therapy.[2][8]

2. **MET signaling (HGF–MET axis)** – By concurrently targeting MET, amivantamab inhibits a critical bypass route that activates PI3K–AKT via ErbB3 and contributes to de novo or acquired resistance to EGFR TKIs in NSCLC with MET amplification or overexpression, thereby restoring pathway suppression and drug sensitivity.[2][5][8]

3. **PI3K–AKT/mTOR pathway** – Dual EGFR/MET blockade reduces PI3K–AKT signaling, a convergence node for multiple RTKs; in EGFR‑ and MET‑altered NSCLC, decreased AKT activity is associated with impaired survival signaling and enhanced antitumor efficacy of bispecific approaches.[2][5][7][8]

4. **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway** – Amivantamab-mediated inhibition of EGFR and MET diminishes upstream activation of the MAPK cascade, limiting proliferative signaling, while reactivation of RAS–MAPK via alternative RTKs (e.g., ERBB2, MET) is a recognized mechanism of resistance to EGFR-directed therapies.[2][4][6][8]

5. **JAK–STAT signaling** – EGFR and MET feed into JAK–STAT (especially STAT3) in NSCLC; suppression of these receptors by amivantamab is expected to attenuate STAT-dependent survival and inflammatory programs, whereas JAK2/STAT3 or IGF1R pathway activation can substitute for EGFR signaling and drive resistance.[4][6][8]

6. **ADCC/ADCP (Fc-mediated innate immune pathways)** – As a fully human IgG1 bispecific, amivantamab engages FcγRIIIa/CD16A on NK cells and macrophages to induce potent ADCC and ADCP against EGFR/MET‑expressing NSCLC cells, with preclinical data showing stronger ADCC than cetuximab and a correlation with interferon‑γ secretion.[2][3][8]

7. **Apoptotic signaling** – By simultaneously shutting down EGFR/MET survival inputs and engaging immune effector mechanisms, amivantamab promotes apoptosis in EGFR exon 20 insertion and MET‑altered NSCLC models, functionally countering anti-apoptotic signaling maintained through PI3K–AKT and related downstream nodes.[2][5][7][8]

8. **Bypass RTK networks (ERBB2, ERBB3, IGF1R, others)** – NSCLC frequently escapes EGFR inhibition through activation of alternative RTKs (MET, ERBB2, ERBB3, IGF1R), which converge on PI3K–AKT and MAPK; amivantamab’s dual EGFR/MET targeting directly addresses two major hubs in this bypass network but may still be challenged by upregulation of other RTKs.[4][5][8]  



## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic NSCLC with EGFR exon 20 insertions, amivantamab has robust activity both post‑platinum and in the first line. In CHRYSALIS (NCT02609776), previously treated patients achieved an ORR of ~40%, median PFS 8.3 months, and OS 22.8 months, leading to FDA accelerated approval as second‑line therapy.[2,5] PAPILLON (NCT04538664) showed that adding amivantamab to carboplatin–pemetrexed in treatment‑naïve EGFR exon 20 insertion NSCLC nearly doubled PFS (11.4 vs 6.7 months) and increased ORR to 73%, supporting first‑line chemo‑antibody use.[4]

For common EGFR exon 19 deletion/L858R mutations after osimertinib, CHRYSALIS‑2 evaluated amivantamab plus lazertinib and reported a 36% ORR with median response duration 9.6 months in a heterogeneous post‑TKI setting, indicating clinically meaningful salvage activity.[1] These data underpin regulatory approval of amivantamab–lazertinib in EGFR Ex19del/L858R‑mutant advanced NSCLC and support its use across first‑ and later‑line settings in this molecular subgroup.[9]

In EGFR‑mutant disease with MET‑driven resistance, CHRYSALIS‑2 subgroup analysis demonstrated marked enrichment of benefit: ORR 61% and markedly prolonged PFS in MET‑positive versus 12% ORR and PFS 4.1 months in MET‑negative tumors, consistent with dual EGFR/MET targeting as a rational strategy for MET‑amplified resistance.[1] Evidence for amivantamab in ALK‑rearranged or other non‑EGFR oncogene‑addicted NSCLC remains indirect, with current use focused on EGFR exon 20 insertions and classical EGFR mutations rather than ALK or other drivers.[1,6]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Amivantamab has no absolute contraindications in the EMA product information, but treatment must be initiated and supervised by clinicians experienced with anticancer monoclonal antibodies and with immediate access to resuscitation for infusion-related reactions (IRRs).[1] IRRs are very common during early infusions; the label mandates premedication (oral and IV corticosteroids, antihistamines, antipyretics), split dosing in week 1, and interruption at first symptoms, with resumption at a reduced rate or permanent discontinuation for recurrent grade 3 or any grade 4 IRR.[1]

Serious pulmonary toxicity is a critical concern. The EMA label requires withholding amivantamab if interstitial lung disease (ILD) or pneumonitis is suspected and permanent discontinuation if ILD/pneumonitis is confirmed.[1] This is consistent with guideline-based caution for EGFR‑directed regimens in oncogene-addicted NSCLC.[5]

Dermatologic and nail disorders (acneiform rash, paronychia, dry skin) are frequent; prophylactic skin care, oral/topical antibiotics, sun protection, and dose interruption/reduction are recommended, with permanent discontinuation for grade 4 skin reactions.[1] The label also highlights venous thromboembolic (VTE) events, particularly when combined with lazertinib, and recommends prophylactic anticoagulation at treatment initiation and discontinuation of amivantamab for recurrent VTE despite adequate anticoagulation.[1] Embryo‑fetal toxicity is expected; effective contraception and avoidance of breastfeeding are advised.[1]  



## 8. Key Clinical Trials

The phase I CHRYSALIS trial (NCT02609776) in EGFR exon 20 insertion–mutated NSCLC progressing after platinum chemotherapy reported an ORR of ~37–40%, median PFS 6.9 months, and median OS 23 months with amivantamab monotherapy.[1,4] The phase III MARIPOSA trial (NCT04487080) showed first‑line amivantamab‑lazertinib significantly prolonged median PFS versus osimertinib (23.7 vs 16.6 months; P<0.001) in EGFR exon 19 deletion/L858R NSCLC.[3] The phase III PAPILLON trial (NCT04538664) demonstrated that adding amivantamab to platinum–pemetrexed chemotherapy in EGFR exon 20 insertion NSCLC improved median PFS to 11.4 vs 6.7 months with chemotherapy alone (P<0.001).[3] MARIPOSA‑2 (NCT04988295) in post‑osimertinib EGFR‑mutant NSCLC showed higher ORR (64% and 63% vs 36%) and longer PFS (8.2–8.3 vs 4.2 months) for amivantamab‑based chemo regimens versus chemotherapy alone.[4]
