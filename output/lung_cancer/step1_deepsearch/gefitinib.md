## 1. Drug Summary

Gefitinib is an oral, first‑generation epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) that targets the ATP‑binding site of the EGFR kinase domain and is used as a genotype‑directed therapy in advanced non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).[2],[8],[9] Activating EGFR mutations—predominantly exon 19 deletions and exon 21 L858R, which together account for ~90% of EGFR alterations—drive ligand‑independent signaling through RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK and PI3K–AKT pathways in lung adenocarcinoma, and confer marked sensitivity to EGFR‑TKIs including gefitinib.[2],[3],[6],[8],[9]  

Clinically, gefitinib and erlotinib were the first EGFR‑TKIs to demonstrate substantial activity in EGFR‑mutated NSCLC, leading to their approval for advanced disease.[8],[9] In randomized phase III settings, first‑generation EGFR‑TKIs such as gefitinib improved median progression‑free survival (PFS) to ~9–13 months compared with platinum doublet chemotherapy in patients with classical sensitizing EGFR mutations, establishing TKIs as the cornerstone of first‑line therapy in this molecular subset.[7],[8],[9] Case‑level and retrospective data indicate that gefitinib can also provide meaningful disease control in some uncommon exon 19 deletion variants (e.g., L747_T751del), with reported median PFS around 12–14 months, although efficacy is heterogeneous across rare subtypes.[3]  

Within the EGFR‑TKI class, gefitinib is positioned as a first‑generation, reversible inhibitor with broad activity against mutant and wild‑type EGFR, in contrast to second‑generation irreversible pan‑ErbB inhibitors (afatinib, dacomitinib) and third‑generation mutant‑selective agents (osimertinib, aumolertinib, furmonertinib, lazertinib, befotertinib).[2],[8],[9] Second‑generation TKIs offer modest PFS gains over gefitinib but at the cost of greater toxicity, while third‑generation TKIs—particularly osimertinib—have become the preferred first‑line standard in many settings due to superior PFS and overall survival, as well as activity against T790M‑mediated resistance.[2],[8],[9] Nonetheless, gefitinib remains an important comparator and treatment option in resource‑limited environments, in combination regimens (e.g., with anti‑angiogenic agents), and for selected patients with EGFR‑mutant lung adenocarcinoma where its efficacy–toxicity profile is acceptable.[1],[8],[9]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Gefitinib is a first‑generation, reversible EGFR tyrosine‑kinase inhibitor that competes with ATP at the intracellular ATP‑binding site of EGFR, thereby preventing receptor autophosphorylation and blocking downstream signaling cascades that drive proliferation, survival, invasion, and angiogenesis in NSCLC.[2],[4],[6] Activating *EGFR* mutations—most commonly exon 19 deletions and the exon 21 L858R substitution—occur in ~85–90% of *EGFR*-mutant NSCLC and define a molecular subset that is highly “addicted” to EGFR signaling and exquisitely sensitive to ATP‑competitive EGFR TKIs such as gefitinib.[1],[6],[7],[10] In clinical series of *EGFR*-mutant advanced NSCLC treated with gefitinib, objective response rates approximate 75–80%, with median progression‑free survival around 9–12 months, confirming that inhibition of mutant EGFR is the key therapeutic mechanism.[1],[4],[6]

Ligand‑independent activation of mutant EGFR stimulates multiple downstream pathways, notably RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK (MAPK), PI3K/AKT/mTOR, and JAK/STAT, which collectively promote malignant cell growth and survival.[2],[6],[10] Gefitinib-mediated blockade of EGFR phosphorylation suppresses these pathways, leading to cell‑cycle arrest and apoptosis in *EGFR*-mutant tumors, and has shown superior progression‑free survival compared with platinum chemotherapy in randomized trials of molecularly selected patients.[4],[5],[10] Structural and functional studies indicate that exon 19 deletions and L858R both reduce ATP affinity and stabilize the active kinase conformation, increasing relative affinity for gefitinib; however, subtle differences in kinase‑domain conformation and downstream signaling translate into distinct clinical behaviors between exon 19 deletion and L858R subgroups.[5],[6],[8] Rare and compound *EGFR* mutations (e.g., G719X alone vs. G719X plus L858R or L861Q) show heterogeneous gefitinib sensitivity, with complex mutations often displaying stronger binding and higher sensitivity than single rare mutations, underscoring the mutation‑specific nature of the drug–target interaction.[7],[8]

Despite initial dependence on mutant EGFR, resistance to gefitinib typically emerges after ~10–12 months and reflects both on‑target and bypass mechanisms. The secondary T790M “gatekeeper” mutation increases ATP affinity and sterically impairs gefitinib binding, accounting for ~50–60% of acquired resistance to first‑generation EGFR TKIs.[6],[10],[15] Other resistance routes include amplification or activation of alternative RTKs (MET, HER2, AXL), downstream mutations in *PIK3CA* or *BRAF*, epithelial–mesenchymal transition, and NF‑κB activation, which reactivate PI3K/AKT and MAPK signaling despite continued EGFR inhibition.[10],[16] YAP/TAZ‑driven Hippo‑pathway dysregulation has emerged as a key EGFR‑independent resistance axis: in *EGFR*-addicted NSCLC models rendered resistant to gefitinib or other EGFR TKIs, YAP activation upregulates AXL and other survival genes, and genetic or pharmacologic inhibition of YAP or AXL can restore TKI sensitivity.[12],[13],[16] Additional work shows that TKI‑bound EGFR can translocate to the cytoplasm and nucleus, where it cooperates with YAP to drive transcription of proliferative and anti‑apoptotic genes, providing a spatially distinct, kinase‑dependent mechanism that attenuates gefitinib efficacy.[12],[14]

Mechanistically, gefitinib’s most pronounced benefit is seen in lung adenocarcinoma with “classical” sensitizing *EGFR* mutations, whereas non‑adenocarcinoma NSCLC harboring *EGFR* mutations derives substantially lower response rates (~27% vs. 66%) and shorter median PFS (~3 vs. 9–10 months), indicating that histologic context and co‑alterations modulate EGFR dependence and TKI response.[3] Within the adenocarcinoma population, clinical and translational data suggest that exon 19 deletions often confer somewhat greater downstream pathway suppression and more favorable survival than L858R across several EGFR‑TKI generations, although both are clearly sensitive to gefitinib and form the core biomarker group for first‑line EGFR‑targeted therapy.[1],[5],[10] Rare and exon 20 insertion mutations, by contrast, frequently show primary resistance to first‑generation TKIs, necessitating alternative agents.[7],[10],[15] Collectively, these molecular and clinical observations underpin the precision‑oncology paradigm in NSCLC: routine genotyping of *EGFR* (with attention to specific exon 19 and 21 variants and selected rare mutations) is essential to identify patients whose tumors are biochemically and biologically poised to respond to gefitinib-based EGFR blockade.[1],[5],[7],[10]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Gefitinib is a first‑generation EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) that targets the ATP‑binding pocket of mutant EGFR, thereby modulating multiple downstream signaling and resistance pathways in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC.[6,7]

- **EGFR signaling / EGFR–RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) axis**  
  - Representative identifiers: KEGG MAPK signaling pathway (e.g., hsa04010); EGFR pathway components highlighted in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC reviews.[6,7]  
  - Regulation: Gefitinib suppresses kinase activity of activating EGFR mutants (e.g., exon 19 deletions, L858R), reducing receptor autophosphorylation and downstream MAPK signaling in sensitive tumors.[6,7]  
  - Effect: Downregulation is associated with tumor shrinkage and progression‑free survival benefits in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC; MAPK pathway reactivation via secondary EGFR mutations (e.g., T790M) or alternative drivers contributes to acquired resistance.[5–7]

- **PI3K–AKT survival signaling**  
  - Identifiers: PI3K/AKT pathway frequently described as a major EGFR effector in NSCLC.[6,7]  
  - Regulation: Effective EGFR blockade by gefitinib reduces PI3K–AKT activation; however, bypass activation through other RTKs (e.g., MET, FGFR, HER2) or PI3K alterations can maintain AKT signaling despite EGFR inhibition.[6,7]  
  - Effect: Suppressed PI3K–AKT signaling favors apoptosis and clinical sensitivity; sustained AKT activity is a hallmark of resistance to first‑generation TKIs.

- **c‑MET and other bypass RTK pathways**  
  - Identifiers: MET, HER2, FGFR, and other oncogenic drivers catalogued in advanced NSCLC (“molecular super‑highway”).[7]  
  - Regulation: When EGFR is inhibited, signaling can shift to parallel RTKs (e.g., MET amplification, HER2 or FGFR activation), restoring downstream MAPK and PI3K–AKT activity independent of EGFR.[6,7]  
  - Effect: Upregulated bypass RTK signaling mediates both primary and acquired resistance to gefitinib and other first‑/second‑generation EGFR TKIs, often prompting transition to later‑generation TKIs or combination strategies.[5–7]

- **EGFR mutation–driven on‑target resistance (T790M and beyond)**  
  - Identifiers: EGFR exon 20 T790M mutation and numerous additional kinase‑domain mutations catalogued in COSMIC and structural reviews.[6,7]  
  - Regulation: Under gefitinib pressure, secondary mutations such as T790M increase ATP affinity and reduce TKI binding, functionally “upregulating” EGFR signaling despite drug presence.[6,7]  
  - Effect: Emergence of T790M is the dominant mechanism of acquired resistance to gefitinib; this on‑target resistance underpins the clinical need for third‑generation TKIs such as osimertinib.[5–7]

- **EMT‑associated and non‑EGFR driver pathways (e.g., KRAS, BRAF, FGFR)**  
  - Identifiers: KRAS, BRAF, FGFR alterations and EMT‑related signaling described as alternative oncogenic subsets in NSCLC.[7]  
  - Regulation: In tumors driven by non‑EGFR alterations or undergoing epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT), EGFR dependence is reduced, and downstream MAPK/PI3K activation becomes uncoupled from EGFR, limiting gefitinib’s impact.[6,7]  
  - Effect: Upregulation of EMT programs or dominance of other oncogenic drivers (KRAS, BRAF, FGFR, etc.) is associated with intrinsic resistance or limited benefit from gefitinib, guiding selection of alternative targeted or combination therapies.[5–7]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In classic EGFR‑mutant lung adenocarcinoma (exon 19 deletions, L858R), gefitinib and other first‑generation TKIs yield high response rates and clear progression‑free survival (PFS) benefit versus chemotherapy; pooled analyses report median PFS around 9.7–9.8 months with gefitinib in EGFR‑mutant disease and superiority to platinum doublets in randomized phase III trials.[1],[8],[10] Nonetheless, most patients progress within about 10–12 months, with acquisition of the T790M gatekeeper mutation as the dominant on‑target resistance mechanism, prompting transition to osimertinib in contemporary practice.[1],[2],[3],[9] Combination approaches (e.g., gefitinib plus chemotherapy or anti‑angiogenic agents) can modestly extend PFS but do not fully abrogate resistance.[2]

For tumors with uncommon EGFR mutations, outcomes on first‑generation TKIs are more heterogeneous. A Thai series of metastatic NSCLC with uncommon or compound EGFR mutations (8.8% of EGFR‑mutant cases) showed an objective response rate of 37.5% and median PFS of 10.2 months on EGFR‑TKIs, mostly first‑generation agents, with better overall survival versus platinum chemotherapy but substantial variability by genotype.[12] Broader analyses indicate that afatinib or other second‑generation TKIs often outperform gefitinib for specific rare variants such as G719X, L861Q, and S768I, highlighting mutation‑level selection of therapy.[2]

In non‑adenocarcinoma NSCLC, particularly squamous cell carcinoma, both EGFR mutation prevalence and gefitinib benefit are attenuated. In a pooled analysis of 33 non‑adenocarcinoma NSCLC patients with EGFR mutations treated with gefitinib, response rate was 27%, disease control 67–70%, and median PFS 3.0 months, all significantly inferior to matched adenocarcinoma cohorts (RR 66%, PFS 9.4 months).[7] A separate squamous‑only series reported EGFR mutations in 5.4% of tested tumors and a median PFS of 1.93 months on gefitinib or erlotinib, supporting use of EGFR‑TKIs in squamous NSCLC only when a clear activating EGFR mutation is present and with tempered expectations of durability.[5]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Gefitinib is contraindicated in patients with hypersensitivity to gefitinib or excipients and during breastfeeding in the EU label.[7] The EMA product information highlights interstitial lung disease (ILD), hepatotoxicity, and serious ocular and gastrointestinal events as key safety concerns requiring close monitoring and, when severe, permanent discontinuation.[7]

ILD occurs in ~1–3% of gefitinib‑treated NSCLC patients and can be fatal.[7,12,16] A detailed case report describes acute ILD 20 days after starting gefitinib with high fever, dyspnea, diffuse ground‑glass opacities, and rapid recurrence on rechallenge, supporting permanent discontinuation after ILD and extreme caution in patients with pre‑existing ILD, prior thoracic radiotherapy, smoking history, or poor performance status.[12,16] Preclinical data suggest gefitinib uniquely induces Fra‑1 (FOSL1)–linked inflammatory signaling compared with erlotinib, consistent with an off‑target ILD liability.[10]

Hepatotoxicity is frequent: the EMA label reports liver function test abnormalities and rare hepatic failure; gefitinib exposure increases in cirrhosis, so Child‑Pugh B/C patients require close monitoring and treatment interruption or discontinuation for severe changes.[7] The label also warns that concomitant CYP3A4 inducers can reduce efficacy, whereas strong CYP3A4 inhibitors and warfarin co‑administration may increase toxicity and bleeding risk, mandating INR monitoring.[7]

Although clinically important QT prolongation is more characteristic of other TKIs, the gefitinib label emphasizes caution with other QT‑prolonging drugs and agents that markedly raise gastric pH, and recommends avoiding strong CYP3A4 inducers and using robust ECG and electrolyte monitoring in high‑risk patients.[7,15] Rare but serious gastrointestinal events, including perforation and very uncommon intestinal obstruction, further support prompt evaluation of new abdominal symptoms and temporary drug interruption while excluding mechanical causes.[7,13] Overall, gefitinib should be avoided or used with extreme caution in patients with active or high‑risk ILD, significant hepatic impairment, unstable cardiovascular disease, or major interacting co‑medications, with early drug withdrawal at first signs of severe pulmonary, hepatic, cardiac, or gastrointestinal toxicity.[7,12,13,16]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Gefitinib is a first‑generation, reversible EGFR TKI that became established as first‑line therapy for advanced EGFR‑mutant NSCLC through multiple randomized phase III trials comparing it with platinum doublets. The landmark IPASS trial in East‑Asian, light/never‑smokers with adenocarcinoma showed that, in the EGFR‑mutant subgroup, gefitinib produced higher response rates and longer progression‑free survival (PFS) than carboplatin–paclitaxel, whereas chemotherapy was superior in EGFR‑wild‑type tumors, firmly establishing EGFR mutation as a predictive biomarker and driving mutation‑selected designs in subsequent studies.[8],[7],[9]

Subsequent genotype‑restricted phase III trials such as WJTOG3405 and NEJ002 compared gefitinib with cisplatin‑based chemotherapy in patients with sensitizing EGFR mutations and consistently demonstrated superior PFS (~9–10 vs ~6 months) and higher objective response rates with gefitinib, with broadly similar overall survival (OS), likely due to cross‑over to TKIs at progression.[8],[21],[6],[7],[9] Network and guideline‑oriented analyses now position first‑generation TKIs (gefitinib, erlotinib, icotinib) as effective first‑line options with median PFS ~9–13 months in EGFR‑mutant disease, but inferior to third‑generation agents such as osimertinib in head‑to‑head comparisons.[6],[12],[21]

Real‑world data from Quebec evaluating publicly funded 1st‑line gefitinib in advanced EGFR‑mutant lung cancer (n=457) report a median OS of 18.9 months (95% CI 16.3–21.9), generally concordant with survival seen in pivotal randomized trials and other observational cohorts, supporting the external validity of trial results in routine practice.[11] Contemporary comparative and network meta‑analyses in predominantly Asian EGFR‑mutant populations confirm that gefitinib improves PFS versus chemotherapy but is outperformed on PFS and OS by second‑ and especially third‑generation TKIs, which are now preferred where available, while gefitinib remains a biomarker‑driven option in settings with limited access to newer agents or as a comparator in ongoing combination trials.[6],[9],[18],[21]
