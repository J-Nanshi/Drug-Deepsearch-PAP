## 1. Drug Summary

Pembrolizumab (KEYTRUDA) is a humanized IgG4κ monoclonal antibody that binds the programmed death‑1 (PD‑1) receptor on T cells and blocks its interaction with PD‑L1 and PD‑L2, thereby releasing PD‑1–mediated inhibitory signaling and restoring antitumor T‑cell activity.[4,8,15] PD‑1 normally recruits SHP‑2 and suppresses proximal T‑cell receptor and CD28 costimulatory pathways, including PI3K–AKT–mTOR and RAS–MAPK, leading to reduced proliferation, cytokine production, and metabolic fitness; pembrolizumab reverses these exhaustion-associated changes in the tumor microenvironment.[4,8,15]

In non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), pembrolizumab is FDA‑approved as first‑line monotherapy for advanced or metastatic tumors with PD‑L1 tumor proportion score (TPS) ≥1% and no EGFR/ALK alterations, and in combination with platinum–pemetrexed (nonsquamous) or carboplatin–(nab)paclitaxel (squamous) irrespective of PD‑L1 status.[11,14,15] Phase III data (KEYNOTE‑189, KEYNOTE‑407) show that adding pembrolizumab to platinum chemotherapy roughly halves the risk of death versus chemotherapy alone in metastatic nonsquamous and squamous NSCLC.[10,11,14] Precision use in lung cancer is anchored in PD‑L1 expression and tumor mutational burden (TMB): high PD‑L1 (≥50%) and elevated TMB identify patients with particularly durable benefit from pembrolizumab monotherapy,[9,16,17,21] whereas EGFR‑ or ALK‑driven tumors generally derive limited benefit from PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade and are preferentially treated with targeted tyrosine‑kinase inhibitors.[9,16,18,19]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Pembrolizumab is a humanized IgG4 monoclonal antibody against PD‑1 that blocks binding of PD‑L1/PD‑L2, preventing SHP‑2 recruitment to PD‑1 ITIM/ITSM motifs and thereby restoring TCR/CD28‑driven PI3K–AKT and RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK signaling, cytokine secretion, and cytotoxic function in exhausted CD8⁺ T cells within the tumor microenvironment.[1,2] In NSCLC, oncogenic pathways (e.g., MYC, PTEN loss) and IFN‑γ–driven adaptive resistance upregulate PD‑L1 on tumor and myeloid cells, creating PD‑1–mediated T‑cell exhaustion that PD‑1 blockade can reverse.[1,4] Clinically, pembrolizumab improves survival versus chemotherapy in advanced NSCLC, particularly when PD‑L1 tumor proportion score ≥50%, and is approved as first‑line monotherapy in this setting.[4,6,8] High tumor mutational burden (TMB) increases neoantigen load and is associated with greater benefit from PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors in NSCLC.[4,8,11] EGFR‑mutant NSCLC typically has low TMB and an immunosuppressive, “cold” microenvironment, explaining its relatively limited benefit from pembrolizumab compared with EGFR‑targeted TKIs.[6,7] In SCLC, pembrolizumab can benefit a subset of patients, but overall responses are modest; PD‑L1 is often low, whereas TMB is high, making TMB a more informative biomarker than PD‑L1 for PD‑1–axis blockade.[8,9]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In advanced NSCLC with PD‑L1 TPS ≥50%, pembrolizumab monotherapy is an established first‑line standard based on KEYNOTE‑024, which showed improved PFS and OS versus chemotherapy; however, only ~45% of these highly PD‑L1–expressing tumors respond, indicating substantial intrinsic resistance within this biomarker‑defined group.[3] The PEMBROMIC study further refines this high‑PD‑L1 segment, showing that radiomics‑predicted KEAP1/NFE2L2 pathway alterations identify patients with significantly longer PFS on first‑line pembrolizumab, suggesting that oxidative‑stress pathway status modulates benefit even when PD‑L1 is ≥50%.[3]

Chemo‑immunotherapy with pembrolizumab plus platinum/pemetrexed (non‑squamous) or platinum/taxane (squamous) improves survival irrespective of PD‑L1 level, and protocols such as NCT03976323 explicitly restrict enrollment to metastatic non‑squamous NSCLC, reflecting current practice of histology‑tailored chemotherapy backbones while maintaining broad pembrolizumab eligibility.[4] Within PD‑L1 ≥50% disease, PEMBROMIC noted a non‑significant trend toward worse PFS in squamous versus adenocarcinoma histology, contrasting with earlier reports where squamous tumors sometimes showed equal or better ICI outcomes, underscoring histology‑dependent heterogeneity under PD‑1 blockade.[3]

Driver‑positive EGFR‑mutant NSCLC remains a biologically distinct subgroup. A dedicated phase II trial (NCT02879994) is testing frontline pembrolizumab in EGFR‑mutant, TKI‑naïve, PD‑L1–positive NSCLC, but its rationale acknowledges EGFR TKIs as the standard and is designed primarily to explore whether response rates can exceed historical expectations, highlighting uncertainty and likely narrower benefit from pembrolizumab in this oncogene‑addicted context.[2] No small‑cell lung cancer data are provided in these sources.

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Pembrolizumab has no formal labeled contraindications, but serious immune‑mediated toxicities are common class effects and drive safety considerations.[4],[6],[8] In NSCLC, immune‑related pneumonitis is particularly critical due to poor baseline lung reserve; grade 3–5 pneumonitis occurred in KEYNOTE‑001 and requires drug interruption, corticosteroids, and permanent discontinuation for severe or life‑threatening events.[4],[6],[8] The FDA and EMA labels highlight serious immune‑mediated reactions including pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, nephritis, severe skin reactions, and endocrinopathies (thyroid dysfunction, hypophysitis, type 1 diabetes).[3],[6],[8]

ASCO/NCCN‑aligned guidelines emphasize early recognition of organ‑specific irAEs, use of CTCAE grading, and prompt glucocorticoid therapy for grade ≥2 toxicities, with permanent discontinuation for most grade 4 events; endocrine irAEs are generally managed with lifelong hormone replacement rather than stopping therapy.[2],[5] Cardiac, hepatic, pulmonary, and neurologic irAEs, though less frequent, carry higher mortality and mandate specialist input and often inpatient care.[2],[5]

Patients with pre‑existing autoimmune disease or prior solid organ transplant were largely excluded from trials and are considered at substantial risk of severe flares or graft rejection; use of pembrolizumab in these settings is generally discouraged and requires individualized risk–benefit assessment.[2],[4] Mild renal or hepatic impairment does not require dose adjustment, but pembrolizumab has not been adequately studied in moderate–severe organ dysfunction, so close monitoring is advised.[3],[4],[6] Regulatory guidance also warns of embryo‑fetal toxicity and recommends avoiding use in pregnancy and during breastfeeding.[4],[6]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

KEYNOTE-024 (NCT02142738) enrolled previously untreated metastatic NSCLC with PD-L1 TPS ≥50% and no EGFR/ALK alterations; pembrolizumab monotherapy improved OS (30.0 vs 14.2 months; HR 0.63) versus platinum doublets, establishing first-line monotherapy in this subgroup.[11]  

KEYNOTE-042 (NCT02220894) randomized untreated stage III/IV NSCLC with PD-L1 TPS ≥1% to pembrolizumab vs platinum chemotherapy, showing OS benefit across TPS ≥50%, ≥20% and ≥1% (e.g., 16.7 vs 12.1 months; HR 0.81 in TPS ≥1%), with greatest benefit at TPS ≥50% and minimal benefit in TPS 1–49%.[5,6,11,12]  

In metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC without EGFR/ALK alterations, KEYNOTE-189 (NCT02578680) demonstrated that adding pembrolizumab to pemetrexed–platinum nearly doubled median OS (22.0 vs 10.7 months; HR 0.56) and improved PFS (9.0 vs 4.9 months; HR 0.48) irrespective of PD-L1 expression.[3,13–16,18,19]
