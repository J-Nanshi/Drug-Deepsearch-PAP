## Drug Summary

Alectinib is an orally administered, highly potent, second‑generation anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK) tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) developed specifically for ALK‑rearranged non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).[1–4] ALK rearrangements occur in ~3–7% of NSCLC, predominantly adenocarcinoma, and define a molecular subset that is highly sensitive to ALK inhibition.[1,3,4] Alectinib received regulatory approval initially for advanced ALK‑positive NSCLC after crizotinib failure and is now a preferred first‑line standard of care for advanced ALK‑positive disease based on phase III trials (ALEX, J‑ALEX) showing superior progression‑free survival, intracranial control, and tolerability versus crizotinib.[2–6,8]

Mechanistically, alectinib is an ATP‑competitive, highly selective ALK inhibitor with minimal activity against MET or ROS1 and comparable potency against RET.[3] It shows approximately three‑fold greater in‑vitro ALK inhibition than crizotinib and retains activity against multiple crizotinib‑resistant ALK mutations (e.g., L1196M, C1156Y, F1174L, 1151Tins, L1152R), though not G1202R.[1,3] Alectinib is not a P‑glycoprotein substrate, achieves high cerebrospinal fluid penetration, and produces robust and durable intracranial responses, including in patients with large or symptomatic brain metastases.[1,4,7,8]

In precision oncology, detection of an ALK fusion (most commonly EML4‑ALK) via molecular profiling mandates ALK‑targeted therapy, with alectinib favored first‑line due to prolonged systemic PFS (~34 months in ALEX), strong CNS protection, and a more favorable grade ≥3 toxicity profile compared with crizotinib and some other next‑generation ALK TKIs.[2–6,8] This positions alectinib as the backbone of biomarker‑driven treatment algorithms for ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, with later‑generation inhibitors (e.g., brigatinib, lorlatinib) reserved for disease progressing on alectinib.[4–6]

## Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, most commonly driven by EML4–ALK fusions, the chimeric kinase constitutively activates canonical oncogenic cascades including RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK), PI3K–AKT–mTOR, and JAK–STAT, promoting proliferation, survival, and angiogenesis.[3],[8],[13] These fusions create ligand‑independent ALK signaling that is both necessary and sufficient for tumor maintenance, defining a classic oncogene‑addicted subset of NSCLC.[3],[7],[13]

Alectinib is a highly selective, second‑generation ALK tyrosine kinase inhibitor that binds the ATP‑binding site within the ALK kinase domain, thereby blocking ALK autophosphorylation and downstream signal transduction.[8],[10],[13] Preclinical work in EML4–ALK‑positive lung cancer models shows that alectinib inhibits ALK autophosphorylation and suppresses phosphorylation of STAT3 and ALK‑dependent survival pathways, leading to growth inhibition and apoptosis; its active metabolite M4 contributes substantially to this activity.[10] Compared with crizotinib, alectinib has greater ALK selectivity and potency and retains activity against several crizotinib‑resistant ALK mutants, although it is less effective against G1202R.[7],[10]

Downstream, alectinib‑mediated ALK blockade attenuates PI3K–AKT and MAPK/ERK signaling, as well as STAT3, reversing pro‑survival and EMT‑associated programs that are characteristic of EML4–ALK–driven lung tumors.[2],[3],[8],[13] Because ALK rearrangements are enriched in lung adenocarcinoma but also occur in other NSCLC histologies, this biochemical mechanism is broadly shared across ALK‑positive subtypes; clinical data and guidelines position alectinib as a preferred first‑line ALK TKI with strong systemic and CNS activity in advanced ALK‑positive NSCLC, including patients with brain metastases.[3],[8],[11],[14]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, the EML4‑ALK fusion constitutively activates three dominant signaling axes—MAPK/MEK/ERK, PI3K/AKT/mTOR, and JAK/STAT3—that drive proliferation, invasion and resistance to apoptosis; alectinib, as a highly selective ATP‑competitive ALK TKI, suppresses ALK autophosphorylation and is expected to down‑regulate all three cascades in sensitive tumors.[2],[6],[8],[11],[22] PI3K/AKT/mTOR is a central pro‑survival pathway in lung cancer, and ALK‑targeted therapies including alectinib down‑modulate AKT and S6 phosphorylation, shifting the balance toward apoptosis; however, bypass activation of PI3K/AKT via MET, ERBB3 or IGF‑1R can sustain survival signaling and contribute to alectinib resistance.[4],[6],[7],[13],[15],[18],[19],[21],[22]

RAS–MAPK signaling is a primary effector of EML4‑ALK: EML4‑ALK engages all three RAS isoforms, and EML4‑ALK‑positive lung adenocarcinoma cells are exquisitely MEK‑dependent, with MAPK reactivation (via KRAS gain, MAP2K1 mutations or feedback relief) driving both intrinsic and acquired resistance to ALK TKIs; combined ALK–MEK inhibition enhances depth and duration of response in preclinical models and is being explored clinically.[1],[6],[8],[12],[13],[18],[22],[23] JAK/STAT3, activated downstream of ALK and other RTKs, up‑regulates anti‑apoptotic and cell‑cycle genes and is repeatedly implicated as a bypass route in ALK‑TKI resistance, so persistent STAT3 activity can blunt alectinib‑induced apoptosis.[3],[6],[8],[11],[12],[19],[21],[23]

Beyond these canonical axes, ALK‑TOPK signaling has been identified as an additional pro‑proliferative module in ALK‑positive NSCLC: ALK directly phosphorylates TOPK at Y74, and co‑inhibition of ALK (alectinib) and TOPK more effectively suppresses ERK‑driven proliferation and promotes apoptosis than ALK blockade alone, highlighting MAPK‑linked cell‑cycle control as a key vulnerability.[3] Finally, apoptosis and cell‑cycle regulation represent the integrated outputs of these pathways: ALK‑driven PI3K/AKT/mTOR and RAS‑MAPK promote G1/S progression and mitochondrial apoptosis resistance, whereas effective ALK inhibition with alectinib restores caspase activation, PARP cleavage and G1 arrest; adaptive or bypass re‑engagement of these pathways underlies most clinically observed patterns of sensitivity and resistance in ALK‑positive NSCLC.[4],[6],[7],[8],[12],[13],[18],[22],[23]

## Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

Alectinib is established as first-line standard of care for advanced ALK‑positive NSCLC, a molecular subtype largely arising within non-squamous/adenocarcinoma histology.[6–8] In the global ALEX trial and Asian trials (J‑ALEX, ALESIA), alectinib (600 mg or 300 mg BID) produced markedly prolonged PFS versus crizotinib (median ~34–35 vs ~10–11 months) with high systemic and intracranial response rates and durable benefit, leading to its preferred status in guidelines.[6–8] Network meta-analysis of first‑line ALK‑rearranged NSCLC confirms that alectinib (300 and 600 mg) ranks among the top regimens for PFS, with favorable OS ranking and lower severe toxicity than ceritinib.[3]

Subtype-specific data beyond classic ALK‑positive adenocarcinoma are limited. Small series suggest ALK‑rearranged squamous NSCLC has shorter PFS on ALK TKIs than non-squamous disease, though individual case reports describe both robust responses and primary resistance to alectinib.[6] Alectinib has also induced partial responses in rare ALK‑positive large‑cell neuroendocrine carcinoma, indicating activity across some non-adenocarcinoma histologies.[6] Across subtypes, resistance emerges via secondary ALK mutations and bypass signaling, and sequencing to later‑generation TKIs (brigatinib, lorlatinib) after alectinib failure is effective, supporting mutation‑guided re‑biopsy strategies.[2,8]

## Contraindications and Safety

Alectinib has no absolute contraindications listed in EU risk management documentation beyond hypersensitivity to the active substance or excipients, but its use requires caution in patients with significant hepatic or renal impairment and pre‑existing bradycardia or cardiac disease.[2,4] In NCCN/ESMO‑aligned practice, alectinib is a preferred first‑line ALK inhibitor because of its generally favorable safety profile and low incidence of severe treatment‑related adverse events compared with other ALK TKIs.[3,5,7]

Hepatotoxicity is a key, label‑reflected risk for ALK TKIs; real‑world FAERS analysis shows significant disproportionality signals for hepatotoxicity with alectinib, characterized by elevated ALT/AST and hepatic enzymes, underscoring the need for vigilant liver function monitoring.[8] In ALEX, liver function abnormalities occurred in about one‑third of alectinib‑treated patients, often recurrent, with grade 3–4 events requiring dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation; guidelines recommend baseline testing and 2‑weekly monitoring for the first 3 months, then periodically, with review of concomitant hepatotoxic drugs and diet.[3]

Other clinically important toxicities include myalgia with CPK elevation, peripheral edema, bradycardia, renal toxicity, and rare interstitial lung disease (ILD).[3,5] Expert guidance recommends CPK every 2 weeks in the first month and as indicated thereafter, with temporary interruption and dose reduction for CPK >5–10× ULN, and counseling on sun protection and edema management.[3,5] Asymptomatic bradycardia typically does not require modification, but symptomatic or severe bradycardia warrants holding alectinib, avoiding other bradycardic agents, resuming at reduced dose once heart rate recovers, and considering permanent discontinuation or pacemaker if recurrent.[3]

Renal impairment during therapy is usually gradual; grade 3 renal injury prompts temporary suspension until creatinine improves to ≤1.5× ULN and resumption at reduced dose, while grade 4 renal injury requires permanent discontinuation.[3,4] Because alectinib is highly protein‑bound, hepatically metabolized (CYP3A4), and associated with hepatotoxicity signals, NCCN/ESMO‑consistent practice emphasizes careful baseline assessment of hepatic/renal function, avoidance or close monitoring of interacting or nephrotoxic/hepatotoxic co‑medications, and structured dose interruption/reduction algorithms to manage intolerable grade ≥2 or any grade 3–4 adverse events.[3,4,6,8]

## Key Clinical Trials

Alectinib’s clinical utility in ALK‑positive NSCLC is defined by one pivotal first‑line phase III trial versus crizotinib and supportive data from broader ALK‑TKI development programs.

**ALEX (BO28984, NCT02075840)** – This global, randomized, open‑label phase III study compared first‑line alectinib with crizotinib in treatment‑naïve, advanced ALK‑rearranged NSCLC.[5–7] Patients received alectinib or crizotinib at standard doses until progression. The trial demonstrated that alectinib more than doubled median progression‑free survival versus crizotinib (≈26 vs 10 months) and reduced CNS progression events, establishing second‑generation ALK TKIs such as alectinib as the preferred first‑line option in ALK‑positive disease.[6,7]

**Crizotinib‑resistant setting and transition to second‑generation ALK TKIs** – Earlier phase I/II and randomized trials with crizotinib showed median PFS ~10–11 months and highlighted frequent CNS failure and on‑target resistance mutations, prompting development of more potent, CNS‑penetrant agents.[2,4,6,7] Second‑generation ALK TKIs including alectinib were initially evaluated in crizotinib‑resistant patients and “were successful in overcoming many of the common resistance mechanisms” with improved CNS activity,[2] providing the efficacy and safety rationale for the subsequent ALEX trial design and for sequencing strategies in precision oncology.
