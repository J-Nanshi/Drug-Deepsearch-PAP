## 1. Drug Summary

Tremelimumab (Imjudo) is a fully human IgG2 monoclonal antibody directed against cytotoxic T‑lymphocyte–associated antigen‑4 (CTLA‑4/CD152), a negative regulator of T‑cell activation expressed primarily on activated and regulatory T cells.[4][16] By blocking CTLA‑4 interaction with B7 ligands (CD80/CD86) on antigen‑presenting cells, tremelimumab removes inhibitory signaling and shifts the balance toward CD28‑mediated co‑stimulation, leading to enhanced T‑cell activation, proliferation, and expansion of antitumor effector responses.[7][12][16] This mechanism underpins its role as an anti‑CTLA‑4 immune checkpoint inhibitor within combination immunotherapy strategies.

In lung cancer, the current FDA‑approved indication for tremelimumab is in combination with durvalumab and platinum‑based chemotherapy for adult patients with metastatic non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) lacking sensitizing EGFR mutations or ALK genomic aberrations.[1] This regimen operationalizes dual checkpoint blockade (CTLA‑4 plus PD‑L1) on a chemotherapy backbone, aligning with the broader paradigm that PD‑1/PD‑L1 plus CTLA‑4 combinations improve objective response rates, progression‑free survival, and overall survival versus chemotherapy alone in advanced lung cancer, particularly in biomarker‑unselected or PD‑L1–low populations.[14] From a precision oncology perspective, tremelimumab is therefore positioned not as a monotherapy but as a CTLA‑4–targeting component of rational combinations aimed at overcoming immune resistance and leveraging NSCLC’s predicted sensitivity to anti‑CTLA‑4–mediated T‑reg depletion in the tumor microenvironment.[9][16]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Tremelimumab is a fully human IgG2 monoclonal antibody that selectively binds cytotoxic T‑lymphocyte–associated antigen‑4 (CTLA‑4), a negative regulator of T‑cell activation expressed on recently activated conventional T cells and regulatory T cells (Tregs).[1] In secondary lymphoid organs, antigen‑presenting cells (APCs) present tumor antigens via MHC together with B7‑1/B7‑2 (CD80/CD86); engagement of CTLA‑4 outcompetes CD28 co‑stimulation and dampens the “second signal,” thereby limiting clonal expansion and effector differentiation.[1,12] By blocking CTLA‑4, tremelimumab restores CD28–B7 co‑stimulation, lowers the activation threshold for naïve and low‑affinity T cells, and broadens the repertoire of tumor‑reactive CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells generated during priming.[1]

In NSCLC, CTLA‑4 blockade with tremelimumab is typically combined with PD‑(L)1 inhibition (e.g., durvalumab) to couple enhanced priming with restoration of effector function in the tumor bed.[1,10,13] Preclinical and early clinical data show that anti‑CTLA‑4 and anti‑PD‑(L)1 act synergistically: CTLA‑4 inhibition amplifies de novo priming and expansion, while PD‑(L)1 blockade reverses exhaustion of activated T cells in the lung tumor microenvironment.[1,10,13] In EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, however, the tumor microenvironment is characterized by low tumor mutational burden, reduced functional CD8⁺ tumor‑infiltrating lymphocytes, and frequent T‑cell anergy, which biologically constrain the benefit of tremelimumab‑containing regimens.[9,12,13] The phase II ILLUMINATE trial of durvalumab+tremelimumab plus platinum–pemetrexed after EGFR‑TKI resistance showed only modest activity (ORR 31% in T790M‑negative and 21% in T790M‑positive cohorts; median PFS 6.5 and 4.9 months), indicating that even intensified T‑cell priming via CTLA‑4 blockade does not fully overcome the “cold,” EGFR‑driven immune phenotype.[10,13] In contrast, dual checkpoint blockade (PD‑1+CTLA‑4) has demonstrated clear survival benefit in EGFR‑wild‑type metastatic NSCLC, supporting greater relevance of tremelimumab‑like mechanisms in smoking‑associated, high‑TMB, non‑oncogene‑driven subtypes.[10,12]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Key immune/signaling pathways modulated downstream of CTLA‑4 blockade by tremelimumab (mainly in combination with durvalumab) in NSCLC, with inferred regulation and association with treatment sensitivity or resistance:

1. **T‑cell receptor (TCR) signaling / CD28 co‑stimulation (KEGG hsa04660)** – CTLA‑4 competes with CD28 for CD80/CD86; tremelimumab blocks CTLA‑4, allowing CD28–B7 engagement and stronger co‑stimulatory signaling, thereby enhancing early T‑cell activation and clonal expansion, which provides the mechanistic basis for combining tremelimumab with PD‑(L)1 inhibitors in NSCLC.[5][18][21]

2. **Effector CD8⁺ T‑cell activation vs. exhaustion (PD‑1, TIM‑3, LAG‑3, TIGIT modules)** – Dual CTLA‑4/PD‑(L)1 blockade expands and activates CD8⁺ T cells (e.g., proliferating Ki‑67⁺ CD8⁺ cells in tremelimumab–durvalumab regimens), but chronic antigen exposure and upregulation of multiple inhibitory receptors in NSCLC drive exhaustion and are linked to primary and acquired resistance to ICIs.[2][8][20][23]

3. **Regulatory T‑cell (Treg)–mediated suppression (FOXP3/CTLA‑4 axis)** – Intratumoral and nodal Tregs are enriched in NSCLC and suppress induction and function of autologous tumor‑specific CTLs; CTLA‑4 is a key Treg effector, and tremelimumab can deplete or functionally inhibit Tregs only partially in humans, so persistent or expanded Treg populations contribute to resistance to tremelimumab‑based regimens.[9][12–14][20]

4. **IFN‑γ / JAK–STAT inflammatory response (KEGG hsa04630)** – Effective CTLA‑4/PD‑(L)1 blockade increases tumor‑reactive T cells and IFN‑γ production, which enhances antigen presentation and Th1‑inflamed TMEs but also induces PD‑L1 and other checkpoints; this IFN‑γ–driven adaptive resistance underlies the rationale for combining tremelimumab with durvalumab in lung cancer.[5][17][21]

5. **PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitory pathway (KEGG hsa05235)** – PD‑L1 on NSCLC cells and myeloid cells engages PD‑1 on activated T cells, limiting effector function; durvalumab blocks PD‑L1–PD‑1/CD80 interactions, and addition of tremelimumab aims to further augment priming and intratumoral T‑cell activity, but excess checkpoint upregulation or immunosuppressive TMEs can still drive resistance.[2][5][18][22]

6. **Cancer‑immunity cycle / antigen release and presentation** – Chemotherapy and radiotherapy in NSCLC increase antigenicity, immunogenic cell death, and dendritic‑cell activation, which synergize with CTLA‑4/PD‑(L)1 blockade to improve priming; failure of antigen presentation (e.g., HLA‑I or B2M loss, DC defects) is a key resistance mechanism limiting benefit from tremelimumab‑containing combinations.[2][17][20][22]

7. **Immunosuppressive myeloid/CAF–Treg networks (VEGF, IDO1, CAF programs)** – VEGF, IDO1, MDSCs, M2‑like TAMs, and specific CAF subsets in NSCLC promote Treg recruitment, suppress DC function, and exclude CD8⁺ T cells, creating immune‑excluded or immunosuppressed phenotypes that correlate with poor response and resistance to CTLA‑4/PD‑(L)1 combinations.[2][17][20]

8. **Alternative checkpoint pathways (LAG‑3, TIM‑3, TIGIT, VISTA)** – Upregulation of non‑redundant inhibitory receptors on CD8⁺ T cells and Tregs during or after PD‑(L)1/CTLA‑4 blockade in NSCLC is associated with progression; co‑expression of PD‑1, LAG‑3, TIM‑3, and TIGIT on CD8⁺ T cells predicts primary resistance, supporting these pathways as escape routes from tremelimumab‑based therapy.[2][18][20][23]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

For unselected metastatic NSCLC, the phase III POSEIDON trial showed that adding a limited course of tremelimumab to durvalumab plus platinum chemotherapy improved progression-free and overall survival versus chemotherapy alone, with benefit observed across both squamous and non-squamous histologies and irrespective of PD-L1 expression.[10] Benefit appeared greater in TMB-high tumors, but clinically meaningful gains were still seen in TMB-low disease, indicating that tremelimumab can enhance chemo–PD-L1 blockade even outside classic “inflamed” biology.[10],[13] POSEIDON also reported overall survival benefit in genomically harder-to-treat subsets, including tumors with STK11, KEAP1, or KRAS mutations and PD-L1–negative disease, suggesting that CTLA‑4 co-blockade can partially overcome primary resistance mechanisms typical of “cold” adenocarcinomas.[2],[7],[10],[15]

In EGFR‑mutant, largely non-squamous NSCLC after TKI failure, the ILLUMINATE phase II trial found only modest activity of durvalumab–tremelimumab plus platinum–pemetrexed, with response rates and PFS similar to chemotherapy benchmarks, despite enrichment for PD-L1–high cases in some cohorts.[14],[16] These data are consistent with broader evidence that EGFR‑driven, low‑TMB tumors remain relatively resistant to checkpoint combinations and are not prime candidates for tremelimumab-based regimens.[1],[16] Direct data in SCLC or other lung subtypes for tremelimumab are lacking; current tremelimumab evidence in lung cancer is therefore confined to metastatic EGFR/ALK–wild type NSCLC and post‑TKI EGFR‑mutant adenocarcinoma.

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Tremelimumab is an anti–CTLA‑4 antibody used only in combination with durvalumab in NSCLC (e.g., POSEIDON regimen); dual checkpoint blockade increases immune‑related adverse events (irAEs) versus PD‑(L)1 monotherapy, particularly grade 3–4 toxicities and treatment discontinuations.[4,10,12] Meta‑analyses across solid tumors show durvalumab+tremelimumab produces similar overall AE rates but higher grade 3–4 treatment‑related AEs, more frequent diarrhea/enterocolitis, rash/pruritus, reduced appetite, and greater risk of discontinuation and (rare) treatment‑related death compared with durvalumab alone.[10,12] CTLA‑4–containing regimens are therefore relatively contraindicated in patients unable to tolerate high‑grade irAEs or prolonged immunosuppression.

Class irAEs of PD‑(L)1/CTLA‑4 blockade involve skin, gastrointestinal tract (colitis/enterocolitis), liver (hepatitis), endocrine organs (thyroid, pituitary, adrenal), lung (pneumonitis), kidney, and less commonly cardiovascular and neurologic systems.[3,4,8,10,12] In NSCLC, pneumonitis is particularly critical because of underlying lung disease and prior thoracic radiotherapy; PACIFIC and other durvalumab‑based programs highlight the need for careful baseline pulmonary assessment and prompt evaluation of new cough, dyspnea, or infiltrates, with early steroids and treatment interruption for suspected immune pneumonitis.[1,4,16]

NCCN/ASCO/ESMO/SITC‑aligned guidance recommends CTCAE‑based grading of irAEs, with continued therapy and close monitoring for grade 1 events, temporary holding and moderate‑dose steroids for most grade 2 toxicities, and high‑dose corticosteroids with treatment discontinuation for grade 3–4 events; permanent discontinuation of the same ICI class is generally advised after severe or life‑threatening irAEs, especially cardiac or neurologic events.[3,5] Protocols for durvalumab+tremelimumab specify permanent discontinuation for any grade >3 pneumonitis, colitis/enterocolitis, or nephritis, and allow hormone‑replacement management without interruption for isolated hypothyroidism.[11]

Special‑risk populations include patients with pre‑existing autoimmune disease, prior severe irAEs, or poor performance status. Retrospective lung‑cancer series with autoimmune comorbidities suggest ICIs can be used with acceptable toxicity when diseases are stable, but flares and de novo irAEs are more frequent, mandating individualized risk–benefit assessment, minimized background immunosuppression, and close multidisciplinary monitoring.[3] Re‑challenge after prior irAEs may be feasible in selected patients, but meta‑analytic data show non‑trivial recurrence risk and emphasize caution, particularly after severe cardiac or neurologic toxicity.[2,5]
