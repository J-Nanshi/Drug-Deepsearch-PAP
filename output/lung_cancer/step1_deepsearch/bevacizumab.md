## 1. Drug Summary

Bevacizumab is a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds all isoforms of vascular endothelial growth factor A (VEGF‑A), preventing interaction with VEGFR‑1 and VEGFR‑2 on endothelial cells and thereby inhibiting pro‑angiogenic signaling.[19] This blockade reduces endothelial proliferation, migration, and survival, normalizes abnormally permeable tumor vasculature, and can enhance delivery of cytotoxic agents and immune effector cells into tumors while diminishing VEGF‑driven immunosuppression in the tumor microenvironment.[18–19,21] Anti‑VEGF–induced vascular normalization and immune modulation provide a mechanistic rationale for combining bevacizumab with chemotherapy and immune checkpoint inhibitors.[18–19,21]

Regulatory labeling from the U.S. FDA specifies bevacizumab (Avastin) for first‑line treatment of unresectable, locally advanced, recurrent, or metastatic non‑squamous non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) in combination with carboplatin and paclitaxel, at 15 mg/kg IV every 3 weeks.[7,10] This indication reflects phase III data showing improved progression‑free survival and overall survival when bevacizumab is added to platinum‑doublet chemotherapy versus chemotherapy alone in advanced non‑squamous NSCLC.[1,22] Because of increased risks of severe or fatal hemorrhage, particularly hemoptysis, its use is restricted to non‑squamous histology and patients without recent serious bleeding.[7,10]

In contemporary NSCLC practice, bevacizumab is deployed mainly in combination regimens. It remains a backbone with platinum–pemetrexed or carboplatin–paclitaxel in advanced non‑squamous disease and is incorporated with PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy in some first‑line metastatic settings to exploit complementary anti‑angiogenic and immunologic mechanisms.[15,18,21–22]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Bevacizumab is a humanized monoclonal antibody that neutralizes soluble VEGF‑A, thereby preventing VEGF‑A from engaging VEGFR2 on tumor‑associated endothelial cells and interrupting a central pro‑angiogenic and immunosuppressive axis in NSCLC.[2,3,15] In lung tumors, VEGF‑A–VEGFR2 signaling promotes endothelial proliferation, abnormal, leaky vasculature, and hypoxia, which together restrict CD8⁺ T‑cell trafficking, favor myeloid‑derived suppressor cells and Tregs, and blunt dendritic‑cell maturation.[3,10,15] VEGF blockade with bevacizumab can “normalize” tumor vessels—reducing permeability and hypoxia, increasing perfusion, and facilitating lymphocyte and drug delivery—while partially reversing VEGF‑driven vascular and myeloid immunosuppression.[3,10,15]

In EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, EGFR activation upregulates VEGF and contributes to an immune‑cold, poorly infiltrated tumor microenvironment that is characteristically resistant to PD‑1/PD‑L1 monotherapy.[2,4,6,8,16] VEGF‑A–mediated angiogenesis and hypoxia reinforce this exclusionary milieu and are linked to EGFR‑TKI resistance via increased immunosuppressive cytokines and myeloid populations.[2,4,7,16] Dual targeting of EGFR and VEGF pathways (e.g., EGFR‑TKI plus bevacizumab) has improved progression‑free survival in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC and is thought to act by both suppressing oncogenic signaling and remodeling the vasculature/immune contexture.[2,4,16]

The same VEGF‑driven barriers underlie primary and acquired resistance to PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors in NSCLC more broadly, making VEGF blockade a rational partner for immunotherapy.[3,10,12,15] In IMpower150, the ABCP regimen (atezolizumab–bevacizumab–carboplatin–paclitaxel) improved PFS and OS versus bevacizumab–chemotherapy in advanced non‑squamous NSCLC, with a signal of benefit in patients with prior EGFR‑TKI exposure, supporting a model in which bevacizumab‑mediated vascular normalization and relief of VEGF‑driven immunosuppression enhance PD‑L1 blockade efficacy.[2,3,4,13,16] More recent bispecific antibodies simultaneously targeting VEGF‑A and PD‑(L)1 (e.g., PM8002/BNT327, ivonescimab) further exploit this biology, achieving high response rates in EGFR‑TKI–resistant EGFR‑mutant NSCLC and underscoring the centrality of VEGF‑A neutralization in conditioning the lung tumor microenvironment for effective immunotherapy.[2,14,16]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

- **VEGF–VEGFR2 angiogenic signaling** – Bevacizumab sequesters VEGF‑A, preventing activation of endothelial VEGFR2 and its downstream PI3K–AKT and MAPK/ERK cascades, thereby suppressing endothelial proliferation, migration, survival, and permeability that drive NSCLC angiogenesis and tumor growth.[6,10,12,13,16]

- **MAPK/ERK pathway** – In EGFR‑mutant, MET‑activated gefitinib‑resistant NSCLC, VEGF/VEGFR2 and MET form a positive feedback loop that sustains ERK signaling; adding bevacizumab to MET and EGFR inhibition disrupts this loop and more completely suppresses ERK phosphorylation, correlating with restored TKI sensitivity.[1,4]

- **PI3K–AKT/mTOR signaling** – VEGFR2 and multiple RTKs converge on PI3K–AKT/mTOR, a central survival and growth axis frequently activated in NSCLC; VEGF blockade with bevacizumab dampens this endothelial PI3K–AKT output, but compensatory activation via MET, EGFR, FGFR or other RTKs can maintain AKT signaling and contribute to resistance.[1,4,6,11]

- **p38 MAPK stress axis** – VEGF–VEGFR2 normally promotes survival through PI3K/AKT and ERK, while p38 MAPK can act as a negative regulator of VEGF/VEGFR2 signaling; chronic VEGF blockade may shift the balance toward stress‑activated p38 and apoptosis in tumor endothelium, but also select for VEGF‑independent survival programs.[8,10,12]

- **Vessel normalization / hypoxia–HIF‑1α signaling** – Bevacizumab‑mediated VEGF inhibition can transiently normalize abnormal tumor vasculature, improving perfusion and drug delivery, whereas excessive or prolonged VEGF blockade enhances hypoxia, HIF‑1α stabilization, and secondary upregulation of VEGF and other pro‑angiogenic factors, favoring a more invasive, resistant phenotype.[6,9,10,15,16]

- **Immune‑modulatory angiogenic signaling** – VEGF‑driven abnormal vasculature and TEC dysfunction suppress leukocyte adhesion and T‑cell infiltration; anti‑angiogenic therapy with bevacizumab can relieve this “vascular immune checkpoint,” and combinations with PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors improve outcomes in advanced NSCLC by converting an immune‑excluded to a more immune‑permissive microenvironment.[6,9,15]

- **Alternative pro‑angiogenic RTK pathways (FGF/FGFR, PDGF/PDGFR, Ang/Tie2, MET)** – Resistance to bevacizumab often involves upregulation or reliance on non‑VEGF angiogenic routes (e.g., FGFR, PDGFR, Ang–Tie2, MET), which activate overlapping PI3K–AKT and MAPK/ERK signaling in endothelial and tumor cells; simultaneous targeting of MET or multi‑target anti‑angiogenic agents is being explored to overcome VEGF‑A–selective blockade.[1,6,10,11,14]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In EGFR‑mutant advanced nonsquamous NSCLC, multiple randomized trials and meta‑analyses show that adding bevacizumab to erlotinib consistently prolongs PFS (HR ~0.59–0.63) and improves ORR, but does not translate into an OS advantage, with a clear increase in grade ≥3 toxicities such as hypertension, proteinuria, diarrhea, and rash.[1–5] Subgroup analyses indicate similar PFS benefit for the two dominant EGFR genotypes (exon 19 deletion and L858R), and meta‑analytic data suggest the combination may partially mitigate the historically poorer prognosis of L858R tumors, though OS remains unchanged.[1,4,5] In this setting, bevacizumab–erlotinib is generally considered a rational first‑line option when osimertinib is unavailable or contraindicated, but the higher toxicity burden and lack of OS gain limit its routine adoption as the preferred standard.[1,4–6]

For EGFR‑mutant NSCLC with baseline brain metastases, pooled randomized data show that bevacizumab–erlotinib significantly prolongs PFS versus erlotinib alone and trends toward better OS (HR for OS ~0.69, p≈0.06), consistent with bevacizumab’s anti‑edema and vascular‑normalizing effects in CNS disease.[1,4] These CNS‑directed benefits are mechanistically supported by evidence that VEGF blockade reduces vascular permeability and brain edema, which may delay intracranial progression under EGFR‑TKI therapy.[1] However, the signal is based on relatively small subgroups, and the emergence of CNS‑active third‑generation TKIs means bevacizumab is more often considered an adjunct in selected high‑risk or resistant cases rather than a universal standard.[4,7]

In EGFR‑mutant NSCLC with malignant pleural or pericardial effusion, exploratory analyses from JO25567 and NEJ026 indicate that erlotinib plus bevacizumab improves PFS over erlotinib alone irrespective of effusion status, with numerically higher ORR and fewer effusion worsenings in patients with baseline pleural/pericardial effusion.[2,8] VEGF is implicated in pleural fluid formation, and VEGF inhibition appears to blunt effusion progression, but patients requiring active effusion management were largely excluded, so evidence in heavily symptomatic malignant pleural effusion remains indirect.[8] Overall, these data support bevacizumab‑containing EGFR‑TKI regimens as a reasonable option for EGFR‑positive NSCLC with pleural involvement, particularly when effusion control is a clinical priority.[2,8]

In EGFR‑TKI–resistant EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, including cases progressing on osimertinib, resistance commonly arises through secondary EGFR mutations (e.g., C797S), bypass pathway activation, and histologic transformation; anti‑VEGF combinations are among several strategies under investigation.[3,7] A detailed case report of first‑line osimertinib resistance with leptomeningeal metastasis and EGFR exon 19 deletion/C797S demonstrated substantial clinical and radiographic improvement, with 35‑month OS, after treatment with erlotinib, platinum–pemetrexed, and bevacizumab, supporting the feasibility of triplet or quadruplet regimens in highly selected CNS‑progressive, TKI‑resistant disease.[3] More broadly, reviews of resistance pathways highlight VEGF/angiogenesis as an adaptive axis and support combining EGFR‑TKIs with anti‑VEGF agents or chemo‑immunotherapy (e.g., atezolizumab–bevacizumab–carboplatin–paclitaxel) after TKI failure, though robust randomized data specific to bevacizumab in post‑osimertinib EGFR‑mutant NSCLC remain limited.[6,7]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Bevacizumab is not the primary investigational product in the CUP protocol MX39795, but its safety management is governed by the current EMA Summary of Product Characteristics, which is designated as the Reference Safety Information.[1] Within this trial, bevacizumab is used only in combination with erlotinib, and there are specific exclusion criteria for that combination, reflecting key contraindications and safety concerns.[1] Patients with conditions that would increase the risk of serious toxicity from bevacizumab (e.g., significant comorbidities, poor organ function, or other protocol‑defined high‑risk features) are excluded from the erlotinib–bevacizumab arm.[1]

The protocol’s global safety framework emphasizes rigorous adverse‑event capture, immediate reporting of serious adverse events and adverse events of special interest, and structured causality and severity assessment.[1] This includes predefined procedures for grading severity, determining causality, and following patients after adverse events, which are critical for managing known bevacizumab‑associated risks such as severe hemorrhage, thromboembolic events, and impaired wound healing.[1] Safety management guidelines require dose interruption, modification, or discontinuation of investigational medicinal products when clinically significant toxicities occur, and mandate extended follow‑up for serious or unexpected events.[1]

In this setting, bevacizumab use is therefore constrained by protocol‑specific exclusion criteria, close monitoring, and conservative rules for treatment interruption, reflecting recognition of its established risks (hemorrhage, thrombosis, gastrointestinal and other perforations, and wound‑healing complications) even though those risks are not detailed in this document.[1] Overall, bevacizumab is deployed only in carefully selected patients under intensive pharmacovigilance, with a low threshold for discontinuation if serious safety signals emerge.[1]
