## 1. Drug Summary

Sotorasib (AMG 510; LUMAKRAS/LUMYKRAS) is an orally active, first‑in‑class, covalent inhibitor of KRAS G12C, a prevalent oncogenic driver in non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). KRAS mutations occur in ~23–30% of NSCLC, with G12C the most frequent subtype and present in ≈13% of lung adenocarcinomas, particularly in smoking‑associated disease.[4,14,17] The KRAS G12C substitution favors the active GTP‑bound state and constitutive signaling through RAS‑RAF‑MEK‑ERK and related pathways, promoting proliferation and survival.[4,9,10,17]

Sotorasib received US FDA accelerated approval in May 2021, and is indicated in the USA, EU, Canada and other regions as monotherapy for adults with KRAS G12C‑mutated, locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC who have received at least one prior systemic therapy (typically platinum‑based chemotherapy and/or PD‑1/PD‑L1 immunotherapy).[1,2,4,6,8,17] It is recommended at 960 mg orally once daily until disease progression or unacceptable toxicity and is generally positioned as a second‑ or later‑line precision therapy, often preferred over docetaxel in this molecularly defined population.[2,4,8,11,17]

Mechanistically, sotorasib selectively binds the switch‑II pocket of GDP‑bound KRAS G12C, forming an irreversible covalent bond with the mutant cysteine at codon 12, locking KRAS in its inactive state, preventing GDP–GTP exchange, and thereby blocking downstream MAPK (RAF–MEK–ERK) and associated signaling.[4,9,10,17–19] This allele‑specific, “OFF‑state” targeting spares wild‑type KRAS and underpins its role as a paradigm precision‑oncology agent for KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC, where it delivers objective response rates around 32–37% and median progression‑free survival ~6–7 months in previously treated patients (CodeBreaK 100, NCT03600883).[9–11,13,17,19]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Sotorasib is a first‑in‑class, orally available small‑molecule KRAS G12C inhibitor that covalently and irreversibly binds the mutant cysteine at codon 12 within the switch II allosteric pocket of KRAS in its inactive, GDP‑bound conformation.[9][10][15][21] By forming a covalent bond with Cys12 via its acrylamide warhead, sotorasib “locks” KRAS G12C in the GDP‑bound state, preventing SOS1‑mediated nucleotide exchange and blocking cycling back to the active GTP‑bound form.[8][9][10][15] This conformational trapping is highly selective for the G12C allele, with minimal modification of wild‑type KRAS, as shown by cysteine‑proteome profiling and lack of ERK inhibition in KRAS G12C‑negative cell lines.[10][15]

In KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC, oncogenic KRAS drives constitutive signaling through the RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) cascade and, to a lesser extent, PI3K–AKT–mTOR, promoting proliferation and survival.[2][8][18][21] Preclinical models demonstrate that sotorasib rapidly and durably suppresses MAPK signaling—measured by reduced ERK phosphorylation—with associated decreases in MEK and RSK phosphorylation, reduced viability, and tumor regression in KRAS G12C‑mutant lung cancer xenografts.[6][10][15][21] In sensitive KRAS G12C‑mutant cell lines, sotorasib can also reduce AKT phosphorylation, whereas in lines with PI3K‑pathway activation (e.g., PIK3CA mutations or PTEN loss) AKT signaling remains relatively preserved, indicating that PI3K–AKT can function as a bypass survival pathway.[6][22]

Clinically, this biochemical mechanism translates into selective antitumor activity in KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC: sotorasib monotherapy produces objective response rates of ~32–37% and disease control in ~80–90% of previously treated patients, with median progression‑free survival around 6–7 months.[9][10][17][21] However, adaptive and acquired resistance frequently emerge through reactivation of RAS–MAPK and PI3K signaling (via RTK/SHP2/SOS1 upregulation, secondary RAS alterations, or PI3K–AKT activation) and through co‑mutational contexts that modulate dependence on KRAS output.[4][6][7][17][22][23] Large clinico‑genomic series in advanced KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC show that co‑mutations in KEAP1, SMARCA4, and CDKN2A define subgroups with inferior outcomes on sotorasib or other KRAS G12C inhibitors, whereas TP53 or STK11 alone are less consistently predictive.[5][11][23] These data support a model in which the core action of sotorasib—covalent inactivation of KRAS G12C and suppression of MAPK (and variably PI3K) signaling—is consistent across tumors, but the depth and durability of pathway shutdown, and thus clinical benefit, are strongly conditioned by co‑alterations that determine signaling redundancy and adaptive capacity in KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC.[2][5][17][21][23]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Sotorasib covalently locks KRAS G12C in its inactive GDP-bound state and acutely suppresses the RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK/MAPK cascade; near-complete ERK inhibition is observed in KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC models, yet adaptive ERK reactivation via upstream RTKs and secondary RAS/MAPK lesions underlies a major axis of resistance and supports combinations with SHP2, MEK or ERK inhibitors.[1],[3],[6],[7]

The PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway, a parallel KRAS effector, can remain active or become reactivated through PIK3CA or PTEN alterations and RTK (e.g., MET, HER2) amplification, providing a KRAS‑independent survival route; PI3K/AKT/mTOR hyperactivation is repeatedly implicated in primary and acquired resistance to KRAS G12C inhibitors and is a key target for vertical (KRAS + mTOR/PI3K) or horizontal (KRAS + PI3K/AKT) co‑inhibition.[2],[3],[5],[7],[10],[16]

Upstream RTKs—including EGFR/HER2 (pan‑ERBB), MET, FGFR and IGF1R—are commonly upregulated or amplified in sotorasib‑resistant KRAS‑mutant LUAD, restoring MAPK and PI3K signaling; MET amplification and ERBB/Aurora kinase–driven bypass signaling are prototypical resistance pathways, and dual ERBB/AURK blockade can eradicate sotorasib‑refractory clones in preclinical LUAD models.[2],[3],[4],[5],[7]

Immune and inflammatory signaling, particularly PD‑L1–JAK2–STAT3–IL‑6, is induced in AMG‑510/sotorasib‑resistant KRAS G12C tumors, driving myeloid‑derived suppressor cell accumulation, CD8⁺ T‑cell exclusion and an immunosuppressive TIME; PD‑L1 inhibition reprograms this milieu and re‑sensitizes resistant tumors to sotorasib, highlighting this axis as a non‑cell‑autonomous resistance pathway.[10]–[12]

Epigenetic pathways centered on the ANP32A–YEATS4–H3K27Ac axis regulate transcriptional programs required for sotorasib‑induced apoptosis; loss of ANP32A reduces H3K27 acetylation and p53 pathway activation, conferring sotorasib resistance that can be reversed by histone deacetylase inhibition, implicating chromatin acetylation as a determinant of sensitivity.[9]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In NSCLC, KRAS G12C is the dominant KRAS variant in lung adenocarcinoma, present in ~13% of adenocarcinomas and enriched in smokers, defining a distinct oncogene‑addicted LUAD subtype for which sotorasib is specifically approved.[1],[5],[11],[13] Across trials and real‑world series, sotorasib produces objective responses in roughly one‑third of heavily pretreated KRAS G12C‑mutant non‑squamous NSCLC, with median PFS ~6–7 months in trials and ~3–5 months in broader practice, confirming meaningful but non‑curative activity in this molecular subset.[5],[11],[13],[16]

Within KRAS G12C‑mutant adenocarcinoma, co‑mutations in STK11, KEAP1, TP53, SMARCA4 and CDKN2A/B stratify biology and sotorasib sensitivity. Exploratory CodeBreaK 100 analyses and subsequent syntheses show responses across PD‑L1 and TMB strata, but consistently higher ORR in STK11‑mutant/KEAP1‑wild‑type tumors and markedly poorer outcomes in KEAP1‑mutant disease, indicating KEAP1‑driven primary resistance whereas isolated STK11 loss does not abrogate benefit.[5],[9],[13],[14],[15] Real‑world data further demonstrate that KEAP1 co‑mutation shortens PFS and OS on sotorasib, while TP53 and STK11 co‑mutations alone have more modest or context‑dependent impact, often influencing durability rather than initial response.[11],[14],[15]

Central nervous system involvement represents a clinically important subtype context. Consensus and observational data indicate that although sotorasib has some intracranial activity, brain metastases are common in KRAS G12C‑mutant non‑squamous NSCLC and real‑world intracranial response rates around 20% with short intracranial PFS highlight limited CNS control and rapid emergence of resistance, prompting evaluation of combinations (e.g., with anti‑VEGF or radiation) and next‑generation inhibitors for patients with high CNS burden.[9],[13],[16] KRAS G12C also occurs in lung squamous cell carcinoma (≈3–4% of squamous NSCLC), but sotorasib evidence is largely extrapolated from small numbers within mixed‑histology trials; available epidemiology suggests similar KRAS‑ and co‑mutation‑driven resistance mechanisms in squamous tumors, potentially compounded by their higher genomic complexity and distinct immune milieu.[11],[12],[15]  



## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Current FDA and EMA labeling list no formal absolute contraindications beyond hypersensitivity to sotorasib or excipients; however, both regulators emphasize use only under the supervision of experienced oncology prescribers and within the approved KRAS G12C‑mutated NSCLC population.[1–3,7,8] Lumykras/Lumakras is under additional/conditional monitoring in the EU, reflecting an evolving safety database.[3,4,8]

Hepatotoxicity is a major identified risk. The FDA label highlights hepatotoxicity (ALT/AST and bilirubin elevations, liver injury) as a key warning, recommending liver function tests every 3 weeks for the first 3 months, then monthly, with dose interruption, reduction, or permanent discontinuation based on severity.[5,7] EMA product information similarly describes hepatotoxicity that can progress to drug‑induced liver injury or hepatitis and provides detailed dose‑modification algorithms, including permanent discontinuation when ALT/AST >3× ULN with bilirubin >2× ULN in the absence of alternative causes.[1,2] A dedicated PK study indicates that moderate or severe hepatic impairment does not substantially alter sotorasib exposure, supporting no dose adjustment in these settings, but careful monitoring remains warranted.[6]

Interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis is an important serious risk. FDA and EMA advise immediate treatment interruption for suspected ILD/pneumonitis and permanent discontinuation if confirmed without another cause, with management including corticosteroids and supportive care.[1,2,5,7]

Drug–drug interactions are clinically significant. Sotorasib exposure is reduced by proton pump inhibitors and H2 antagonists; labels recommend avoiding these or separating dosing from local antacids (LUMAKRAS 4 hours before or 10 hours after).[5,7] Strong CYP3A4 inducers should be avoided, and co‑administration with sensitive CYP3A4 or P‑gp substrates should be minimized or dose‑adjusted due to risk of reduced substrate efficacy (CYP3A4) or increased substrate toxicity (P‑gp).[5,7] EMA materials also flag use in moderate/severe hepatic impairment as important potential risk within the risk‑management plan, reinforcing the need for enhanced vigilance.[3]
