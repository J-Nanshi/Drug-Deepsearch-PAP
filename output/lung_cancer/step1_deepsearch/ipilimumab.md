## 1. Drug Summary

Ipilimumab is a fully human IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds cytotoxic T‑lymphocyte–associated protein 4 (CTLA‑4) on activated T cells, blocking its high‑affinity interaction with B7‑1/B7‑2 (CD80/CD86) on antigen‑presenting cells and thereby preventing inhibitory signaling that dampens early T‑cell priming.[7,13] CTLA‑4 blockade enhances CD28‑mediated costimulation, expands effector and memory T‑cell pools, and reduces regulatory T‑cell–mediated immunosuppression, augmenting antitumor immunity predominantly in lymphoid tissues.[6–8]

Ipilimumab monotherapy has shown little efficacy in NSCLC and SCLC and is not used alone in lung cancer.[1,3,5,11] Early phase II/III trials combining ipilimumab with platinum‑based chemotherapy in advanced NSCLC and SCLC produced at best modest gains in immune‑related progression‑free survival without consistent overall‑survival benefit and with substantial immune‑related toxicity, limiting further development of chemo–ipilimumab doublets.[3,5]

The current role of ipilimumab in lung cancer is almost entirely in dual checkpoint blockade with PD‑1 inhibitors, particularly nivolumab. In advanced NSCLC without actionable EGFR/ALK alterations, nivolumab plus low‑dose ipilimumab (with or without limited platinum‑doublet chemotherapy) improves response rates and long‑term overall survival compared with chemotherapy alone, including in PD‑L1–negative disease, and has led to FDA approval of nivolumab–ipilimumab as a first‑line option in metastatic NSCLC.[3,6,8,9,11,12] In SCLC, nivolumab–ipilimumab improves response preferentially in tumors with high tumor mutational burden but has not consistently prolonged overall survival in phase III maintenance or first‑line settings; CTLA‑4–containing regimens in SCLC remain investigational.[1–3]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Ipilimumab is a fully human IgG1 monoclonal antibody against CTLA‑4, an inhibitory receptor expressed on activated effector T cells and constitutively on regulatory T cells (Tregs).[2][9][13][16] CTLA‑4 competes with CD28 for binding to B7‑1/B7‑2 (CD80/CD86) on antigen‑presenting cells in lymphoid organs, dampening T‑cell priming, IL‑2 production, and clonal expansion.[2][5][14][16] By blocking CTLA‑4, ipilimumab restores CD28‑mediated co‑stimulation, enhancing de novo priming and expansion of tumor‑reactive CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells and increasing intratumoral effector/Treg ratios.[2][5][9][16]

In early‑stage NSCLC, neoadjuvant ipilimumab plus chemotherapy induced profound activation of circulating and tumor‑infiltrating CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells without major depletion of circulating Tregs, supporting a primarily priming/activation mechanism in this setting.[2] In advanced NSCLC and SCLC, combining ipilimumab with PD‑1 blockade (e.g., nivolumab) provides complementary early (CTLA‑4) and late (PD‑1) checkpoint release, with particular benefit in tumors with high tumor mutational burden, where abundant neoantigens amplify CTLA‑4–dependent priming and CD8⁺ cytotoxic responses.[3][6][7][8]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

For metastatic NSCLC, current evidence in the provided sources focuses on PD‑1 inhibitors, neoadjuvant combinations, and chemotherapy backbones rather than ipilimumab-containing regimens. Neoadjuvant PD‑1 inhibitor plus platinum chemotherapy in resectable stage I–III NSCLC yields high major pathological response and pathological complete response rates with acceptable toxicity, supporting strong immunogenicity of NSCLC but not directly defining a role for CTLA‑4 blockade or ipilimumab in this setting.[4] Ongoing neoadjuvant and perioperative trials testing nivolumab with or without ipilimumab in earlier-stage NSCLC (e.g., NCT02998528, NCT03158129) aim to clarify whether adding CTLA‑4 inhibition improves cure rates, but mature, lung‑subtype–specific outcome data for ipilimumab are not yet available.[5,8]

In extensive‑stage SCLC, first‑line carboplatin/etoposide plus ipilimumab (ICE) showed high objective response but substantial toxicity; baseline autoantibody positivity correlated with better outcomes and severe neurologic immune toxicity, suggesting a narrow biomarker‑defined subgroup may benefit.[2] However, in the phase III CheckMate 451 trial (NCT02538666), maintenance nivolumab–ipilimumab after chemotherapy did not improve overall survival versus placebo, though higher tumor mutational burden (≥13 mut/Mb) was associated with a trend toward better survival, and PD‑L1 combined positive score appeared prognostic rather than predictive.[7] These data indicate that, in SCLC, ipilimumab is not standard of care, and any precision‑oncology use would likely require stringent biomarker selection (e.g., autoantibodies, high TMB) and careful toxicity management.[2,3,7]

Evidence specific to CNS metastases from NSCLC for ipilimumab‑containing regimens is not available in the provided sources; NCT05012254 is evaluating nivolumab–ipilimumab plus chemotherapy in stage IV lung cancer with brain metastases, but results are pending.[1]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Ipilimumab carries a boxed warning for severe and potentially fatal immune‑mediated toxicities (enterocolitis, hepatitis, dermatitis including TEN, neuropathies, endocrinopathies), reflecting systemic T‑cell activation; the FDA label lists no absolute contraindications but mandates permanent discontinuation and high‑dose systemic corticosteroids for severe reactions.[3,6,7] Combination nivolumab–ipilimumab further increases the incidence and grade of immune‑related adverse events (irAEs) compared with PD‑1 monotherapy, including higher rates of colitis, transaminase elevation, and pneumonitis, and more frequent treatment discontinuation.[5,8,18] In NSCLC, dual PD‑1/CTLA‑4 regimens are effective but recognized as higher‑toxicity options in perioperative and advanced‑disease settings.[11,12,16]

For lung cancer patients, pre‑existing autoimmune disease, prior severe irAEs, and significant baseline organ dysfunction (notably severe interstitial lung disease, uncontrolled cardiovascular disease, or prior cardiotoxic therapies) warrant extreme caution, as combination ICI and prior thoracic radiotherapy are linked to increased risks of pneumonitis and pericardial disease.[11,20,23,24] Cardiovascular irAEs (myocarditis, perimyocarditis, pericarditis, arrhythmias, heart failure, vasculitis) are rare (<1%) but highly lethal; risk is higher with PD‑1 plus CTLA‑4 combinations than with monotherapy, and myocarditis often occurs early in the treatment course.[17,19,20,23,24]

Pneumonitis and colitis are particularly critical in NSCLC, where limited pulmonary reserve and perioperative use can magnify morbidity; combination nivolumab–ipilimumab significantly increases pneumonitis risk over nivolumab alone.[11,18] Consensus immunotherapy and NCCN‑aligned guidance recommend holding ICIs for most grade ≥2 irAEs, initiating systemic glucocorticoids (0.5–2 mg/kg/day prednisone equivalent, IV for life‑threatening events), and using additional immunosuppressants (e.g., infliximab, mycophenolate) for steroid‑refractory toxicities, with permanent discontinuation of ipilimumab‑containing regimens for grade 4 or steroid‑refractory serious irAEs.[3,6,7,9,11,14,19] Continuous multidisciplinary monitoring (oncology, pulmonology, cardiology, gastroenterology, endocrinology) and at least 1‑year post‑treatment surveillance are advised to detect late‑onset irAEs in lung cancer populations.[11,19,23,24]



## 8. Key Clinical Trials

- **CheckMate 227 Part 1 (NCT02477826)** – Phase III, first-line stage IV/recurrent NSCLC without EGFR/ALK alterations; nivolumab + ipilimumab versus platinum-doublet chemotherapy in PD‑L1 ≥1% and <1% populations improved 5‑year overall survival (PD‑L1 ≥1%: 24% vs 14%; PD‑L1 <1%: 19% vs 7%) and prolonged duration of response, with most 5‑year survivors off treatment and no new safety signals.[1,4,7]

- **CheckMate 9LA (NCT03215706)** – Phase III, first-line metastatic/recurrent NSCLC without sensitizing EGFR/ALK alterations; nivolumab + ipilimumab plus two cycles of platinum-doublet chemotherapy versus four cycles of chemotherapy alone achieved sustained overall survival benefit at 5 years (HR 0.73; 5‑year OS 18% vs 11%), with consistent advantage across PD‑L1 strata, histologies, and in patients with baseline brain metastases, and durable responses without new long‑term safety concerns.[3,10,11]

- **Japanese subanalysis of CheckMate 227 Part 1 (NCT02477826)** – Phase III subgroup analysis in 143 Japanese patients with metastatic NSCLC; nivolumab + ipilimumab versus chemotherapy yielded 5‑year OS rates of 46% vs 34% (PD‑L1 ≥1%) and 36% vs 19% (PD‑L1 <1%), with markedly longer median duration of response (up to 59.1 vs 7.1 months) and 59% of 5‑year survivors off treatment for ≥3 years without subsequent systemic therapy.[2,5]
