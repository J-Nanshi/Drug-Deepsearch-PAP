## Drug Summary

Larotrectinib (VITRAKVI) is an orally administered, highly selective small‑molecule inhibitor of the tropomyosin receptor kinases TRKA, TRKB, and TRKC, encoded by *NTRK1–3*.[4][5][8] By binding to the TRK kinase domain, it blocks signaling from oncogenic *NTRK* fusion proteins, reducing proliferation and survival in TRK fusion–driven tumors.[5][8] The drug is metabolized predominantly via CYP3A4 and is available as 25‑mg and 100‑mg capsules and a 20‑mg/mL oral solution.[4][5][8]

Larotrectinib received accelerated, tumor‑agnostic FDA approval in 2018 for adult and pediatric patients with solid tumors that harbor an *NTRK* gene fusion (without a known acquired resistance mutation), are metastatic or unresectable (or where surgery would cause severe morbidity), and have no satisfactory alternative treatments or have progressed after prior therapy.[4][5][8] This approval was based on pooled phase I/II basket trials (LOXO‑TRK‑14001, SCOUT, NAVIGATE; including NCT02122913 and NCT02576431) showing an overall response rate of ~75% and durable responses across diverse histologies.[5][6][9][16]

In lung cancer, *NTRK* fusions represent a rare but actionable driver subset of non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), with reported incidence generally <5% and often much lower.[5][9][16] Larotrectinib and entrectinib have both demonstrated meaningful activity in *NTRK* fusion–positive NSCLC within multi‑tumor basket cohorts, leading contemporary NSCLC reviews and guidelines to list larotrectinib as a targeted option for advanced or metastatic *NTRK* fusion–positive disease alongside other biomarker‑directed therapies.[9][14][16] Routine broad molecular profiling using next‑generation sequencing (e.g., FoundationOne CDx, which is FDA‑approved as a companion diagnostic for *NTRK1/2/3* fusions in solid tumors eligible for larotrectinib) is therefore recommended to identify this small but highly responsive lung cancer population.[1][9][16]

## Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Larotrectinib is a first‑in‑class, highly selective, CNS‑active oral inhibitor of TRKA/B/C that blocks ATP binding to the TRK catalytic domain with low nanomolar potency and minimal activity on other kinases.[13] TRK receptors, encoded by NTRK1/2/3, normally transduce neurotrophin signals via ligand‑induced dimerization, autophosphorylation, and recruitment of adaptor proteins that activate MAPK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR, and PLCγ‑PKC pathways controlling proliferation, survival, differentiation, and growth.[7],[13],[14] In NSCLC and other solid tumors, chromosomal rearrangements juxtapose the 3′ NTRK kinase domain to 5′ partner genes, creating fusion proteins with intact TRK kinase domains and partner‑derived dimerization motifs that drive constitutive, ligand‑independent signaling and oncogenic “TRK addiction.”[7],[13],[14]

By occupying the ATP‑binding pocket of TRKA/B/C, larotrectinib prevents autophosphorylation of the fusion kinase and downstream recruitment of signaling complexes, thereby extinguishing MAPK and PI3K cascades and related PLC‑dependent signaling in NTRK‑fusion–positive tumor cells.[7],[13],[14] This results in growth arrest and tumor regression across histologies, including NSCLC: pooled phase I/II analyses show high objective response rates in NTRK‑fusion lung cancers, and a dedicated series of 26 lung cancer patients treated with larotrectinib achieved an ORR of 83% with durable responses.[4],[14] A case report of SQSTM1–NTRK2 fusion–positive NSCLC with ongoing complete remission on larotrectinib further supports that this mechanism extends to TRKB‑driven disease, indicating that in NTRK‑rearranged NSCLC the drug acts by shutting down a central TRK‑MAPK/PI3K oncogenic node rather than exploiting histology‑specific biology.[14]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

Larotrectinib targets constitutively active TRK fusion proteins, which drive key growth and survival pathways in NTRK‑rearranged NSCLC. TRK fusions upregulate the MAPK cascade (RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK), PI3K–AKT signaling, and PLCγ-dependent signaling, promoting proliferation, survival, and metastasis across tumor types, including lung cancer.[6,7] In MSigDB/KEGG, these correspond to canonical pathways such as KEGG hsa04010 (MAPK signaling), hsa04151 (PI3K–Akt signaling), and hsa04070 (phosphatidylinositol/PLC signaling), while Reactome and MSigDB contain neurotrophin/NGF–TRKA signaling gene sets that capture upstream TRK activation.[6,7]

Inhibition of TRK by larotrectinib downregulates these pathways, and clinical imaging in NTRK1‑rearranged NSCLC demonstrates tumor regression consistent with suppression of MAPK, PI3K, and PLCγ signaling.[6] However, off‑target resistance mechanisms frequently converge on reactivation of the same cascades: reactivation of RAF–MEK–ERK signaling, often via BRAF V600E or KRAS G12D, and MET amplification can restore MAPK and PI3K–AKT activity and drive resistance to TRK inhibitors in NTRK‑fusion tumors, including lung cancer.[1,7] These bypass RTK and downstream alterations are therefore functionally “upregulated” resistance pathways that maintain MAPK/PI3K output despite continued TRK blockade, supporting combination strategies (e.g., TRK plus MEK or MET inhibition) in resistant NTRK‑positive NSCLC.[1,7,8]  



## Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic NTRK fusion–positive NSCLC (predominantly adenocarcinoma), larotrectinib yields high and durable activity. In a pooled analysis of 20 patients with TRK fusion–positive lung cancer treated on NCT02576431 and NCT02122913, the investigator-assessed ORR was 73% among 15 evaluable patients, with median DOR 33.9 months, PFS 35.4 months, and OS 40.7 months, exceeding historical chemotherapy and chemo‑immunotherapy benchmarks.[3] Responses were rapid and deep, including one complete response and durable disease control in heavily pretreated patients.[3] These data support larotrectinib as preferred first‑line systemic therapy in advanced TRK fusion–positive NSCLC when the fusion is identified before initial treatment.[3,10]

In TRK fusion–positive NSCLC with baseline brain metastases, larotrectinib demonstrates clinically meaningful intracranial efficacy. In the lung cancer–specific analysis, patients with CNS metastases had an ORR of 63%, with durable systemic DOR and PFS similar to the overall cohort, consistent with larotrectinib’s CNS-active profile.[3] A detailed case report of EPS15–NTRK1–rearranged metastatic lung adenocarcinoma with >10 untreated brain metastases showed a brisk systemic and intracranial partial response by 4 weeks and complete intracranial response by 16 weeks on NAVIGATE (NCT02576431), without prior CNS radiation.[2] Broader TRK inhibitor series also report ~70% intracranial ORR in adults with brain metastases, reinforcing robust CNS penetration and durable control across TRK fusion–positive solid tumors, including NSCLC.[10,16]

## Contraindications and Safety

Larotrectinib has no formal contraindications in the FDA label, but significant warnings relate to central nervous system (CNS) effects, skeletal fractures, hepatotoxicity, and embryo‑fetal toxicity.[10–12,16] CNS adverse reactions (dizziness, cognitive impairment, mood and sleep disorders) are common; patients should avoid driving or hazardous activities if symptomatic, and dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation is recommended for Grade ≥3 events.[10,12,16] This is particularly relevant in NSCLC, where baseline brain metastases and CNS‑active polypharmacy are frequent.

Hepatotoxicity is a key dose‑limiting toxicity: ALT/AST, alkaline phosphatase, and bilirubin must be checked before treatment, every 2 weeks for the first 1–2 months, then monthly or as clinically indicated.[10,12,14,16] For Grade ≥3 transaminase elevations, Vitrakvi should be withheld until recovery and then resumed at a reduced dose, or permanently discontinued for severe or recurrent events.[10,14,16] Moderate to severe hepatic impairment (Child‑Pugh B/C) requires a 50% reduction in starting dose.[9–12,16]

Larotrectinib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4; strong or moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors increase exposure and require dose reduction or close toxicity monitoring, while strong or moderate inducers decrease exposure and mandate dose escalation if co‑administration cannot be avoided.[9–12,14,16] In lung cancer patients, careful review of concomitant TKIs, antifungals, anticonvulsants, and immunotherapy regimens is essential to avoid interactions and overlapping hepatotoxicity or pneumonitis.

Embryo‑fetal toxicity necessitates effective contraception for females of reproductive potential and avoidance of breastfeeding during therapy; lactation is not recommended.[9–12,14,16] EMA notes that most adverse effects (fatigue, dizziness, gastrointestinal symptoms, and liver enzyme elevations) are mild to moderate and manageable with dose modifications, supporting an acceptable benefit–risk profile in advanced NTRK‑fusion NSCLC when standard monitoring and risk‑minimisation measures are applied.[13]

## Key Clinical Trials

Larotrectinib’s lung cancer evidence is derived from three pivotal, tissue‑agnostic trials: the adult phase I dose‑escalation study LOXO‑TRK‑14001 (NCT02122913), the pediatric/AYA phase I/II SCOUT trial (NCT02637687), and the phase II basket trial NAVIGATE (NCT02576431), which together form the core registration dataset and are repeatedly pooled in integrated analyses.[4],[10],[11],[13],[16]

In a pooled analysis of 159 patients with TRK fusion–positive solid tumors across these three trials, the overall objective response rate (ORR) was 79%, with durable median progression‑free survival (PFS) of 28.3 months and good tolerability; within this dataset, NSCLC patients achieved an ORR of 75% (9/12) by RECIST, indicating that lung cancer activity was comparable to other histologies.[4],[10] A modeling study that extracted NSCLC‑specific survival data for 12 patients with TRK fusion–positive metastatic NSCLC treated on NCT02122913 and NCT02576431 projected a median total survival of 7.0 years and suggested superior life‑years and quality‑adjusted life‑years versus entrectinib, despite small sample size and 13‑month in‑trial follow‑up.[3]

Multiple comparative‑effectiveness analyses have subsequently used the pooled larotrectinib population from LOXO‑TRK‑14001, SCOUT, and NAVIGATE as the “trial arm” against real‑world standard‑of‑care cohorts, including adults with advanced TRK fusion–positive NSCLC.[1],[8] After matching on tumor type and line of therapy, larotrectinib was associated with substantially longer overall survival and PFS than non‑TRK‑inhibitor regimens, reinforcing that the high response rates observed in the pivotal trials translate into clinically meaningful survival gains for lung cancer patients with *NTRK* fusions.[1],[3],[5],[8],[10],[11]
