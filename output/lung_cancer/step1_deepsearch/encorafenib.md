## 1. Drug Summary

Encorafenib is an oral, selective kinase inhibitor that targets mutant BRAF within the RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway, a central signaling cascade controlling proliferation, differentiation, and survival.[3,7] In BRAF V600E–mutant tumors, constitutive BRAF kinase activity hyperactivates MEK and ERK, driving oncogene addiction to MAPK signaling; combining BRAF and MEK inhibition provides deeper and more durable pathway suppression than either alone and has become a standard targeted strategy in several solid tumors.[2,3,7,16] Encorafenib is approved (as BRAFTOVI) in fixed-dose combination with the MEK inhibitor binimetinib (MEKTOVI) for unresectable or metastatic BRAF V600E/K–mutant melanoma, and in combination with cetuximab (± mFOLFOX6) for BRAF V600E–mutant metastatic colorectal cancer (mCRC).[4,8–11,16]

In melanoma and mCRC, encorafenib-based regimens improve progression-free and overall survival versus earlier BRAF inhibitors, with a safety profile characterized mainly by gastrointestinal toxicity, fatigue, ocular events, hepatotoxicity, hemorrhage, cardiomyopathy, and new primary malignancies; many events are mitigated by pairing with a MEK inhibitor and by proactive monitoring.[4,8–11,16] These experiences established encorafenib as a potent BRAF V600–directed agent and validated dual BRAF/MEK blockade as a precision-oncology backbone across BRAF-dependent histologies.[2,3,7,11,16]

In non–small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), BRAF mutations occur in ~2–4% of cases, with V600E representing the dominant class I, kinase-activating allele and a clear actionable driver.[2,5,12,14,15] The phase II PHAROS trial (NCT03915951) evaluated encorafenib 450 mg once daily plus binimetinib 45 mg twice daily in BRAF V600E–mutant metastatic NSCLC and demonstrated high objective response rates (75% in treatment‑naïve and 46% in previously treated patients) and prolonged progression-free survival, with a safety profile consistent with melanoma experience.[12,15] These data supported US FDA approval of encorafenib plus binimetinib for BRAF V600E–mutant metastatic NSCLC and positioned this combination, alongside dabrafenib plus trametinib, as a preferred first‑line targeted option, translating the drug’s BRAF‑selective, MAPK‑suppressive mechanism into a precision-oncology standard for this molecularly defined lung cancer subset.[2,9–13,15]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Encorafenib is a second‑generation, ATP‑competitive RAF inhibitor in the type 1.5 (αC‑OUT/DFG‑IN) class that shows high selectivity for RAF kinases and was designed to more potently inhibit BRAFV600 than earlier agents.[7,9] Like vemurafenib and dabrafenib, it preferentially binds monomeric BRAFV600 over RAF dimers, so it effectively suppresses MAPK signaling in BRAFV600E‑mutant cells but can paradoxically activate ERK in BRAF‑wild‑type, RAS‑active settings through RAF dimerization.[5,7] In BRAFV600E, the valine‑to‑glutamate substitution at codon 600 produces a constitutively active, RAS‑independent BRAF monomer, driving continuous MEK and ERK phosphorylation and transcriptional programs that promote proliferation and survival.[3,6,9,11] By occupying the ATP‑binding pocket of mutant BRAF, encorafenib blocks MEK phosphorylation, leading to reduced ERK activity and downstream effects such as decreased c‑Myc–driven cell‑cycle progression and survival signaling.[2,8,11]

In NSCLC, BRAF mutations occur in ~1–2% of cases and are largely confined to adenocarcinoma; V600E accounts for roughly half of BRAF alterations and ~2% of all NSCLC.[1,3,6,9,11] Class I BRAFV600E mutations signal as active monomers and generate high MAPK output that functions as a dominant oncogenic driver, analogous to melanoma but within a lung‑specific signaling milieu.[3,6,11] Pharmacologic inhibition of mutant BRAF in this context sharply decreases ERK signaling, producing tumor regressions when the tumor is “oncogene‑addicted” to BRAFV600E; however, the degree of pathway inhibition required and the strength of ERK‑mediated negative feedback differ by tissue type, contributing to variability in sensitivity between lung and other cancers.[5,9] As in other BRAFV600E tumors, ERK normally enforces negative feedback on RTKs and RAS; BRAF inhibition relieves this feedback, promoting RTK upregulation, RAS activation, and RAF dimerization, which can restore ERK signaling and drive adaptive resistance to single‑agent RAF blockade.[5,7]

These considerations underpin the clinical development of encorafenib in combination with the MEK1/2 inhibitor binimetinib. Dual vertical inhibition of BRAF and MEK provides deeper and more sustained ERK suppression, reduces paradoxical ERK activation in BRAF‑wild‑type cells, and mitigates cutaneous hyperproliferative toxicities seen with BRAF monotherapy.[2–4,7,9] In BRAFV600E‑mutant metastatic NSCLC, the phase II PHAROS trial (NCT03915951) showed that encorafenib 450 mg once daily plus binimetinib 45 mg twice daily achieved high objective response rates and durable responses in both treatment‑naïve and previously treated cohorts, leading to FDA approval and guideline endorsement of this combination as a preferred targeted regimen.[1,3,9,10,12] Mechanistically, the combination is best suited to class I BRAFV600E NSCLC, where signaling is BRAF‑monomer–driven and highly MEK‑dependent; in contrast, class II/III BRAF mutants, which signal via RAS‑dependent dimers and often co‑activate other pathways, show limited sensitivity to current monomer‑selective RAF inhibitors and remain an unmet need.[1,3,6,7,9]

From a precision‑oncology standpoint, encorafenib’s mechanism translates into a clear biomarker‑defined use case: patients with metastatic NSCLC harboring BRAFV600E (class I) identified by broad molecular profiling are candidates for encorafenib plus binimetinib, whereas non‑V600 or dimer‑dependent BRAF alterations should generally not be treated with current BRAF/MEK doublets outside trials.[1,3,6,7,9,11,12] Understanding MAPK‑centric resistance mechanisms—such as BRAF splice variants or amplification, secondary NRAS/KRAS or MEK mutations, and activation of parallel survival pathways like PI3K–AKT or autophagy—supports rational development of next‑generation RAF inhibitors (including dimer and pan‑RAF agents) and combinatorial strategies (e.g., adding ERK, SHP2, PI3K, or ULK inhibitors) aimed at sustaining ERK suppression and extending benefit in BRAFV600E‑mutant lung cancer.[2,4,5,7,9,12]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic NSCLC with *BRAF* V600E (class I) mutations, encorafenib plus binimetinib has demonstrated high and durable activity and is now an FDA‑approved, guideline‑preferred targeted option. In the phase II PHAROS trial (NCT03915951), treatment‑naïve patients achieved an ORR of 75% with non‑estimable median PFS at the primary analysis, while previously treated patients had an ORR of 46% and median PFS of 9.3 months, with median duration of response not reached and 16.7 months, respectively.[5,6,8,9] Safety was manageable, with mainly grade ≤2 adverse reactions and low permanent discontinuation rates, supporting use as first‑line therapy when a *BRAF* V600E mutation is identified.[5,7–10]

By contrast, current BRAF inhibitors, including encorafenib, are not effective for class II/III non‑V600 *BRAF* mutations because monomer‑selective inhibition can paradoxically activate BRAF dimers.[5,7] Reviews and expert resources emphasize that non‑V600 *BRAF*‑mutant NSCLC remains an unmet need and that patients should preferentially receive immunotherapy and/or chemotherapy or be enrolled in trials of next‑generation RAF dimer inhibitors rather than off‑label encorafenib.[5,7] No clinical data were identified for encorafenib in SCLC, and available BRAF‑targeted approvals and trials are confined to NSCLC with *BRAF* V600E; thus, encorafenib‑based therapy currently has no role in SCLC outside of research settings.[3,4,9]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Encorafenib (Braftovi) has no formal contraindications in US or EU product information, but labels and regulatory reviews emphasize serious, sometimes life‑threatening toxicities that require baseline risk assessment and close monitoring, particularly relevant in NSCLC patients with high cardiopulmonary and thrombo‑hemorrhagic comorbidity burdens.[1][4][5][7][20] Treatment is restricted to tumors harboring a BRAF V600E mutation; use in BRAF wild‑type NSCLC is specifically discouraged because paradoxical MAPK activation may promote tumor growth.[1][4][5][11][20]

Class‑specific risks include new primary malignancies and tumor promotion in RAS‑mutant or BRAF wild‑type clones. Both FDA and EMA recommend full‑body dermatologic examination before, during, and after therapy, and consideration of permanent discontinuation if RAS‑mutant non‑cutaneous primaries emerge.[1][4][5][8][11][20] In metastatic NSCLC—where cumulative tobacco exposure and prior thoracic irradiation are common—this secondary‑cancer risk and need for long‑term surveillance are particularly important.[10][11]

Cardiovascular and electrophysiologic toxicity are central concerns. Cardiomyopathy and QT prolongation are highlighted boxed‑level warnings: LVEF should be obtained at baseline, 1 month, then every 2–3 months; the combination has not been established in patients with LVEF <50%.[1][4][5][8][20] Dose‑dependent QTc prolongation was seen in PHAROS, with QTcF >500 ms in a subset of patients; labels mandate electrolyte correction, avoidance of other QT‑prolonging drugs where possible, and withholding or discontinuation according to severity.[1][5][20] Given the high prevalence of ischemic heart disease, prior anthracyclines, and QT‑active polypharmacy in advanced NSCLC, many guidelines recommend baseline cardiology review before starting encorafenib plus binimetinib.[10][18][20]

Ocular and hepatic toxicity, hemorrhage, and embryo‑fetal risk further shape safety management. Uveitis is attributed mainly to encorafenib, while serous retinopathy/retinal pigment epithelial detachment and retinal vein occlusion are classically linked to binimetinib; regulatory guidance calls for baseline ophthalmologic assessment and urgent review of any visual symptoms, with treatment interruption, dose reduction, or permanent discontinuation for persistent or severe events.[1][4][5][8][20] Hepatotoxicity (grade ≥3 ALT/AST elevation) requires regular liver‑function monitoring and structured dose interruption or discontinuation algorithms.[1][4][5][7][20] Major hemorrhagic events have been reported in melanoma, CRC, and PHAROS; in the NSCLC cohort, hemorrhage was among the most frequent causes of permanent discontinuation for both encorafenib and binimetinib, and a treatment‑related intracranial hemorrhage (grade 5) occurred in the updated PHAROS analysis, underscoring the need for caution in patients on therapeutic anticoagulation or with brain metastases.[5][20][24]

Across melanoma, CRC, and NSCLC, the most common adverse reactions (≥25%) with encorafenib‑based regimens are gastrointestinal (nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal pain), fatigue, musculoskeletal pain, rash, and, in NSCLC, visual impairment, dyspnea, constipation, and cough.[1][4][5][11][20] In PHAROS, most events were grade ≤2 and manageable with supportive care and dose modification; treatment‑related discontinuation of encorafenib or binimetinib occurred in ~5% of patients, with hemorrhage and cardiac/ocular events the leading causes.[20][21][24] Pyrexia—a major practical problem with dabrafenib plus trametinib—was less frequent and generally low‑grade with encorafenib plus binimetinib, which may favor this regimen in frail or heavily pretreated NSCLC patients.[11][18][21][22] The combination is embryo‑fetotoxic and may impair male fertility; effective non‑hormonal contraception and avoidance of breastfeeding are mandated, and use in pregnancy should be avoided unless no safer alternatives exist.[1][4][5]

**Concise narrative summary**

The pivotal evidence for encorafenib in lung cancer is the phase II PHAROS trial (NCT03915951), where encorafenib plus binimetinib produced high ORR (75% treatment‑naïve; 46% previously treated), long DOR (40.0 and 16.7 months), and prolonged PFS/OS with a largely manageable toxicity profile, leading to FDA approval for adults with BRAF V600E‑mutant metastatic NSCLC.[1,3,5,7,8,11] Updated OS data show a median 47.6‑month survival in treatment‑naïve patients, among the longest reported with targeted therapy in this genotype.[5] The ongoing ENCO‑BRAF phase II trial (NCT04526782) is expanding the evidence base for the same doublet across lines of therapy, while the Landscape 1011 umbrella study (NCT04585815) is evaluating triplet encorafenib/binimetinib plus the PD‑1 inhibitor sasanlimab to explore whether integrating immunotherapy can further enhance durable responses in BRAFV600E‑mutant NSCLC.[8,10,16]
