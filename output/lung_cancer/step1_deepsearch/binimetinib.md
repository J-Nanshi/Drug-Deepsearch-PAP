## 1. Drug Summary

Binimetinib (MEK162, ARRY‑162, ARRY‑438162; Mektovi) is an orally bioavailable, highly selective, non‑ATP‑competitive inhibitor of MEK1/2 within the mitogen‑activated protein kinase (MAPK) cascade. Dysregulation of the RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK pathway is implicated in the progression and tumorigenesis of a broad array of cancers, including non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), where it contributes to uncontrolled proliferation and survival signaling.[1] By targeting MEK1/2, binimetinib is designed to attenuate downstream ERK activation in tumors driven by upstream alterations such as KRAS or BRAF mutations, providing a pathway‑directed approach relevant across tumor types.

Clinically, binimetinib is approved (as Mektovi) for use in combination regimens in other solid tumors, and has been investigated in multiple early‑ and mid‑phase lung cancer trials. A 2024 systematic review of seven clinical studies (228 participants) in lung cancer found that binimetinib—alone or combined with agents such as encorafenib or chemotherapy—can achieve objective response rates up to 75% and median progression‑free survival up to 9.3 months in selected populations, but overall showed nonsignificant efficacy with increased toxicity compared with traditional chemotherapy in unselected patients.[1,2] These findings underscore that, in lung cancer, binimetinib is best positioned as a precision oncology agent for genomically defined MAPK‑addicted subsets rather than as a broadly active cytotoxic alternative.

## 4. Primary Targets (Human)

Binimetinib (Mektovi) is an orally available, selective, allosteric inhibitor of MEK1 and MEK2 within the RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway, used clinically in combination with encorafenib for BRAF V600–mutant melanoma and as part of BRAF/MEK inhibitor combinations in BRAF‑mutant NSCLC.[5],[6],[8] MEK1/2 are the only known substrates of RAF kinases and the exclusive upstream activators of ERK1/2, positioning them as critical signaling nodes in BRAF‑ and RAS‑driven lung cancers.[5],[6]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Binimetinib is a selective MEK1/2 inhibitor; direct binimetinib–lung cancer pathway data are limited, so key pathways are inferred from MEK/RAF–MAPK biology, binimetinib/BRAF data, and NSCLC signaling.

1. **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK; KEGG hsa04010)** – Core oncogenic axis in NSCLC, frequently activated by KRAS and BRAF mutations; MEK inhibition suppresses ERK phosphorylation and downstream proliferation/survival programs, generally conferring sensitivity in MAPK‑addicted tumors, including BRAF‑mutant and some KRAS‑mutant NSCLC.[6][11][12]

2. **PI3K–AKT–mTOR (KEGG hsa04151)** – Highly dysregulated in NSCLC and tightly cross‑talking with MAPK; MEK blockade can upregulate PI3K/AKT signaling as compensatory survival, so PI3K–AKT–mTOR activation usually mediates resistance, while dual MEK–PI3K/AKT inhibition enhances apoptosis and antitumor activity.[1][2][3][6][16]

3. **Autophagy (KEGG hsa04140)** – RAS/RAF/MAPK activation promotes a basal, often cytoprotective autophagy; RAF/MEK inhibition further induces autophagy that can sustain survival and drive resistance, and genetic or pharmacologic autophagy inhibition augments the efficacy of RAF/MEK‑targeted therapy, indicating intact autophagy is typically resistance‑associated.[5][13]

4. **Cell‑cycle regulation (Cyclin D–CDK4/6 axis; KEGG hsa04110)** – MAPK signaling drives E2F, G1–S transition, and cyclin D–CDK4/6 activity; MEK inhibition reduces E2F/G2M transcriptional programs, but co‑alterations in CDK4/6 and other cell‑cycle genes can blunt response, and combined MEK–CDK4/6 targeting overcomes resistance in BRAF‑mutant and KRAS/BRAF‑co‑altered models.[11][12][15]

5. **JAK–STAT (KEGG hsa04630)** – A major pro‑survival axis in lung cancer; PI3K/Akt, Ras/MAPK, and JAK/STAT pathways cooperate to sustain proliferation and survival, and JAK/STAT3 activation is implicated in resistance to targeted therapies, so persistent JAK–STAT signaling after MEK blockade is expected to confer resistance.[4][6][9]

6. **NF‑κB signaling (KEGG hsa04064)** – Activated downstream of KRAS and inflammatory cues in NSCLC; contributes to anti‑apoptotic and drug‑resistant phenotypes, and can be engaged as a bypass when MAPK is inhibited, making NF‑κB upregulation a resistance‑linked pathway.[5][9][16]

7. **Apoptosis regulation (p53/Bcl‑2 family; KEGG hsa04210)** – PI3K/Akt and MAPK pathways converge on Bcl‑2 family proteins and p53; effective MEK inhibition can shift the balance toward apoptosis, whereas concurrent activation of PI3K/Akt or loss of p53 dampens binimetinib‑induced cell death and promotes resistance.[4][6][7][12]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic NSCLC with class 1 *BRAF V600E* mutations, binimetinib is used only in combination with encorafenib. The phase II PHAROS trial (NCT03915951) demonstrated high objective response rates (75% in treatment‑naïve; 46% in previously treated patients) and durable benefit, with median PFS of 30.2 and 9.3 months, respectively, and median OS 47.6 and 22.7 months on extended follow‑up.[1,4,7,8,21] These data underpinned FDA and EMA approvals and guideline endorsement of encorafenib–binimetinib as a preferred option for *BRAF V600E*-mutant metastatic NSCLC.[3,5,8,18,21]

For non‑V600 (class 2/3) *BRAF*-mutant NSCLC, prospective encorafenib–binimetinib data are lacking; a systematic review across BRAF/MEK combinations suggests modest, heterogeneous activity, with higher response rates in class 2 than class 3 disease, and emphasizes the need for combination strategies beyond MAPK blockade alone.[8,17,18] In KRAS‑mutant NSCLC, a phase II trial of binimetinib plus hydroxychloroquine (NCT04735068) showed no objective responses, short median PFS (1.9 months), and substantial grade ≥3 toxicity, indicating limited utility of this MEK‑based regimen in this subtype.[9] MAP2K1‑mutant NSCLC and small‑cell lung cancer have not been prospectively studied with binimetinib-containing regimens; available evidence for MEK inhibition in RAS‑driven NSCLC is largely extrapolated from other MEK inhibitors and remains investigational.[10,11,24]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Binimetinib (MEKTOVI) has no absolute contraindications on the US label, but its approved NSCLC use is restricted to combination with encorafenib and to patients without baseline left‑ventricular dysfunction, prolonged QTc (>480 ms), uncontrolled hypertension, or a history/evidence of retinal vein occlusion in PHAROS.[6],[8] New primary cutaneous and non‑cutaneous malignancies have been added as a labeled risk; cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma and skin papilloma each occurred in 2% of PHAROS patients, prompting recommendations for regular dermatologic examinations before, during, and for at least 6 months after treatment.[6] MEKTOVI causes embryo‑fetal toxicity in animals; labels advise effective contraception during therapy and for 30 days after the last dose.[6]

Cardiac toxicity includes left‑ventricular dysfunction/cardiomyopathy and QTc prolongation. In PHAROS, serious cardiac adverse reactions (including myocardial infarction and left‑ventricular dysfunction) were observed, and left‑ventricular dysfunction/cardiomyopathy contributed to dose interruptions, reductions, and permanent discontinuations.[6],[8] Guidelines and regulatory documents recommend baseline echocardiogram or MUGA, reassessment at 1 month, then every 2–3 months, with withholding, dose reduction, or discontinuation based on LVEF decline or symptomatic heart failure; electrolytes and QTc should be monitored, especially in patients on other QT‑prolonging drugs.[8],[10]

Ocular toxicity is a class effect of MEK inhibitors. In PHAROS, visual impairment occurred in 29% of patients, with serous retinopathy (2%) and uveitis (1%); prior melanoma data also show uveitis and rare retinal vein occlusion.[8],[10] Labels and expert reviews recommend baseline ophthalmologic assessment where feasible, regular symptom‑directed eye exams, and urgent evaluation for any new visual disturbance, with permanent discontinuation if retinal vein occlusion is confirmed.[6],[8]

Serious non‑cardiac, non‑ocular risks include hemorrhage, hepatotoxicity, interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis, and CPK elevation/rhabdomyolysis. In PHAROS, serious adverse reactions occurred in 38% of patients; hemorrhage in 6% (including fatal intracranial hemorrhage), diarrhea in 4.1%, and myocardial infarction and pleural effusion in 2% each.[6],[8] Grade 3–4 liver test elevations occurred in up to 10% (AST) and 9% (ALT), and ILD/pneumonitis in about 1%.[6] Labels and practical guidance recommend baseline and at least monthly liver function tests, CPK, and creatinine, with prompt evaluation of dyspnea or new pulmonary symptoms; suspected ILD or rhabdomyolysis warrants interruption and possible discontinuation.[6],[8],[10] Drug–drug interaction management focuses mainly on encorafenib (CYP3A substrate); strong/moderate CYP3A inhibitors or inducers and substrates of OATP1B1/1B3/BCRP should be avoided or carefully monitored, and encorafenib dose reduced if binimetinib is held.[8]  



## 8. Key Clinical Trials

The LIMIT phase II trial (NCT04735068) tested binimetinib 45 mg BID plus hydroxychloroquine 400 mg BID in previously treated advanced KRAS‑mutant NSCLC; among 9 patients, no objective responses were observed, median PFS was 1.9 months, OS 5.3 months, and the study stopped early for lack of efficacy.[8–9]  

The phase II PHAROS trial (NCT03915951) evaluated encorafenib 450 mg QD plus binimetinib 45 mg BID in BRAF V600E‑mutant metastatic NSCLC, showing high ORR (75% treatment‑naïve; 46% previously treated) and durable disease control, leading to FDA approval of this combination in this population.[10]
