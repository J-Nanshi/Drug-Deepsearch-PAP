## 1. Drug Summary

Vemurafenib is an oral, small‑molecule kinase inhibitor that selectively targets mutant BRAF, with greatest potency against the BRAF V600E substitution, leading to inhibition of downstream MAPK/ERK signaling and suppression of proliferation in BRAF‑mutant tumor cells while sparing most wild‑type BRAF activity.[5] It is approved for unresectable or metastatic melanoma with BRAF V600 mutation and for Erdheim–Chester disease, but it does not carry a formal regulatory indication for lung cancer.[5,7]  

In non–small cell lung carcinoma (NSCLC), BRAF mutations are relatively uncommon (roughly a low single‑digit percentage of cases), and V600E represents the dominant oncogenic variant within this subset, defining a distinct, targetable molecular subgroup.[3,10,12] Clinical experience from basket and lung‑specific phase II studies demonstrates that vemurafenib monotherapy can induce meaningful responses and disease control in advanced BRAF V600–mutant NSCLC, whereas tumors with non‑V600 BRAF alterations derive little or no benefit, underscoring the mutation‑specific nature of its activity.[1,3,4,12]  

Within contemporary precision oncology algorithms for NSCLC, combined BRAF/MEK inhibition (e.g., dabrafenib plus trametinib) is generally preferred for BRAF V600E–mutant disease, but vemurafenib remains an important targeted BRAF inhibitor that may be used off‑label when standard combinations are unavailable, contraindicated, or not tolerated.[3,10,12]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Vemurafenib is a potent, highly selective ATP-competitive inhibitor of mutant BRAF V600E that suppresses BRAF kinase activity and downstream MAPK (RAF–MEK–ERK) signaling, thereby blocking proliferation of malignant cells carrying this mutation.[6,9] In NSCLC, BRAF V600E is a class I mutation that constitutively activates MEK1/2 and ERK in a RAS-independent manner, driving uncontrolled growth and survival via persistent MAPK pathway signaling.[1,5,13,15] By inhibiting BRAF V600E, vemurafenib functionally “switches off” this oncogenic MAPK dependence in BRAF V600E‑mutant lung tumors, leading to reduced ERK phosphorylation, loss of proliferative signaling, and tumor regression in a subset of patients.[1,5,10,12,15] Clinical basket and NSCLC-specific phase II data show objective responses in BRAF V600E–mutant, but not non‑V600, NSCLC, consistent with a mechanism tightly linked to V600E‑driven MAPK addiction.[1,4,5,13,15]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In NSCLC, BRAF mutations occur in ~1.5–3.5% of cases and are largely confined to adenocarcinoma, with V600E representing about half of BRAF alterations and showing enrichment in women, never‑smokers and tumors with aggressive micropapillary growth patterns.[1][8][13] Across basket and NSCLC‑specific phase II cohorts, vemurafenib monotherapy in metastatic BRAF V600–mutant NSCLC yields objective response rates around 42–45%, median progression‑free survival ~5–7 months, and overall survival ~10 months, indicating meaningful but time‑limited benefit in this molecular subset.[8][15] Activity is essentially restricted to V600E: in the AcSé NSCLC cohort, no objective responses were seen among patients with non‑V600 BRAF mutations, underscoring that class II/III variants (often in smokers and occasionally non‑adenocarcinoma histologies) are intrinsically resistant to first‑generation BRAF inhibition.[1][8][15] V600E‑mutant tumors generally exhibit poor sensitivity to platinum chemotherapy and limited benefit from PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors, whereas targeted BRAF±MEK inhibition improves survival, leading contemporary guidelines and expert reviews to favor dabrafenib–trametinib or other BRAF/MEK combinations over vemurafenib monotherapy, reserving single‑agent vemurafenib for selected V600E‑mutant adenocarcinoma patients who cannot tolerate combinations.[1][4][8][15]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Vemurafenib is a BRAF kinase inhibitor used across tumor types with BRAF V600 alterations, and its class safety profile must be integrated with lung cancer–specific comorbidities. In NSCLC, patients often have significant baseline cardiopulmonary disease, prior thoracic radiotherapy, and polypharmacy, all of which can amplify known TKI toxicities such as QTc prolongation, hepatotoxicity, and dermatologic events highlighted for other kinase inhibitors and immune-oncology regimens in lung cancer populations.[3],[6],[7] Careful baseline assessment (ECOG status, cardiac history, liver function, concomitant drugs) and ongoing pharmacovigilance are therefore critical.

Cutaneous toxicities, including rash, photosensitivity, and secondary skin tumors, are characteristic of BRAF-directed therapy, and similar dermatologic signals are seen across targeted and immunotherapy programs for NSCLC, where chronic low‑grade toxicities can accumulate over prolonged treatment courses.[3] Given the high cumulative UV exposure and frequent prior radiotherapy in lung cancer, strict photoprotection, early dermatology referral, and regular full‑skin examinations are prudent. For patients with interstitial lung disease (ILD), targeted agents have been associated with pneumotoxicity and treatment‑related exacerbations; although data are strongest for EGFR TKIs, any new or worsening dyspnea or infiltrates on vemurafenib should prompt urgent evaluation and drug interruption.[6]

Cardiac risk requires particular attention. Broad TKI and immunotherapy experience in NSCLC shows clinically meaningful QTc prolongation, arrhythmias, and treatment‑related cardiopulmonary events, especially when combined with thoracic radiation or in patients with underlying structural heart disease.[3],[6],[7] For vemurafenib, baseline and periodic ECGs, electrolyte optimization, and avoidance or minimization of other QT‑prolonging agents are recommended, with dose interruption or discontinuation if significant QTc prolongation or ventricular arrhythmias occur. In parallel, hepatotoxicity and other serious adverse events described in multi‑agent precision oncology trials (including vemurafenib‑containing combinations) support routine liver function monitoring and early dose modification in lung cancer patients, who often have hepatic metastases and concurrent systemic therapies.[7],[8]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

The AcSé Vemurafenib NSCLC cohort (phase II, NCT02304809) enrolled 118 previously treated patients with BRAF‑mutant NSCLC; among 101 with BRAF V600 mutations, the Bayesian-estimated ORR was 44.9%, with median response duration 6.4 months, PFS 5.2 months, and OS 10.0 months.[1]

In a phase II multicohort basket study of vemurafenib in BRAF V600–mutant non-melanoma cancers, the NSCLC cohort (20 patients) showed an ORR of 42% and median PFS of 7.3 months.[2]

Expanded NSCLC cohort data from this basket program reported median PFS 6.1 months and median OS 15.4 months in previously treated patients receiving vemurafenib.[2]
