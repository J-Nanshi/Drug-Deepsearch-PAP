## 1. Drug Summary

Repotrectinib (AUGTYRO) is a next‑generation, oral macrocyclic tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) that selectively targets ROS1 and TRK (TRKA/B/C) and is clinically developed for adults with locally advanced or metastatic ROS1‑positive non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and for patients with NTRK fusion–positive solid tumors, including NSCLC. It was rationally designed as a compact macrocycle with very high potency against ROS1 and TRK and with a binding mode that accommodates key on‑target resistance mutations, especially solvent‑front substitutions such as ROS1 G2032R and NTRK G595R/G623R, as well as other ROS1 kinase domain mutations (e.g., D2033N, L2026M). As a type I ATP‑competitive inhibitor, repotrectinib binds the active kinase conformation and minimizes steric clash with bulky solvent‑front residues, enabling inhibition of a broad spectrum of resistance variants that limit first‑generation TKIs.

In ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, repotrectinib has demonstrated greater preclinical potency than earlier agents such as crizotinib and entrectinib, together with robust central nervous system (CNS) penetration that translates clinically into high intracranial response rates and durable control of brain metastases. The phase 1/2 TRIDENT‑1 trial (NCT03093116) established repotrectinib’s efficacy in ROS1‑positive NSCLC, with high objective response rates and prolonged progression‑free survival in TKI‑naïve patients, and meaningful activity—including against ROS1 G2032R—in patients previously treated with one ROS1 TKI. In NTRK fusion–positive solid tumors, TRIDENT‑1 also showed durable systemic and intracranial responses in both TRK‑TKI–naïve and pretreated cohorts, supporting repotrectinib as a next‑generation option for NTRK‑driven NSCLC.

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Repotrectinib is a next‑generation, compact macrocyclic tyrosine kinase inhibitor that selectively targets ROS1 and TRKA/B/C, with additional ALK activity.[1],[3] In ROS1‑ and NTRK‑fusion–positive NSCLC, constitutively active fusion kinases drive tumorigenesis via MAPK and PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling, promoting proliferation and survival.[2],[4],[5] By binding within the ATP pocket of ROS1 and TRK, repotrectinib inhibits kinase phosphorylation, thereby suppressing these downstream pathways and inducing antitumor effects.[3],[4]

Its small, three‑dimensional macrocyclic structure creates a reduced binding interface that circumvents steric hindrance from bulky solvent‑front and gatekeeper substitutions, enabling potent inhibition of wild‑type and mutant ROS1 (including G2032R) and TRK solvent‑front mutants (TRKA G595R, TRKB G639R, TRKC G623R).[1],[3],[4] Repotrectinib was also engineered for enhanced CNS penetration, achieving robust intracranial activity in ROS1+ NSCLC and NTRK‑fusion–positive tumors.[1],[3],[6] Clinically, this design supports durable responses in ROS1 fusion–positive NSCLC, including G2032R‑mutant disease, and provides a targeted option for the rare NTRK fusion–positive NSCLC subtype.[1],[3],[6]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Repotrectinib is a next‑generation macrocyclic TKI that targets ROS1 and TRK (NTRK1–3), thereby modulating several canonical lung cancer signaling cascades that are shared across RTK fusion–driven NSCLC.[4,5,7,8]

- **ROS1 fusion signaling (KEGG: hsa04012 via ROS1; Reactome: R-HSA-8848021 “Signaling by ROS1”) – Down – Sensitive**  
  ROS1 fusions constitutively activate downstream MAPK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR and JAK–STAT pathways in NSCLC; inhibition of ROS1 kinase by TKIs suppresses this oncogenic signaling and induces tumor regression in ROS1‑rearranged disease.[2,4,5,6,8]

- **TRK (NTRK1/2/3) signaling (KEGG: hsa04722; Reactome: R-HSA-187037 “Signaling by NTRKs”) – Down – Sensitive**  
  NTRK fusions (e.g., TPM3–NTRK1) drive ligand‑independent TRK activation with downstream MAPK/ERK and PI3K/AKT signaling; TRK inhibition in fusion‑positive tumors blocks these cascades and is associated with high response rates across solid cancers, including rare NTRK‑fusion NSCLC.[4,5]

- **MAPK (RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK) pathway (KEGG: hsa04010; Reactome: R-HSA-5673001) – Down – Sensitive / Up – Resistant**  
  ALK, ROS1 and NTRK fusions converge on RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK to promote proliferation and survival; RTK inhibition downregulates MAPK output, whereas MAPK reactivation via KRAS, BRAF or MEK alterations or MET amplification mediates bypass resistance to TKIs.[1,2,4,6–8]

- **PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway (KEGG: hsa04151; Reactome: R-HSA-109704) – Down – Sensitive / Up – Resistant**  
  PI3K–AKT–mTOR is a central survival axis downstream of multiple RTKs (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, MET, NTRK); its suppression contributes to TKI responses, while activating PIK3CA mutations, PTEN loss or MET amplification can sustain signaling and blunt TKI efficacy.[2,4,6–8]

- **JAK–STAT pathway (KEGG: hsa04630; Reactome: R-HSA-9020591) – Down – Sensitive**  
  ALK, ROS1 and TPM3‑fusion kinases activate JAK/STAT (especially STAT3), promoting proliferation and anti‑apoptosis; effective upstream kinase inhibition reduces STAT signaling and is implicated in clinical benefit in fusion‑positive NSCLC.[1,2,5,8]

- **Cell‑cycle control (KEGG: hsa04110; Reactome: R-HSA-69278 “Cell Cycle”) – Down – Sensitive**  
  Oncogenic RTK–MAPK/PI3K signaling upregulates cyclin–CDK activity and drives G1–S progression; targeted inhibition of driver fusions leads to cell‑cycle arrest, a key component of responses in oncogene‑addicted lung cancers.[2,4,8]

- **Apoptosis and survival signaling (KEGG: hsa04210; Reactome: R-HSA-109581 “Apoptosis”) – Up (apoptotic signaling) – Sensitive**  
  RTK blockade diminishes PI3K–AKT and NF‑κB–mediated anti‑apoptotic signaling, shifting the balance toward apoptosis in fusion‑driven NSCLC; conversely, pathway reactivation or alternative survival routes underlie acquired resistance.[2,4,6]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In ROS1 fusion–positive NSCLC, repotrectinib is a next‑generation macrocyclic TKI designed to overcome on‑target resistance, including the recalcitrant ROS1 G2032R solvent‑front mutation, while improving CNS penetration through its low molecular weight.[3,4,5] In the phase 1/2 TRIDENT‑1 trial, ROS1‑rearranged TKI‑naïve patients achieved an objective response rate of 79% with a median PFS of 35.7 months, and those who had received one prior ROS1 TKI but no chemotherapy had a 38% response rate and median PFS of 9.0 months, with notable intracranial activity in patients with CNS metastases.[3,4] These outcomes compare favorably with earlier ROS1 TKIs that are limited by brain penetration and solvent‑front resistance, supporting repotrectinib as a preferred first‑line and post‑TKI option in ROS1‑driven NSCLC.[3,4,6]

In NTRK fusion–positive NSCLC, repotrectinib acts as a highly potent pan‑TRK inhibitor against wild‑type TRKA/B/C fusions and a broad spectrum of resistance mutations, including solvent‑front (TRKA G595R, TRKC G623R), gatekeeper, xDFG, and compound mutants.[1,2,5] Preclinical models show tumor regression in LMNA–NTRK1 xenografts with single and compound TRK mutations, and cellular assays demonstrate ≥10‑fold higher potency than selitrectinib across wild‑type and mutant TRK variants.[1,2] Clinically, TRIDENT‑1 (NCT03093116) has provided proof‑of‑concept in metastatic NTRK‑rearranged NSCLC, with durable responses in both TKI‑naïve and entrectinib‑pretreated patients, including a case with EML4–NTRK3 NSCLC harboring a TRKC G623R solvent‑front mutation who achieved 65% tumor regression and ongoing benefit beyond 9 months.[1,2] These data support repotrectinib as a rational first‑line or resistance‑setting option for the ultra‑rare NTRK fusion–positive NSCLC population.[1,2,4,5]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

No formal contraindications are listed for repotrectinib, but regulatory reviews emphasize class‑typical CNS, hepatic, pulmonary, musculoskeletal, metabolic, and embryo‑fetal risks that are highly relevant in NSCLC.[13,18] The approved regimen is 160 mg once daily for 14 days then 160 mg twice daily until progression or unacceptable toxicity; dose reductions or interruptions are key tools to manage adverse events (AEs).[9,11,13,16,18]

Neurologic and vestibular AEs—especially dizziness, dysgeusia, paresthesia, ataxia, cognitive effects, and broader neurologic events—are the dominant toxicity signal and common reasons for dose modification.[9,11,13,14] Exposure–response modeling shows higher exposure increases grade ≥2 dizziness and neurologic AEs, as well as dose reduction/interruption risk, although the incremental AE risk at 160 mg QD/BID versus 160 mg QD is modest while efficacy is superior.[9,10,17,19] Patients should avoid driving or operating machinery until their individual susceptibility to dizziness or ataxia is clear.[12,13]

Hepatotoxicity is usually low‑grade but frequent: ALT and AST elevations occurred in ~34–40% of patients, with >5×ULN in ~2–3%; the label recommends baseline liver tests, then every 2 weeks in month 1 and monthly thereafter, with treatment interruption if ALT/AST >5×ULN or if any elevation is accompanied by symptoms or jaundice.[11–13,18] Repotrectinib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4 and is a CYP3A inducer; strong CYP3A inhibitors/inducers and sensitive CYP3A substrates should generally be avoided or carefully managed to prevent loss of efficacy or toxicity from co‑medications.[9,12,13,16,18]

Less common but clinically important risks include interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis, myalgia with CPK elevation, hyperuricemia, skeletal fractures, and embryo‑fetal toxicity, necessitating pulmonary vigilance, periodic CPK and uric acid monitoring, bone‑health assessment in long‑term therapy, and strict contraception.[11–13,18] Overall, TRIDENT‑1 shows that most AEs are low‑grade and manageable with temporary holds and stepwise dose reductions, supporting chronic use in ROS1‑positive NSCLC and NTRK‑positive tumors when integrated with precision‑oncology monitoring of CNS status, liver function, and polypharmacy.[9,11–14,17–19]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

The registrational phase 1/2 TRIDENT‑1 trial (NCT03093116) evaluated repotrectinib in advanced solid tumors, including multiple ROS1+ NSCLC cohorts defined by prior ROS1 TKI and chemotherapy exposure.[17,18,21] In TKI‑naïve ROS1+ NSCLC (n=71), ORR was 79% with median PFS 35.7 months and long median DOR 34.1 months; robust intracranial activity was reported (CNS ORR 89% in measurable brain metastases).[17,18,20,21] In patients with one prior ROS1 TKI and no chemotherapy (n=56), ORR was 38% with median PFS 9.0 months, and repotrectinib retained activity against ROS1 G2032R (ORR 59%, 10/17).[17,18,20] Early TRIDENT‑1 data in broader ROS1+ NSCLC cohorts similarly showed high ORR in TKI‑naïve (≈82–90%) and meaningful CNS responses.[3,5,6,11,19]
