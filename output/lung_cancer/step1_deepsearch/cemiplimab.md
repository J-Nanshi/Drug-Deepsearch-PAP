## 1. Drug Summary

Cemiplimab (Libtayo) is a recombinant fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody that binds the programmed cell death‑1 (PD‑1) receptor on T cells and blocks interaction with its ligands PD‑L1 and PD‑L2, thereby countering PD‑1–mediated inhibition of antitumour immune responses and restoring helper and cytotoxic T‑cell function.[1][3] By releasing this checkpoint, cemiplimab increases the pool and activity of effector T cells capable of recognizing and killing non–small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cells.[1][3]

Regulatory approvals in NSCLC include first‑line monotherapy for adults with locally advanced (not candidates for surgery or definitive chemoradiation) or metastatic NSCLC whose tumours express PD‑L1 on ≥50% of tumour cells and lack *EGFR*, *ALK*, or *ROS1* genomic aberrations, and first‑line combination therapy with platinum‑based chemotherapy (PBC) for locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC without these driver alterations irrespective of PD‑L1 expression.[1][3][4] In EMPOWER‑Lung 1 (NCT03088540), cemiplimab monotherapy significantly improved overall and progression‑free survival versus platinum‑doublet chemotherapy in advanced NSCLC with PD‑L1 ≥50%, supporting its approval as a first‑line PD‑1 inhibitor option.[6][7]

Within the contemporary NSCLC treatment paradigm, cemiplimab is positioned alongside pembrolizumab as a frontline PD‑1–blocking therapy. For PD‑L1 ≥50% disease without actionable drivers, cemiplimab monotherapy is an alternative to pembrolizumab monotherapy; in broader PD‑L1 populations, cemiplimab plus PBC is an alternative to other chemo‑immunotherapy regimens for first‑line treatment of advanced or metastatic NSCLC.[1][3][6]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Cemiplimab is a fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody that binds PD‑1 on activated T cells and blocks its interaction with PD‑L1 and PD‑L2, thereby antagonizing PD‑1–mediated inhibitory signaling and restoring helper and cytotoxic T‑cell function against tumor cells.[2][4][9][10] In NSCLC, PD‑L1 is frequently overexpressed on tumor cells and tumor‑associated immune cells, contributing to an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, T‑cell exhaustion, and impaired cytokine production and cytotoxicity.[5][6][18]

By relieving this checkpoint, cemiplimab reinvigorates CD8⁺PD‑1⁺ tumor‑infiltrating T cells and enhances antitumor immunity, with particularly favorable genomic and immunophenotypic profiles observed in tumors with very high PD‑L1 tumor proportion score (TPS ≥90%).[10][15] Clinically, cemiplimab is used as first‑line monotherapy in advanced NSCLC with high PD‑L1 expression (TPS ≥50%) without EGFR/ALK/ROS1 alterations, and its activity appears especially pronounced in the “ultra‑high” PD‑L1 and squamous NSCLC subsets that are highly dependent on the PD‑1/PD‑L1 axis for immune escape.[1][8][9][15][16][21]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Below are key immune and signaling pathways modulated downstream of PD‑1 blockade in NSCLC and other solid tumors, with directionality and association to sensitivity or resistance where supported by the provided sources.

1. **HALLMARK_INTERFERON_GAMMA_RESPONSE (MSigDB)** – Upregulated in responders to PD‑(L)1 blockade; IFN‑γ signaling enhances antigen presentation (MHC I/II, immunoproteasome) and chemokine production (CXCL9/10/11), and higher IFNG/IFN‑γ‑response signatures correlate with improved outcomes to anti‑PD‑1/PD‑L1 in NSCLC and across tumors.[1],[4],[6],[7],[9],[14],[16] Sensitivity-associated.

2. **Antigen processing and presentation / immunoproteasome (GOCC_PROTEASOME_COMPLEX; HLA/MHC pathways)** – Upregulated in responders, with higher expression of IFN‑γ–inducible components (PSMB8, PSMB9, PSMB10, PSME1, PSME2, TAP1) and MHC I/II; intact pathways support effective neoantigen display and checkpoint‑blockade benefit, whereas B2M or antigen‑presentation defects drive resistance.[2],[4],[7],[9]

3. **T cell receptor (TCR) signaling and effector T‑cell activation (e.g., CD3D, CD7, IL2‑STAT5 signaling, ALLOGRAFT_REJECTION, “immune‑activated” M‑2 signature)** – Functionally upregulated after PD‑1 blockade, reflected by increased T‑cell infiltration, CD3/T‑cell receptor genes, and IL2‑STAT5 pathway enrichment in responders; these signatures mark a “hot,” immune‑inflamed TME associated with sensitivity.[1],[2],[6],[7]

4. **HALLMARK_INTERFERON_ALPHA_RESPONSE / inflammatory response (MSigDB)** – Frequently co‑upregulated with IFN‑γ response and other inflammatory programs in responders; contributes to an inflamed microenvironment and improved antigen presentation, but chronic activation can accompany acquired resistance with dysfunctional inflammation.[1],[6],[7],[9]

5. **WNT/β‑catenin signaling (HALLMARK_WNT_BETA_CATENIN_SIGNALING)** – Downregulated in responding tumors under PD‑1/CTLA‑4 blockade, whereas higher WNT/β‑catenin and related transcription factors (e.g., TCF7L1) associate with T‑cell exclusion and non‑response, supporting a resistance role.[1],[6],[7]

6. **TGF‑β signaling (HALLMARK_TGF_BETA_SIGNALING)** – Upregulated in non‑responders and resistant states; TGF‑β suppresses antigen presentation and T‑cell/NK activity and promotes Treg and myeloid suppressor networks, contributing to primary and acquired resistance to PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade.[2],[3],[6],[7]

7. **PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway (KEGG: hsa04151)** – Frequently activated downstream of PTEN loss in NSCLC; promotes secretion of TGF‑β and CXCL10, recruitment of Tregs, and suppression of CD8⁺ T‑cell activity, thereby driving resistance to PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors.[2]

8. **Alternative immune checkpoint pathways (e.g., LAG‑3, TIM‑3, TIGIT; HALLMARK_TNFA_SIGNALING_VIA_NFKB, IL6_JAK_STAT3_SIGNALING)** – Upregulated in tumors with acquired resistance, with increased LAG‑3/TIM‑3 expression on TILs and IL‑6–JAK–STAT3/NF‑κB pathway activity; these compensatory inhibitory circuits are linked to loss of sensitivity to PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade.[2],[3],[6],[7]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In PD-L1 ≥50% advanced NSCLC without *EGFR/ALK/ROS1* alterations, cemiplimab monotherapy improves OS and PFS versus chemotherapy and shows PFS and ORR advantages with comparable OS and safety versus pembrolizumab, supporting its use as a leading single-agent option in this biomarker-defined group.[10] Network meta-analyses restricted to PD-L1 ≥50% further rank cemiplimab as providing the greatest OS benefit versus chemotherapy among single-agent ICIs, although chemo-ICI combinations may yield higher response rates without clear OS gain.[12]

Across histologies, phase III EMPOWER-Lung 3 demonstrates that adding cemiplimab to platinum doublet chemotherapy improves OS, PFS, and response in both squamous and non-squamous NSCLC, irrespective of PD-L1 level, in tumors lacking *EGFR/ALK/ROS1* aberrations.[6,15] Large network meta-analyses indicate that cemiplimab (alone or with chemotherapy) is among the most effective regimens in both squamous and non-squamous cohorts, with particularly strong OS ranking in squamous disease.[9,13,16]

Evidence consistently derives from non–oncogene-addicted NSCLC; trials exclude *EGFR*, *ALK*, and *ROS1* alterations, and guideline and meta-analytic frameworks position cemiplimab for driver-negative tumors.[6,10,12,15] For patients with brain metastases, EMPOWER-Lung 1 and subsequent reviews show that cemiplimab retains systemic and intracranial activity, with marked OS and PFS benefit versus chemotherapy in PD-L1 ≥50% disease and stable brain lesions, but data remain limited and extrapolated from selected, treated or asymptomatic CNS cohorts.[19,21,24]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

No specific cemiplimab (Libtayo) NSCLC label or guideline documents are available in the provided sources, so only class‑level, PD‑1–inhibitor–based safety principles can be inferred. Contraindications are expected to include prior severe hypersensitivity to the drug or its excipients and the need for administration by clinicians experienced with immune checkpoint inhibitors. In patients with significant pre‑existing autoimmune disease or prior severe immune‑related toxicity to another PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitor, use generally requires individualized risk–benefit assessment and close multidisciplinary monitoring, in line with PD‑1 class practice.

Immune‑mediated toxicities are the dominant safety concern. Across PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors, clinically important events include pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, endocrinopathies (thyroid dysfunction, hypophysitis, adrenal insufficiency, type 1 diabetes), nephritis, severe cutaneous reactions, and less common but high‑risk neurologic and cardiac events such as meningitis, encephalitis, myositis, and myocarditis.[2,4,5,7,8] These typically require treatment interruption or discontinuation plus prompt systemic corticosteroids, following established nivolumab, pembrolizumab, and atezolizumab algorithms.[2,4,5,7,8]

For NSCLC, pulmonary and cardiac toxicities are particularly critical. Any new or worsening dyspnea, cough, chest pain, or arrhythmia should trigger urgent evaluation for pneumonitis or myocarditis, with drug withholding and high‑dose steroids for grade ≥2 events, mirroring other PD‑1 labels.[2,4,5,7,8] Baseline and periodic assessment of liver enzymes, renal function, and thyroid/adrenal axes is recommended, with management thresholds extrapolated from existing PD‑1/PD‑L1 product information.[2,4,5,7,8]

Infections significantly modify risk. A FAERS‑based analysis of checkpoint inhibitors showed that concurrent bacterial, viral, or fungal infections substantially increase the risk of immune‑related events, including pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, nephritis, and myocarditis, across the class.[6] For NSCLC patients receiving cemiplimab, active serious infections should be treated and stabilized, and vigilance for infectious complications maintained, to reduce the likelihood and severity of immune‑mediated toxicity, consistent with broader PD‑1 safety experience.[6]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

The phase III EMPOWER-Lung 1 trial (NCT03088540) randomized patients with advanced NSCLC and PD-L1 ≥50% to cemiplimab versus platinum-doublet chemotherapy; at 35‑month follow-up, cemiplimab nearly doubled median OS (26.1 vs 13.3 months) and improved PFS (8.1 vs 5.3 months), with consistent benefit across squamous and non-squamous histologies.[6] EMPOWER-Lung 3 (NCT03409614) evaluated cemiplimab plus platinum-doublet chemotherapy irrespective of PD-L1; in the final 2‑year analysis, the combination improved OS (21.1 vs 12.9 months) and PFS (8.2 vs 5.5 months) versus chemotherapy alone, with benefit maintained across PD-L1 strata and histologic subgroups.[9,12]
