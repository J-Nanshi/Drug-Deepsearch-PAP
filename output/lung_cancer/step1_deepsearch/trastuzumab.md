## 1. Drug Summary

Trastuzumab is a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds the extracellular domain of HER2/ERBB2, blocking receptor dimerization, attenuating downstream PI3K/AKT/mTOR and RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK signaling, and engaging immune effector mechanisms such as antibody‑dependent cellular cytotoxicity.[2,4,5] It is a foundational HER2‑targeted agent in breast and gastric cancers, where HER2 amplification/overexpression (IHC 3+ or IHC 2+/ISH+) identifies patients who derive substantial survival benefit when trastuzumab is added to chemotherapy.[3,4,7]

In NSCLC, HER2 alterations comprise kinase‑domain mutations (predominantly exon 20 insertions), amplification, and protein overexpression, together accounting for roughly 1–4%, 2–5%, and 6–30% of cases, respectively, with enrichment in adenocarcinoma and never‑smokers.[1,3,6,8,9] Early phase II studies of trastuzumab plus platinum–taxane chemotherapy in HER2‑positive NSCLC showed response rates and overall survival similar to chemotherapy alone, with only a suggestion of benefit in the rare IHC 3+ subset, and trastuzumab monotherapy in HER2‑altered NSCLC produced no objective responses despite disease stabilization in some patients.[3,6,7,10] These data, together with preclinical evidence that HER2 mutations can drive signaling from intracellular pools relatively inaccessible to antibody blockade, explain the limited activity of unconjugated HER2 antibodies in NSCLC and the absence of lung‑cancer‑specific approvals for trastuzumab.[6,7]

By contrast, HER2‑directed antibody–drug conjugates (ADCs) have reshaped precision oncology for HER2‑mutant NSCLC. Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd), which couples trastuzumab to a membrane‑permeable topoisomerase I inhibitor via a cleavable linker, delivers a high drug‑to‑antibody ratio and exerts a bystander effect independent of high HER2 surface density.[2,13,16] In DESTINY‑Lung01 and DESTINY‑Lung02, T‑DXd achieved objective response rates around 50–55%, median progression‑free survival ~8 months, and meaningful overall survival in previously treated HER2‑mutant NSCLC, leading to FDA accelerated approval as the first targeted therapy for this molecular subset.[1,2,3,8,10,13] Current NSCLC practice therefore positions trastuzumab itself as an investigational component of combination regimens or legacy ADCs (e.g., T‑DM1), while trastuzumab deruxtecan is the clinically relevant HER2‑directed agent for HER2‑mutant—and more broadly HER2‑altered—NSCLC.[1–3,8,10,13,16]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

In NSCLC, HER2 (ERBB2) is an oncogenic driver whose activation phosphorylates tyrosine residues and triggers MEK/ERK and PI3K/AKT signaling, thereby enhancing proliferation and migration of lung cancer cells.[1] Trastuzumab, a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody, is the prototypic HER2‑directed “mono‑antibody” used alongside HER2‑targeted TKIs and ADCs in this axis.[1] By binding the extracellular domain of HER2, trastuzumab functionally antagonizes this receptor‑driven signaling, a mechanism that is conceptually relevant in HER2‑mutant or amplified NSCLC where HER2 activation is a central upstream event.[1]

Because HER2‑mutant NSCLC often harbors exon 20 insertions, which constitutively activate downstream MEK/ERK and PI3K/AKT pathways, complete pathway shutdown with trastuzumab alone is challenging.[1] This has led to a broader therapeutic strategy in lung cancer in which HER2 is targeted by multiple modalities—TKIs, monoclonal antibodies such as trastuzumab, and ADCs like trastuzumab deruxtecan—to intensify blockade of HER2‑driven signaling and to deliver cytotoxic payloads specifically to HER2‑expressing tumor cells.[1,6] In this context, trastuzumab’s primary role is high‑affinity HER2 engagement at the cell surface, providing receptor‑level inhibition and a targeting scaffold that can be combined with agents aimed at downstream pathways or immune modulation.[6]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HER2-overexpressing, largely non-squamous NSCLC (IHC 2+/3+), adding trastuzumab to gemcitabine–cisplatin or other platinum doublets has not improved response rate, PFS, or OS versus chemotherapy alone, although a tiny subgroup with IHC 3+ or FISH‑positive tumors showed numerically higher responses and longer PFS.[1],[3],[10] Trastuzumab monotherapy in pretreated HER2‑altered NSCLC produced no objective responses despite reasonable disease control, underscoring that protein overexpression alone is an inadequate predictor for naked antibody benefit in this setting.[2],[10] Across series, HER2 overexpression spans adenocarcinoma and squamous histologies, but no histology-specific advantage for trastuzumab-based regimens has emerged.[4],[6],[11]

In HER2‑mutant (predominantly exon 20 insertion) adenocarcinoma, unconjugated trastuzumab has limited activity, whereas trastuzumab-based ADCs have reshaped outcomes. Phase II data for trastuzumab emtansine (T‑DM1) show ORRs of ~38–55% in HER2 exon 20–mutant NSCLC, with shorter PFS and heterogeneous benefit.[5],[6] Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd) achieves higher and more durable responses: ORR ~55–72.7% and PFS 8.2–14 months in HER2‑mutant cohorts, leading to regulatory approval as the first targeted therapy for pretreated HER2‑mutant NSCLC.[2],[5],[8],[10] Resistance in this subtype appears more related to ADC payload and HER2 expression dynamics than to trastuzumab’s canonical signaling blockade.[2],[8]

In HER2‑amplified NSCLC, trastuzumab plus chemotherapy has not clearly outperformed chemotherapy alone in unselected amplified/overexpressing populations, and HER2 amplification is now viewed more as an EGFR‑TKI resistance mechanism than as a primary trastuzumab target.[2],[9],[16] In EGFR‑mutated NSCLC with acquired HER2 amplification, trastuzumab–paclitaxel after TKI failure produced a 46% ORR, with responses enriched in tumors with IHC 3+ or high copy number (≥10), suggesting a role for trastuzumab in this molecularly defined resistance niche rather than in de novo HER2‑amplified disease.[9],[16] Robust data for trastuzumab in SCLC are lacking, and clinically meaningful benefit of naked trastuzumab in lung cancer is now largely confined to its role as the antibody backbone of ADCs in HER2‑mutant NSCLC.[2],[5],[8],[10]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Trastuzumab (including IV products, subcutaneous fixed-dose combinations, and biosimilars) has no formal contraindications in the standalone IV labels, but PHESGO is contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to pertuzumab, trastuzumab, hyaluronidase, or excipients.[1–4] All trastuzumab-containing products carry boxed warnings for cardiomyopathy, pulmonary toxicity, and embryo‑fetal toxicity; IV formulations additionally highlight serious infusion reactions.[1–6]

Cardiotoxicity (decline in LVEF, symptomatic CHF) is the dominant risk, highest with concurrent or sequential anthracyclines.[4–6,13] Labels require baseline and serial LVEF monitoring, with treatment interruption or discontinuation for clinically significant decline.[2,4–6] Older adults and those with pre‑existing cardiac disease, diabetes, hypertension, or prior anthracyclines have higher cardiotoxicity rates, necessitating geriatric‑adapted risk assessment and cautious use.[13] In lung cancer—where patients are often elderly, smokers, and heavily pretreated—these cardiac precautions are particularly critical.

Serious infusion reactions and pulmonary events (interstitial pneumonitis, ARDS) typically occur during or within 24 hours of infusion; all IV labels mandate interruption for dyspnea or hypotension and permanent discontinuation for anaphylaxis, angioedema, interstitial pneumonitis, or ARDS.[2,4–6] Observational data show most infusion reactions occur during the infusion (5–92 minutes) and are uncommon after completion, but rare severe anaphylaxis and pulmonary toxicity have been reported.[7] In lung cancer patients with limited pulmonary reserve or underlying ILD, any new or worsening dyspnea, cough, or hypoxia should prompt immediate evaluation and drug discontinuation per label.[2,4–6]

Embryo‑fetal toxicity is a boxed warning across all trastuzumab products: in‑pregnancy exposure can cause oligohydramnios, oligohydramnios sequence (pulmonary hypoplasia, skeletal abnormalities), and neonatal death.[2,4–6,20,22] Labels require pregnancy testing before initiation, effective contraception during treatment and for several months after the last dose, and detailed counseling on fetal risk.[2,4–6,20,22] Finally, trastuzumab can exacerbate chemotherapy‑induced neutropenia when combined with myelosuppressive regimens, necessitating close blood count monitoring in platinum‑based lung cancer combinations.[1–6]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Trastuzumab has been evaluated in HER2‑altered NSCLC both as a “naked” antibody in combination with chemotherapy and as the HER2‑targeting backbone of antibody–drug conjugates (ADCs). Below are the most informative studies for precision oncology.

**Randomized phase II: gemcitabine–cisplatin ± trastuzumab (HER2‑positive stage IIIB/IV NSCLC)** – In this trial, untreated HER2‑positive patients (IHC‑selected) received gemcitabine/cisplatin with or without weekly trastuzumab until progression.[9] Among 103 eligible patients, overall efficacy was similar between arms (response rate 36% vs 41%; median PFS 6.1 vs 7.0 months), but a small subgroup with HER2 IHC 3+ or FISH‑positive tumors had an 83% response rate and median PFS 8.5 months, suggesting potential benefit when HER2 is strongly overexpressed or amplified.[9,13] Trastuzumab did not increase chemotherapy toxicity; one case of symptomatic cardiotoxicity was reported.[9]

**Single‑arm phase II: cisplatin/gemcitabine + trastuzumab (HER2‑overexpressing stage IIIB/IV NSCLC)** – This feasibility study enrolled chemonaive patients with HER2‑overexpressing NSCLC (IHC ≥1+ or elevated serum HER2 antigen) to cisplatin/gemcitabine plus maintenance trastuzumab.[10] Among 21 patients, partial responses occurred in 38%, 1‑year survival was 62%, and median time to progression was 36 weeks, with no pharmacokinetic interaction between trastuzumab and chemotherapy.[10] HER2 IHC 3+ was rare (~5% of screened cases), and the authors proposed serum‑ELISA pre‑screening to enrich for true IHC 3+ disease, underscoring the need for stringent biomarker selection in trastuzumab‑based NSCLC trials.[10,13]

**DESTINY‑Lung01 and DESTINY‑Lung02: trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd) in HER2‑mutant NSCLC** – In the phase II DESTINY‑Lung01 single‑arm cohort of previously treated HER2‑mutant advanced NSCLC, T‑DXd achieved an ORR of ~55%, median PFS 8.2 months, and OS 17.8 months, with substantial ILD risk at 6.4 mg/kg.[2,3,8,15,16] The randomized phase II DESTINY‑Lung02 (NCT04644237) compared 5.4 vs 6.4 mg/kg and showed similar or higher ORR at 5.4 mg/kg (53.8% vs 42.9%) but markedly lower grade ≥3 AEs and ILD (5.9% vs 14%), establishing 5.4 mg/kg as the preferred dose and supporting FDA accelerated approval for previously treated HER2‑mutant NSCLC.[2,3,5,8,15,16] A phase III first‑line trial, DESTINY‑Lung04 (NCT05048797), is now comparing T‑DXd with pembrolizumab‑chemotherapy in untreated HER2 exon 19/20‑mutant non‑squamous NSCLC, aiming to define whether upfront HER2‑directed ADC therapy should replace chemo‑immunotherapy in this molecular subset.[2,3,8,12,15,16]
