## 1. Drug Summary

Crizotinib is an oral, small‑molecule type I ATP‑competitive tyrosine kinase inhibitor initially developed to target the MET proto‑oncogene but subsequently found to potently inhibit ALK and ROS1, along with additional kinases such as RON, AXL, and others.[2] In NSCLC, it exemplifies precision oncology by targeting tumors driven by ALK or ROS1 rearrangements, which define distinct molecular subsets largely mutually exclusive of other classic oncogenic drivers and enriched in lung adenocarcinoma.[1,11,12]

In ALK‑rearranged advanced NSCLC, crizotinib was the first‑generation ALK TKI and showed substantial benefit over chemotherapy, with phase III data (e.g., PROFILE 1014) demonstrating superior progression‑free survival (mPFS ~10.9 vs. 7.0 months) and higher objective response rates, establishing it as an early standard of care before being superseded by more CNS‑penetrant second‑ and third‑generation ALK inhibitors.[1,4,13] For ROS1‑rearranged metastatic NSCLC (~1–2% of cases), crizotinib became the first FDA‑approved ROS1‑directed therapy based on PROFILE 1001 and subsequent studies, achieving objective response rates around 72%, median PFS ~19 months, and median overall survival exceeding 4 years, though with limited intracranial activity and frequent CNS progression over time.[1,10,11] Crizotinib’s multi‑target profile, strong activity in ALK‑ and ROS1‑driven NSCLC, and biomarker‑guided use have made it a foundational agent in the evolution of targeted therapy for metastatic lung cancer.[1,2,10–12]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Crizotinib is an orally available, ATP‑competitive, type I tyrosine kinase inhibitor originally developed as a MET inhibitor and later recognized as a potent inhibitor of ALK and ROS1, with higher biochemical potency against ROS1 than ALK.[5,14] By binding to the ATP‑binding site of these kinases in their active (DFG‑in) conformation, crizotinib blocks autophosphorylation and downstream signaling required for tumor cell proliferation and survival.[5,14]

In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC (most commonly EML4‑ALK), chromosomal fusions place the ALK kinase domain under the control of heterologous promoters, generating constitutively active fusion kinases that drive oncogenesis via RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR, and JAK/STAT pathways.[5,11,14] ALK‑positive tumors are highly dependent on ALK signaling, and crizotinib-induced ALK inhibition leads to rapid pathway dephosphorylation, growth arrest, and apoptosis; clinically, this translates into objective response rates (ORR) around 60% and median PFS ~9 months in advanced ALK‑rearranged NSCLC.[10,14]

ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC (1–2% of cases) is driven by fusions such as CD74‑ROS1, SLC34A2‑ROS1, SDC4‑ROS1, and others, which constitutively activate the ROS1 kinase domain and downstream PI3K/AKT/mTOR, JAK/STAT, and MAPK/ERK signaling.[3,7,11,13] Crizotinib potently inhibits ROS1 phosphorylation and downstream effectors, producing ORRs ≈70% and median PFS 16–19 months in multiple phase I/II cohorts, with median OS exceeding 4 years in some series.[2,3,7,10,13,14] Different ROS1 fusion partners can modulate subcellular localization and relative MAPK activation, and CD74‑ROS1 may be associated with higher CNS involvement, but overall sensitivity to crizotinib remains high across fusion types.[3,7,13]

MET (the HGF receptor encoded on chromosome 7q) is another primary target of crizotinib.[5,6,14] While MET exon 14–skipping NSCLC is now more commonly treated with dedicated MET TKIs, MET amplification or overexpression can act as a primary driver or as a bypass resistance pathway after ALK or ROS1 inhibition.[1,2,3,11,14] In MET‑driven contexts, crizotinib binding to MET’s ATP pocket suppresses MET‑dependent activation of PI3K/AKT and RAS/MAPK cascades, though this is less central to its current lung cancer use.

Mechanistically, crizotinib’s clinical efficacy in NSCLC is tightly linked to molecular subtype. ALK‑rearranged tumors show robust but generally shorter disease control, reflecting lower intrinsic ALK potency and frequent emergence of on‑target ALK mutations and off‑target bypass signaling.[1,5,10,14] ROS1‑rearranged tumors, which often occur in younger never‑smokers with adenocarcinoma histology, tend to have longer PFS on crizotinib, consistent with greater ROS1 dependence and higher drug potency against ROS1.[2,3,7,10,14] Across both ALK‑ and ROS1‑positive NSCLC, crizotinib exerts its antitumor effect by shutting down the addicted fusion kinase and convergent downstream MAPK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR, and JAK/STAT signaling networks that sustain proliferation, survival, and invasion.[3,4,7,11,14]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Crizotinib inhibits ALK, ROS1 and MET, thereby suppressing multiple downstream signaling cascades that are constitutively activated by these receptor tyrosine kinases in NSCLC, including PI3K–AKT, RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) and JAK–STAT pathways, which drive proliferation, survival and motility.[2][3][6][12] Spatial proteomic/transcriptomic profiling of ALK‑rearranged pulmonary adenocarcinomas shows that oncogenic MAPK and PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling are consistently overrepresented in tumor regions and exhibit low intratumoral variability, supporting their role as core effector pathways of ALK signaling.[12] Crizotinib-mediated ALK blockade is therefore expected to downregulate these pathways in sensitive tumors, promoting apoptosis and cell-cycle arrest, although detailed apoptotic and autophagy pathway readouts specific to crizotinib-treated NSCLC are not reported in the provided sources.

Resistance to crizotinib frequently involves pathway reactivation or bypass. Secondary ALK kinase-domain mutations (e.g., L1196M, G1269A, F1174L/V, L1152R, G1202R, S1206Y, 1151Tins) can maintain ALK signaling and downstream AKT and ERK phosphorylation despite drug exposure, functionally restoring PI3K–AKT and MAPK outputs and conferring resistance.[1][2][3][8] Off-target resistance arises through activation of parallel RTKs (EGFR, HER2, KIT, MET, IGF1R) that reactivate ERK and AKT signaling, as well as through MET amplification, which drives persistent RAS–RAF–MAPK, PI3K–AKT and STAT signaling even when EGFR or ALK are inhibited.[3][7][8] EMT and associated ECM/EMT programs—linked to ALK-driven activation of PI3K–AKT and MAPK and to fibronectin (FN1)–high, EMT-enriched regions in ALK‑rearranged tumors—are also implicated in resistance to ALK TKIs, including crizotinib.[4][6][8][12]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In *ROS1*-rearranged advanced NSCLC, crizotinib produces high and durable responses: in PROFILE 1001 (NCT00585195), ORR was 72% with median PFS ~19 months and median OS 51.4 months, establishing a benchmark for this molecular subgroup.[1,2] Large real‑world and retrospective cohorts confirm ORR ~72–86% and median PFS ~16–19 months, with benefit clearly superior to platinum chemotherapy.[3,5,6,7] Resistance is nearly universal and dominated by on‑target *ROS1* kinase domain mutations (especially G2032R, but also D2033N, S1986F/Y and others) plus off‑target mechanisms and CNS progression, supporting rebiopsy and sequencing to next‑generation ROS1 TKIs.[5,7,8] Precision‑oncology practice therefore treats *ROS1* fusions as a classic, mutually exclusive driver in adenocarcinoma, prioritizing upfront crizotinib (or newer ROS1 inhibitors where available) in never/light smokers with advanced disease.[3,5,6]

In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, crizotinib is an established ALK/ROS1/MET TKI and improves survival over chemotherapy in first‑line metastatic disease (PROFILE 1014), with median OS not reached versus 47.5 months on chemotherapy.[4] Although complete responses are rare radiographically, isolated cases of complete metabolic and morphologic response on FDG‑PET/CT and CT have been documented, sustained beyond 2 years.[4] Given limited CNS penetration and the emergence of more potent, CNS‑active next‑generation ALK inhibitors, crizotinib is now often reserved for settings where these newer agents are unavailable, but it remains a key proof‑of‑concept for ALK‑targeted therapy.[4,5,7]

For MET‑altered NSCLC, current molecular‑testing guidelines place *MET* among the “should test” genes alongside *RET*, *ERBB2* and *KRAS*, while *ALK*, *EGFR* and *ROS1* remain “must test,” reflecting a more heterogeneous and less well‑defined MET‑driven subset.[9] Crizotinib’s MET‑inhibitory activity underpins its use in MET‑addicted disease, but dedicated, more selective MET inhibitors are increasingly favored; detailed exon 14–specific crizotinib outcomes are not provided in these sources.[5,9]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

The EMA variation assessment report for XALKORI notes no absolute contraindications but emphasizes clinically significant hepatic, pulmonary, cardiac, and ocular risks that constrain use.[6] In pooled NSCLC datasets, hepatotoxicity is a prominent identified risk, with frequent ALT/AST elevations and rare severe drug‑induced liver injury; the CHMP requires regular liver function monitoring and dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation according to severity.[6] The risk management plan highlights hepatotoxicity as a key safety concern requiring ongoing pharmacovigilance and risk‑minimization measures.[6]

Serious pulmonary toxicity, including interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis, is recognized in the EMA assessment, with fatal cases reported; any treatment‑related ILD generally mandates permanent discontinuation.[6] Cardiac safety issues include QT interval prolongation and bradycardia; the assessment and updated product information recommend ECG and electrolyte monitoring in patients with cardiac risk factors and careful use with other QT‑prolonging or bradycardic agents.[6] Visual disorders (e.g., visual impairment, photopsia) are very common; the EMA places ocular events among important identified risks, recommending ophthalmologic evaluation and treatment interruption or discontinuation for severe visual symptoms.[6]

The EMA report also underscores clinically relevant CYP3A‑mediated drug–drug interactions: crizotinib is a CYP3A substrate and moderate inhibitor, so coadministration with strong CYP3A inhibitors or inducers, or with narrow‑therapeutic‑index CYP3A substrates, requires avoidance or close monitoring to prevent altered exposure and toxicity.[6]

| MET amplification (METamp) with METΔex14 | Up | Resistant (dose-dependent) | Selective amplification of the METΔex14 allele increases receptor density and signaling output, which can outstrip achievable crizotinib exposure; METamp is enriched at acquired resistance and can coexist with METΔex14, supporting persistent MET addiction despite TKI therapy. | [4][6][7][8] |
| MET–STAT3 transcriptional axis | Up | Resistant/persistent | MET activation recruits STAT3, promoting transcription of survival genes; in METΔex14 models, prolonged receptor signaling sustains STAT3 activation, which may contribute to cell persistence under MET inhibition and interact with MAPK/PI3K pathways to blunt crizotinib-induced apoptosis. | [4][6] |
| ROS1 fusion–SHP2–RAS–MAPK cascade | Down by crizotinib | Sensitive | ROS1 fusions constitutively activate SHP2–RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK and PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathways; crizotinib potently inhibits ROS1 kinase, producing high response rates and median PFS ~19 months in ROS1-rearranged NSCLC, with marked tumor shrinkage until secondary resistance develops. | [11][12][14][16] |
| ROS1 kinase domain solvent-front/gatekeeper (G2032R, D2033N, L2026M) | Up (secondary mutations) | Resistant | Solvent-front mutations G2032R and D2033N and gatekeeper L2026M remodel the ATP pocket, creating steric hindrance or altered hydrophobicity that markedly reduces crizotinib affinity while preserving ROS1 signaling; G2032R accounts for ~35–40% of post-crizotinib ROS1 resistance and confers high-level cross-resistance to several first/second-generation ROS1 TKIs. | [10][11][12][14][16] |
| EGFR/HER2-driven bypass in ROS1+ NSCLC | Up | Resistant | In ROS1-rearranged lung cancer progressing on crizotinib, activation of EGFR or HER2 establishes alternative RTK inputs that reactivate JAK/STAT3, PI3K/AKT/mTOR, and MAPK signaling, reducing dependence on ROS1; HER2-mediated bypass and EGFR activation are documented off-target resistance mechanisms necessitating combined RTK or downstream pathway blockade. | [11][12][14] |
| KRAS/MAPK-driven bypass in ROS1+ NSCLC | Up | Resistant | In ROS1 fusion-positive patients relapsing on crizotinib or lorlatinib, acquired KRAS G12C mutations, KRAS amplification, NRAS mutation, and MAP2K1 mutation are detected and correlate with ROS1-independent MAPK reactivation; these lesions sustain ERK signaling despite ROS1 inhibition, suggesting that combining ROS1 TKIs with MEK/ERK pathway inhibitors may overcome resistance. | [10][11][14] |
