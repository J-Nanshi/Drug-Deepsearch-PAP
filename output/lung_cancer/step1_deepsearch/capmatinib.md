## Drug Summary

Capmatinib is an oral, highly selective type Ib small‑molecule inhibitor of the mesenchymal–epithelial transition factor (MET) receptor tyrosine kinase, developed to target MET‑driven tumors, particularly non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) with MET exon 14 (METex14) skipping alterations. It is approved in several jurisdictions, including the United States, for adult patients with metastatic NSCLC whose tumors harbor METex14 skipping, as detected by an FDA‑approved test, reflecting its role as a precision oncology agent for this genomically defined subset of lung cancer.

METex14 skipping results in loss of a critical juxtamembrane domain involved in CBL‑mediated ubiquitination and degradation of MET, leading to impaired receptor turnover, increased MET stability at the cell surface, and sustained activation of downstream signaling pathways (e.g., RAS–MAPK, PI3K–AKT, STAT) that promote proliferation, survival, invasion, and metastasis. Capmatinib binds to the ATP‑binding site of MET, inhibiting MET autophosphorylation and phosphorylation of downstream effectors, thereby suppressing MET‑dependent oncogenic signaling and tumor cell growth.  

In clinical development, capmatinib demonstrated substantial antitumor activity in METex14‑mutated advanced NSCLC, including in treatment‑naïve and previously treated patients, with meaningful systemic and intracranial responses. These data established capmatinib as a preferred first‑line MET‑selective tyrosine kinase inhibitor for METex14‑positive NSCLC in current precision oncology practice.

## Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Capmatinib is an oral, ATP‑competitive, type Ib tyrosine kinase inhibitor that is highly selective for MET, with ≥10,000‑fold selectivity over other kinases in biochemical assays.[10],[20] It binds the activated MET kinase domain, engaging Y1230 in the activation loop, and blocks ATP binding, thereby inhibiting MET autophosphorylation and downstream signaling through PI3K/AKT, RAS/MAPK/ERK, STAT3 and mTOR/S6 pathways that drive proliferation, survival, invasion and epithelial–mesenchymal transition in NSCLC.[6],[11],[14],[15]

MET exon 14 skipping removes the juxtamembrane CBL‑binding degron (Y1003), impairing ubiquitination and lysosomal degradation, which stabilizes MET and prolongs oncogenic signaling; high‑level MET amplification similarly produces ligand‑independent, constitutive MET activation.[1],[6],[9],[12],[16] Preclinical and clinical data show that tumors with MET exon 14 skipping or high MET copy number are particularly sensitive to capmatinib, whereas MET‑wild‑type models are largely insensitive, supporting its use in MET‑addicted NSCLC.[8],[11],[15],[18],[20]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

- **HGF/MET signaling axis (c-MET receptor pathway)** – Capmatinib is a selective MET tyrosine kinase inhibitor that blocks ligand‑dependent and ligand‑independent MET activation in MET‑altered NSCLC, leading to down‑regulation of MET signaling and marked sensitivity in MET exon 14–skipping or high‑level MET‑amplified tumors.[1]

- **PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway** – Oncogenic MET activation drives PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling to promote survival and growth in NSCLC; capmatinib down‑modulates this pathway in MET‑dependent tumors, whereas alternative PI3K–AKT activation via other receptor tyrosine kinases or downstream alterations can sustain signaling and confer resistance.[1]

- **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway** – MET stimulates the RAS–MAPK cascade to support proliferation and invasion; capmatinib indirectly suppresses this pathway through MET inhibition, but KRAS alterations or activation of parallel RTKs can maintain MAPK output and reduce drug sensitivity.[1]

- **JAK–STAT signaling** – MET can couple to JAK–STAT, enhancing survival and inflammatory/EMT programs; incomplete JAK–STAT suppression or compensatory cytokine‑driven STAT activation may allow continued growth signaling despite MET blockade and contribute to resistance in NSCLC.[1]

- **EMT‑related programs** – MET activation promotes epithelial–mesenchymal transition, increasing motility and drug tolerance; capmatinib sensitivity is associated with dependency on MET‑driven epithelial–mesenchymal plasticity, while persistent EMT programs may underlie intrinsic or acquired resistance.[1]

## Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In MET exon 14 skipping (METex14)–positive NSCLC, the phase 2 GEOMETRY mono‑1 trial showed that capmatinib, a type Ib MET TKI, achieved an objective response rate (ORR) of 67.9%, median progression‑free survival (mPFS) 12.4 months, and median overall survival (mOS) 20.8 months in treatment‑naive patients, with lower ORR (40.6%) and shorter mPFS (5.4 months) in previously treated patients, indicating greater benefit when used upfront.[3] METex14 NSCLC is associated with aggressive biology and poor prognosis, but selective MET TKIs such as capmatinib have substantially improved outcomes in this molecularly defined adenocarcinoma‑predominant subgroup.[3,6]

For high‑level MET‑amplified NSCLC, MET amplification is recognized as a targetable driver but with generally more modest responses to MET TKIs than METex14 disease.[3,6] In EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, MET amplification is a common off‑target resistance mechanism to EGFR TKIs; combination strategies pairing an EGFR TKI with a MET inhibitor (including capmatinib in early trials) have shown clinically meaningful response rates in MET‑amplified or MET‑overexpressing tumors, supporting dual‑pathway blockade in this composite molecular subtype.[3,4,6,8]

METex14 and MET amplification occur across histologies, including adenocarcinoma, sarcomatoid, and less frequently squamous NSCLC, but robust capmatinib efficacy data are largely derived from non‑squamous cohorts.[3,6,7] Squamous or sarcomatoid NSCLC with MET amplification can respond to MET TKIs, though available data (mainly with crizotinib) suggest poorer outcomes than in adenocarcinoma, highlighting the need for histology‑stratified studies of capmatinib in these rarer subtypes.[4,6,7]

Capmatinib has no formal contraindications in the U.S. label, but carries multiple serious warnings. The FDA prescribing information highlights interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis, hepatotoxicity, pancreatic toxicity, hypersensitivity, photosensitivity, and embryo‑fetal toxicity as key safety concerns, with no boxed warning section specified.[3],[4] NCCN‑aligned guidance recommends that patients with MET exon 14–mutated NSCLC starting capmatinib be counseled on these risks and monitored closely, particularly older patients and those with comorbidities.[13]

ILD/pneumonitis is uncommon but potentially fatal. In GEOMETRY mono‑1, ILD/pneumonitis occurred in 4.8% of patients, including grade 3 events and one death; the label mandates permanent discontinuation for ILD of any grade and immediate interruption if suspected.[3],[4] Case reports describe capmatinib‑induced ILD appearing within days to weeks of therapy, improving after drug discontinuation and, in some cases, corticosteroids, underscoring the need for rapid evaluation of new cough, dyspnea, or ground‑glass opacities on imaging.[5],[7],[13]

Hepatotoxicity is a labeled important identified risk in the EMA risk‑management plan and FDA label, with ALT/AST elevations in ~15% and grade 3–4 events in ~7% of trial patients.[2],[3] A fatal case of severe acute hepatitis with renal deterioration has been reported, reinforcing recommendations for baseline and periodic liver tests and prompt dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation per label algorithms.[1],[2],[3] Pancreatic enzyme elevations and pancreatitis are also identified risks, requiring amylase/lipase monitoring and permanent discontinuation for pancreatitis.[2],[3]

Peripheral edema is the most common adverse reaction (≥20%) and a MET‑TKI class effect; NCCN‑referenced guidance emphasizes supportive measures (compression, elevation, diuretics) and dose modification when symptomatic.[3],[8],[13] Capmatinib carries a specific photosensitivity warning—patients should limit UV exposure and use protective clothing and sunscreen.[3],[8],[13] The drug can cause embryo‑fetal harm, so effective contraception and avoidance in pregnancy and breastfeeding are advised.[3],[4],[13] Strong or moderate CYP3A inducers should be avoided due to drug–drug interactions that may reduce capmatinib exposure.[3],[4]

## Key Clinical Trials

The pivotal phase II GEOMETRY mono‑1 trial (NCT02414139) evaluated capmatinib 400 mg BID in advanced NSCLC with MET exon 14 skipping (METex14) or high‑level MET amplification (gene copy number ≥10).[3],[10],[16] In METex14‑positive disease, treatment‑naïve patients achieved an ORR of 68% with median PFS 9.7–12.4 months, while previously treated patients had ORR ~40–41% and median PFS ~5.2–5.4 months.[3],[5],[10],[15],[16] In MET‑amplified NSCLC with gene copy number ≥10, ORR was 40% in treatment‑naïve and 29% in pretreated cohorts, with shorter PFS around 4.2 months.[1],[10],[16] GEOMETRY mono‑1 also demonstrated intracranial activity, with intracranial responses in most patients with brain metastases.[16]
