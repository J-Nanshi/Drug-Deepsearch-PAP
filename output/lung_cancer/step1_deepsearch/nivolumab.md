## Drug Summary

Nivolumab (Opdivo) is a fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody directed against programmed death‑1 (PD‑1), a key inhibitory immune checkpoint receptor expressed on activated T cells that down‑regulates antitumor immunity through interaction with its ligands PD‑L1 and PD‑L2.[1,2] By binding PD‑1 and preventing ligand engagement, nivolumab blocks recruitment of SHP‑2 and reverses downstream inhibition of PI3K–AKT and RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK signaling in T cells, thereby restoring cytokine production, cytotoxic granule release, and metabolic fitness in the tumor microenvironment.[1] This PD‑1/PD‑L1 axis blockade has become a central component of modern immuno‑oncology and is approved across multiple solid tumors, including non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).[1,2,6]

In lung cancer, nivolumab is FDA‑approved for previously treated advanced squamous and nonsquamous NSCLC that has progressed after platinum‑based chemotherapy, independent of PD‑L1 expression.[2,6] Nivolumab‑based regimens (nivolumab alone or in combination with ipilimumab and/or chemotherapy) are incorporated into contemporary NSCLC treatment algorithms as part of immune checkpoint blockade strategies that complement targeted therapy and anti‑angiogenic approaches.[2,6] In small‑cell lung cancer (SCLC), nivolumab has been evaluated as monotherapy and in combination with ipilimumab in relapsed extensive‑stage disease, leading to regulatory approval in later‑line settings and demonstrating durable responses in a subset of patients, though phase III data have shown limited overall survival benefit and substantial immune‑related toxicity with dual checkpoint blockade.[5–8] Overall, nivolumab exemplifies precision immunotherapy by selectively releasing PD‑1‑mediated brakes on antitumor T‑cell responses in lung cancer.

## Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Nivolumab is a human IgG4 monoclonal antibody that binds the programmed death‑1 (PD‑1) receptor on activated T cells and blocks its interaction with the ligands PD‑L1 and PD‑L2 expressed on tumor cells and other cells in the tumor microenvironment.[3,11,15,16] Engagement of PD‑1 by PD‑L1/PD‑L2 normally leads to phosphorylation of the PD‑1 cytoplasmic ITIM/ITSM motifs, recruitment of SHP‑2, and dephosphorylation of CD3ζ, ZAP‑70, and CD28, which suppresses PI3K–AKT, RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK, and PLCγ1–PKC–NF‑κB signaling, thereby reducing cytokine production, cytotoxic granule release, proliferation, and metabolic fitness and driving T‑cell exhaustion.[1,3,16] By occupying PD‑1, nivolumab prevents SHP‑2 recruitment and restores these downstream pathways, enhancing effector CD8⁺ T‑cell activity and broader T‑cell–mediated antitumor immunity within lung tumors.[1,3,15,16]

In NSCLC, PD‑1 is highly expressed on tumor‑infiltrating lymphocytes, including exhausted CD8⁺ T cells, while PD‑L1 is frequently upregulated on tumor and myeloid cells; clinical trials such as CheckMate 017 and 057 demonstrated that nivolumab improves overall survival versus docetaxel in previously treated squamous and nonsquamous NSCLC, confirming that its primary antitumor effect is immune‑mediated rather than cytotoxic.[1,11,16] Nivolumab monotherapy is now an established option across advanced NSCLC, and combinations with ipilimumab and/or chemotherapy further exploit complementary mechanisms of T‑cell priming and effector reinvigoration.[1,3,8,16] In SCLC, PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade is being explored (e.g., nivolumab‑based combinations in extensive‑stage disease), leveraging the typically high mutational burden and potential neoantigen load, although overall sensitivity is lower and optimal benefit often requires chemo‑immunotherapy or dual‑checkpoint strategies.[2,3,8,14]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

Key signaling and immune pathways modulated in NSCLC in the context of PD‑1 blockade (e.g., nivolumab) and associated with sensitivity or resistance include:

- **PD‑1/TCR signaling axis (no single KEGG ID for PD‑1; overlaps with TCR signaling hsa04660)** – PD‑1 engagement attenuates proximal TCR signaling and JAK/STAT‑dependent transcription of IL‑2 and IFN‑γ, promoting T‑cell exhaustion; anti‑PD‑1 antibodies restore these pathways and effector function, associating their re‑activation with treatment sensitivity.[1]

- **IFN‑γ–JAK/STAT signaling (KEGG JAK‑STAT hsa04630; MSigDB Hallmark “INTERFERON_GAMMA_RESPONSE”)** – IFN‑γ from reinvigorated T cells enhances antigen presentation and macrophage activation, but loss‑of‑function alterations in JAK1/2 or downstream negative regulation of STAT signaling drive acquired resistance to PD‑1 blockade.[1,3]

- **Antigen processing and presentation (KEGG hsa04612)** – PD‑1 blockade enhances dendritic‑cell and macrophage antigen‑presenting functions; epigenetic or transcriptional suppression of antigen presentation and T‑cell–attracting chemokines is linked to immune escape and resistance.[1,3]

- **PI3K–AKT pathway and metabolic checkpoint signaling (KEGG hsa04151)** – PTEN loss and PI3K activation contribute to PD‑L1 upregulation and an immunosuppressive TME, reducing sensitivity to ICIs, while modulation of PTEN/AMPK and related metabolic checkpoints can influence response.[1,3]

- **VEGF/angiogenesis pathway (KEGG hsa04370)** – VEGFA overexpression is associated with non‑inflamed immune signatures and reduced CD8⁺/Th1 gene expression, linking angiogenic signaling to resistance and providing a rationale for anti‑VEGF plus PD‑1 blockade combinations.[2]

- **Alternative immune checkpoints and exhaustion programs (e.g., LAG‑3, TIM‑3, VISTA; no single KEGG ID)** – Upregulation of non‑PD‑1 inhibitory receptors under PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade sustains T‑cell exhaustion and mediates secondary resistance.[1,3]

- **Tumor‑mutational‑burden/neoantigen and TIL‑related signatures (no single KEGG ID)** – High tumor mutation burden and robust CD8⁺/NK/DC gene signatures correlate with ICI‑responsive solid tumors such as NSCLC, whereas “cold” or non‑inflamed profiles are linked to primary resistance.[2,3]

## Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In advanced NSCLC overall, PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade is a key immunotherapy modality, positioned alongside chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and anti‑angiogenic agents in contemporary treatment algorithms.[1] Nivolumab, as an anti‑PD‑1 antibody, fits within this broader class of immunotherapies directed at PD‑1/PD‑L1, which are highlighted as central immune targets for NSCLC management.[1] Because NSCLC is highly heterogeneous, with oncogene‑defined subsets and diverse microenvironmental states, clinical benefit from PD‑1 blockade is concentrated in a subset of patients, and resistance—both primary and acquired—remains common.[1,2] Mechanistically, resistance can arise from inadequate CD8⁺ T‑cell priming/recognition or local immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments, phenomena described broadly for PD‑1/PD‑L1 therapies and applicable to nivolumab‑treated NSCLC.[2]

For histologic subtypes, NSCLC is dominated by LUAD and LUSC, which differ in gene‑expression profiles and proliferative behavior but currently share similar access to PD‑1–directed therapy.[1] LUAD frequently carries targetable oncogenic drivers, so nivolumab is typically deployed after failure of targeted therapy or chemotherapy, in a setting where prior treatments and co‑occurring genomic alterations may modulate immune responsiveness.[1] LUSC, which tends to proliferate faster and has a different mutational spectrum, is often managed primarily with chemo‑immunotherapy or immunotherapy‑based strategies; PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors are considered important because classic driver mutations are less prevalent.[1] Across both LUAD and LUSC, immunotherapy benefit is closely linked to PD‑1/PD‑L1 axis biology rather than a single histologic feature, reinforcing the need for biomarker‑driven rather than purely histology‑driven nivolumab selection.[1,2]

In SCLC, PD‑1/PD‑L1 antibodies are recognized as approved agents at the class level, but the disease is biologically and clinically distinct from NSCLC, with more aggressive behavior and different immune interactions.[2] The general PD‑1/PD‑L1 resistance mechanisms—such as lack of effective CD8⁺ T‑cell recognition and strong local immunosuppression—are particularly relevant in SCLC and likely contribute to the comparatively lower and less durable responses seen with checkpoint blockade in this subtype.[2] Consequently, SCLC remains more refractory to PD‑1 pathway inhibition than NSCLC, and combination strategies (e.g., with chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or other immunomodulators) are emphasized in the broader PD‑1/PD‑L1 literature as a way to overcome intrinsic resistance—concepts that inform ongoing nivolumab‑based development in SCLC.[2]

## Contraindications and Safety

Nivolumab has no formal absolute contraindications in the EMA SmPC, but use is constrained by immune‑mediated toxicities and limited experience in key high‑risk groups.[5] Solid organ transplant rejection has been added as an “immune system disorder” with frequency “not known,” and the SmPC warns that PD‑1 inhibitors, including nivolumab, may increase rejection risk; treatment should only proceed after weighing potential antitumor benefit against the risk of graft loss in transplant recipients.[1] Lung cancer trials also routinely excluded patients with uncontrolled autoimmune disease or significant interstitial lung disease, so guidelines recommend individualized use with extreme caution in these populations.[3],[5]

Immune‑related adverse reactions (irARs) are central to nivolumab safety. The SmPC and CHMP assessment identify immune‑mediated pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, endocrinopathies (thyroid dysfunction, hypophysitis, adrenal insufficiency, type 1 diabetes), nephritis/renal dysfunction, severe skin reactions, myocarditis, and other irARs as important identified risks requiring structured monitoring and management algorithms.[3],[5],[7] In the Opdivo EU assessment for NSCLC, immune‑mediated events were generally manageable with dose interruption, systemic corticosteroids, and permanent discontinuation for severe or recurrent high‑grade toxicity.[3],[6]

Pulmonary toxicity is particularly critical in NSCLC, where baseline lung reserve is often limited. ICI‑related pneumonitis (ICIP) is the most frequent and lethal pulmonary irAE, accounting for ~28% of ICI‑related deaths across cancers; incidence with PD‑1/PD‑L1 monotherapy is typically 3–5% in trials but higher in real‑world lung cancer cohorts (up to ~25–30%), especially with underlying COPD/ILD or combination immunotherapy.[13] Risk is higher with PD‑1 vs PD‑L1 inhibitors and with dual checkpoint blockade; grade ≥3 ICIP is associated with markedly worse progression‑free survival.[13]

From a precision‑oncology perspective, major safety priorities in lung cancer are: (1) avoiding or very cautiously using nivolumab in solid‑organ transplant recipients and patients with severe active autoimmune disease;[1],[5] (2) integrating pulmonary status (pre‑existing ILD, COPD, prior thoracic radiotherapy) and other organ comorbidities into regimen choice and monitoring intensity;[3],[5],[13] and (3) implementing long‑term, multidisciplinary surveillance for delayed and chronic irAEs, which can arise months to years into or after therapy and often require coordinated specialist and survivorship care.[16]
