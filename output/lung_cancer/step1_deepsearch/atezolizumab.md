## 1. Drug Summary

Atezolizumab (Tecentriq) is a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds programmed death‑ligand 1 (PD‑L1) and blocks its interaction with PD‑1 and B7.1, thereby releasing PD‑1/PD‑L1–mediated inhibition of cytotoxic T cells and restoring antitumor immune activity.[7][8][9] As a PD‑L1–blocking antibody, it is used as monotherapy or in combination regimens across multiple solid tumors, with lung cancer representing a major therapeutic area.[7][8]

In non–small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), intravenous atezolizumab and the subcutaneous co‑formulation atezolizumab/hyaluronidase (Tecentriq Hybreza) are approved as: adjuvant monotherapy after resection and platinum chemotherapy for stage II–IIIA NSCLC with PD‑L1 expression on ≥1% of tumor cells; first‑line monotherapy for metastatic NSCLC with high PD‑L1 expression (TC ≥50% or IC ≥10%) and no EGFR/ALK alterations; first‑line combination with bevacizumab, paclitaxel, and carboplatin or with nab‑paclitaxel and carboplatin for metastatic non‑squamous NSCLC without EGFR/ALK aberrations; and treatment of metastatic NSCLC after progression on platinum chemotherapy.[1][3][5][6][7][8]

In extensive‑stage small cell lung cancer (ES‑SCLC), atezolizumab is approved in combination with carboplatin and etoposide as first‑line therapy, with continued atezolizumab maintenance, reflecting its role as a backbone PD‑L1 inhibitor in this setting.[1][3][6][7][8] Precision‑oncology use of atezolizumab in lung cancer relies on integrating PD‑L1 expression, driver alterations (e.g., EGFR/ALK exclusion for certain indications), stage (adjuvant vs metastatic), histology, and prior systemic therapy to individualize PD‑L1 blockade–based regimens.[2][4][5][7][8]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Atezolizumab is a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody against PD‑L1 that sterically blocks PD‑L1 binding to PD‑1 and B7‑1 (CD80), while preserving PD‑1/PD‑L2 interactions and minimizing Fc‑mediated depletion of PD‑L1–expressing immune cells.[8–9] PD‑1 ligation normally recruits SHP phosphatases to ITIM/ITSM motifs, dephosphorylating CD28/TCR signaling components and driving T‑cell exhaustion; blockade restores CD8⁺ T‑cell activation, cytokine production, and tumor cell killing in the NSCLC tumor microenvironment.[9,11]

In advanced NSCLC, PD‑L1 is frequently overexpressed on tumor and immune cells and is a central mechanism of immune evasion, providing the biological basis for PD‑1/PD‑L1–directed therapy.[2,8,11] Clinically, higher PD‑L1 expression enriches for atezolizumab benefit but is imperfect, as survival gains are seen across PD‑L1 strata.[8] Tumor mutational burden (TMB) reflects neoantigen load and can further stratify benefit from atezolizumab and other ICIs, though cutoffs and predictive value are context‑dependent.[3,5,8] Oncogenic drivers such as *EGFR* and *ALK* typically confer low TMB and “cold” microenvironments with attenuated ICI benefit, whereas co‑mutations like *TP53* with *EGFR* or *KRAS* associate with higher PD‑L1 and TMB and may favor response to PD‑(L)1 blockade.[1,2,5]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic non-squamous NSCLC without actionable drivers, atezolizumab is used as PD‑L1–directed monotherapy in PD‑L1–high tumors or in combination with carboplatin–paclitaxel and bevacizumab (ABCP) irrespective of PD‑L1 level.[1,5] In IMpower150, transcriptomic subtyping (NMF1–4) showed that NMF4—adenocarcinoma‑enriched, B‑/T‑cell–inflamed with elevated PD‑L1 and favorable TMB—derived clear PFS/OS benefit from ABCP versus chemotherapy or atezolizumab–chemotherapy, consistent with dependence on a pre‑existing adaptive immune infiltrate.[1] NMF1, enriched for basal/squamous‑like features, also benefited from ABCP, suggesting that squamous‑like tumors with less intrinsically inflamed microenvironments may require intensified chemo‑immunotherapy plus VEGF blockade.[1]

NMF2 combined high tumor PD‑L1 with macrophage/monocyte and TGF‑β‑CAF enrichment and the lowest CD8⁺ T‑cell levels; STK11/KEAP1 alterations were more frequent, aligning with a metabolically hostile, “immune‑cold” TME and relative resistance to atezolizumab despite PD‑L1 positivity.[1,3] NMF3, characterized by well‑differentiated adenocarcinoma signatures, showed similar outcomes across regimens, implying limited incremental benefit from adding atezolizumab in an already favorable, moderately inflamed context.[1] More broadly, EGFR‑ or ALK‑driven NSCLC remains less responsive to PD‑(L)1 blockade, whereas KRAS‑mutant tumors—especially without STK11/KEAP1 co‑mutation—tend to have higher TMB/PD‑L1 and better ICI outcomes.[3,5,6]

In ES‑SCLC, adding atezolizumab to platinum–etoposide improves OS and 24‑month survival but most patients relapse, reflecting an intrinsically aggressive, variably inflamed disease.[4] A transcriptionally defined SCLC‑I subtype with higher CD8A/B expression derives greater survival benefit from EP‑atezolizumab, indicating that inflamed SCLC subsets are more atezolizumab‑sensitive while others remain largely resistant.[4,9]  



## 7. Contraindications and Safety

The current U.S. Tecentriq label lists no formal contraindications, but emphasizes the potential for severe and fatal immune‑mediated adverse reactions affecting any organ system, including pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, endocrinopathies, dermatologic reactions, nephritis/renal dysfunction, and solid organ transplant rejection.[1,2] Baseline and periodic monitoring of liver enzymes, creatinine, and thyroid function are recommended, with dose withholding or permanent discontinuation according to severity.[2]

The MX39795 protocol and its appendices specify that pre‑existing autoimmune diseases and immune deficiencies can contraindicate or strongly discourage atezolizumab, providing a structured list of such conditions.[3] Safety guidance for atezolizumab in this program highlights myelitis and facial paresis as identified risks and upgrades hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis from a potential to an identified risk, prompting heightened vigilance and early high‑dose steroids or other immunosuppression for severe immune‑mediated events.[3]

Infusion‑related and administration‑related reactions, including cytokine‑release syndrome, are adverse events of special interest with defined interruption, rate‑reduction, or discontinuation algorithms.[2,3] The FDA label also warns of serious complications of allogeneic HSCT given before or after PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade.[2] Embryo‑fetal toxicity is a key concern: atezolizumab can cause fetal harm, so effective contraception is advised and pregnancy exposures require expedited reporting and follow‑up.[2,3]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

In extensive-stage SCLC, the phase III IMpower133 trial (NCT02763579) established first-line atezolizumab plus carboplatin/etoposide as a new standard, improving median OS (12.3 vs 10.3 months; HR 0.70) and PFS (5.2 vs 4.3 months; HR 0.77) over chemotherapy alone, with benefit irrespective of PD-L1 status.[2],[5],[6]  

For previously treated advanced NSCLC, the phase III OAK trial (NCT02008227) showed atezolizumab 1200 mg q3w significantly prolonged OS versus docetaxel (13.8 vs 9.6 months; HR 0.73) across PD-L1 expression levels and histologies, with fewer grade 3–4 adverse events.[1],[9],[13]  

Across phase III programs (OAK, IMpower110, IMpower130), atezolizumab consistently demonstrated OS benefit over or added to platinum-based chemotherapy in metastatic NSCLC, particularly in PD-L1–high tumors and in first-line chemo‑immunotherapy combinations.[1],[6],[8],[13]
