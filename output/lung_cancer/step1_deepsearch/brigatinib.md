## 1. Drug Summary

Brigatinib (AP26113) is an orally administered, next/third‑generation ATP‑competitive ALK tyrosine kinase inhibitor developed to treat *ALK*-rearranged NSCLC, particularly after resistance or intolerance to crizotinib.[3–5] It received accelerated FDA approval in 2017 for patients with locally advanced or metastatic ALK‑positive NSCLC who have progressed on or are intolerant to crizotinib, based on Phase I/II and ALTA data showing objective response rates of ~54–72%, intracranial response rates of 53–67%, and median progression‑free survival (PFS) of about 12.9–13.2 months in the post‑crizotinib setting.[3,4] Consensus guidance and Canadian recommendations position brigatinib as a later‑line option after failure of earlier‑generation ALK TKIs (alectinib, ceritinib), with potential use as salvage therapy in patients progressing on second‑generation inhibitors.[5,6]

The pivotal Phase III ALTA‑1L trial (NCT02737501) randomizes ALK TKI‑naïve patients with advanced ALK‑positive NSCLC to brigatinib (90 mg lead‑in then 180 mg daily) versus crizotinib 250 mg twice daily; the primary endpoint of PFS by blinded independent review was met, supporting brigatinib as a first‑line alternative to crizotinib.[4,5] Mechanistically, brigatinib is a dimethylphosphine‑oxide–containing bisanilinopyrimidine TKI with potent activity against ALK and broad in‑vitro coverage of secondary ALK resistance mutations (e.g., C1156Y, I1171S/T, V1180L, L1196M, L1152R/P, E1210K, G1269A), enabling activity in many crizotinib‑refractory tumors.[3,4] Early pulmonary toxicity within the first week of treatment prompted adoption of a 7‑day 90 mg lead‑in before escalation to 180 mg daily, which is the recommended regimen in advanced ALK‑positive NSCLC.[3,4]  

**Key references:** Sabari et al., OncoTargets Ther 2017; Jain & Chen, Lung Cancer Targets Ther 2017; Melosky et al., Curr Oncol 2018.[3–5]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Brigatinib (AP26113) is an orally available, next‑generation ALK tyrosine kinase inhibitor with additional preclinical activity against EGFR and other kinases.[1],[12] In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, it potently inhibits EML4–ALK–driven signaling, suppressing downstream RAS–MAPK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR and JAK–STAT pathways that mediate proliferation and survival.[1],[10],[15] Biochemically, brigatinib is a highly potent inhibitor of native EML4–ALK (IC₅₀ ~14 nM) and retains activity (IC₅₀ <50 nM) against a broad spectrum of ALK resistance mutations, including C1156Y, L1152R/P, I1171N/S/T, V1180L, L1196M, G1269A and E1210K, with preclinical inhibition also reported for G1202R.[1],[12]

This broad mutational coverage underpins its role after crizotinib and in selected alectinib‑resistant settings such as I1171N, where clinical complete responses with durable intracranial control have been observed.[1],[11],[12] As a later‑generation ALK TKI with improved CNS penetration relative to crizotinib, brigatinib achieves high intracranial response rates in ALK‑positive advanced NSCLC, addressing the high burden of brain metastases characteristic of this molecular subtype.[1],[9],[16]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, EML4‑ALK drives a network of RTK‑like signaling axes—principally RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR and JAK–STAT—that promote proliferation, survival and metastasis; these same cascades are re‑engaged through bypass RTKs (EGFR, HER2/3, MET, IGF‑1R) when ALK is inhibited, underpinning resistance to ALK TKIs including brigatinib.[1],[2],[9],[12],[13],[15] EML4‑ALK forms cytoplasmic compartments that cluster RAS/MAPK and JAK/STAT effectors; ALK inhibition dissolves these compartments and enables GRB2 and other adaptors to relay signals from alternative RTKs, restoring downstream pathway activity.[15] Across lung cancer, constitutive activation and crosstalk between MAPK and PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathways, often coupled to JAK–STAT, is a central driver of growth and a major mechanism of adaptive and acquired resistance to targeted agents.[1],[6],[8],[12],[13]

Key downstream pathways relevant to brigatinib’s activity and resistance in ALK‑, ROS1‑ or other RTK‑driven NSCLC include:

1. **PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling** (KEGG hsa04151) – A dominant survival axis downstream of ALK, EGFR, HER2/3, MET and IGF‑1R; its constitutive activation via driver signaling, MET or IGF‑1R amplification, or PTEN loss sustains proliferation and anti‑apoptosis and mediates TKI resistance.[1],[2],[8],[9],[11],[13]

2. **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway** (KEGG hsa04010) – Critical for EML4‑ALK‑driven survival, with MEK inhibition (e.g., trametinib) enhancing and prolonging responses to ALK TKIs in preclinical EML4‑ALK models; KRAS/NRAS/BRAF/MAP2K1 alterations reactivate ERK and cause resistance to EGFR and ALK inhibitors.[1],[8],[9],[12],[15]

3. **JAK–STAT (especially STAT3) signaling** (KEGG hsa04630) – EML4‑ALK maintains JAK–STAT activation, upregulating anti‑apoptotic proteins such as BCL‑2 and MCL‑1; STAT3 activation via SERPINB4 or bypass RTKs contributes to ALK‑TKI resistance and is a rational co‑target with ALK.[2],[6],[12],[15]

4. **RTK bypass networks (EGFR/HER2/HER3, MET, IGF‑1R)** – Bypass activation of EGFR, HER2/HER3, MET or IGF‑1R preserves PI3K–AKT and ERK signaling when ALK is inhibited, and is a well‑documented resistance mechanism to first‑ and second‑generation ALK TKIs; combined ALK plus EGFR, MET or IGF‑1R blockade can restore sensitivity in preclinical models.[9],[11],[12],[13],[15]

5. **Hippo–YAP axis and Src family kinases** – Src and YES1 activate MAPK, PI3K–AKT–mTOR and IL‑6–JAK–STAT3, and crosstalk with Hippo/YAP; YAP upregulation reinforces EGFR/MAPK signaling and is implicated in TKI resistance, suggesting that Src or YAP inhibition may augment ALK/EGFR TKI efficacy.[3],[11],[12],[13]

6. **Cell‑cycle (Cyclin D–CDK4/6–RB) control** – RTK‑driven MAPK and PI3K signaling upregulate cyclin D and CDK4/6, overcoming p53/p16 checkpoints to drive G1–S progression; p53 status modulates sensitivity to PI3K/mTOR inhibition in NSCLC cell lines, supporting combined targeting of ALK/RTKs and cell‑cycle machinery.[2],[4],[6],[13]

7. **Apoptosis and pro‑survival BCL‑2 family programs** – PI3K–AKT and ERK limit pro‑apoptotic effectors (e.g., BIM) and enhance anti‑apoptotic proteins (BCL‑2, MCL‑1); in ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, STAT3‑mediated BCL‑2/MCL‑1 induction and EMT‑associated changes (e.g., via Slug) blunt ALK‑TKI‑induced cell death and foster resistance.[2],[6],[12],[15]

8. **EMT, plasticity and microenvironmental signaling** – EMT and cellular plasticity, driven by pathways including RAS/MAPK, PI3K–AKT, STAT3, NF‑κB, YAP and AXL, enable bypass of oncogene addiction and are repeatedly linked to resistance to EGFR and ALK TKIs; CAF‑ and TAM‑derived signals (e.g., IL‑6/STAT3, growth factors) further reinforce these pathways.[3],[9],[12],[13],[14]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

Brigatinib’s principal activity is in ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, a distinct molecular subset typically affecting younger, often non‑smoking patients with adenocarcinoma histology.[3,7,16] In ALK TKI–naive disease, the phase III ALTA‑1L trial showed brigatinib markedly prolongs PFS versus crizotinib (BIRC median 24.0 vs 11.0 months; HR 0.49) with superior intracranial control, supporting its use as a first‑line ALK‑targeted agent, including in patients with baseline brain metastases.[1,6,19]  

In the post‑crizotinib setting, phase I/II and ALTA phase II data demonstrate high systemic response rates (~45–56%) and median PFS of ~13–17 months with the 180 mg (with 7‑day 90 mg lead‑in) regimen, alongside intracranial ORR of 50–67% and intracranial PFS around 13–18 months, indicating substantial benefit for CNS‑metastatic ALK‑positive NSCLC.[2,3,4,8] Brigatinib retains in‑vitro and clinical activity against multiple secondary ALK mutations (e.g., I1171N, V1180L, L1196M), and durable responses have been reported after alectinib failure driven by I1171N, highlighting its role in molecularly guided sequencing.[5,11,15,19]  

Variant‑specific data are emerging: EML4–ALK v3 is associated with more aggressive CNS disease and higher rates of solvent‑front G1202R, a mutation that can mediate resistance to brigatinib and other second‑generation TKIs, often necessitating transition to lorlatinib.[9,10,14,19] Beyond ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, brigatinib shows clinically meaningful efficacy in ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC (ORR 71% in TKI‑naive; median PFS 12 months), but responses after prior ROS1 TKI are more modest and activity in other ROS1‑positive solid tumors appears limited.[18,20,22]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

The FDA multidisciplinary review for brigatinib notes no absolute contraindications but highlights early-onset interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis as a key serious risk, with events clustering in the first week and sometimes being fatal.[2] New or worsening dyspnea, cough, or hypoxia require immediate interruption, evaluation, and permanent discontinuation for severe (grade 3–4) or recurrent events.[2]

Hypertension, bradycardia, and other cardiovascular effects were frequent and clinically significant in trials; regular blood pressure and heart rate monitoring is recommended, with dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation for persistent grade ≥3 abnormalities.[2] Metabolic toxicities include hyperglycemia, often requiring initiation or intensification of antihyperglycemic therapy, and creatine phosphokinase (CPK) elevation with myalgia; these mandate baseline and periodic laboratory monitoring and dose modification according to severity.[2]

Hepatotoxicity (ALT/AST and bilirubin elevations), pancreatic enzyme increases (amylase/lipase), and visual disturbances were also observed and are managed with scheduled laboratory tests, patient counseling, and dose holds or reductions as per grading.[2] Embryo‑fetal toxicity was identified in non‑clinical studies; pregnancy should be avoided and effective contraception used during treatment.[2]

Brigatinib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4 and CYP2C8, and the review emphasizes avoiding strong CYP3A inhibitors/inducers or adjusting brigatinib dose when co‑administration is unavoidable.[2] Patients with significant hepatic or renal impairment require careful dose selection and monitoring because of altered exposure and potentially increased toxicity.[2]  



## 8. Key Clinical Trials

In crizotinib-pretreated *ALK*-rearranged NSCLC, brigatinib demonstrated response rates of 54%–72%, intracranial response rates of 53%–67%, and median PFS ~12.9–13.2 months in Phase I/II studies, leading to FDA breakthrough and orphan designations.[1] The randomized Phase II ALTA study (NCT02094573) compared dosing strategies (including a 90 mg lead‑in to 180 mg daily) and confirmed substantial and durable systemic and CNS activity in this post‑crizotinib setting.[1] ALTA‑1L (NCT02737501) is an ongoing Phase III head‑to‑head trial of first‑line brigatinib versus crizotinib in ALK inhibitor–naïve metastatic ALK+ NSCLC and is expected to define its role as an upfront standard of care.[1]
