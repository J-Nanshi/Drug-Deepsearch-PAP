## 1. Drug Summary

Cabozantinib (XL184) is an oral, small-molecule multikinase inhibitor that primarily targets MET, AXL, and VEGFR2, with additional activity against RET, KIT, FLT3, TIE2, and ROS1, thereby simultaneously modulating tumor cell signaling and angiogenesis.[18],[19],[24] It is approved as monotherapy for progressive metastatic medullary thyroid carcinoma and as a systemic therapy for advanced renal cell carcinoma and hepatocellular carcinoma, where it improves progression-free survival and induces objective responses.[18],[20],[24]

In lung cancer, MET activation and overexpression are implicated in tumor progression, poor prognosis, and resistance to EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs).[2],[19],[23] Cabozantinib has demonstrated clinical activity in advanced NSCLC, including EGFR‑mutant disease progressing on EGFR TKIs and in EGFR–wild-type non-squamous NSCLC, where cabozantinib-containing regimens improved progression-free survival versus erlotinib alone.[17],[21],[23] Preclinical data show that cabozantinib inhibits MET and AXL phosphorylation and downstream SRC signaling, suppressing HGF/MET- and GAS6/AXL-driven migration and invasion, supporting its potential role in precision oncology for MET- and RET/ROS1-driven NSCLC and for overcoming bypass-mediated resistance to EGFR TKIs.[18],[19],[21],[23]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Cabozantinib is an oral, ATP‑competitive small‑molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitor that targets MET, VEGFR1–3, AXL, RET, KIT, FLT3, TRKB and TIE2, and has shown preclinical and early clinical activity in NSCLC.[5,16] By binding the ATP pocket of these receptors, it prevents autophosphorylation and recruitment of adaptor proteins such as GRB2 and GAB1, thereby attenuating canonical downstream cascades including RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK, PI3K/AKT/mTOR, JAK/STAT and SRC/FAK pathways that regulate proliferation, survival, motility, EMT and angiogenesis.[5,8]

### MET and VEGFR2: oncogenic signaling and bypass resistance in NSCLC

In NSCLC, MET is frequently activated by amplification, exon‑14–skipping mutations, overexpression, or HGF‑driven autocrine/paracrine loops, driving PI3K/AKT and RAS/RAF/ERK signaling and promoting invasion, EMT and angiogenesis.[5,8] MET amplification is a prototypical “bypass” mechanism of resistance to EGFR TKIs, restoring ErbB3‑PI3K/AKT signaling and accounting for ~5–22% of resistance after first/second‑generation EGFR TKIs and 7–15% after first‑line osimertinib.[3,4] MET/HGF overactivity can also mediate resistance to RET, ALK and KRAS‑directed agents in NSCLC.[3,8] Cabozantinib directly inhibits MET and all three VEGFRs, so in lung tumors it can simultaneously blunt MET‑driven oncogenic signaling in tumor cells and VEGFR2‑dependent angiogenesis in the microvasculature, a dual mechanism that is analogous to the microenvironment‑directed activity seen with other VEGFR‑TKIs.[5,10,14]

### AXL and EMT‑linked drug‑tolerant states

AXL, a TAM‑family RTK, is broadly expressed in NSCLC and is tightly linked to EMT, invasive growth, and drug resistance.[7,18,21] In EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, AXL activation—often via its ligand GAS6—interacts with EGFR and HER3 to maintain downstream MAPK/ERK, PI3K/AKT and SRC signaling, conferring intrinsic and acquired resistance to EGFR TKIs including osimertinib; pharmacologic AXL inhibition reverses this resistance in preclinical models.[7,19,21] AXL upregulation is also an established resistance mechanism to multiple TKIs in NSCLC (EGFR, ALK, others) and promotes an immune‑evasive, EMT‑high phenotype.[6,7,18,20] Because cabozantinib is a potent inhibitor of AXL as well as MET and VEGFR2,[5,10,12] its mechanism in lung cancer extends beyond anti‑angiogenesis to suppression of AXL‑driven EMT programs and drug‑tolerant persister populations, particularly in EGFR‑, ALK‑ or ROS1‑driven tumors that have escaped lineage‑specific TKIs via AXL upregulation.[6,7,18–21]

### Integration into precision‑oncology contexts

Cabozantinib’s multikinase profile is particularly relevant in several molecular NSCLC subsets. MET amplification and HGF overexpression are recurrent bypass mechanisms in EGFR‑mutant disease; combinations of EGFR TKIs with MET‑targeted agents (including cabozantinib) are therefore being evaluated to re‑suppress shared PI3K/AKT and MAPK signaling in resistant tumors.[3,4,8,12] In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, AXL‑GAS6 signaling from tumor‑associated macrophages and CAFs can drive resistance to alectinib, and dual ALK/AXL inhibition with other TKIs (e.g. gilteritinib) overcomes this phenotype, underscoring how AXL‑positive, EMT‑like states constitute a generalizable resistance axis in oncogene‑addicted lung cancers.[20] ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC that acquires ROS1 kinase‑domain mutations (e.g. G2032R, D2033N) remains sensitive to cabozantinib in preclinical systems, and a phase II study is evaluating cabozantinib in NSCLC with RET/ROS1/NTRK fusions or increased AXL/MET activity, positioning it as a salvage option where MET and AXL bypass, EMT and ROS1 resistance co‑exist.[11,13,15,21]

Collectively, in lung cancer cabozantinib acts through convergent inhibition of MET‑, VEGFR‑ and AXL‑driven networks in both tumor cells and the microenvironment: (i) blocking MET‑ and AXL‑mediated bypass of EGFR/ALK/ROS1 inhibition; (ii) collapsing VEGFR2‑dependent tumor vasculature; and (iii) targeting EMT‑high, immune‑evasive subclones that are enriched in advanced, TKI‑resistant NSCLC.[3–8,11,12,18–21]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In MET exon 14–altered NSCLC, a dedicated phase II trial of cabozantinib (NCT01639508) enrolled 23 patients with isolated MET exon 14 alterations and three with concurrent exon 14 alteration plus amplification; most (86%) had previously received a type I MET TKI.[13][20] Cabozantinib achieved a 20% objective response rate (5/25 assessable patients) with responses lasting 4–39 months and a median progression-free survival (PFS) of 4.5 months, supporting its role as a type II MET TKI capable of rescuing resistance after capmatinib/tepotinib or crizotinib.[13][20] Case-level data also suggest intracranial and hepatic responses in MET exon 14–mutant NSCLC progressing on crizotinib, indicating potential activity in brain and visceral metastases.[3][11]

For MET‑amplified NSCLC, the same cabozantinib phase II trial included two patients with isolated MET amplification and three with concurrent amplification plus exon 14 skipping, but outcomes were reported in aggregate, precluding amplification-specific efficacy estimates.[13][20] Broader MET‑amplified NSCLC literature emphasizes that high‑level amplification is an actionable driver and that type I MET TKIs (capmatinib, tepotinib, crizotinib) are guideline‑listed options, with type II inhibitors like cabozantinib being explored particularly in the post–type I TKI resistance setting.[6][9][10][11]

In other oncogene‑defined NSCLC subsets, cabozantinib has demonstrated clinically meaningful activity against several resistance-driven targets. In RET‑rearranged NSCLC, a phase II trial (also NCT01639508) reported a 28% response rate and median PFS of 6 months, establishing RET fusions as actionable with cabozantinib.[22] In ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, cabozantinib produced durable disease control in a CD74‑ROS1–positive patient treated for 20 months and, separately, achieved 12‑month PFS in a ROS1‑positive case with lorlatinib‑resistance mutation L2086F, supporting its use as salvage therapy in ROS1‑driven resistance settings.[17][19] In EGFR‑mutated NSCLC with acquired resistance, cabozantinib plus erlotinib yielded partial responses and disease stabilization after prior EGFR TKI failure, and individual cases with acquired RET fusions (e.g., CCDC6‑RET) responded to cabozantinib, underscoring its utility in complex resistance landscapes.[8][23]

For small cell lung cancer, no human clinical or translational data specifically evaluating cabozantinib were identified in the provided sources. Consequently, cabozantinib’s activity, optimal setting, and resistance patterns in SCLC remain undefined based on the current evidence set.

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Cabozantinib has no absolute contraindications in the current U.S. COMETRIQ label; earlier boxed warnings for gastrointestinal (GI) perforation, fistulas, and hemorrhage were removed in 2020, but these events remain prominent warnings and precautions and require close monitoring and treatment interruption or discontinuation when severe.[1] Regulatory reviews for CABOMETYX similarly emphasize class-typical VEGFR/MET TKI risks, including severe hemorrhage, GI perforation and fistula, thromboembolic events, and impaired wound healing, with recommendations to hold cabozantinib before and after major surgery until adequate wound healing is achieved.[1,2,4]

Across large phase III programs in renal cell and hepatocellular carcinoma, the most frequent adverse events include diarrhea, decreased appetite, palmar–plantar erythrodysesthesia, fatigue/asthenia, nausea, vomiting, hypertension, and transaminase elevations; grade ≥3 toxicities occur in a substantial proportion of patients and often necessitate dose reductions or interruptions.[2–4] Serious risks highlighted in regulatory documents and combination‑trial protocols include hypertensive crisis, arterial and venous thromboembolism, osteonecrosis of the jaw, proteinuria/nephrotic syndrome, reversible posterior leukoencephalopathy syndrome, and, when combined with atezolizumab, overlapping immune‑related toxicities requiring immune‑modulating management.[2–5,7]

For metastatic NSCLC, NCCN guidance on VEGFR TKIs and immune checkpoint inhibitors underscores clinically significant risks of pneumonitis, hemorrhage, and thromboembolism, with permanent discontinuation recommended for severe or life‑threatening pneumonitis.[9,14,16] The phase III atezolizumab–cabozantinib versus docetaxel trial (NCT04471428) excludes or mandates caution in patients with recent major cardiovascular events, high bleeding risk (including central lung lesions with cavitation or tumor invasion of major vessels), uncontrolled hypertension, or prior severe immune‑related events, and aligns cabozantinib management with its Investigator’s Brochure, including vigilance for venous/arterial thrombosis, angioedema, and musculoskeletal disorders.[5,7] In lung cancer practice, careful baseline assessment of cardiopulmonary status, tumor‑related bleeding risk (e.g., central squamous tumors), brain metastases, and prior immunotherapy toxicities is essential before initiating cabozantinib‑based regimens.[2,4,7,9,13,16]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

### COSMIC-021 (NCT03170960): Phase 1b cabozantinib ± atezolizumab after prior ICI

COSMIC-021 is a multicenter phase 1b study in advanced solid tumors; the NSCLC expansion included stage IV non-squamous NSCLC without *EGFR/ALK/ROS1/BRAF V600E* alterations, after progression on one prior PD-(L)1 inhibitor and ≤2 prior systemic regimens.[6] Patients received cabozantinib 40 mg daily plus atezolizumab 1200 mg q3w (n=81) or cabozantinib 60 mg daily monotherapy (n=31).[6] In the combination cohort, investigator-assessed ORR was 20% (BIRC 10%), median PFS ~4.5–4.7 months, and median OS was numerically longer than with cabozantinib alone (ORR 6%, median PFS ~3.4–3.5 months), with manageable but frequent grade 3–4 TRAEs (44–48%) and two grade 5 TRAEs.[6] These results showed modest activity and supported phase III testing but did not identify clear biomarker-enriched subgroups.[6]

### ECOG-ACRIN 1512 (NCT01708954): Phase II erlotinib vs cabozantinib vs combination in EGFR–wild-type NSCLC

ECOG-ACRIN 1512 randomized patients with previously treated advanced non-squamous *EGFR* wild-type NSCLC (1–2 prior lines) to erlotinib 150 mg, cabozantinib 60 mg, or erlotinib 150 mg plus cabozantinib 40 mg daily.[5] The primary endpoint was PFS versus erlotinib; 111 eligible patients formed the primary analysis set.[5] Median PFS was 1.8 months with erlotinib, 4.3 months with cabozantinib (HR 0.39), and 4.7 months with the combination (HR 0.37), with a signal for OS improvement but substantially higher grade ≥3 toxicity in cabozantinib-containing arms (70–72% vs 33%).[5] Investigators concluded that cabozantinib-based regimens had clinically meaningful activity superior to erlotinib alone in this EGFR–wild-type population and warranted further study.[5]

### CONTACT-01 (NCT04471428): Phase III atezolizumab + cabozantinib vs docetaxel after chemo-immunotherapy

CONTACT-01 was an open-label phase III trial in metastatic NSCLC (non-squamous and squamous) with progression after platinum chemotherapy plus anti–PD-(L)1 therapy, randomizing patients 1:1 to atezolizumab 1200 mg q3w plus cabozantinib 40 mg daily or docetaxel 75 mg/m² q3w, with OS as the primary endpoint.[7,18] Among 186 vs 180 treated patients, median OS was 10.7 vs 10.5 months (HR 0.88; P=0.3668), while median PFS was modestly longer with the combination (4.6 vs 4.0 months; HR 0.74).[7,18] Safety was consistent with known profiles but with more treatment-related grade 5 AEs and dose modifications in the combination arm; overall, the regimen did not improve OS and is not supported as a standard post–chemo-immunotherapy option in unselected metastatic NSCLC.[7,18]

| RET fusion signaling under multikinase vs selective inhibition | Down (incompletely with MKI) | Partially Sensitive / Functionally Resistant | Multikinase RET TKIs (cabozantinib, vandetanib, lenvatinib) show only modest efficacy and high off‑target toxicity in RET‑fusion NSCLC, likely due to incomplete RET suppression at tolerable doses; this functional limitation underlies the shift toward highly selective RET TKIs, but demonstrates that RET pathway downregulation by cabozantinib can still confer some clinical benefit. | [9],[10],[13],[15],[16] |
| ROS1 fusion signaling (CD74‑ROS1 and kinase‑domain mutants) | Down | Sensitive | Cabozantinib potently inhibits ROS1; it produced a rapid, near‑complete response in a CD74‑ROS1‑rearranged lung adenocarcinoma with a crizotinib‑resistant D2033N solvent‑front mutation, and controlled disease for 1.5 years in a CD74‑ROS1‑positive NSCLC case, indicating that suppression of ROS1‑driven MAPK/PI3K/STAT3 signaling mediates sensitivity, including in some solvent‑front and gatekeeper mutants. | [17],[18],[19],[20],[21],[22],[23],[24] |
| ROS1 solvent‑front resistance mutations (e.g., D2033N, G2032R) | Down | Sensitive (to cabozantinib; Resistant to type I TKIs) | Structural and cellular data show that D2033N disrupts crizotinib binding but not cabozantinib binding; cabozantinib inhibits CD74‑ROS1 D2033N with low‑nanomolar IC₅₀ and induces apoptosis, and preclinical work indicates activity against G2032R and other solvent‑front mutations, making downregulation of this altered ROS1 pathway a key mechanism to overcome type I TKI resistance. | [17],[18],[19],[22],[23],[24] |
| ROS1 L2086F and compound ROS1 resistance mutations | Down | Sensitive (to cabozantinib) | ROS1 L2086F is refractory to all available ROS1 TKIs except cabozantinib; preclinical studies show cabozantinib retains strong potency against L2086F and L2086F‑containing compound mutants, and combined lorlatinib–cabozantinib therapy achieved meaningful clinical responses, supporting cabozantinib‑mediated suppression of L2086F‑mutant ROS1 signaling as a salvage pathway. | [17],[22],[24] |
| VEGFR2‑mediated angiogenesis | Down | Sensitive | Cabozantinib targets VEGFR2 in addition to MET, RET and ROS1; anti‑angiogenic effects via VEGFR2 blockade are recognized contributors to efficacy of multikinase inhibitors in thoracic malignancies, and are likely to augment direct oncogene pathway inhibition in NSCLC by reducing tumor vascular supply and altering the microenvironment. | [3],[9],[13],[15],[19],[20] |
| AXL‑driven bypass and microenvironmental signaling | Down | Sensitive (bypass blockade) | Cabozantinib inhibits AXL, a kinase implicated as a bypass driver and resistance mediator in multiple oncogene‑addicted NSCLCs; by targeting AXL alongside MET/RET/ROS1, cabozantinib can theoretically suppress alternative survival routes engaged after selective TKI therapy, contributing to resensitization or prolonged disease control. | [3],[9],[15],[19] |
| RET pathway with co‑occurring MET/KRAS alterations | Up (bypass/co‑drivers) | Resistant (to RET‑selective TKIs; potentially Sensitive to broader RTK blockade) | RET‑fusion NSCLC can acquire resistance to selective RET inhibitors through MET amplification or KRAS activation; multikinase agents such as cabozantinib, which inhibit both RET and MET and have broader RTK coverage, may more effectively downregulate these convergent signaling axes than RET‑selective agents alone in such co‑altered tumors. | [9],[10],[13],[15],[16] |
| mTOR pathway activation in ROS1‑positive NSCLC | Up | Resistant | In a CD74‑ROS1‑positive NSCLC treated with cabozantinib, acquired resistance was associated with emergence of an mTOR L1433F mutation and increased mTOR mutation abundance, suggesting that upregulated PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling can bypass upstream RTK inhibition and sustain proliferation despite continued MET/ROS1/RET/VEGFR2 blockade. | [20] |
| TP53 mutation–associated genomic instability | Up (genomic stress / apoptosis evasion) | Resistant | The same cabozantinib‑resistant CD74‑ROS1 case acquired a TP53 G245S mutation, a known loss‑of‑function alteration; defective TP53 can diminish apoptosis in response to RTK pathway inhibition and facilitate clonal evolution under cabozantinib pressure, contributing to resistance despite continued suppression of the primary ROS1 signaling pathway. | [20] |
| MET/HGF signaling in METex14‑altered NSCLC | Down | Sensitive | MET exon 14 skipping impairs receptor degradation, causing sustained MET activation and downstream RAS/MAPK and PI3K/AKT signaling; cabozantinib, a type II MET TKI, suppresses this pathway and produced objective responses and disease control in METex14‑altered NSCLC, including after prior MET TKIs. | [5],[7],[8],[13],[16],[20] |
| MET‑driven resistance to EGFR TKIs (MET amplification) | Down | Sensitive (to cabozantinib; Resistant to EGFR TKIs) | MET amplification is a common bypass mechanism of acquired resistance to EGFR TKIs in NSCLC; by directly inhibiting MET, cabozantinib can attenuate MET‑dependent PI3K/AKT and MAPK signaling that persists despite EGFR blockade, providing a rational strategy in EGFR‑mutant, MET‑amplified resistance settings. | [2],[3],[4],[5],[7],[8],[11],[23] |
| RET fusion signaling (e.g., KIF5B‑RET, CCDC6‑RET) | Down | Sensitive | RET fusions drive oncogenic signaling via MAPK and PI3K/AKT in a subset of NSCLC; cabozantinib inhibits RET and achieved ~28% ORR and ~6‑month PFS in RET‑rearranged NSCLC, and has induced responses in EGFR‑mutant tumors with acquired RET fusions, demonstrating clinically meaningful RET pathway suppression. | [4],[8],[9],[10],[13],[15],[16],[22],[23] |
| ROS1 fusion signaling (CD74‑ROS1 and kinase‑domain mutants) | Down | Sensitive | ROS1 fusions activate MAPK, PI3K/AKT and STAT3; cabozantinib potently inhibits ROS1 and produced durable disease control (up to ~20 months) in CD74‑ROS1‑positive NSCLC, including cases with resistance to prior ROS1 TKIs, indicating effective downregulation of ROS1‑driven oncogenic signaling. | [2],[7],[11],[13],[15],[17],[18],[19],[20],[21],[22],[23],[24] |
| ROS1 solvent‑front and L2086F resistance mutations | Down | Sensitive (to cabozantinib; Resistant to other ROS1 TKIs) | Solvent‑front (e.g., D2033N, G2032R) and L2086F ROS1 mutations disrupt binding of type I ROS1 TKIs; structural and cellular data show cabozantinib retains low‑nanomolar potency against these mutants, and clinical reports describe prolonged benefit in lorlatinib‑resistant L2086F‑mutant disease, implicating cabozantinib‑mediated suppression of mutant ROS1 signaling. | [17],[18],[19],[20],[21],[22],[23],[24] |
| VEGF–VEGFR2‑mediated angiogenesis (VEGFA/VEGFC–KDR/NRP2) | Down | Sensitive when VEGF‑driven; VEGFC‑high subtypes relatively Resistant | Cabozantinib targets VEGFR2/KDR and exhibits strong anti‑angiogenic activity; in NSCLC and endothelial models, VEGFA/VEGFR2 signaling drives proliferation and neovascularization, whereas VEGFC–KDR–NRP2 and co‑activated FGF/YAP–TAZ pathways are linked to relative resistance, with combined VEGF/VEGFC axis blockade enhancing cabozantinib’s antiproliferative and anti‑angiogenic effects. | [1],[3],[5],[6],[7],[8] |
| AXL/TAM (GAS6–AXL) axis and EMT | Down | Sensitive (bypass blockade); EMT‑high state marks relative Resistance to upstream TKIs | AXL is frequently upregulated in NSCLC, promotes EMT, invasion and immune evasion, and mediates resistance to EGFR, ALK and other TKIs; cabozantinib inhibits AXL and reduces GAS6‑induced migration and invasion, suggesting that downregulation of AXL/TAM signaling can suppress EMT‑linked drug‑tolerant states and enhance sensitivity to targeted therapy. | [2],[3],[5],[6],[7],[8],[18],[19],[20],[21] |
| PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling | Down (via RTK blockade); can be secondarily Up via mTOR mutation | Sensitive when RTK‑driven; Resistant when mTOR is activated downstream | MET, ROS1, RET, AXL and VEGFR converge on PI3K–AKT–mTOR; cabozantinib downregulates this axis by inhibiting upstream RTKs, but an mTOR L1433F mutation emerging in a cabozantinib‑treated CD74‑ROS1 case was associated with resistance, indicating that downstream mTOR activation can bypass RTK inhibition. | [5],[8],[19],[20] |
| RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) cascade | Down (secondary to RTK inhibition) | Sensitive | Oncogenic RTKs targeted by cabozantinib (MET, RET, ROS1, AXL, VEGFR2) signal through RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK to drive proliferation and survival; cabozantinib’s multikinase inhibition therefore convergently reduces ERK activity, limiting compensatory MAPK upregulation that underlies resistance to more selective TKIs. | [3],[5],[7],[8],[13],[15] |
| Hippo–YAP/TAZ oncogenic program | Down (with VEGFC/KDR/NRP2 blockade plus cabozantinib) | Sensitive when YAP/TAZ are suppressed; Resistant when YAP/TAZ remain high | In NSCLC models, VEGFC upregulates YAP and TAZ, promoting survival and therapy resistance; knockdown of VEGFC/KDR/NRP2 decreases YAP/TAZ and, together with cabozantinib, more strongly inhibits proliferation, implicating a VEGF–Hippo axis that modulates cabozantinib responsiveness. | [3],[8] |
| EMT and invasion program (TGF‑β/Smad, Wnt/β‑catenin, RTK‑driven EMT) | Down (EMT drivers) | Sensitive when EMT is suppressed; EMT‑high tumors relatively Resistant | EMT, driven by TGF‑β, Wnt/β‑catenin and RTKs such as MET and AXL, underlies metastasis and broad TKI resistance in NSCLC; by inhibiting MET, AXL and pro‑angiogenic cues, cabozantinib can reduce EMT and invasive behavior, although strongly EMT‑high tumors may remain relatively resistant. | [5],[6],[8],[18],[19],[21],[23] |
| TP53 mutation–associated genomic instability | Up (genomic stress / apoptosis evasion) | Resistant | In a cabozantinib‑treated CD74‑ROS1‑positive NSCLC, acquired TP53 G245S (loss‑of‑function) coincided with resistance; defective TP53 impairs apoptosis and facilitates clonal evolution under RTK inhibition, allowing tumor cells to persist despite continued suppression of MET/ROS1/RET/VEGFR2 signaling. | [20] |
