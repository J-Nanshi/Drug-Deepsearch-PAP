## 1. Drug Summary

Tepotinib is an oral, highly selective, reversible, ATP‑competitive small‑molecule inhibitor of the MET receptor tyrosine kinase (type Ib MET TKI), designed to potently block MET phosphorylation and downstream signaling (RAS/MAPK, PI3K–AKT, STAT) that drive proliferation, survival, invasion, and brain metastasis in MET‑dependent tumors.[21][22] MET exon 14 (METex14) skipping removes the juxtamembrane c‑Cbl binding site, impairs receptor ubiquitination and degradation, and leads to sustained MET activation; this alteration defines an oncogene‑addicted NSCLC subset (~3–4% of cases) with poor prognosis and limited benefit from immunotherapy.[13][18][23] 

As a precision-oncology agent, tepotinib is approved in multiple regions (Japan, US, EU, Canada) for adults with advanced or metastatic NSCLC harboring METex14 skipping, at a recommended dose of 450 mg orally once daily until disease progression or unacceptable toxicity.[9][10][12][13] The pivotal phase II VISION trial (NCT02864992) showed clinically meaningful and durable activity in METex14 NSCLC—overall response rate ~46–56%, median progression-free survival ~8.5–11 months, and intracranial responses in patients with brain metastases—supporting its role as a targeted standard of care for this molecularly defined NSCLC subgroup across lines of therapy.[1][2][6][13][22][23]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Tepotinib is an oral, highly selective, ATP‑competitive type Ib MET tyrosine kinase inhibitor that binds the MET catalytic domain and blocks MET autophosphorylation and signaling in MET‑dependent tumors, including NSCLC with MET exon 14 (METex14) skipping or MET amplification.[4],[8] Under physiologic conditions, hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) binding induces MET dimerization and phosphorylation of Y1234/Y1235 in the kinase domain and Y1349/Y1356 in the C‑terminal docking site, which recruits SH2‑containing adaptors (e.g., Gab1, Grb2, Src, p85) and activates PI3K/AKT, MAPK/ERK, STAT3, JAK/STAT, and NF‑κB pathways that promote proliferation, survival, motility, invasion, epithelial–mesenchymal transition, angiogenesis, and stemness.[4],[5],[8]

In NSCLC, METex14 results from aberrant splicing that deletes exon 14 and removes the juxtamembrane Y1003 CBL‑binding degron, impairing ubiquitin‑mediated receptor degradation and causing increased MET stability and sustained activation.[8] METex14, MET amplification, and MET overexpression are established oncogenic drivers in a molecular subset of NSCLC and are all sensitive to MET TKIs.[4],[8] By occupying the ATP‑binding pocket and engaging the MET activation loop (around Y1230), tepotinib prevents MET kinase activity, thereby shutting down downstream PI3K/AKT, RAS/MAPK/ERK, JAK/STAT, STAT3, and NF‑κB signaling required for tumor cell growth and survival in MET‑addicted tumors.[4],[5],[8]

Clinically, tepotinib has demonstrated meaningful antitumor activity in advanced NSCLC with METex14 skipping in the phase II VISION trial, with an objective response rate of ~45% and median progression‑free survival of 8.5–8.9 months, confirming that sustained pharmacologic MET blockade can effectively control METex14‑driven disease.[6],[8] In EGFR‑mutant NSCLC with acquired MET amplification as a resistance mechanism to EGFR TKIs, combination strategies such as tepotinib plus gefitinib or tepotinib plus osimertinib restore inhibition of shared downstream pathways and improve progression‑free and overall survival compared with chemotherapy, illustrating that MET inhibition can resuppress reactivated PI3K/AKT and RAS/MAPK signaling in this resistance setting.[3],[6] Nonetheless, activation or amplification of alternate receptor tyrosine kinases and downstream nodes (e.g., KRAS, PIK3CA) can maintain PI3K/AKT and MAPK/ERK activity despite MET blockade, and preclinical data show that co‑inhibition of SHP2, a central transducer for multiple RTKs, synergizes with tepotinib and delays or overcomes resistance in MET‑altered lung cancer models.[3],[4]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

- **MET/HGF axis (MET receptor tyrosine kinase signaling)**  
  - Regulation: Tepotinib is a highly selective type I MET TKI that blocks kinase activity and downstream MET signaling in MET exon 14–skipping or MET‑amplified NSCLC, including EGFR‑mutant tumors with MET‑driven acquired resistance to EGFR TKIs.[1][5][6][7]  
  - Sensitivity/Resistance: High‑level MET amplification or MET exon 14 skipping sensitizes tumors to MET inhibition, whereas secondary MET kinase mutations (e.g., D1228, Y1230, D1246) or loss of MET amplification can mediate resistance to MET‑targeted regimens, including tepotinib‑based combinations.[2][5][8]

- **PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway**  
  - Regulation: MET amplification acts as a major bypass track for EGFR TKIs, re‑engaging downstream survival cascades such as PI3K–AKT despite EGFR blockade in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC.[5][6]  
  - Sensitivity/Resistance: Dual EGFR–MET inhibition (e.g., tepotinib plus gefitinib or osimertinib) can suppress MET‑driven bypass signaling and restore sensitivity after EGFR TKI failure in MET‑amplified disease.[1][2][6][7]

- **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway**  
  - Regulation: Aberrant MET signaling maintains MAPK activity as an EGFR‑independent resistance route following first‑ to third‑generation EGFR TKIs.[5][6]  
  - Sensitivity/Resistance: By inhibiting MET, tepotinib disrupts this MAPK‑mediated bypass, contributing to the improved progression‑free and overall survival seen with tepotinib plus EGFR TKIs in MET‑amplified EGFR‑mutant NSCLC.[6][7]

- **JAK/STAT (including STAT3) pathway**  
  - Regulation: MET is a pleiotropic RTK whose oncogenic activation can feed into multiple downstream pathways, including JAK/STAT, in addition to PI3K–AKT and MAPK.[5]  
  - Sensitivity/Resistance: MET‑driven JAK/STAT signaling represents part of the broader resistance network after EGFR inhibition; combining tepotinib with EGFR TKIs targets this MET‑centered signaling hub in MET‑altered NSCLC.[2][5][6]

- **Epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT) / invasive growth program**  
  - Regulation: MET dysregulation (amplification or exon 14 skipping) is a key oncogenic pathway in NSCLC and is implicated in aggressive biology and treatment resistance.[5][6]  
  - Sensitivity/Resistance: MET‑directed therapy with tepotinib in MET‑ex14 or MET‑amplified tumors can counteract MET‑driven invasive and EMT‑like programs, translating into durable clinical responses in appropriately selected patients.[5][6]

- **RTK crosstalk and bypass signaling (EGFR and other drivers)**  
  - Regulation: MET amplification is the most frequent EGFR‑independent resistance mechanism to osimertinib and other EGFR TKIs, functioning as a parallel RTK that sustains downstream signaling when EGFR is inhibited.[1][2][5][6]  
  - Sensitivity/Resistance: Clinical trials (INSIGHT, INSIGHT 2) show that combining tepotinib with gefitinib or osimertinib overcomes MET‑amplification–mediated resistance, yielding high objective response rates and prolonged survival in EGFR‑mutant, MET‑amplified NSCLC.[1][2][6][7]

- **MET‑centered resistance network (secondary MET mutations and loss of amplification)**  
  - Regulation: Under dual EGFR–MET TKI pressure, tumors can evolve on‑target MET resistance mutations (e.g., D1246H) or lose MET amplification, reshaping signaling dependencies.[2]  
  - Sensitivity/Resistance: These adaptive changes diminish sensitivity to tepotinib‑based combinations and may necessitate switching MET inhibitor class (type I vs II) or targeting alternative emergent drivers.[2][5][8]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

Tepotinib is a selective MET inhibitor used in advanced NSCLC with MET exon 14 (METex14) skipping, a canonical oncogenic driver now recognized among the routinely targetable alterations in metastatic disease.[2] METex14 occurs predominantly in adenocarcinoma but is also enriched in pulmonary sarcomatoid carcinoma; class data for selective MET TKIs, including tepotinib and capmatinib, show substantial objective responses and durable disease control across these histologies, supporting a driver‑ and not histology‑restricted paradigm.[2] MET amplification is more often a secondary resistance mechanism (e.g., after EGFR‑TKIs) than a primary driver; MET inhibitors, including tepotinib, are under active investigation here, but clinical benefit appears less consistent and remains exploratory.[1]

Current approvals for MET TKIs (capmatinib, tepotinib, savolitinib in China) are confined to advanced/metastatic METex14 NSCLC; extension into perioperative settings is conceptually supported by the success of other targeted agents and immunotherapy in early‑stage driver‑positive disease, but MET‑directed perioperative data are not yet mature.[2,3] Selective MET TKIs demonstrate intracranial antitumor activity and are being developed in populations with brain metastases, an important consideration given the high metastatic burden (including bone and brain) observed in oncogene‑driven NSCLC.[1,2]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Regulatory documents list hypersensitivity to tepotinib or excipients (including lactose) as the only formal contraindication; however, both FDA and EMA emphasize cautious use in patients with significant comorbidities and in those with severe renal (CrCl <30 mL/min) or hepatic impairment (Child‑Pugh C), where use is not recommended owing to lack of data.[1,2] As a class, MET TKIs are generally well tolerated but require proactive monitoring and early dose modification for grade ≥3 adverse events to maintain adherence and limit treatment interruptions in an often elderly NSCLC population.[4,8,9]

Peripheral edema is the hallmark, dose‑limiting toxicity of tepotinib, occurring in about 65–70% of patients in VISION, with grade 3 events in ~9–10% and very rare grade 4 or discontinuations.[8–10,13] Edema typically has an insidious but early onset (median ~8 weeks), may be accompanied by hypoalbuminemia or pleural effusion, and is best managed with early non‑pharmacologic measures (compression, limb elevation, physical activity) plus timely dose interruption or reduction; frequent short treatment breaks and prolonged reduced‑dose therapy were effective without loss of activity.[2,9,10] NCCN‑aligned expert guidance and MET‑focused consensus documents stress patient education and early reporting of swelling to prevent functional decline.[8,13–15]

Gastrointestinal toxicities (nausea, diarrhea, vomiting) are common (each ~20–30%) but usually grade 1–2, occur mainly in the first weeks, and tend to resolve with continued therapy; taking tepotinib with food and using standard antiemetics/antidiarrheals minimizes the need for dose modification.[2,8,9] ALT/AST elevations (~12% overall; grade ≥3 ~3%) are generally asymptomatic and reversible; EMA recommends baseline and periodic liver tests, with interruption, dose reduction, or permanent discontinuation for higher‑grade or bilirubin‑associated injury.[2,9,10] Mild creatinine increases (~26%) reflect OCT2/MATE inhibition of tubular secretion rather than structural nephrotoxicity, so creatinine‑based eGFR should be interpreted cautiously and alternative GFR assessments considered to avoid unnecessary dose changes.[2,4,9,10]

Interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis is uncommon but serious: a low incidence was seen in VISION, yet a tepotinib‑related fatal ILD case is reported in real‑world and consensus data.[9,13] Labels mandate immediate interruption if ILD is suspected, evaluation for alternative causes, and permanent discontinuation if drug‑related ILD is confirmed.[2,9,13] QTc prolongation has been infrequent but warrants ECG and electrolyte monitoring in patients with risk factors or concomitant QT‑prolonging drugs.[2] From a drug–drug interaction standpoint, tepotinib is a P‑gp substrate but shows only weak interaction with strong CYP3A/P‑gp modulators; acid‑reducing agents and most CYP3A/P‑gp inhibitors/inducers are not expected to cause clinically relevant exposure changes, though general TKI DDI vigilance (polypharmacy review, avoidance of narrow‑index P‑gp substrates when possible) remains advisable.[2,4,8–10]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Tepotinib has been primarily evaluated in the single‑arm phase II VISION trial (NCT02864992), enrolling advanced/metastatic NSCLC with MET exon 14 skipping detected by tissue and/or liquid biopsy.[1,3,5–8,11,14] Across cohorts A+C (N=313), the objective response rate (ORR) was 51.4% with median duration of response (mDOR) 18.0 months; treatment‑naïve and previously treated patients showed similar ORR (~45–57%) and median progression‑free survival (mPFS ~11–14 months), supporting METex14 as a bona fide oncogenic driver.[3,7,8,11,14] Long‑term analyses showed meaningful intracranial activity (intracranial ORR 66.7% in patients with measurable brain metastases) and durable benefit irrespective of age, including patients ≥80 years.[3,11,14,17] Safety in METex14 NSCLC is characterized by class‑typical peripheral edema, creatinine increase, hypoalbuminemia, and mild gastrointestinal events; grade ≥3 treatment‑related adverse events occur in ~25–40% with low discontinuation rates.[3,4,7,8,14]

Early phase work defined the recommended phase II dose. A first‑in‑man trial in Western patients with advanced solid tumors (NCT01014936) established 500 mg once daily as a biologically active dose based on pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic modeling and ≥95% MET phosphorylation inhibition, without reaching a maximum tolerated dose.[7,10,14] A dedicated Japanese phase I study (NCT01832506) confirmed 500 mg once daily as the recommended phase II dose, with no dose‑limiting toxicities up to 500 mg and a safety profile dominated by grade 1–2 edema and transaminase elevations.[10]

Beyond METex14 NSCLC, combination strategies target MET‑mediated resistance to EGFR TKIs. In the randomized phase Ib/II INSIGHT trial (NCT01982955), tepotinib plus gefitinib in EGFR‑mutant, T790M‑negative NSCLC with MET amplification after prior EGFR‑TKI achieved marked benefit versus chemotherapy in the MET‑amplified subgroup: mPFS 16.6 vs 4.2 months and mOS 37.3 vs 13.1 months, with ORR 66.7% vs 42.9%.[14] These data underpin the ongoing INSIGHT‑2 phase II study (NCT03940703) of tepotinib plus osimertinib in first‑line osimertinib‑relapsed, EGFR‑mutant, MET‑amplified NSCLC, where preliminary results indicate an ORR of 54.5% in centrally confirmed MET‑amplified disease.[14,20]
