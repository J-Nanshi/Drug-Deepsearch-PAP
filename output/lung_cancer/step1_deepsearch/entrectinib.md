## 1. Drug Summary

Entrectinib (Rozlytrek) is an orally active, CNS‑penetrant small‑molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitor that selectively targets TRKA/B/C (encoded by NTRK1/2/3), ROS1 and ALK.[3][17][21] It was rationally designed to cross the blood–brain barrier and is a weak P‑glycoprotein substrate, enabling sustained CNS exposure and robust intracranial antitumor activity in preclinical brain tumor models.[2][17]  

Regulatory approvals include treatment of adults with ROS1‑positive metastatic non‑small‑cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and of adults and pediatric patients with NTRK fusion‑positive solid tumors; in the US, these indications were granted in 2019 via accelerated or regular approval pathways, and the EMA issued a conditional marketing authorisation in 2020 for ROS1‑positive NSCLC not previously treated with ROS1 inhibitors.[4][7][9][15][20] In an integrated analysis of the phase I/II ALKA‑372‑001, STARTRK‑1 and STARTRK‑2 trials, entrectinib 600 mg once daily produced an objective response rate (ORR) of ~67–68%, median duration of response 15.7–20.5 months, and median progression‑free survival ~15.7–16.8 months in ROS1 TKI‑naïve locally advanced/metastatic ROS1‑fusion–positive NSCLC, with similar efficacy in patients with and without baseline CNS metastases.[2][3][5] Intracranial ORR was ~49–79% and median intracranial duration of response ~12.9 months, confirming clinically meaningful CNS activity.[2][5]  

Mechanistically, entrectinib inhibits TRK, ROS1 and ALK fusion kinases, blocking downstream MAPK and PI3K/AKT signaling, thereby suppressing proliferation and inducing apoptosis in oncogene‑addicted tumors.[4][17][20] Based on its systemic and intracranial efficacy and manageable safety profile, contemporary guidelines and regulatory reviews position entrectinib as a preferred first‑line targeted therapy for metastatic ROS1‑positive NSCLC, particularly when CNS metastases are present or anticipated.[3][5][18][20]  

**Key references:** integrated ROS1‑positive NSCLC analyses (ALKA‑372‑001, STARTRK‑1, STARTRK‑2),[2][3][5] FDA approval communication,[7] EMA assessment and EU product information,[4][15][20] and pharmacology/CNS‑penetration data.[17][21]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Entrectinib is an orally available, ATP‑competitive tyrosine kinase inhibitor that targets ROS1, ALK and TRKA/B/C (NTRK1/2/3), and was specifically designed for high CNS penetration.[2][5][7] In ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, ROS1 fusions generate constitutively active kinases that drive downstream RAS–MAPK and PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling, promoting proliferation and survival; entrectinib potently inhibits ROS1, leading to blockade of these pathways and tumor regression.[3][6][7][8] Compared with crizotinib, entrectinib shows markedly higher in‑vitro anti‑ROS1 potency and superior blood–brain barrier penetration, translating into high systemic and intracranial response rates in ROS1‑positive NSCLC with brain metastases.[1][3][7]

NTRK1/2/3 fusions similarly produce ligand‑independent TRK activation, sustaining AKT/PI3K/ERK and PLCγ signaling; TRK inhibition with entrectinib induces profound growth inhibition in NTRK‑rearranged cancers, including lung tumors.[4][8] In NTRK‑fusion NSCLC, entrectinib’s concurrent targeting of TRK and ROS1/ALK and its CNS activity enable durable control of both extracranial disease and brain metastases.[2][5][7]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

1. **MAPK/ERK signaling (e.g., KEGG hsa04010; Reactome R-HSA-5673001)** – In ROS1‑rearranged HCC78 NSCLC cells, entrectinib inhibits ROS1 and reduces ERK phosphorylation, consistent with pathway downregulation in sensitive cells; in resistant HCC78ER cells harboring KRAS G12C and KRAS amplification, ERK remains activated despite ROS1 blockade, supporting MAPK/ERK reactivation as a key resistance driver.[1],[19]

2. **PI3K/AKT signaling (e.g., KEGG hsa04151; Reactome R-HSA-1257604)** – Entrectinib decreases AKT phosphorylation in ROS1‑dependent HCC78 cells, indicating downregulation of PI3K/AKT in responders; in TPM3‑ROS1 CUTO28‑ER cells with MET amplification, MET activation sustains PI3K/AKT signaling and proliferation despite continued entrectinib, mediating resistance.[1],[4],[18]

3. **JAK/STAT3 signaling (e.g., KEGG hsa04630; Reactome R-HSA-198745)** – ROS1 inhibition by entrectinib reduces downstream STAT3 activity in sensitive ROS1‑rearranged models, whereas MEK inhibition in KRAS‑mutant HCC78ER cells induces compensatory STAT3 activation, limiting the efficacy of ROS1+MEK co‑targeting and contributing to residual resistance.[1],[19]

4. **RAS signaling (e.g., KEGG hsa04014)** – Acquisition of KRAS G12C and KRAS amplification in HCC78ER cells activates RAS signaling, uncoupling ERK from ROS1 and driving robust entrectinib resistance; similar RAS/MAPK reactivation via KRAS or BRAF alterations is described as a recurrent bypass route in ROS1‑TKI–resistant NSCLC.[1],[3],[5],[23]

5. **TRK (NTRK) signaling (e.g., KEGG hsa04080; Reactome R-HSA-187037)** – In NTRK‑fusion–positive NSCLC and other solid tumors, entrectinib potently inhibits TRK and downstream MAPK/PI3K/PLCγ pathways, with pathway downregulation correlating with high response rates; solvent‑front mutations such as NTRK3 G623R restore TRK signaling and confer on‑target resistance.[9],[12],[14],[16]

6. **MET signaling (e.g., KEGG hsa04010 MET branch; Reactome R-HSA-6806834)** – In ROS1+ NSCLC models chronically exposed to entrectinib, ecDNA‑mediated MET amplification leads to strong MET overexpression and activation, which re‑engages PI3K/AKT and RAS/MAPK pathways and drives resistance; MET or SHP2 inhibition can suppress these downstream signals and partially restore sensitivity.[4],[7],[21]

7. **DNA damage/apoptosis pathways (e.g., Reactome R-HSA-73894, R-HSA-109581)** – In ROS1‑rearranged HCC78 cells, entrectinib induces p‑p53, p‑H2AX and cleaved PARP, indicating activation of DNA damage response and apoptotic pathways that underlie drug sensitivity; these pro‑apoptotic outputs are attenuated once bypass signaling (e.g., KRAS–ERK) or MET‑driven survival pathways dominate in resistant cells.[1],[19]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, entrectinib produces high objective response rates and prolonged progression‑free survival in TKI‑naïve patients, with median PFS around 15–19 months and substantial intracranial activity, reflecting strong oncogene dependence and CNS penetration.[10],[14],[16] Nonetheless, resistance emerges via both kinase‑domain mutations (e.g., G2032R, F2004V, L2026M) and bypass signaling; preclinical and clinical data show L2026M and G2032R confer marked entrectinib resistance, often necessitating switch to next‑generation ROS1 TKIs.[9],[13–15]

For NTRK fusion–positive NSCLC, pooled basket analyses of entrectinib demonstrate high response rates (ORR ~57–70%) and durable benefit across tumor types, including lung, with meaningful intracranial responses in patients with baseline brain metastases.[1],[3],[5],[7] Case reports of EML4::NTRK3‑rearranged lung adenocarcinoma and other NTRK fusion–positive NSCLCs document rapid, deep systemic and CNS responses to entrectinib, supporting its use as first‑line therapy in metastatic TRK‑driven disease.[5–7]

Across these biomarker‑defined subgroups, entrectinib is best positioned as initial targeted therapy in ROS1‑rearranged and NTRK fusion–positive metastatic NSCLC, while progression should prompt comprehensive molecular reassessment to identify on‑target TRK/ROS1 mutations or off‑target alterations and guide transition to next‑generation TKIs or rational combinations.[2],[7],[12–16]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

The FDA communication for entrectinib highlights serious, potentially dose‑limiting toxicities including congestive heart failure, central nervous system (CNS) effects, skeletal fractures, hepatotoxicity, hyperuricemia, QT interval prolongation, and vision disorders.[1] These events warrant baseline and periodic cardiac, hepatic, and metabolic monitoring in lung cancer patients, with dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation according to severity as per regulatory labeling and guideline practice.

CNS adverse reactions (cognitive impairment, dizziness, dysesthesia, mood or sleep changes, ataxia) are characteristic and clinically important because entrectinib is designed for CNS‑penetrant activity.[1] They may impair performance status and daily functioning; clinicians should counsel patients and consider early dose modification if neurologic symptoms emerge.

Off‑target systemic toxicities include fatigue, constipation, dysgeusia, edema, diarrhea, nausea, dyspnea, myalgia, arthralgia, weight gain, cough, vomiting, pyrexia, anemia, and vision disorders; serious adverse reactions occur in a substantial minority of treated patients.[1] Skeletal fractures and hyperuricemia require attention to bone health and uric acid management, particularly in frail or comorbid NSCLC populations.

Entrectinib is metabolized by CYP3A, and the label emphasizes clinically relevant drug–drug interaction risks with strong/moderate CYP3A inhibitors or inducers and with other QT‑prolonging agents.[1] Co‑administration should be avoided or managed with dose adjustment and ECG/electrolyte monitoring. Entrectinib is embryo‑fetotoxic; pregnancy must be avoided, and breastfeeding is contraindicated during treatment.[1]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Entrectinib’s development in lung cancer is anchored in three histology‑independent phase I/II trials—ALKA‑372‑001 (EudraCT 2012‑000148‑88), STARTRK‑1 (NCT02097810), and STARTRK‑2 (NCT02568267)—that enrolled adults with locally advanced/metastatic solid tumors harboring *ROS1* or *NTRK* fusions.[10–12,14,15] Across studies, eligible patients had ECOG 0–2, measurable disease by RECIST 1.1, prior systemic therapy allowed but no prior ROS1/TRK inhibitor, and asymptomatic or treated/stable CNS metastases were permitted, mirroring real‑world advanced NSCLC populations.[10–12,14,15]

In the integrated *ROS1* fusion‑positive NSCLC analysis from ALKA‑372‑001, STARTRK‑1, and STARTRK‑2, 53 ROS1‑TKI‑naïve patients received entrectinib ≥600 mg once daily; ORR was 77%, with median duration of response (DoR) 24.6 months and median PFS 24.6 or 19.0 months in early reports, and intracranial ORR 55% with median intracranial DoR 12.9 months in 20 patients with baseline brain metastases, establishing robust systemic and CNS activity.[12,14,15] An updated integrated dataset (efficacy‑evaluable N=161, follow‑up ≥6 months) confirmed ORR 67.1%, median DoR 15.7 months, median PFS 15.7 months, and 12‑month OS 81%; in 24 patients with measurable baseline CNS disease, intracranial ORR was 79.2% and median intracranial PFS 12.0 months, supporting entrectinib as a CNS‑active first‑line option for *ROS1*‑positive NSCLC.[14]

For *NTRK* fusion‑positive solid tumors, including NSCLC, an integrated analysis of ALKA‑372‑001, STARTRK‑1, and STARTRK‑2 identified 54 adult patients with metastatic/locally advanced *NTRK1/2/3* fusion‑positive cancers treated at ≥600 mg daily; ORR was 57% (7% complete), median DoR 10 months, and median PFS 11.2 months.[10] Lung cancer constituted one of the largest tumor types in this cohort, with an ORR of 70% and clinically meaningful intracranial responses in patients with baseline brain metastases, underpinning the tumor‑agnostic approval of entrectinib for *NTRK* fusion‑positive NSCLC.[5,6,10]
