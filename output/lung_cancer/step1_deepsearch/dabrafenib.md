## 1. Drug Summary

Dabrafenib is an oral, ATP-competitive inhibitor of mutant BRAF, with highest activity against class I BRAF V600E, which constitutively activates the RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway and drives proliferation and survival in a small but actionable subset of NSCLC.[4] BRAF mutations are identified in ~1.5–3.5% (up to 4%) of NSCLC, roughly half being V600E; these tumors are predominantly adenocarcinomas and show distinct clinicopathologic features, with V600E more often in women and never‑smokers.[1,4,10] BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC is recognized as an oncogene‑addicted subset and is recommended for routine molecular testing in advanced disease within guideline-based NGS panels.[4,14]

In metastatic BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC, dabrafenib has been developed mainly in combination with the MEK1/2 inhibitor trametinib to achieve deeper and more durable MAPK blockade. In the pivotal phase II BRF113928 trial (NCT01336634), dabrafenib plus trametinib produced objective response rates of 63.9–68.4%, median PFS ~10–10.2 months, and median OS ~17–18.2 months in both treatment‑naïve and pretreated stage IV BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC, with a manageable toxicity profile dominated by pyrexia and gastrointestinal/skin events.[2–4] These results led to regulatory approval of dabrafenib–trametinib for metastatic BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC and its adoption as a standard first‑line targeted regimen in international guidelines, including ESMO.[1,3,4,10]

Within precision oncology, dabrafenib exemplifies a mutation‑specific, histology‑anchored therapy: it is highly effective in BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC but not in non‑V600E BRAF variants, and its benefit varies across tumor types, supporting the need for accurate molecular stratification and disease‑context–specific deployment.[4,7,9,10]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Dabrafenib is an oral small‑molecule BRAF kinase inhibitor that selectively targets mutant BRAF, particularly BRAFV600E, within the RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) signaling cascade that regulates cellular proliferation, survival, migration, angiogenesis, and cell‑cycle progression.[1],[2] The V600E substitution in the activation loop constitutively activates BRAF kinase, driving MEK1/2 and ERK1/2 phosphorylation independent of upstream RAS and receptor tyrosine kinases, and acts as an oncogenic driver in a subset (≈1–3%) of NSCLCs.[1],[2] By inhibiting BRAFV600E, dabrafenib suppresses downstream MAPK signaling, thereby inhibiting growth and survival of BRAFV600‑mutant cells; this effect is enhanced when combined with the allosteric MEK1/2 inhibitor trametinib, which more completely blocks MAPK pathway output and improves antitumor activity versus either agent alone.[1]

In BRAFV600E‑mutant NSCLC, the biologic behavior is aggressive and relatively chemotherapy‑resistant, but targeted inhibition of BRAF with or without MEK inhibition yields higher response rates than cytotoxic chemotherapy, consistent with pathway addiction to MAPK signaling.[2] Class I BRAF mutations (all V600 variants, including V600E) signal as active monomers and are the primary molecular context in which dabrafenib ± trametinib has demonstrated robust clinical benefit in lung cancer trials.[2] In contrast, non‑V600 class II (kinase‑activating dimers) and class III (kinase‑impaired, RAS‑dependent) BRAF mutations rely on RAF dimers and/or upstream RAS activity; available clinical and preclinical data in NSCLC indicate more limited and heterogeneous sensitivity to selective BRAF inhibition, and these non‑V600 classes are not established indications for dabrafenib‑based therapy.[2]  



## 4. Primary Targets (Human)

### Main human molecular targets of dabrafenib relevant to lung cancer

| Target | Role in lung cancer biology and relation to dabrafenib |
|--------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| BRAF V600E (mutant BRAF kinase) | Class I activating mutation present in ~1.5–3.5% of NSCLC, driving RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK signaling independent of RAS and promoting proliferation and survival, particularly in adenocarcinoma.[1],[8],[15] Dabrafenib is an ATP‑competitive BRAF kinase inhibitor that selectively inhibits BRAF V600 monomers, suppressing MAPK signaling and forming the backbone of approved dabrafenib–trametinib therapy in metastatic BRAF V600E NSCLC.[1],[5],[8],[16] |
| Other activating BRAF V600 mutants (e.g., V600K) | V600K and related class I mutants also signal as active monomers with high kinase activity toward MEK–ERK, though they are much less common in NSCLC than melanoma.[1],[2],[16] Dabrafenib potently inhibits BRAF V600 variants and, in combination with trametinib, is approved in a tumor‑agnostic fashion for unresectable/metastatic solid tumors with BRAF V600E, supporting extrapolation to rare non‑V600E class I BRAF‑mutant thoracic tumors.[1],[2],[16] |
| Non‑V600 mutant BRAF (high‑ and impaired‑kinase) | Non‑V600 BRAF mutations account for ~50% of BRAF‑mutant NSCLC and can be either high‑kinase or impaired‑kinase, yet both classes activate ERK via RAF dimers (often BRAF–CRAF).[4],[7],[8] In lung epithelial and NSCLC models, dabrafenib alone only weakly suppresses ERK and may even induce paradoxical ERK activation in CRAF‑overexpressing or impaired‑kinase contexts, whereas dabrafenib plus trametinib produces more durable ERK inhibition, growth arrest, and apoptosis, providing a rationale to target selected non‑V600 BRAF‑mutant NSCLC with combined RAF/MEK blockade.[4],[7] |
| Wild‑type BRAF in BRAF‑WT NSCLC (off‑target) | In BRAF‑wild‑type lung tumors, BRAF participates in RAS‑dependent RAF dimer signaling; type I RAF inhibitors like dabrafenib can promote RAF dimerization and transactivate CRAF, causing paradoxical ERK hyperactivation.[4],[7],[10],[16] Consistent with this biology, the dabrafenib label and pharmacogenomic summaries state the drug is not indicated for wild‑type BRAF solid tumors because of tumor‑promotion risk, restricting its NSCLC use to confirmed BRAF V600E disease.[1],[3],[8] |
| MEK1/2 (MAP2K1/2; indirect via trametinib combination) | MEK1/2 are the immediate downstream kinases of RAF in the MAPK cascade and are essential effectors of BRAF‑driven NSCLC.[2],[6],[8],[14] In BRAF‑mutant lung cancer models and in clinical BRAF V600E NSCLC, dual targeting of BRAF (dabrafenib) and MEK (trametinib) more completely suppresses ERK signaling, enhances response rates and durability versus dabrafenib monotherapy, and mitigates paradoxical MAPK‑driven toxicities such as cutaneous squamous‑cell carcinoma.[4],[5],[8],[10],[14] |
| ZAK (MAP3K20; off‑target stress‑kinase) | ZAK is a MAP3K upstream of JNK and p38 with reported tumor‑suppressive roles in lung cancer and broad expression in lung and other tissues.[10],[12] BRAF inhibitors, including dabrafenib, bind ZAK as an off‑target; vemurafenib and related analogs potently inhibit ZAK and other JNK‑pathway kinases, suppressing JNK‑dependent apoptosis and contributing to cutaneous squamous‑cell carcinoma, whereas dabrafenib retains substantial ZAK activity at clinically relevant concentrations, which may partly explain its lower cSCC rates while still posing a theoretical risk of modulating stress‑response and apoptotic signaling in lung epithelium.[10],[11],[12] |

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In BRAF V600E–mutated lung adenocarcinoma, particularly when arising as an acquired resistance mechanism to EGFR TKIs such as osimertinib, dabrafenib plus trametinib is positioned as an alternative targeted strategy once EGFR-directed therapy fails.[2] This shift reflects the oncogenic dominance of BRAF V600E in the MAPK pathway, where dual BRAF/MEK blockade can restore disease control in a subset of patients, although prospective NSCLC-specific efficacy datasets remain limited.[2],[5] Brain metastases are common in this population, and the need for CNS-active systemic therapy plus local radiotherapy is frequent.[2],[5]

For BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC with brain metastases, dabrafenib/trametinib is biologically attractive because BRAF/MEK inhibition has demonstrated intracranial activity in BRAF-mutant melanoma brain metastases and in other BRAF V600E–driven CNS tumors, supporting cross-indication CNS penetration and efficacy.[1],[3],[7] However, combined use with cranial radiotherapy (SRS or WBRT) introduces a meaningful risk of radiation necrosis, as BRAFi/MEKi can exacerbate RT-induced vascular injury, blood–brain barrier disruption, and neuroinflammation via MAPK modulation and paradoxical pathway activation in normal endothelial cells.[2],[5],[8] Consensus safety statements from ESMO–ESTRO therefore recommend cautious integration of RT with BRAF/MEK inhibitors, often favoring treatment breaks or RT adaptation in high‑risk scenarios, especially for brain and high-dose skin fields.[8] Overall, dabrafenib-based regimens can be effective across several BRAF V600E–driven lung cancer contexts, but subtype-specific resistance biology and RT-related neurotoxicity necessitate individualized sequencing and close multidisciplinary oversight.[2],[5],[6],[8]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

For metastatic NSCLC with BRAF V600E, dabrafenib is used in combination with trametinib, and regulatory evaluation emphasizes that the overall safety profile is consistent with that seen in other BRAF‑mutant tumors, without new formal contraindications specific to lung cancer.[3] Treatment should only be initiated in patients with confirmed BRAF V600E mutation by a validated assay, and standard exclusion criteria for serious uncontrolled comorbidities and poor performance status apply as in other targeted‑therapy programs.[3,4]

Pyrexia is the defining dabrafenib‑related toxicity and a leading cause of dose interruption or reduction; episodes may be associated with hypotension, dehydration, renal impairment, or systemic inflammatory features.[3] FDA guidance for the Tafinlar + Mekinist combination recommends prompt interruption of dabrafenib (with or without trametinib), antipyretics, evaluation for infection, and stepwise dose reduction or intermittent dosing for recurrent or severe pyrexia.[3] 

Serious non‑cutaneous and cutaneous events are important class toxicities. Dabrafenib monotherapy increases the risk of cutaneous squamous‑cell carcinoma and other hyperproliferative lesions, while addition of trametinib reduces but does not eliminate this risk; regular dermatologic examinations throughout treatment and for several months after discontinuation are advised.[3] Ocular toxicities (retinal pigment epithelial detachment, retinal vein occlusion) and venous or arterial thromboembolic events are primarily linked to trametinib; label‑directed management includes withholding or discontinuing trametinib and careful monitoring if dabrafenib is continued alone.[3]

Cardiomyopathy (decline in left‑ventricular ejection fraction) and interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis have been observed and require baseline and periodic cardiac imaging and clinical vigilance for new or worsening dyspnea or cough.[3,4] In NSCLC trials, decreases in ejection fraction and pulmonary events were among frequent causes of permanent discontinuation.[3] Hemorrhagic events, including serious and fatal bleeds, have been reported; patients should be educated about bleeding symptoms, and grade 3–4 hemorrhage mandates interruption or discontinuation of both agents according to FDA‑aligned dose‑modification algorithms.[3] Routine laboratory monitoring (electrolytes, liver function, complete blood counts) is recommended, as targeted‑therapy reviews in NSCLC highlight the need to detect treatment‑emergent organ toxicities early.[4]



## 8. Key Clinical Trials

The most relevant evidence for dabrafenib (DAB) ± trametinib (TRAM) in BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC comes from a dedicated phase II program and subsequent guideline‑level synthesis.

### Phase II BRF113928 program – dabrafenib ± trametinib in BRAF V600E NSCLC

A multicohort, open‑label phase II trial evaluated dabrafenib alone (cohort A) and in combination with trametinib (cohorts B and C) in metastatic BRAF V600E–mutant NSCLC.[3,6] Dabrafenib monotherapy produced an overall response rate (ORR) of 33%, with disease control in 58% of largely pretreated patients, establishing single‑agent activity but with modest durability.[3] In cohort B (previously treated), dabrafenib 150 mg BID plus trametinib 2 mg QD achieved a median progression‑free survival (PFS) >9 months and ORR >50%, with a manageable but notable toxicity profile (pyrexia, anemia, decreased appetite, confusional state) and no treatment‑related deaths.[3] In cohort C (treatment‑naïve), the same combination yielded a response rate around 64% and PFS in the 10–15‑month range, with similar toxicity, supporting first‑line use in this genotype.[3,6]

Based on these results, the FDA approved dabrafenib plus trametinib for metastatic NSCLC harboring BRAF V600E in 2017, and contemporary reviews now recommend front‑line combination BRAF/MEK inhibition for advanced BRAF V600E NSCLC, citing response rates of ~64% and PFS up to 14.6 months, with overall survival around 24.6 months in first‑line settings.[6,8] These data underpin the positioning of dabrafenib–trametinib as a preferred targeted option over chemo‑immunotherapy in this rare oncogenic subset.[5,6,8]
