## 1. Drug Summary

Osimertinib (Tagrisso, AZD9291) is an oral, third‑generation, irreversible EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) designed to selectively inhibit activating EGFR mutations (exon 19 deletions, L858R) and the resistance‑associated T790M mutation while sparing wild‑type EGFR.[1],[5],[11],[15],[21] Structurally, it contains an acrylamide moiety that forms a covalent bond with cysteine‑797 in the ATP‑binding pocket of mutant EGFR, supported by hydrogen bonding to Met793, leading to sustained blockade of downstream signaling (RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK, PI3K/AKT, JAK/STAT) and apoptosis in EGFR‑addicted NSCLC cells.[1],[5],[13],[21]

Clinically, osimertinib is established as standard first‑line therapy for advanced or metastatic NSCLC harboring common sensitizing EGFR mutations, based on the phase III FLAURA trial, which showed superior progression‑free and overall survival versus first‑generation TKIs and robust central nervous system (CNS) activity.[6],[13],[15] It is also approved as second‑line treatment for T790M‑positive EGFR‑mutant NSCLC after failure of earlier‑generation EGFR TKIs, supported by the AURA program and AURA3, where it improved PFS and CNS outcomes over platinum–pemetrexed chemotherapy.[4],[8],[11],[13],[21] In early‑stage disease, 3 years of adjuvant osimertinib after complete resection of stage IB–IIIA EGFR‑mutant NSCLC (exon 19 deletion or L858R) significantly prolongs disease‑free survival and reduces CNS recurrence in ADAURA, leading to global regulatory approvals in the adjuvant setting.[6],[9],[13],[14],[16],[18]

Despite these advances, virtually all patients eventually develop resistance via heterogeneous EGFR‑dependent mechanisms (e.g., C797S and other tertiary kinase‑domain mutations) and EGFR‑independent alterations such as MET amplification, HER2 activation, oncogenic fusions, downstream MAPK/PI3K pathway reactivation, epithelial–mesenchymal transition, and histologic transformation to small‑cell or squamous carcinoma.[1],[3],[7],[8],[13],[15],[17],[19],[21],[24] Precision oncology for EGFR‑mutant lung cancer therefore centers on molecularly guided use of osimertinib across lines of therapy, systematic tissue and/or liquid re‑biopsy at progression, and rational combination or sequential strategies to overcome or delay resistance.

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Osimertinib is an oral, third‑generation EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor designed to selectively target activating EGFR mutations (primarily exon 19 deletions and L858R) and the gatekeeper resistance mutation T790M in NSCLC.[1][4][6][7] Structurally, it is an aminopyrimidine TKI bearing an acrylamide “warhead” that forms an irreversible covalent bond with cysteine 797 (C797) within the ATP‑binding cleft of the EGFR kinase domain, while sparing wild‑type EGFR.[4][6][7][13] This covalent engagement, together with high affinity for mutant EGFR, underlies its ~200‑fold greater potency against T790M‑positive EGFR versus wild‑type and minimizes off‑target toxicities in skin and gastrointestinal tissues.[4][6][7]

In EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, exon 19 deletions and L858R create ligand‑independent receptor activation and oncogene addiction; acquisition of T790M after first‑/second‑generation TKIs increases ATP affinity and sterically hinders earlier TKIs.[1][4][7] Osimertinib maintains ATP‑competitive binding in the active conformation of mutant EGFR and, via covalent C797 linkage, achieves sustained inhibition of both sensitizing and T790M‑positive receptors, suppressing downstream PI3K–AKT and RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK signaling and inducing tumor regression in L858R/T790M xenografts.[1][4][6][7][13] The drug shows marked selectivity in biochemical and cellular assays, with nanomolar IC₅₀ values against mutant EGFR and far weaker activity against wild‑type, explaining its favorable therapeutic window in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC.[1][4][6]

Osimertinib was also explicitly engineered for central nervous system (CNS) penetration. Compared with gefitinib, erlotinib, and afatinib—which are limited by unfavorable physicochemical properties and efflux via P‑glycoprotein/BCRP at the blood–brain barrier—osimertinib has sufficient permeability to overcome efflux, achieves higher brain and cerebrospinal fluid concentrations in preclinical models, and produces intracranial tumor regression.[5][6] Clinically, this translates into robust CNS objective responses, prolonged CNS progression‑free survival, and reduced incidence of new brain metastases when used as first‑line therapy in EGFR‑mutant advanced NSCLC, including patients with baseline CNS disease and leptomeningeal metastases.[2][5][6]

Covalent C797 binding is mechanistically central but also defines a key on‑target resistance route. The tertiary EGFR C797S mutation substitutes serine for cysteine at the covalent binding site, abrogating irreversible bond formation by osimertinib and other third‑generation covalent TKIs.[3][4][7][8][10][12][13] C797S emerges in ~7% of patients treated with first‑line osimertinib and in 10–26% after second‑line use, often in the context of retained T790M.[3][7][10][12] When C797S and T790M occur in trans, combined first‑ and third‑generation TKIs can, in principle, target each allele; in the more common cis configuration, all currently available EGFR TKIs are ineffective, motivating development of fourth‑generation reversible inhibitors that can inhibit EGFR L858R/T790M/C797S without relying on covalent C797 engagement.[3][8][12][13]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Osimertinib suppresses oncogenic EGFR signaling and its canonical downstream RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK and PI3K/AKT/mTOR cascades in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, but resistance commonly reflects re‑activation of these same pathways via EGFR tertiary mutations (e.g., C797S), EGFR amplification, or downstream RAS/RAF alterations.[1][2][4][5] MET/HGF pathway activation (by MET amplification or HGF overexpression) provides a prototypical bypass route that restores ERBB3/PI3K/AKT and RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK signaling and drives epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT), invasion, and metastasis despite EGFR blockade.[1][3][7]

IGF1R signaling is another key bypass axis: osimertinib‑tolerant, AXL‑low EGFR‑mutant tumors show FOXA1‑driven IGF1R upregulation, maintenance of EGFR–IGF1R–Gab1/IRS1 complexes, and survival signaling; transient IGF1R inhibition plus osimertinib can eradicate such tumors.[10][14][16] At the non‑coding RNA level, circ_PPAPDC1A promotes acquired resistance by sponging miR‑30a‑3p, derepressing IGF1R, and activating the IGF1R/PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway.[10][16] EMT and stem‑like programs, often linked to MET, AXL, and MUC1‑C, further uncouple survival from EGFR, sustaining ERK/AKT activation and drug tolerance.[1][3][6][8]

Additional resistance‑relevant pathways include AXL and FGFR1 (supporting survival of high‑AXL tumors and drug‑tolerant persisters), HER3 (upregulated via IRE1α after osimertinib and capable of re‑engaging ErbB–PI3K/AKT signaling), and stress/immune pathways such as cGAS–STING, which can be co‑opted therapeutically in combination with anti‑HER3 antibodies.[11][13][15]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic EGFR‑mutant lung adenocarcinoma, third‑generation EGFR‑TKIs such as osimertinib are standard first‑line therapy, with FLAURA demonstrating markedly prolonged PFS versus first‑generation TKIs and robust intracranial activity, including in patients with baseline brain metastases.[10] Outcomes remain heterogeneous: co‑mutations (e.g., TP53, RB1) and high metastatic burden, particularly multiple metastatic sites and brain involvement, are associated with inferior PFS and OS despite initial responses, underscoring the need for intensified or combination strategies in high‑risk adenocarcinoma subsets.[3,4,11,14]

In early‑stage resected EGFR‑mutant NSCLC (predominantly adenocarcinoma), ADAURA showed that three years of adjuvant osimertinib after surgery (with or without chemotherapy) produced large DFS gains across stage IB–IIIA, with hazard ratios around 0.23–0.27 and a clinically meaningful reduction in CNS recurrence, leading to regulatory approval and guideline endorsement for stage II–IIIA disease.[2,3,4,6] ctDNA‑based MRD analyses from ADAURA indicate that molecular residual disease is more frequently detectable in higher stages and precedes radiologic relapse, suggesting that integrating MRD with adjuvant osimertinib could further refine risk‑adapted strategies.[7]

For locally advanced, unresectable stage III EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, consolidation osimertinib after chemoradiotherapy is supported by the general paradigm that systemic peri‑radiation therapy improves control of micrometastatic disease, and by extrapolation from its CNS‑protective and survival benefits in metastatic and resected settings, although direct randomized data remain limited.[1,3,5] Across advanced and perioperative contexts, resistance patterns are broadly shared—on‑target EGFR mutations (e.g., C797S), MET/HER2 amplification, activation of downstream PI3K/AKT and MAPK pathways, and lineage plasticity with small‑cell or squamous transformation—emerging more frequently after first‑line osimertinib and necessitating repeat tissue and liquid biopsy to guide subsequent, subtype‑tailored interventions.[8,10–12,14,15]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Osimertinib (Tagrisso) has no absolute contraindications in the EMA assessment report; instead, use is restricted by serious warnings and the need for close monitoring in high‑risk patients.[3] Interstitial lung disease (ILD)/interstitial pneumonia is a key adverse event of special interest, with cases leading to treatment discontinuation and rare fatalities; ILD was the most frequent cause of permanent discontinuation in ADAURA, underscoring the need for prompt evaluation of new or worsening dyspnea, cough, or fever and permanent discontinuation if ILD is confirmed.[2],[3]

Cardiac toxicity centers on QTc prolongation and cardiomyopathy/left ventricular dysfunction. In early‑stage EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, QTc prolongation occurred in ~9% of patients on adjuvant osimertinib versus 2% on placebo, with grade 3 events and treatment discontinuations reported; regular ECG and electrolyte monitoring are advised in patients with baseline risk factors or concomitant QT‑prolonging drugs.[2],[3] Decreases in ejection fraction and broader cardiomyopathy‑type events were observed in ~4–6% of osimertinib‑treated patients in adjuvant and advanced‑disease settings, generally low‑grade but occasionally requiring dose modification or discontinuation.[2],[4]

Meta‑analytic data in advanced NSCLC show that osimertinib overall reduces the incidence of grade ≥3 adverse events versus controls, but increases dermatologic toxicities (rash, paronychia) and is associated with serious but less frequent events including cardiotoxicity, ILD/interstitial pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, and severe liver injury.[4] Common, typically manageable toxicities relevant to NSCLC practice include diarrhea, rash, dry skin, paronychia, stomatitis, and fatigue, which are usually low‑grade and handled with supportive care and dose interruption or reduction according to label guidance.[2]  



## 8. Key Clinical Trials

In metastatic, treatment‑naïve EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, the phase III FLAURA trial (NCT02296125) established first‑line osimertinib as standard of care, demonstrating significantly longer PFS (18.9 vs 10.2 months; HR 0.46) and improved OS (38.6 vs 31.8 months; HR 0.80) versus gefitinib/erlotinib, with lower rates of CNS metastases (6% vs 15%), highlighting its CNS penetration and superiority within EGFR‑selected populations.[2,5]  

In the adjuvant setting for completely resected stage II–IIIA EGFR‑mutant NSCLC, ADAURA (NCT02511106) showed that 3 years of osimertinib significantly improved disease‑free survival, including 24‑month DFS of 89% versus 52% with placebo and reduced local and distant (including CNS) relapse, leading to its adoption as adjuvant standard regardless of PD‑L1 status.[1,6,7]  

For unresectable stage III EGFR‑mutant NSCLC without progression after chemoradiotherapy, the LAURA trial demonstrated that maintenance osimertinib markedly prolonged PFS and produced an 83% reduction in risk of CNS progression or death compared with placebo (CNS PFS HR 0.17; median not reached vs 14.9 months), reinforcing continuous EGFR‑targeted suppression as a precision strategy to delay intracranial failure in this molecularly defined cohort.[1]
