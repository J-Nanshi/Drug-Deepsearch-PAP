## Drug Summary

Ceritinib is a second‑generation, oral, ATP‑competitive ALK tyrosine kinase inhibitor developed to overcome crizotinib resistance in ALK‑rearranged NSCLC.[4,6,13] It is approximately 20‑fold more potent than crizotinib against native ALK in enzymatic assays and also inhibits ROS1 and IGF‑1R/insulin receptors at higher concentrations, providing a broader target spectrum relevant to lung cancer.[4,6,12,13] In the phase I ASCEND‑1 trial, ceritinib at 750 mg once daily achieved an ORR of 58% and median PFS of 7 months in predominantly ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, including patients pretreated with crizotinib, leading to accelerated FDA approval in 2014 for metastatic ALK‑positive NSCLC after crizotinib progression or intolerance.[5,6,8,13]

Subsequent phase II/III ASCEND studies established ceritinib across lines of therapy in ALK‑positive NSCLC. In heavily pretreated, crizotinib‑resistant patients (ASCEND‑2; NCT01685060), ceritinib produced an ORR of 38.6% and median PFS of 5.7 months, with clinically meaningful activity in those with baseline brain metastases.[5,6,8] In ALK‑inhibitor‑naïve but previously chemotherapy‑treated patients (ASCEND‑3; NCT01685138), ORR was ~64% and median PFS 11.1 months.[4–6,8] The pivotal first‑line phase III ASCEND‑4 trial (NCT01828099) demonstrated superior PFS versus platinum–pemetrexed chemotherapy (16.6 vs 8.1 months; ORR 73% vs 27%), including improved systemic and intracranial control, leading to extension of FDA approval to treatment‑naïve metastatic ALK‑positive NSCLC.[1,6,8]

Beyond ALK, ceritinib has clinically relevant activity in ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, with a phase II study in 32 ROS1‑positive patients showing an ORR of 62% and median PFS 9.3 months overall, and 19.3 months in crizotinib‑naïve patients, including intracranial disease control in a majority of those with brain metastases.[9,11,12] Current guidelines and expert reviews position ceritinib as an effective precision oncology agent for ALK‑rearranged NSCLC after crizotinib and as an evidence‑based first‑line option where newer ALK TKIs are unavailable or unsuitable, and as a ROS1‑directed TKI in selected settings.[4,6,8,11,12]

## Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Ceritinib is an ATP-competitive, second‑generation ALK tyrosine kinase inhibitor developed to overcome crizotinib resistance and improve CNS penetration in ALK‑rearranged NSCLC.[2],[9],[13] Structurally derived from TAE684, it has ~20‑fold higher biochemical potency against ALK than crizotinib and greater kinase selectivity, while retaining inhibitory activity against ROS1, IGF‑1R and insulin receptor at higher concentrations.[9],[13] By binding the ALK ATP‑binding pocket, ceritinib blocks autophosphorylation of the ALK kinase domain and suppresses signaling from constitutively active ALK fusion proteins such as EML4‑ALK, which drive 3–7% of NSCLC, predominantly adenocarcinoma in younger, never‑ or light‑smokers.[2],[4],[7],[9]

EML4‑ALK and other ALK fusions constitutively activate canonical oncogenic cascades including PI3K–AKT/mTOR, RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) and JAK–STAT, as well as a broader bypass network (EGFR, KIT, MET, IGF‑1R).[1],[2],[5],[7],[9],[13] In ALK‑rearranged lung models, ALK inhibition leads to coordinated dephosphorylation of AKT, ERK and STAT3, cell‑cycle arrest and apoptosis, consistent with “oncogene addiction” to ALK signaling.[1],[5],[8],[9],[15] Ceritinib retains activity against several crizotinib‑resistant ALK mutations (L1196M, G1269A, S1206Y, I1171T) by accommodating structural changes within the ATP pocket, but has limited potency against solvent‑front mutations such as G1202R.[9],[10],[13] Clinically, this enhanced target coverage translates into meaningful progression‑free survival in both crizotinib‑pretreated and TKI‑naïve ALK‑positive advanced NSCLC.[2],[7],[9]

Although ROS1 is a distinct insulin‑receptor family RTK, its kinase domain shares high homology with ALK, and several ALK inhibitors—including ceritinib—also inhibit ROS1.[6],[10],[12],[14] ROS1 fusions (e.g., CD74‑ROS1, SLC34A2‑ROS1) constitutively activate PI3K–AKT/mTOR, MAPK and JAK–STAT pathways, driving an aggressive but highly TKI‑sensitive NSCLC subset (~1–2%).[6],[12] Ceritinib demonstrates clinical activity in ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, particularly in crizotinib‑naïve disease, but its efficacy is reduced against common crizotinib‑resistant ROS1 mutations such as G2032R and D2033N, which are solvent‑front analogues of ALK G1202R/D1203N and sterically hinder inhibitor binding.[6],[10],[12],[15] Overall, ceritinib’s therapeutic effect in lung cancer derives from potent blockade of ALK‑ or ROS1‑driven signaling nodes (PI3K–AKT, MAPK, JAK–STAT), leading to suppression of proliferation and survival in ALK‑ and a subset of ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC.

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

Ceritinib directly inhibits ALK, leading to rapid downregulation of ALK phosphotyrosine sites and decreased phosphorylation in canonical downstream PI3K–AKT and ERK/MAPK signaling components in ALK‑fusion lung adenocarcinoma cells.[4] In the H3122 EML4‑ALK–driven NSCLC line, targeted phosphoproteomics showed that 93% of significantly regulated phosphosites were downregulated after 24 h of ceritinib, including sites in PI3K/AKT and ERK/MAPK pathways, consistent with suppression of proliferative and survival signaling.[4]

ALK inhibition with ceritinib also reduces activating phosphosites on PTPN11 (SHP2) and on Gab1, dampening PTPN11‑dependent propagation of ERK/MAPK signaling.[4] However, bypass activation of ERBB family receptors (EGFR, ERBB2, ERBB3) can restore PI3K/AKT and RAS/MAPK activity and drive resistance to ALK TKIs, including ceritinib, in EML4‑ALK‑positive NSCLC.[1],[4] ERBB3‑high states, often HRG‑driven, are linked to persistent AKT and ERK phosphorylation and survival of ALK‑inhibited cells, positioning the ERBB–PI3K–AKT and ERBB–RAS–MAPK axes as key adaptive pathways associated with ceritinib resistance.[1]

Ceritinib‑induced pathway modulation also extends to autophagy: MS‑based proteomic fingerprinting in lung cancer models identified autophagy pathway changes as part of ceritinib’s broader biochemical impact, suggesting that altered autophagy may contribute to cellular adaptation or stress responses under ALK blockade.[3] Collectively, these data place ALK signaling (Reactome R-HSA-2672351), PI3K–AKT (e.g., KEGG hsa04151), ERK/MAPK (e.g., KEGG hsa04010), ERBB signaling (e.g., KEGG hsa04012), PTPN11/Gab1‑mediated RAS–MAPK, and autophagy (e.g., KEGG hsa04140) as the principal pathways functionally modulated by ceritinib in ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, with downregulation generally associated with drug sensitivity and ERBB‑driven reactivation associated with resistance.[1],[3],[4]  



## Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, ceritinib is a second‑generation ALK TKI that is ~20‑fold more potent than crizotinib and active against common secondary ALK resistance mutations, supporting its role after crizotinib failure and in ALK‑inhibitor–naïve disease.[2,16,19] Across early ASCEND studies, ALK‑positive patients (predominantly adenocarcinoma, never/light‑smokers) achieved response rates around 55–70% with median PFS up to ~18 months in ALK‑inhibitor–naïve cohorts, but gastrointestinal toxicity frequently required dose reductions.[2,16,19] Ceritinib also shows intracranial activity, an important consideration given the high CNS burden in ALK‑positive NSCLC, though newer agents such as alectinib and lorlatinib now provide longer PFS and superior CNS control, so ceritinib is generally positioned as an alternative rather than preferred first‑line or sequencing option.[2,9,16]

In ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, ceritinib directly targets ROS1 and is recognized as a potent ROS1 inhibitor. A dedicated phase II trial in 32 ROS1‑positive patients reported an ORR of 62%, disease control rate 81%, median PFS 9.3 months overall and 19.3 months in crizotinib‑naïve patients, with 63% intracranial disease control among those with brain metastases.[4,6] A larger review summarizing the same study describes ORR 67%, median PFS 19.3 months and median OS 24 months, with diarrhea, nausea and anorexia as the most common adverse events.[5,6] These data support ceritinib as an effective option for ROS1‑rearranged adenocarcinoma—typically in younger never‑smokers with high CNS risk—particularly where crizotinib or newer CNS‑optimized ROS1 TKIs (e.g., entrectinib, repotrectinib, taletrectinib) are unavailable or after their use.[5–8,10,11]

## Contraindications and Safety

Ceritinib has no absolute contraindications in current regulatory documents, but its use is constrained by dose‑limiting toxicities that drive intensive monitoring and dose modification, including in pediatric ALK‑positive malignancies where similar safety rules are applied.[1] The pediatric phase I protocol for LDK378 (ceritinib) defines dose‑limiting toxicities (DLTs) to guide maximum tolerated dose/recommended dose and mandates close surveillance for severe gastrointestinal (GI) events, hepatotoxicity, and other serious toxicities, with structured rules for interruption, reduction, and discontinuation.[1]

GI toxicity (notably diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain) is an early and frequent DLT; persistent grade ≥2 or any grade ≥3–4 GI events despite optimal supportive care trigger dose interruption and stepwise dose reduction on recovery.[1] Hepatic laboratory abnormalities are treated as adverse events of special interest, with predefined thresholds for ALT/AST and bilirubin elevations requiring interruption, more frequent testing, and permanent discontinuation for severe or recurrent injury.[1]

The protocol also highlights cardiopulmonary and metabolic risks: ECG and laboratory monitoring are required, and serious events such as clinically significant arrhythmias, suspected drug‑related interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis, or severe metabolic derangements (e.g., marked hyperglycemia or pancreatitis‑like enzyme elevations) are managed as DLTs with mandatory discontinuation.[1] Overall, the ceritinib development program embeds intensive safety monitoring, rapid management of hepatotoxicity, GI toxicity, cardiopulmonary events, and metabolic abnormalities, which are directly applicable to adult metastatic ALK‑positive NSCLC practice.[1]

## Key Clinical Trials

Ceritinib development in lung cancer spans phase I–III ASCEND studies in ALK‑rearranged NSCLC and a dedicated phase II trial in ROS1‑rearranged disease.

The first‑in‑human phase I ASCEND‑1 trial (NCT01283516) established 750 mg once daily as the recommended dose and demonstrated substantial activity in advanced ALK‑rearranged NSCLC irrespective of prior ALK TKI exposure. In the updated analysis of 246 patients treated at 750 mg, investigator‑assessed ORR was 72.3% in ALK inhibitor‑naïve (n=83) and 56.4% in ALK‑pretreated patients (n=163), with median PFS 18.4 and 6.9 months, respectively.[3,5,12] In patients with baseline brain metastases (n=124), intracranial disease control was 78.9% in ALK‑naïve and 65.3% in ALK‑pretreated cohorts, supporting meaningful CNS activity.[5,12]

ASCEND‑2 (phase II) enrolled 140 heavily pretreated ALK‑positive NSCLC patients who had received ≥2 prior lines including platinum chemotherapy and crizotinib. Ceritinib achieved an investigator‑assessed ORR of 38.6%, disease control rate 77.1%, median duration of response 9.7 months, and median PFS 5.7 months. Among 100 patients with baseline brain metastases, ORR was 33% and median PFS 5.4 months, with intracranial ORR 45% and intracranial disease control 80% in those with active brain lesions.[3]

ASCEND‑3 (NCT01685138) evaluated ceritinib 750 mg fasted in 124 ALK‑inhibitor‑naïve, chemotherapy‑pretreated patients (39.5% with brain metastases). Final results showed investigator‑assessed ORR 67.7%, median PFS 16.6 months, and median OS 51.3 months, confirming durable benefit in this setting.[8] The randomized phase III ASCEND‑4 compared first‑line ceritinib with platinum–pemetrexed in treatment‑naïve metastatic ALK‑positive NSCLC. Ceritinib significantly improved PFS (16.6 vs 8.1 months; HR 0.55) and ORR (73% vs 27%); a similar PFS advantage was seen in patients with baseline CNS metastases, though not statistically significant.[4,6,11,14]

ASCEND‑5, a phase III trial in patients previously treated with platinum chemotherapy and crizotinib, randomized 231 ALK‑positive NSCLC patients to ceritinib versus single‑agent chemotherapy. Median PFS by blinded independent review was 5.4 months with ceritinib versus 1.6 months with chemotherapy (HR 0.49), and ORR 39.1% versus 6.9%, establishing ceritinib superiority in the post‑crizotinib setting.[3,18] Beyond ALK, a phase II study in ROS1‑rearranged advanced NSCLC reported ORR 62% and median PFS 9.3 months overall, with PFS 19.3 months in crizotinib‑naïve patients and intracranial ORR 63% in those with brain metastases, indicating robust systemic and CNS efficacy in ROS1‑positive disease.[11,17] Collectively, these trials underpin ceritinib’s role across treatment‑naïve, chemotherapy‑pretreated, and crizotinib‑resistant ALK‑positive NSCLC, including patients with CNS involvement, and support its activity in ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC.
