## 1. Drug Summary

Dacomitinib (PF‑00299804, Vizimpro) is an oral, second‑generation, irreversible EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor that covalently targets multiple ErbB/HER family members (EGFR/HER1, HER2, HER4).[11,14,15] By forming an irreversible bond in the ATP‑binding cleft, it provides sustained pan‑HER blockade, suppressing downstream proliferative and survival signaling in EGFR‑addicted non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).[11,12,14,15] This broader, more durable inhibition underpins its development as a precision therapy for tumors driven by activating EGFR mutations.

Regulatory approvals were based on the phase III ARCHER 1050 trial (NCT01774721), which randomized 452 patients with unresectable/metastatic NSCLC harboring common sensitizing EGFR mutations (exon 19 deletion or exon 21 L858R) to first‑line dacomitinib 45 mg once daily versus gefitinib 250 mg once daily.[10,12,14,16] Dacomitinib significantly improved progression‑free survival (median 14.7 vs 9.2 months; HR ~0.59) and overall survival (34.1 vs 26.8 months) compared with gefitinib, with objective response rates around 75% and 72%, respectively.[2,10,12,16] On this basis, the FDA, EMA and other agencies approved dacomitinib for first‑line treatment of metastatic or locally advanced NSCLC with activating EGFR mutations, specifically exon 19 deletions and exon 21 L858R substitutions, detected by validated assays.[9,10,12,14]

Clinically, dacomitinib has not shown benefit in molecularly unselected NSCLC and its use is recommended only in patients with known EGFR‑sensitizing mutations.[3,5,13] Compared with first‑generation EGFR TKIs, it is associated with higher rates of class‑typical toxicities (diarrhea, rash, stomatitis, paronychia), often necessitating dose reductions; however, dose modification does not appear to compromise efficacy.[12,13,15,16] Within the EGFR‑mutant NSCLC landscape, dacomitinib is now considered an important first‑line option for fit patients, alongside other EGFR TKIs, as part of biomarker‑driven precision oncology.[6,12–14]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Dacomitinib is a second‑generation, irreversible EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor that also targets other ERBB family members (pan‑HER/ErbB inhibitor).[6][8] Like afatinib, it covalently binds the ATP‑binding site of EGFR, leading to sustained inhibition of receptor autophosphorylation and downstream signaling through RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK and PI3K–AKT pathways that are critical for NSCLC cell proliferation and survival.[4][6][8] EGFR is frequently activated in NSCLC by exon 19 deletions or exon 21 L858R mutations, which render the kinase constitutively active and highly oncogenic; these “sensitizing” mutations are the primary molecular context in which dacomitinib exerts its clinical benefit.[3][6][8]

In the phase III ARCHER 1050 trial, dacomitinib, as a first‑line EGFR‑TKI, significantly prolonged progression‑free survival versus gefitinib in advanced NSCLC with EGFR exon 19 deletion or L858R, leading to its FDA approval in this setting.[3][6] Pan‑HER blockade may help suppress compensatory signaling via HER2 and HER3, which are part of the same ERBB family and can heterodimerize with EGFR to activate PI3K/AKT and MAPK cascades in lung cancer.[2][4][8] However, broader ERBB inhibition also contributes to on‑target toxicities such as dermatologic and gastrointestinal adverse events, necessitating dose adjustments in clinical practice.[6][8]

As with other first‑ and second‑generation EGFR‑TKIs, acquired resistance to dacomitinib is expected to arise through on‑target EGFR alterations (particularly T790M), amplification of bypass receptors (e.g., MET, HER2), activation of alternative RTKs such as AXL and IGF1R, downstream pathway activation (PI3K/AKT, RAS/MAPK, STAT3), epithelial–mesenchymal transition, and histologic transformation to small‑cell lung cancer.[2][3][4] EGFR T790M is recognized as the most common mechanism of resistance to earlier‑generation EGFR‑TKIs in NSCLC, and second‑generation pan‑HER inhibitors, including dacomitinib, do not reliably overcome this mutation, so T790M‑positive progression is typically managed with third‑generation EGFR‑TKIs.[2][3][4][6]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic NSCLC with common *EGFR* exon 19 deletions or exon 21 L858R, dacomitinib as first-line therapy achieves high objective response rates (~75%) and significantly prolongs PFS (~14.7 vs. 9.2 months) and OS (~34.1 vs. 27 months) compared with gefitinib in ARCHER 1050, leading to regulatory approval for these mutations.[1],[9],[12] Subgroup analyses indicate particularly pronounced OS benefit in L858R, whereas multiple datasets suggest exon 19 deletion generally confers longer survival across EGFR‑TKIs, consistent with biological differences and a higher propensity of L858R tumors to acquire T790M and other concomitant alterations.[4],[9]

For uncommon *EGFR* mutations (e.g., G719X, L861Q, S768I), most mutation‑specific data derive from afatinib, which shows substantial activity; dacomitinib is approved broadly for “EGFR‑mutant NSCLC” but its efficacy by individual rare variant is less well characterized, and current guidelines still favor agents with stronger evidence in these subgroups.[4],[9],[13] In *EGFR* exon 20 insertions, clinical and preclinical data show de novo resistance to most approved EGFR‑TKIs; only rare insertions introducing glycine at position 770 (e.g., D770delinsGY) appear sensitive to dacomitinib, and these variants are uncommon, so exon 20 insertion NSCLC is generally managed with newer exon 20–selective agents rather than dacomitinib.[2]

Across histologic subtypes, *EGFR* mutations are enriched in lung adenocarcinoma and much less frequent in lung squamous cell carcinoma (LUSC); EGFR‑mutant LUSC represents a small minority and is not the main evidence base for dacomitinib.[4],[11],[16] Consequently, real‑world and trial use of dacomitinib is effectively confined to non‑squamous (predominantly adenocarcinoma) *EGFR*‑mutant NSCLC without baseline brain metastases, where it provides durable disease control but resistance—dominated by on‑target T790M and off‑target bypass activation—typically emerges within about a year, at which point transition to third‑generation EGFR‑TKIs or combination strategies is required.[9],[10],[17],[19]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Regulatory labels list no absolute contraindications other than hypersensitivity to dacomitinib or excipients.[1][2] In the US label, “Contraindications: None” is specified, but serious warnings and precautions function as de facto contraindications in high‑risk patients.[5][8]

Interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis is a key boxed‑type warning: severe and fatal ILD occurred in ~0.5% of patients; treatment must be withheld for new/worsening dyspnea, cough, or fever and permanently discontinued if ILD is confirmed.[2][5][8] Patients with prior ILD were not studied and should be approached with extreme caution.[1][2]

Diarrhea is very common (any grade ~86%; grade 3–4 ~11%; rare fatal cases) and may cause dehydration and renal impairment; labels mandate early, aggressive antidiarrheal therapy, hydration, and dose interruption/reduction for ≥grade 2 events.[2][5][6][8] Dermatologic toxicities (rash, erythematous and exfoliative skin conditions) are also frequent (rash ~78%; grade 3–4 ~21%) and may require topical/oral antibiotics, steroids, sun protection, and dose modifications.[2][5][8]

Transaminase elevations and rare hepatotoxicity, including a fatal hepatic failure case, have been reported; periodic liver‑function monitoring and dose interruption/reduction for severe laboratory abnormalities are recommended.[2][4] Embryo‑fetal toxicity is highlighted; effective contraception and avoidance in pregnancy and breastfeeding are advised.[5][8]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Dacomitinib’s development in NSCLC has followed the ARCHER program, with multiple phase II–III studies in both unselected and EGFR‑mutant populations.[3,5,6,8] Early randomized phase III trials in molecularly unselected, previously treated NSCLC—ARCHER 1009 (dacomitinib vs erlotinib) and NCIC BR‑26 (dacomitinib vs placebo after chemotherapy and first‑generation EGFR TKI)—showed no progression‑free survival (PFS) or overall survival (OS) advantage for dacomitinib, but higher toxicity, effectively excluding a role in EGFR‑unselected second‑line settings.[3,5,6,8]

The pivotal ARCHER 1050 trial (NCT01774721) is a multinational, open‑label, randomized phase III study comparing first‑line dacomitinib with gefitinib in advanced EGFR‑mutant (exon 19 deletion or L858R) NSCLC; patients with brain metastases were excluded.[3,6–8] ARCHER 1050 demonstrated significantly longer median PFS with dacomitinib (14.7 vs 9.2 months) and longer duration of response (14.8 vs 8.3 months) compared with gefitinib, at the cost of higher rates of grade ≥3 adverse events (63% vs 41%), particularly diarrhea, rash, and paronychia.[6,8] Updated analyses showed an OS benefit for dacomitinib (median 34.1 vs 26.8 months; HR 0.760), representing the first demonstration of a first‑/second‑generation EGFR TKI improving OS over another EGFR TKI in the frontline EGFR‑mutant setting and leading to regulatory approval as a first‑line option.[6,8]

| EMT (epithelial–mesenchymal transition) program | Up | Resistant | EMT, driven by factors such as TGF‑β and hypoxia‑induced HIF‑1α, activates transcription factors (Slug, TWIST, Snail) that repress E‑cadherin and promote mesenchymal phenotypes in lung cancer.[1],[7] EMT is closely linked to EGFR‑TKI resistance in EGFR‑mutant NSCLC cell lines and patient tumors, reducing dependence on EGFR/ERBB signaling so that even irreversible pan‑HER inhibition by dacomitinib becomes less effective.[1],[7] | [1],[7] |
| Hypoxia/HIF‑1α/HIF‑2α axis | Up | Resistant | Hypoxia induces HIF‑1α/HIF‑2α, which upregulate genes promoting invasion, angiogenesis, and EMT in lung cancer; EGFR signaling can also drive HIF expression independently of hypoxia.[1] Under hypoxic and HIF‑activated conditions, NSCLC cells may maintain survival and invasive capacity despite EGFR blockade with dacomitinib, contributing to incomplete responses or resistance. | [1] |
| TGF‑β–Smad/PI3K/MEK EMT‑signaling | Up | Resistant | TGF‑β signaling induces EMT via Smad3, PI3K/AKT/mTOR, and MEK/ERK cascades in NSCLC, enhancing migration and invasion.[7] Because dacomitinib acts upstream at ERBB receptors, TGF‑β–driven EMT and its downstream pathways can sustain malignant behavior and are implicated in resistance to EGFR‑TKIs, including second‑generation agents. | [7] |
| KRAS‑driven MAPK/PI3K signaling | Up | Resistant | KRAS mutations (especially G12C, G12V, G12D) are frequent in NSCLC and constitutively activate MAPK and PI3K pathways independent of upstream EGFR activity.[7] In tumors harboring KRAS or other strong downstream drivers, dacomitinib’s EGFR/ERBB inhibition is less impactful, leading to primary resistance despite adequate target engagement. | [7] |
| Immune checkpoint (PD‑1/PD‑L1)–modulated microenvironment | Up | Resistant (to immunotherapy; contextual for EGFR‑TKIs) | EGFR‑mutant NSCLC shows relatively low PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitor responsiveness and distinct PD‑L1 patterns, and retrospective analyses indicate lower response rates to anti‑PD‑1/PD‑L1 antibodies in this population.[7] While not directly targeted by dacomitinib, this immune‑evasive microenvironment interacts with EGFR‑driven biology and may limit the benefit of combining EGFR‑TKIs with immunotherapy in EGFR‑mutant lung cancer. | [7] |
