## Drug Summary

Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd, DS‑8201, Enhertu) is a HER2‑directed antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) composed of trastuzumab linked via a cleavable tetrapeptide to a highly potent topoisomerase I inhibitor (deruxtecan/DXd), with a high drug‑to‑antibody ratio of ~8 that enables delivery of multiple cytotoxic molecules per antibody.[1],[7] The payload stabilizes topoisomerase I–DNA complexes, inducing DNA double‑strand breaks and apoptosis.[7] T‑DXd exhibits membrane permeability and a pronounced bystander effect, allowing killing of neighboring tumor cells with low or heterogeneous HER2 expression—an important feature in lung tumors where HER2 expression and mutation status can be spatially heterogeneous.[4],[7]

HER2 mutations, particularly exon 20 insertions, define a rare (≈2–4%) but aggressive subset of non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) associated with poor prognosis and limited benefit from conventional chemotherapy and immune checkpoint blockade, creating a strong rationale for HER2‑targeted ADCs.[4],[7] In HER2‑mutant advanced NSCLC, the phase II DESTINY‑Lung01 trial reported an objective response rate (ORR) of 55%, disease control rate of 92%, median progression‑free survival (PFS) of 8.2 months, and median overall survival of 17.8 months in previously treated patients, establishing clinically meaningful activity.[7] These data, together with subsequent studies, supported regulatory approvals for HER2‑mutant unresectable or metastatic NSCLC and incorporation of T‑DXd into guidelines as a later‑line targeted option.[4],[7]

Beyond metastatic disease, a recent case report described perioperative use of neoadjuvant and adjuvant T‑DXd in a patient with *ERBB2* exon 20‑mutant lung adenocarcinoma, achieving tumor downstaging, high stromal replacement (70%) at resection, and prolonged remission, highlighting the potential of T‑DXd in earlier‑stage HER2‑mutant NSCLC, although this remains investigational.[4] Overall, T‑DXd exemplifies next‑generation ADC design—humanized anti‑HER2 antibody, optimized cleavable linker, and a potent topoisomerase I inhibitor payload—delivering selective cytotoxicity to HER2‑altered lung cancers with a clinically validated benefit–risk profile in the advanced setting.[1],[4],[7]

## Identifiers & Synonyms

Trastuzumab deruxtecan is a HER2‑directed antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) composed of a trastuzumab‑based monoclonal antibody linked via a cleavable peptide linker to a topoisomerase I inhibitor payload (DX‑8951f/deruxtecan).[4,9] It is also referred to as trastuzumab‑deruxtecan in chemical/structural databases such as PubChem, which list it as an ADC targeting human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2).[3] In regulatory and clinical oncology contexts, the full US generic name is fam‑trastuzumab deruxtecan‑nxki, and the FDA approval summary explicitly notes its development code DS‑8201a and the commonly used abbreviation T‑DXd.[4,6] The proprietary (brand) name used in clinical practice and trials is Enhertu (ENHERTU), marketed by Daiichi Sankyo and partners.[1,4,6]  

In precision oncology for NSCLC, trastuzumab deruxtecan is consistently described as a HER2‑targeted ADC for HER2‑mutated or HER2‑overexpressed tumors, including advanced non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).[2,4,6,7,9] Key lung‑cancer–focused reviews and regulatory documents use the following principal synonyms interchangeably: “trastuzumab deruxtecan,” “trastuzumab‑deruxtecan,” “DS‑8201” or “DS‑8201a,” “T‑DXd,” “fam‑trastuzumab deruxtecan‑nxki,” and “Enhertu.”[2,4,6,7,9] Specific ChEMBL or DrugBank identifiers are not provided in the available sources and therefore cannot be reliably reported.

## Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd, DS‑8201) is a HER2‑targeted antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) composed of a trastuzumab backbone linked via a cleavable tetrapeptide linker to a highly potent, membrane‑permeable topoisomerase I inhibitor payload (deruxtecan, DXd), with a high drug‑to‑antibody ratio of ~8.[8] The conjugate retains trastuzumab’s ability to recognize the extracellular domain of HER2 (ERBB2), which is altered in ~2–4% of NSCLC, most commonly by exon 20 insertions and other activating mutations enriched in female, never‑smoker adenocarcinoma.[5] In NSCLC, clinical and translational data indicate that HER2 mutations, rather than HER2 overexpression alone, best predict sensitivity to anti‑HER2 ADCs, making HER2‑mutant tumors the principal target population.[8]

After intravenous administration, T‑DXd binds HER2 on the surface of HER2‑altered lung cancer cells and is internalized by receptor‑mediated endocytosis.[8] HER2‑mutant NSCLC often exhibits constitutive HER2 signaling and efficient receptor trafficking, which favor ADC uptake even when immunohistochemical HER2 levels are modest.[5,8] Within lysosomes, tumor‑associated proteases cleave the tetrapeptide linker, releasing free DXd into the cytosol.[8] This linker is designed to be relatively stable in plasma but efficiently cleaved in the lysosomal compartment, thereby enriching payload release in HER2‑altered cells and minimizing systemic exposure.

DXd binds and stabilizes topoisomerase I–DNA cleavage complexes, leading to accumulation of DNA single‑ and double‑strand breaks, replication fork collapse, and apoptosis.[8] In HER2‑mutant NSCLC, this targeted delivery of a topoisomerase I inhibitor translates into high objective response rates and durable disease control in heavily pretreated patients: in DESTINY‑Lung01, 91 patients with HER2‑mutant NSCLC achieved an ORR of 55%, disease control rate of 92%, median PFS 8.2 months, and median OS 17.8 months; similar activity was confirmed in DESTINY‑Lung02.[8] Importantly, DXd is membrane‑permeable, enabling diffusion from HER2‑positive cells into neighboring tumor cells with lower or heterogeneous HER2 expression, producing a “bystander effect” that broadens cytotoxic coverage in spatially heterogeneous HER2‑mutant lung tumors.[8] Early phase studies in HER2‑mutant NSCLC and broader HER2‑altered solid tumors suggest that this bystander killing contributes to the high response rates (overall ORR 72.7% and median PFS 11.3 months in a small HER2‑mutant NSCLC series) and supports activity even when HER2 expression is variable within and between lesions.[8]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

Key signaling and DNA damage–related pathways modulated by trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd) in HER2‑mutant NSCLC, with qualitative effects on drug sensitivity or resistance:

- **ERBB2/HER2 signaling (KEGG: hsa04012)** – T‑DXd is an anti‑HER2 antibody–drug conjugate; NSCLC indication requires an activating HER2 (ERBB2) mutation, reflecting dependence on HER2 signaling for tumor growth.[1] Preservation of HER2 expression/mutation and ongoing pathway addiction are therefore associated with **sensitivity**, whereas loss of HER2‑driven signaling would be expected to confer **resistance**.

- **PI3K–AKT pathway (KEGG: hsa04151)** – As a canonical HER2‑downstream survival pathway, PI3K–AKT transduces anti‑apoptotic signals in ERBB2‑altered tumors. By targeting HER2 and delivering a cytotoxic payload, T‑DXd functionally disrupts upstream activation, so tumors that remain PI3K–AKT–dependent are more likely to be **sensitive**, while compensatory activation via alternative receptors may promote **resistance**.

- **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway (KEGG: hsa04010)** – HER2 activates MAPK signaling to drive proliferation in NSCLC.[1] T‑DXd does not directly inhibit MAPK kinases, but HER2 blockade plus DNA‑damaging payload–induced cell death diminishes MAPK‑driven growth; persistent MAPK activation via bypass mutations could underlie **resistance**.

- **Cell‑cycle regulation (KEGG: hsa04110)** – The deruxtecan payload is a topoisomerase I inhibitor that induces replication‑associated DNA damage, particularly in rapidly cycling cells.[1] HER2‑driven NSCLC with high proliferative indices is thus more **sensitive** to T‑DXd‑mediated cell‑cycle arrest and death.

- **Apoptosis pathways (KEGG: hsa04210)** – Payload‑induced DNA damage initiates apoptotic cascades in tumor cells.[1] Efficient HER2‑mediated internalization and adequate biomarker‑defined target engagement favor robust apoptosis and **sensitivity**, whereas defects in apoptotic machinery may contribute to **resistance**.

- **DNA damage response (DDR) / replication stress (e.g., ATM/ATR signaling; KEGG: hsa04115)** – Topoisomerase I inhibition generates DNA strand breaks and replication stress; tumors with limited DDR capacity are more **sensitive** to T‑DXd, while those with efficient repair pathways can better tolerate damage and show **resistance**.[1]

- **Pharmacogenomic biomarker–defined HER2 dependency** – Regulatory agencies require demonstration of an activating HER2 mutation for NSCLC treatment with T‑DXd, underscoring the role of ERBB2 as a predictive biomarker.[1,2] Strong alignment between drug labeling and pharmacogenomic testing supports the concept that intact HER2‑driven signaling and accurate genomic selection are key determinants of **sensitivity** in HER2‑mutant NSCLC.[1,2]

## Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HER2‑mutant (ERBB2‑mutant) non‑squamous NSCLC, trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd) shows the highest activity among HER2‑directed strategies. In DESTINY‑Lung01, previously treated HER2‑mutant patients achieved an objective response rate (ORR) of 55%, median progression‑free survival (PFS) 8.2 months, and overall survival (OS) 17.8 months, leading to FDA accelerated approval; DESTINY‑Lung02 confirmed robust efficacy with ORR ~49–56% and favored the lower 5.4 mg/kg dose due to reduced grade ≥3 toxicity.[2][6–8] These results contrast sharply with modest benefit from HER2 TKIs and trastuzumab‑ or T‑DM1‑based regimens (ORR generally ≤30–50% with PFS ~5 months), underscoring a strong oncogene‑addicted biology where HER2 mutation, rather than amplification, is the dominant predictive biomarker in NSCLC.[8][12]

In HER2‑overexpressing/amplified NSCLC without canonical HER2 mutations, T‑DXd demonstrates intermediate activity. In DESTINY‑Lung01, HER2‑overexpressing (IHC 2+/3+) NSCLC treated with T‑DXd had ORR 24.5–34.1% and median PFS 5.4–6.7 months, clearly lower than in HER2‑mutant disease but superior to historical trastuzumab or T‑DM1 outcomes.[2][6][7][12] Systematic comparison of ADC trials indicates that HER2‑mutant patients derive deeper and more durable responses (median duration of response 16.8 months vs. 6.2 months in overexpressing tumors), supporting mutation‑focused testing and the view that HER2 protein overexpression alone is a weaker predictor of T‑DXd benefit in lung cancer.[7][12]

Across NSCLC subtypes, HER2‑altered tumors cluster within the oncogene‑addicted, predominantly adenocarcinoma population, often in never‑ or light‑smokers, where targeted agents outperform immunotherapy or chemotherapy alone.[3][8–9][15] Emerging data suggest T‑DXd retains activity in patients with stable brain metastases, with systemic ORR in HER2‑mutant cohorts (~54–60%) comparable in those with and without CNS involvement, raising the possibility of a central nervous system niche, particularly for HER2‑mutant or HER2‑3+ brain‑metastatic disease.[6][9][15]

## Contraindications and Safety

Formal absolute contraindications for trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd) are limited, but regulatory and safety documents emphasize that it should not be used in patients with a known serious hypersensitivity to trastuzumab deruxtecan or its excipients, and that patients with significant baseline pulmonary compromise or a history of clinically significant interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis require extreme caution and often exclusion from trials.[2],[3],[5] The EMA risk‑management documentation lists ILD/pneumonitis and neutropenia as important identified risks and highlights older age and renal impairment as factors warranting particular vigilance.[5],[7]

ILD/pneumonitis is the major life‑threatening toxicity in lung cancer patients: pooled DESTINY data show adjudicated drug‑related ILD in ~11–12% of patients overall, with symptomatic (grade ≥2) events mandating permanent discontinuation.[7] In DESTINY‑Lung01, all‑grade pneumonitis rates reached 20–26% at 6.4 mg/kg in HER2‑overexpressing or HER2‑mutant NSCLC, with grade ≥3 events in up to 6.6% and multiple fatal cases, whereas 5.4 mg/kg was associated with lower incidence (7.3–12.9%), supporting use of the lower dose in NSCLC.[2],[6],[8] Guidance from pooled DESTINY protocols and pneumonitis reviews recommends baseline assessment, close symptom surveillance, prompt CT imaging, early corticosteroids, and permanent discontinuation for grade ≥2 ILD/pneumonitis.[2],[7],[8]

Cardiac dysfunction is an important but less frequent risk, attributed to the trastuzumab backbone. Breast‑cancer–focused safety reviews and pooled DESTINY analyses report decreases in LVEF and rare heart failure, supporting baseline and periodic LVEF monitoring and avoidance in patients with uncontrolled cardiac disease, with interruption or discontinuation in the event of clinically meaningful LVEF decline.[3],[4],[7] Myelosuppression—particularly neutropenia, anemia, and thrombocytopenia—is common; grade ≥3 neutrophil count decreases up to ~50% were seen at 6.4 mg/kg in gastric cancer studies, and pooled DESTINY data show frequent neutropenia and anemia with dose holds, reductions, and use of G‑CSF according to protocol algorithms.[1],[3],[7]

Prior therapies and lung‑cancer–specific context modify risk. The DESTINY‑Lung protocols excluded patients with uncontrolled ILD and incorporated detailed dose‑modification and ILD adjudication procedures, reflecting the high baseline pneumonitis risk in NSCLC and potential additive toxicity after prior chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and thoracic radiotherapy.[2],[8] Contemporary lung‑cancer pneumonitis reviews emphasize that ADC‑related pneumonitis, including with T‑DXd, may be dose‑dependent and particularly hazardous in patients with pre‑existing lung disease or prior immune checkpoint inhibitors, underscoring the need for stringent baseline screening, multidisciplinary ILD management, and a low threshold to stop therapy at early signs of pulmonary toxicity.[8]

## Key Clinical Trials

Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd, fam‑trastuzumab deruxtecan‑nxki) received accelerated FDA approval for unresectable or metastatic HER2‑mutant NSCLC based primarily on the phase II DESTINY‑Lung01 trial (NCT03505710).[2,6,8] In DESTINY‑Lung01, among 91 previously treated patients with HER2‑mutated NSCLC, T‑DXd produced an objective response rate (ORR) of 55%, disease control rate (DCR) 92%, median progression‑free survival (PFS) 8.2 months, and median overall survival (OS) 17.8 months, establishing HER2 mutation as a strong predictive biomarker.[8] A parallel HER2‑overexpressing cohort (IHC2+/3+, n=49) showed lower activity (ORR 24.5%, DCR 69%, median PFS 5.4 months), with similar ORR in IHC3+ (20%) and IHC2+ (25.6%) tumors, indicating limited stratification by IHC level alone.[6,8]

Subsequent development focused on dose optimization and geographic expansion. DESTINY‑Lung02 (NCT04644237) compared 5.4 vs 6.4 mg/kg in HER2‑mutant NSCLC; pooled analyses cited in NSCLC ADC reviews highlight 5.4 mg/kg as the preferred dose because it maintains robust efficacy while reducing interstitial lung disease (ILD) risk, and this regimen is now the recommended NSCLC dose in the US label.[2,4] In the Chinese phase II DESTINY‑Lung05 study (NCT05610686; N=72), HER2‑mutant NSCLC treated with T‑DXd 5.4 mg/kg achieved an ORR of 58.3% and a 12‑month PFS rate of 55.1%, reinforcing consistent activity across populations.[4]

T‑DXd has also been evaluated in tumor‑agnostic programs enrolling HER2‑positive or HER2‑mutated solid tumors, including lung cancer, and these data supported the 2024–2025 US accelerated approval for unresectable or metastatic HER2‑positive (IHC 3+) solid tumors after prior systemic therapy.[2,5,6] Ongoing trials such as DESTINY‑Lung03 (phase Ib; T‑DXd plus durvalumab ± chemotherapy in newly diagnosed HER2‑positive advanced NSCLC) and DESTINY‑Lung04 (phase III; first‑line T‑DXd vs pembrolizumab‑chemotherapy in HER2‑mutant NSCLC) aim to define the role of T‑DXd earlier in the treatment algorithm and in combination regimens.[7,8] Collectively, these studies position T‑DXd as a precision therapy for HER2‑mutant NSCLC with high response rates and clinically meaningful PFS/OS, while underscoring the need for ILD monitoring and careful dose selection.[1,2,4,6–8]
