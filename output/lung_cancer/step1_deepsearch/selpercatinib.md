## 1. Drug Summary

Selpercatinib (LOXO‑292; Retevmo) is an oral, ATP‑competitive, highly selective RET tyrosine kinase inhibitor developed to target RET fusions and activating RET mutations across tumor types, with non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) as a major indication.[5][7] RET fusions act as oncogenic drivers in ~1–2% of NSCLC, most commonly in adenocarcinoma, and define a genomically distinct, oncogene‑addicted subtype suitable for precision therapy.[3][5][7] Based on the phase 1/2 LIBRETTO‑001 trial (NCT03157128), selpercatinib received FDA approval for advanced RET fusion‑positive NSCLC and thyroid cancers, and later tissue‑agnostic approval for RET fusion‑positive solid tumors.[2][5][7]

Mechanistically, RET activation drives downstream RAS/MAPK/ERK, PI3K/AKT, and JAK/STAT signaling, promoting proliferation and survival.[5][7] Selpercatinib was engineered for high potency against diverse RET fusions and mutations (including V804 “gatekeeper” mutations) with minimal off‑target kinase inhibition, which differentiates it from older multikinase inhibitors such as cabozantinib and vandetanib that are limited by VEGFR‑mediated toxicities.[1][5][7] In RET fusion‑positive NSCLC within LIBRETTO‑001, selpercatinib achieved objective response rates of ~61% in heavily pretreated and 84% in treatment‑naïve patients, with median progression‑free survival around 22–25 months and substantial intracranial activity, supporting its use as a frontline standard in RET‑driven lung cancer.[5][7][8]

As experience has grown, selpercatinib has become a central component of precision oncology paradigms, analogous to EGFR, ALK, and ROS1 inhibitors, with ongoing phase III trials (e.g., LIBRETTO‑431, NCT04194944) directly comparing first‑line selpercatinib to platinum‑pemetrexed ± pembrolizumab in RET fusion‑positive NSCLC.[5][7] Emerging data also define resistance mechanisms—on‑target RET solvent‑front mutations (e.g., G810 substitutions) and off‑target bypass via MET or KRAS amplification—informing next‑generation RET inhibitors and rational combination strategies.[1][3][4][6]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Selpercatinib (LOXO‑292) is an orally bioavailable, ATP‑competitive small‑molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitor that is highly selective for RET, with >100‑fold lower activity against VEGFR2 and minimal activity on other kinases.[1,3,7,9] RET fusions in NSCLC juxtapose 5′ dimerization domains from partners such as KIF5B, CCDC6, or NCOA4 to the 3′ RET kinase domain, creating chimeric proteins with ligand‑ and co‑receptor‑independent RET autophosphorylation.[1,3,6,8,11] Constitutively active RET then drives downstream RAS–MAPK/ERK, PI3K–AKT/mTOR, and JAK–STAT signaling, promoting proliferation, survival, and tumor progression in the ~1–2% of NSCLCs that are RET‑fusion–positive.[2,3,5,6,8,10,11]

By occupying the ATP‑binding pocket of RET, selpercatinib potently inhibits wild‑type RET and multiple oncogenic RET fusions and mutations, including gatekeeper V804L/M, thereby blocking phosphorylation‑dependent recruitment of adaptor proteins and shutting down MAPK, PI3K–AKT, and JAK–STAT cascades in RET‑addicted tumor cells.[1,3,7,9] This RET‑selective inhibition translates into high systemic and intracranial response rates in RET fusion‑positive NSCLC, including in patients with brain metastases, reflecting effective CNS penetration and target engagement.[1,3,7,9,10] Clinical activity has been observed across diverse RET fusion partners, and responses to selective RET TKIs do not appear to differ substantially by partner, supporting functional fusion‑partner independence in RET‑driven lung cancers.[1,4,5,7,9,11]

Selpercatinib’s strong CNS activity is particularly relevant in RET‑rearranged NSCLC, in which brain metastases are common; objective intracranial responses of ~90% have been reported in LIBRETTO‑001, and case reports describe regression of brain lesions in RET fusion‑positive large‑cell neuroendocrine carcinoma on selpercatinib.[1,2,3,7,9,10] Collectively, these features—high RET selectivity, potent suppression of RET‑driven MAPK/PI3K/JAK–STAT signaling, CNS penetrance, and efficacy across fusion partners—underpin its role as a precision therapy for RET fusion‑positive NSCLC.

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

No valid external URLs or curated pathway resources (e.g., KEGG, Reactome, MSigDB) were available in the provided source material, so specific database identifiers or primary-data–based pathway annotations cannot be cited.

In general mechanistic terms, selpercatinib is a highly selective RET tyrosine kinase inhibitor; in RET‑altered NSCLC its dominant effect is to inhibit RET autophosphorylation and downstream signaling cascades that control proliferation and survival. RET pathway blockade is expected to downregulate MAPK/ERK and PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling, which are key mediators of mitogenic and prosurvival outputs in RET‑fusion lung cancers; suppression of these pathways is typically associated with drug sensitivity, reflected by reduced proliferation and increased apoptosis in RET‑dependent cells.

Inhibition of RET‑driven signaling can also attenuate JAK–STAT activity and perturb cell‑cycle regulatory networks (e.g., cyclins/CDKs), promoting G1 arrest and decreased clonogenic growth in sensitive tumors. Conversely, acquired resistance to selpercatinib frequently involves restoration or bypass of these same signaling axes (e.g., reactivation of MAPK or PI3K–AKT signaling via alternative kinases or parallel receptor tyrosine kinases), reducing reliance on RET and attenuating selpercatinib sensitivity. ## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

Selpercatinib is approved for adult patients with locally advanced or metastatic RET fusion–positive NSCLC, based on LIBRETTO‑001, which showed high objective response rates (ORR) and durable responses in both treatment‑naïve and platinum‑pretreated populations.[5],[6] In the FDA regular‑approval dataset (n=316), ORR was 84% in 69 treatment‑naïve patients (median duration of response [DOR] 20.2 months) and 61% in 247 platinum‑pretreated patients (median DOR 28.6 months), supporting use across lines of therapy.[5] Accelerated‑approval data in an earlier cohort similarly showed ORR 64% in 105 previously platinum‑treated patients and 85% in 39 systemic‑therapy–naïve patients, with ≥6‑month DOR in most responders.[6]

The FDA labels and reviews describe efficacy in RET fusion–positive NSCLC as a class, without differentiating outcomes by specific fusion partner such as KIF5B–RET or CCDC6–RET, indicating consistent activity across RET‑rearranged adenocarcinoma subtypes.[4] RET fusion–positive NSCLC is recognized as a distinct molecular subset for which selpercatinib is positioned as a targeted, orally administered, highly selective RET inhibitor.[3],[4] Both the LIBRETTO‑001 trial and subsequent regulatory documents emphasize meaningful systemic tumor shrinkage and prolonged disease control in this biomarker‑defined subgroup.[3],[5],[6]

Selpercatinib also demonstrates clinically relevant activity against brain metastases in RET fusion–positive NSCLC. The LIBRETTO‑001 and ARROW trial–focused review notes that selpercatinib has shown efficacy for brain metastases, consistent with its design as a CNS‑active RET inhibitor.[3] This CNS activity is important given the high incidence of intracranial disease in advanced NSCLC and supports its use in patients with baseline or emerging brain metastases.[3],[5]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

No usable regulatory, clinical‑trial, or guideline URLs were provided in the source material, so specific FDA/EMA label text, NCCN NSCLC guideline recommendations, and any associated identifiers cannot be cited directly. The following is therefore a general structural summary and must not be treated as a fully sourced or exhaustive safety description for selpercatinib.

Key safety concerns in lung cancer patients include hepatotoxicity (elevations in transaminases and bilirubin), QT interval prolongation, hypertension, hemorrhagic events, and hypersensitivity reactions. In clinical practice, these are typically managed with baseline risk assessment, periodic liver function tests and ECGs, blood pressure monitoring, and dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation when clinically significant abnormalities occur.

Selpercatinib is primarily metabolized via CYP3A, so strong CYP3A inhibitors may increase exposure and toxicity risk, while strong CYP3A inducers may reduce exposure and efficacy; labels generally recommend avoidance, dose adjustment, or enhanced monitoring when such combinations cannot be avoided. Concomitant use with other QT‑prolonging drugs or agents with significant hepatotoxic or hemorrhagic potential is usually approached cautiously, with individualized risk–benefit evaluation.

Use in specific populations (hepatic or renal impairment, pregnancy, lactation, pediatrics, older adults) is typically guided by organ‑function data and limited clinical experience, with more restrictive use or enhanced monitoring in higher‑risk groups. For authoritative, up‑to‑date details on contraindications, warnings, dose modifications, and monitoring requirements, clinicians should consult the current FDA and EMA prescribing information and the latest NCCN NSCLC guidelines directly.

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Within these constraints, pivotal evidence for selpercatinib in RET fusion–positive NSCLC comes from:  
- An early‑phase, single‑arm registrational study (commonly referred to as LIBRETTO‑001) that evaluated selpercatinib across lines of therapy in RET‑altered cancers, including advanced NSCLC. This trial established clinically meaningful systemic activity and intracranial responses in patients with measurable CNS metastases, supporting regulatory approvals and positioning selpercatinib as a preferred RET‑selective tyrosine kinase inhibitor in both previously treated and treatment‑naïve RET fusion–positive NSCLC.  
- A subsequent randomized phase III first‑line trial (commonly referred to as LIBRETTO‑431) that compared selpercatinib with platinum‑based chemotherapy regimens, with or without immunotherapy, in metastatic RET fusion–positive NSCLC. This trial demonstrated superior progression‑free efficacy and reduced CNS progression with selpercatinib versus standard chemo‑(immuno)therapy, reinforcing selpercatinib as a first‑line standard of care in this molecularly defined population.
