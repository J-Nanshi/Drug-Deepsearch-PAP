## 1. Drug Summary

Trametinib is an oral, selective MEK1/2 inhibitor that blocks MEK kinase activity and prevents RAF‑dependent phosphorylation of ERK within the RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway, thereby attenuating proliferative and survival signaling downstream of oncogenic BRAF V600E.[3],[6] In lung cancer, trametinib is used almost exclusively in combination with the BRAF inhibitor dabrafenib, providing dual blockade of the MAPK cascade and reducing paradoxical pathway activation and secondary cutaneous malignancies seen with BRAF inhibitor monotherapy.[2],[3],[7]

For human lung cancer, the clinically established indication is metastatic non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) harboring BRAF V600E mutations, where dabrafenib 150 mg twice daily plus trametinib 2 mg once daily achieved overall response rates of approximately 61–64% and median progression‑free survival around 10–11 months in the phase II BRF113928 trial, leading to FDA approval for metastatic BRAF V600E‑mutant NSCLC.[2],[5–7] Regulatory and guideline reviews now consider dabrafenib plus trametinib a preferred targeted regimen for this rare NSCLC molecular subset, with activity in both treatment‑naïve and previously treated stage IV disease.[2],[3],[6]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Trametinib is an oral, selective inhibitor of MEK1 and MEK2, key kinases in the RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) cascade that transmit proliferative and survival signals downstream of RAS and RAF. In NSCLC driven by constitutive MAPK activation (e.g., KRAS or NF1 alterations), MEK blockade with trametinib suppresses ERK phosphorylation and can induce growth arrest and apoptosis in MAPK‑dependent cells.[1–3] However, MEK inhibition rapidly relieves ERK‑mediated negative feedback, leading to adaptive reactivation of upstream receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) and MAPK signaling. In KRAS‑mutant lung cancer models, trametinib induces RTK‑specific feedback that is ERBB3‑dominated in epithelial‑like cells and FGFR1‑dominated in mesenchymal‑like cells, limiting durable pathway suppression.[1,4,6] Similar RTK‑ and MAP2K4‑JNK‑JUN–driven feedback loops have been shown to upregulate ERBB2/ERBB3 and blunt sustained ERK inhibition after trametinib exposure in KRAS‑mutant lung cancer cells.[3] Thus, in BRAF‑ or other MAPK‑driven NSCLC subsets, trametinib’s antitumor activity depends on the degree of MAPK pathway addiction versus the capacity for RTK‑mediated feedback reactivation.

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In *BRAFV600E*‑mutant NSCLC (predominantly adenocarcinoma), dabrafenib–trametinib is an “anchor” targeted regimen: basket and MATCH‑style trials demonstrate tumor‑agnostic activity with meaningful response rates and durable progression‑free survival across *BRAFV600E* solid tumors, supporting its approval for metastatic *BRAFV600E* NSCLC as a genomically matched therapy.[1,2,4,7] Responses are driven by dual blockade of mutant BRAF and MEK within the MAPK cascade, but acquired resistance via MAPK reactivation or alternative pathways is common, underscoring the need for ongoing molecular profiling.[1,4,6,7]

For non‑V600E *BRAF* NSCLC, contemporary alteration‑matched reviews emphasize that most clinical development and approvals are restricted to *BRAFV600E*, with no established role for trametinib‑based combinations in class II/III *BRAF* tumors.[2,4,6] These rarer subtypes are typically managed as non–oncogene‑addicted NSCLC or via trial enrollment, reflecting limited prospective efficacy data and a heterogeneous co‑mutation landscape.[2,4,6]

In *KRAS*‑mutant NSCLC, downstream MEK inhibition is biologically rational, but clinical practice has shifted toward direct KRAS G12C inhibitors and immunotherapy; reviews highlight MEK‑pathway targeting (including BRAF/MEK) as an investigational combination strategy rather than a standard option.[4,6,8] KRAS‑mutant tumors often harbor co‑alterations (e.g., *TP53*, *STK11*, *KEAP1*) and display complex resistance biology, so trametinib is generally reserved for trials exploring multi‑pathway blockade.[6,8]

For EGFR‑TKI–resistant NSCLC with acquired *BRAF* or MAPK alterations, current resistance frameworks prioritize next‑generation EGFR TKIs, MET/HER2/HER3‑directed agents, and immunotherapy; none of the recent landscape or regulatory‑focused reviews define a standard role for trametinib in this niche.[2,6,7] Instead, these patients are viewed as candidates for individualized, biomarker‑driven trials testing novel combinations rather than routine BRAF/MEK doublets.[2,4,6,7]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Trametinib is used almost exclusively in combination with a BRAF inhibitor (e.g., dabrafenib) and shares the class toxicity profile of BRAF/MEK inhibition. Key adverse events include a characteristic fever syndrome (pyrexia), hepatotoxicity, cardiomyopathy with reduced left ventricular ejection fraction, and ocular toxicities such as MEK inhibitor–associated central serous chorioretinopathy and retinal detachment.[1] EMA and guideline discussions for MEK inhibitors emphasize that these toxicities are generally reversible but can be serious, requiring structured monitoring and dose interruptions or reductions.[1,2]

In BRAFV600‑mutant solid tumors (including NSCLC), BRAF/MEK combinations frequently cause fever episodes that may necessitate temporary treatment interruption, antipyretics, short courses of corticosteroids, and stepwise dose re‑escalation once symptoms resolve.[1] Cardiomyopathy risk justifies baseline and periodic echocardiography, with discontinuation or dose modification for clinically meaningful declines in ejection fraction.[1,2] Ocular events mandate prompt ophthalmologic evaluation and interruption or discontinuation of trametinib for retinal vein occlusion or persistent serous retinopathy.[1]

Cutaneous toxicity (rash, hyperkeratotic lesions, secondary skin neoplasms) and laboratory abnormalities (liver enzyme elevations, cytopenias, electrolyte disturbances) require regular skin examination and periodic blood counts and chemistries.[1–3] NCCN/ESMO‑type guidance for BRAF/MEK therapy stresses proactive patient education, early reporting of visual, cardiac, respiratory, or febrile symptoms, and careful selection in patients with significant baseline cardiac dysfunction or uncontrolled ocular disease.[1,2]
