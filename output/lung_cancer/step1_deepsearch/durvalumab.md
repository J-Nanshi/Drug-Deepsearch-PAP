## 1. Drug Summary

Durvalumab (IMFINZI®) is a selective, high‑affinity human IgG1 monoclonal antibody targeting programmed death‑ligand 1 (PD‑L1), blocking its interaction with PD‑1 and CD80 and thereby restoring cytotoxic T‑cell recognition and killing of tumor cells.[5] In the phase III PACIFIC trial, consolidative durvalumab (10 mg/kg every 2 weeks for up to 12 months) after platinum‑based concurrent chemoradiotherapy (CRT) significantly improved progression‑free and overall survival in patients with unresectable stage III non‑small‑cell lung cancer (NSCLC) who had not progressed after CRT, establishing the “PACIFIC regimen” as the global standard of care in this setting.[5–7,13] Long‑term follow‑up confirmed durable benefit, with approximately half of patients alive at 4–5 years on durvalumab versus about one‑third on placebo.[5,6,13]

In extensive‑stage small‑cell lung cancer (ES‑SCLC), the phase III CASPIAN trial showed that adding durvalumab to first‑line platinum–etoposide chemotherapy, followed by durvalumab maintenance, significantly improved overall survival and increased 12‑month progression‑free survival and response rates compared with chemotherapy alone; this led to regulatory approval of durvalumab plus etoposide–platinum as a first‑line standard of care in ES‑SCLC.[5,9,10,13] Across lung cancer stages, durvalumab exemplifies precision immuno‑oncology: PD‑1/PD‑L1 checkpoint blockade is integrated with chemoradiation and perioperative strategies, while biomarkers such as PD‑L1 expression, tumor mutational burden, and minimal residual disease are being developed to refine patient selection and optimize stage‑specific, biomarker‑driven immunotherapy.[5,7,10,13]

## 2. Identifiers & Synonyms

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Durvalumab is a human IgG1 monoclonal antibody targeting programmed death-ligand 1 (PD‑L1), a key immune checkpoint ligand upregulated in many NSCLC tumors and associated with response to PD‑(L)1 blockade.[1,3] By binding PD‑L1, durvalumab prevents its interaction with PD‑1 on T cells and with CD80 on antigen‑presenting cells, thereby interrupting an inhibitory axis that normally dampens T‑cell activation and effector function in the lung tumor microenvironment.[2] This checkpoint blockade re‑enables cytotoxic T‑cell–mediated recognition and killing of tumor cells, which underlies the survival gains seen with PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors in advanced NSCLC and in chemo‑immunotherapy regimens for extensive‑stage SCLC.[2,8]

In NSCLC, PD‑L1 expression and tumor mutational burden (TMB) are established but imperfect predictive biomarkers: higher PD‑L1 and TMB associate with improved pathological and clinical responses to PD‑(L)1 blockade, yet substantial heterogeneity and intrapatient variability limit their standalone utility.[1,3,4] SCLC generally exhibits lower tumor‑cell PD‑L1 but very high TMB and profound immune evasion; nonetheless, adding PD‑L1 inhibitors to platinum–etoposide improves outcomes, indicating that even modest PD‑L1 expression on tumor or immune cells can be sufficient for clinically meaningful T‑cell reinvigoration in this highly immunogenic setting.[2,6,8] From a precision‑oncology perspective, durvalumab’s benefit in lung cancer is best understood by integrating PD‑L1 levels, TMB, co‑mutation patterns (e.g., TP53 co‑mutations linked to higher PD‑L1/TMB), and immune contexture (degree and pattern of T‑cell infiltration) rather than relying on any single biomarker.[1,3,4,6]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In unresectable stage III NSCLC after concurrent chemoradiotherapy, the PACIFIC trial established durvalumab consolidation as global standard of care, with marked PFS and OS gains versus placebo and broad benefit across real‑world populations.[1,3,6,7,13,19] Benefit is seen irrespective of histology, but multiple analyses indicate attenuated efficacy in tumors with oncogenic drivers (EGFR, ALK) and in PD‑L1–negative disease, leading some guidelines and expert reviews to caution against routine durvalumab use in EGFR‑mutated stage III NSCLC.[2,3,5,6,13] High tumor PD‑L1 expression (≥50%) consistently correlates with superior PFS/OS on durvalumab in retrospective cohorts, and blood‑based markers such as clearance of circulating tumor cell clusters and lower on‑treatment platelet counts further stratify benefit and resistance risk.[4,5,8] Nonetheless, PD‑L1 <1% and residual CTC clusters or thrombocytosis after CRT are associated with earlier progression, highlighting a subset with primary resistance despite consolidation therapy.[5,6,8]

In metastatic NSCLC, durvalumab ± tremelimumab did not improve PFS or OS over chemotherapy in unselected first‑line populations in MYSTIC, and the NEPTUNE trial failed its OS endpoint in patients selected solely by high blood TMB, indicating that bTMB ≥20 mut/Mb alone is insufficient to predict benefit for this regimen.[9,10,12,13] Exploratory analyses suggested possible signal in very high bTMB ranges, but these findings remain hypothesis‑generating and have not translated into regulatory approvals.[10,12] Overall, other PD‑(L)1–based chemo‑immunotherapy combinations have supplanted durvalumab‑based regimens in metastatic NSCLC, particularly given limited activity in EGFR/ALK‑driven tumors and the lack of a robust, validated biomarker for durvalumab in this setting.[9,12,13,14,19]

For extensive‑stage SCLC, CASPIAN demonstrated that adding durvalumab to platinum–etoposide improves OS versus chemotherapy alone, leading to first‑line approval and establishing chemo‑immunotherapy as a new standard.[20–22] However, benefit remains modest, nearly all patients relapse, and conventional biomarkers such as PD‑L1 expression and blood TMB have not reliably predicted which ES‑SCLC patients derive durable benefit, with IMpower133 showing no association between bTMB and outcome and CASPIAN lacking a clearly actionable biomarker signal.[17,20–23] Emerging transcriptomic subtyping (e.g., SCLC‑I “inflamed” subtype) and HLA class II alleles (e.g., DQB1*03:01) may enrich for immunotherapy benefit, but these approaches are still exploratory and not yet integrated into routine selection of durvalumab for ES‑SCLC.[20,22,23]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Durvalumab has no absolute contraindications in major regulatory guidance but carries class‑typical risks of immune‑related adverse events (irAEs) that can be severe or fatal and involve virtually any organ system.[6],[7],[8] In lung cancer, irAEs most commonly include dermatologic toxicity, hepatitis, colitis/diarrhea, endocrinopathies (thyroiditis, hypo‑/hyperthyroidism, hypophysitis, diabetes), and pneumonitis.[1],[6],[7] Perioperative and neoadjuvant ICI series in resectable NSCLC report any‑grade irAEs in roughly one‑half of patients and grade ≥3 events in ~5–13%, underlining the need for structured monitoring and standardized management algorithms.[6]

Pneumonitis/radiation pneumonitis is a critical toxicity in stage III NSCLC treated with chemoradiotherapy (CRT) followed by durvalumab consolidation, the current standard for unresectable disease based on PACIFIC.[2],[4],[8] Network meta‑analysis across solid tumors shows that sequential ICI after RT (as in PACIFIC) improves survival without increasing grade ≥3 toxicity compared with concurrent RT–ICI, supporting the NCCN/ESMO strategy of post‑CRT durvalumab rather than fully concurrent radio‑immunotherapy in this setting.[3],[8] Close surveillance for new or worsening cough, dyspnea, or infiltrates is essential; high‑dose corticosteroids and treatment interruption/discontinuation are required for moderate–severe pneumonitis.[3],[6]

Durvalumab and other PD‑(L)1 inhibitors can cause immune‑mediated hepatitis and a range of endocrinopathies, mandating baseline and periodic liver function and thyroid testing, with prompt hormone replacement and immunosuppression for grade ≥2 events.[1],[6],[7] Perioperative guidance for NSCLC emphasizes that severe irAEs (e.g., myocarditis, severe colitis, high‑grade pneumonitis) may delay or preclude surgery and must be managed within a multidisciplinary framework.[6] Infusion reactions and rare neurologic or cardiac irAEs also require readiness for rapid recognition and escalation of care.[6],[7]

Patients with pre‑existing autoimmune disease, prior solid‑organ transplant, or clinically significant interstitial lung disease (ILD) were largely excluded from pivotal durvalumab trials; available ICI experience indicates higher rates of irAE flares, ILD exacerbation, and allograft rejection in these groups, so use demands individualized risk–benefit assessment and intensive monitoring.[6],[7],[8] In extensive‑stage SCLC, where durvalumab is combined with platinum–etoposide, paraneoplastic autoimmune phenomena and prior thoracic RT further heighten vigilance for neurologic, endocrine, and pulmonary irAEs.[1],[7] Pregnancy and breastfeeding are generally discouraged during PD‑(L)1 blockade due to potential fetal immune perturbation and lack of human lactation data, and contraception is recommended for both sexes during and after treatment.[4],[7],[8]

| Tumor immune phenotypes: inflamed vs immune‑excluded vs immune‑desert (composite immune‑signature programs) | Inflamed: IFN‑γ/PD‑L1/T‑cell genes Up; Excluded/Desert: stromal, VEGF, WNT/TGF‑β Up | Inflamed → Sensitive; Excluded/Desert → Resistant | NSCLC tumors with inflamed, CD8‑T‑cell–infiltrated, PD‑L1‑high microenvironments respond best to PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade, underpinning durvalumab benefit post‑CRT; immune‑excluded/desert phenotypes driven by stromal barriers, VEGF, and TGF‑β signaling show poor responses, explaining primary resistance in a subset of stage III and metastatic patients. | [1],[2],[4],[5],[7],[13] |
| Chemoradiotherapy‑induced immunogenic modulation (CRT → antigen release and PD‑L1 upregulation) | Up (antigen release, PD‑L1) | Sensitive | Definitive CRT alone yields modest long‑term survival, but adding durvalumab increases median PFS and 4–5‑year OS; CRT enhances tumor antigen release, dendritic‑cell priming, and PD‑L1 upregulation, creating a transient window in which PD‑L1 blockade consolidates and amplifies anti‑tumor immunity in unresectable stage III NSCLC. | [3],[6],[7],[8],[13] |
| Immune‑related pneumonitis / lung tissue injury and repair pathways | Up (inflammatory lung signaling) | Resistant (when early, treatment‑limiting) | In durvalumab consolidation cohorts, treatment‑emergent pneumonitis—particularly early‑onset events leading to discontinuation—is associated with worse PFS and OS, suggesting that exaggerated or misdirected lung‑inflammatory pathways can curtail durvalumab exposure and undermine long‑term benefit, despite class‑typical overall safety. | [2],[3],[4],[6],[7],[8] |
| SCLC immune contexture and transcriptional subtypes (e.g., SCLC‑I “inflamed” subtype) | Up (inflamed SCLC‑I programs) | Sensitive (hypothesized) | In ES‑SCLC, adding durvalumab to EP chemotherapy improves OS but with modest absolute benefit and no robust PD‑L1/TMB biomarker; emerging data suggest that an “inflamed” SCLC‑I transcriptional subtype and certain HLA class II alleles may enrich for benefit from PD‑(L)1 blockade, providing a mechanistic basis for differential durvalumab sensitivity across SCLC subtypes. | [4],[5],[9],[10],[20],[21],[22],[23] |
