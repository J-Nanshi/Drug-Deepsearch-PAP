## Drug Summary

Lorlatinib is an oral, third‑generation, macrocyclic ATP‑competitive tyrosine kinase inhibitor that selectively targets ALK and ROS1 fusion kinases, with additional activity against a limited set of kinases (e.g., TRK, FAK) at higher concentrations.[4],[6] It was rationally designed to overcome on‑target resistance to earlier ALK TKIs, including the solvent‑front mutation G1202R, and to retain broad activity across multiple single ALK resistance mutations.[1],[4],[5] In ALK‑rearranged NSCLC, lorlatinib potently suppresses downstream MAPK and PI3K/AKT signaling, inducing tumor regression in models with diverse EML4‑ALK variants and resistance profiles.[3],[5],[6]

Clinically, lorlatinib is approved for ALK‑positive advanced/metastatic NSCLC both after prior ALK TKIs and as first‑line therapy. The phase III CROWN trial demonstrated superior progression‑free survival and intracranial control versus crizotinib in untreated ALK‑positive NSCLC, establishing lorlatinib as a high‑efficacy frontline option.[1],[3],[4],[6],[13],[18] In earlier‑line and salvage phase I/II programs, it produced systemic response rates around 40–70% across heavily pretreated ALK‑positive populations, with enhanced benefit when tumors harbored ALK resistance mutations, reflecting continued ALK dependence.[4],[5],[13],[18]

Central nervous system activity is a defining feature. Lorlatinib’s macrocyclic structure and low P‑gp efflux yield high CNS penetration, with robust intracranial response rates and durable control of brain metastases in both ALK‑ and ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC.[2],[4],[6],[9],[13],[18] This underpins its preferential use in patients with baseline or high‑risk CNS disease. In ROS1‑positive NSCLC, lorlatinib is not yet globally licensed but is recognized in guidelines as a later‑line option after crizotinib or entrectinib, with meaningful systemic and intracranial activity but limited efficacy against key ROS1 resistance mutations such as G2032R and L2026M.[6],[7]

## Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Lorlatinib is a third‑generation, reversible, ATP‑competitive small‑molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitor that selectively targets anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK) and ROS proto‑oncogene 1 (ROS1), designed specifically to address on‑target resistance that emerges with earlier ALK TKIs in NSCLC.[3],[5],[18] It binds the ATP pocket of the ALK kinase domain and retains potency against a broad spectrum of secondary ALK mutations, including the gatekeeper L1196M and the solvent‑front G1202R, which are common drivers of resistance to crizotinib and second‑generation agents.[1],[5],[9],[18] By inhibiting ALK/ROS1 signaling, lorlatinib suppresses downstream MAPK and PI3K–AKT pathways critical for proliferation and survival in ALK‑rearranged and ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC.[3],[11]

A defining feature of lorlatinib is its macrocyclic structure and optimization to minimize P‑glycoprotein–mediated efflux, resulting in high blood–brain barrier penetration and robust intracranial activity.[5],[18],[21] In phase I/II and the phase III CROWN trial, lorlatinib produced high intracranial response rates and durable CNS control in ALK‑positive NSCLC, including patients with baseline brain metastases, reflecting effective CNS target inhibition.[7],[18],[21] Clinically, ALK‑rearranged adenocarcinoma (3–7% of NSCLC) is the principal indication, and lorlatinib is now an approved first‑line standard based on its superiority to crizotinib in systemic and CNS outcomes in CROWN.[3],[7],[9],[21] In ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, lorlatinib provides a CNS‑penetrant option with substantial intracranial responses after crizotinib, particularly in patients with brain‑only progression, although resistance mutations such as ROS1 G2032R and L2086F can emerge on therapy.[7],[8]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

Lorlatinib is a third-generation ALK/ROS1 TKI with high CNS penetration that suppresses key downstream survival pathways driven by ALK and ROS1 fusions in NSCLC, while resistance frequently emerges via reactivation of these same cascades or activation of bypass RTKs.[1,3–5,7] In ALK‑rearranged disease, EML4‑ALK activates PLC‑γ, JAK–STAT, PI3K–AKT–mTOR and RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK; third‑generation ALK TKIs such as lorlatinib are specifically designed to inhibit resistant ALK mutants (e.g., G1202R) and thereby shut down these signals, although bypass activation through other RTKs (EGFR, MET, RET) or downstream nodes can restore pathway output and drive resistance.[1,4,5,7] In ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, ROS1 fusions signal via RAS/RAF/MAPK, PI3K/AKT/mTOR and STAT3; lorlatinib demonstrates potent activity against multiple crizotinib‑resistant ROS1 kinase mutations (e.g., G2032R, L2026M, S1986Y/F, D2033N), but resistance can still arise through MAPK reactivation and off‑target alterations such as KRAS or BRAF mutations.[2,3,5,6]

High‑level key pathways modulated in ALK‑ or ROS1‑driven NSCLC include:

- **PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling** (e.g., KEGG hsa04151): downstream of ALK and ROS1; effective ALK/ROS1 inhibition by lorlatinib is expected to downregulate this survival axis, while PI3K/MTOR/PTEN alterations are observed as dominant bypass mechanisms after first‑line lorlatinib, sustaining resistance.[3,5,7]  
- **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway** (e.g., KEGG hsa04010): a core effector of ALK and ROS1 fusions; lorlatinib-mediated on‑target blockade reduces MAPK output, but KRAS/NRAS/BRAF/MAPK alterations and broader MAPK reactivation are recurrent convergent resistance mechanisms in both ALK‑ and ROS1‑positive NSCLC.[1–3,5,7]  
- **JAK–STAT signaling** (e.g., KEGG hsa04630): ALK activates JAK–STAT (particularly STAT3), promoting proliferation and survival; ALK inhibition with lorlatinib should suppress this pathway, yet downstream STAT3 activation can be maintained by alternative RTKs or cytokine receptors, contributing to off‑target resistance.[1,3,5,7]  
- **PLC‑γ–dependent signaling** (e.g., KEGG hsa04072/linked to RTK pathways): ALK homodimerization activates PLC‑γ, which supports mitogenic and survival programs; lorlatinib’s inhibition of ALK is expected to attenuate PLC‑γ signaling, but compensatory RTK activation can partially restore this arm.[1,3]  
- **RTK bypass networks (EGFR/MET/RET and related ErbB signaling; e.g., KEGG hsa04012, hsa04014)**: amplification or activation of EGFR, MET, RET and other RTKs is documented as a major bypass route after lorlatinib, re‑engaging PI3K–AKT and MAPK signaling despite continued ALK inhibition.[1,3,4,7]  

Collectively, lorlatinib’s activity in ALK‑ and ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC reflects potent on‑target fusion kinase inhibition with consequent suppression of PI3K–AKT, MAPK, JAK–STAT and PLC‑γ pathways, while resistance is largely mediated by reactivation of these cascades through off‑target RTK and downstream mutations.[1–7]

## Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

In ALK‑rearranged adenocarcinoma, lorlatinib is positioned as a third‑generation ALK TKI with high CNS penetration and is now one of the five FDA‑approved ALK TKIs for advanced disease.[1] The CROWN phase III trial (NCT03052608) established first‑line lorlatinib versus crizotinib in metastatic ALK‑positive NSCLC; trial data summarized in contemporary reviews show markedly reduced CNS progression and support lorlatinib as a preferred option in molecularly selected adenocarcinoma with high brain‑metastatic risk.[1,7,8] Case‑based and retrospective series further indicate that lorlatinib retains activity in heavily pretreated ALK‑positive adenocarcinoma, although sequential resistance with complex ALK mutations and co‑alterations (e.g., PIK3CA, RB1) can drive primary or rapid secondary resistance.[2,7]

ALK‑positive NSCLC is particularly prone to brain metastases (cumulative incidence ~60% at 6 years), making CNS‑metastatic disease a critical subtype for lorlatinib.[7] In the CROWN study, the 12‑month cumulative CNS progression rate on lorlatinib was reported as 7% in patients with baseline brain metastases and 1% without, underscoring its preventive and therapeutic intracranial efficacy.[7] Broader brain‑metastasis literature supports using CNS‑active TKIs such as lorlatinib as initial systemic therapy in asymptomatic ALK‑rearranged NSCLC with multiple brain metastases, often deferring radiotherapy or reserving it for consolidation or salvage in oligometastatic patterns.[1,3] ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC shares clinical features with ALK‑positive disease, but current reviews emphasize crizotinib and next‑generation ROS1 inhibitors; lorlatinib is discussed mainly as a later‑line option in ROS1‑driven subsets with emerging resistance, with less mature, subtype‑specific data than in ALK‑rearranged adenocarcinoma.[4,5]  



## Contraindications and Safety

Lorlatinib has a distinctive but generally manageable toxicity profile in ALK/ROS1‑driven NSCLC. As a CYP3A‑metabolized TKI, its exposure can be altered by strong CYP3A modulators; broader TKI guidance highlights that strong CYP3A inhibitors or inducers can respectively increase or decrease TKI efficacy/toxicity, underscoring the need to avoid potent CYP3A inducers and to carefully manage interacting drugs.[1] Expert consensus emphasizes baseline assessment of comorbidities and concomitant medications, with particular attention to cardiovascular and neurological status before starting lorlatinib.[3]

Hyperlipidemia is the hallmark adverse event. Across clinical trials and real‑world cohorts, hypercholesterolemia and hypertriglyceridemia are the most frequent AEs, typically emerging early and often requiring at least one lipid‑lowering agent; management guidelines recommend statin therapy chosen with attention to CYP450 interactions and, if needed, dose interruption or reduction, which generally does not compromise efficacy.[3,5] Metabolic derangements can extend to weight gain and dysglycemia, as illustrated in ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC where lorlatinib‑related dyslipidemia and elevated hemoglobin A1c were controlled with dose reductions and statin therapy.[4,5]

Neurocognitive and psychiatric effects (cognitive slowing, mood changes, confusion, speech disturbance) are recognized class toxicities of highly CNS‑penetrant TKIs such as lorlatinib and often prompt temporary interruption and dose reduction rather than discontinuation.[1,3] Cardiovascular safety requires vigilance: post‑marketing pharmacovigilance has identified rare but serious events including pulmonary arterial hypertension and pericardial effusion, reinforcing the need for baseline and periodic cardiovascular evaluation and prompt investigation of dyspnea or cardiopulmonary symptoms.[3] Overall, expert panels conclude that lorlatinib’s risks are predictable and usually reversible with proactive monitoring, multidisciplinary management, and structured patient education, and should not preclude its use in the absence of clear contraindications.[3,6]

## Key Clinical Trials

### CROWN phase III trial – first-line ALK-positive advanced NSCLC (B7461006; NCT03052608)

CROWN is a global, randomized, open-label phase III trial comparing lorlatinib 100 mg once daily with crizotinib 250 mg twice daily in previously untreated, advanced ALK‑positive NSCLC (NCT03052608).[4,8,12] At the primary analysis, median progression‑free survival (PFS) by blinded independent central review was not reached with lorlatinib versus 9.3 months with crizotinib (HR 0.28; 95% CI 0.19–0.41), establishing a marked first‑line efficacy advantage.[4,7,12] With 5‑year follow‑up, median PFS remains unreached and the landmark 5‑year PFS is ~60%, representing the longest PFS reported for any single‑agent targeted therapy in metastatic solid tumors, with very low rates of new brain metastases (only 4/114 without baseline CNS disease).[4,9,12] Lorlatinib also showed superior intracranial control (time to intracranial progression not reached vs 16.4 months with crizotinib; HR 0.06), supporting its preferred use in ALK‑rearranged, CNS‑prone NSCLC within precision oncology algorithms.[4,9,12]

### Global phase I/II ALK/ROS1-positive NSCLC trial (NCT01970865)

The ongoing multicenter, open‑label phase I/II trial NCT01970865 evaluates continuous lorlatinib dosing in advanced ALK‑ or ROS1‑positive NSCLC across molecularly and treatment‑defined expansion cohorts.[2,5,6,13] Phase I established 100 mg once daily as the recommended dose, with broad activity in heavily pretreated ALK‑positive patients and substantial intracranial responses, including in those with ≥2 prior ALK TKIs.[5,6] In phase II, lorlatinib demonstrated high systemic and CNS response rates across ALK cohorts stratified by prior TKI exposure and showed clinically meaningful activity in ROS1‑rearranged NSCLC, particularly in TKI‑naïve disease, supporting its role as a salvage and, in some settings, earlier‑line precision therapy.[2,5,13] These data underpinned regulatory approvals for lorlatinib in second‑/third‑line ALK‑positive NSCLC and informed its pharmacokinetic and drug–drug interaction profile relevant to long‑term targeted treatment.[2,6,11]

### Early-phase and real-world CNS-focused evidence in oncogene-addicted NSCLC

In an early phase I trial summarized within a broader review of brain metastases in oncogene‑addicted NSCLC, lorlatinib achieved an overall response rate of 39% and median PFS 6.9 months in 111 heavily pretreated patients (two or three prior TKIs ± chemotherapy), with 48% intracranial response among those with baseline brain metastases.[1] These findings highlighted lorlatinib’s CNS penetrance and activity against ALK and ROS1 resistance mutations, leading to Breakthrough Therapy designation and prioritization of CNS endpoints in subsequent studies, including CROWN.[1,4,5] Collectively, across phase I/II and phase III programs, lorlatinib consistently delivers durable systemic control and high intracranial response rates, positioning it as a cornerstone ALK TKI for patients with baseline or emergent CNS disease.[1,2,4,5,12,13]

| Cell‑cycle checkpoint pathways (TP53/RB1/STK11 and CDKN2A axis) | Up (loss/inactivation of suppressors) | Resistant | Co‑alterations in TP53, RB1, STK11 and CDKN2A are frequent in NSCLC and brain metastases and correlate with worse outcomes and TKI resistance; loss of these tumor suppressors promotes proliferation, genomic instability, and lineage plasticity, reducing dependence on single‑driver ALK signaling under lorlatinib. | [9], [12], [14], [15], [16], [17] |
| DNA‑damage and chromatin‑remodeling pathways | Up | Resistant | Multi‑omics studies show recurrent alterations in genes such as ATM, KEAP1, SETD2, ARID1A and SMARCA4 in advanced and brain‑metastatic NSCLC; these changes enhance adaptability and may facilitate acquisition of complex ALK mutations and off‑target resistance pathways during prolonged lorlatinib exposure. | [12], [15], [16], [17] |
| CNS metastasis–associated pathways (TP53/MAPK/PI3K and YAP1 networks) | Up | Mixed (Sensitive systemically, sanctuary‑site risk) | Lorlatinib provides deep intracranial control, yet brain‑metastasis specimens from NSCLC are enriched for alterations in TP53, MAPK, PI3K/mTOR, cell‑cycle genes and YAP1; these networks may underlie CNS tropism and influence the biology of residual or emergent brain disease despite effective ALK inhibition. | [3], [10], [15], [16] |
| Immune‑microenvironment and TKI‑tolerant cell programs | Up | Resistant | Liquid‑biopsy and multi‑omics reviews emphasize that drug‑tolerant persister cells, immune‑evasive microenvironments, and macrophage‑driven immunosuppression enable survival under targeted therapies; such non‑genomic and immunologic adaptations likely contribute to lorlatinib resistance when no ALK or clear bypass alterations are detected. | [15], [16], [17] |
