## 1. Drug Summary

Pralsetinib (Gavreto; formerly BLU‑667) is an oral, next‑generation, small‑molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitor that selectively targets the RET receptor tyrosine kinase, including RET fusion proteins and activating RET mutations.[1][12] RET fusions occur in ~1–2% of non‑small cell lung cancers (NSCLC), predominantly adenocarcinoma, and act as oncogenic drivers via ligand‑independent activation of RET and downstream PI3K/AKT, RAS/MAPK and related signaling, defining a distinct molecular subset suitable for targeted therapy.[3][6][10][12]

In the global phase I/II ARROW trial (NCT03037385), pralsetinib at 400 mg once daily produced rapid, durable responses in advanced RET fusion‑positive NSCLC. Objective response rates were ~61–72% in platinum‑pretreated patients and ~70–72% in treatment‑naïve patients, with prolonged progression‑free survival and meaningful intracranial activity, including an intracranial response rate of 70% in patients with measurable brain metastases.[3][6][10][14] Safety was generally manageable; the most frequent grade ≥3 treatment‑related adverse events were neutropenia, anemia, hypertension and other hematologic toxicities.[1][3][11][14]

Based on ARROW, pralsetinib received regulatory approvals in multiple regions, including the US and EU, for metastatic or advanced RET fusion‑positive NSCLC, and is positioned as a genotype‑directed therapy requiring confirmed RET fusion by validated testing.[1][4][10][12][15] Real‑world and meta‑analytic data across selective RET inhibitors (pralsetinib and selpercatinib) show high objective response and disease control rates with relatively low incidence of severe adverse events in RET fusion‑positive NSCLC.[6][7][20] Within precision oncology, pralsetinib exemplifies targeted inhibition of an oncogene‑addicted subset and is considered a preferred systemic option for RET fusion‑positive metastatic NSCLC in appropriate regulatory settings.[10][12][15]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Pralsetinib (BLU‑667) is an oral, highly selective RET tyrosine kinase inhibitor that potently targets RET fusions common in NSCLC, including KIF5B–RET and CCDC6–RET.[1][7][8] RET fusions generate constitutively active chimeric receptors that signal independently of ligand through RAS–MAPK (RAF/MEK/ERK), PI3K–AKT/mTOR, PKC and JAK–STAT pathways, driving proliferation, survival and metastasis in lung adenocarcinoma.[1][7][8][14] 

By binding the RET kinase domain and inhibiting RET autophosphorylation, pralsetinib suppresses these downstream cascades, leading to tumor regression and intracranial activity in metastatic RET fusion‑positive NSCLC.[1][4][7][13] RET‑rearranged NSCLC represents a distinct molecular subtype, enriched in younger, never‑ or light‑smokers with poorly differentiated adenocarcinoma, low PD‑L1 expression and low tumor mutational burden, and is largely mutually exclusive with other classic drivers.[9][13][15] Pralsetinib retains activity against RET V804L/M gatekeeper mutants and shows markedly higher selectivity for RET over VEGFR2 than multikinase inhibitors, reducing off‑target anti‑angiogenic toxicities.[7][8][11]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Under effective RET blockade by pralsetinib in RET fusion–positive NSCLC, key downstream pathways are suppressed, and this downregulation is generally associated with treatment sensitivity; reactivation via on‑ or off‑target mechanisms underlies resistance:

1. **RET signaling / RTK axis (overlaps KEGG hsa04010)** – RET fusions constitutively activate the RET kinase and its docking tyrosines (e.g., Y1062, Y1096), driving multiple downstream cascades.[9,16] Pralsetinib selectively inhibits RET autophosphorylation and signaling, and this downregulation is required for clinical responses in ARROW and real‑world cohorts.[6,8–10] Reactivation through RET solvent‑front (G810) or other on‑target mutations restores signaling and confers resistance.[9,10,15]

2. **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK / MAPK pathway (KEGG hsa04010)** – RET fusion proteins activate RAS/MAPK to promote proliferation and survival.[3,7,9,16] Pralsetinib-mediated RET inhibition downregulates MAPK signaling in sensitive tumors; acquired resistance commonly involves bypass reactivation via MET amplification, KRAS amplification/mutation, or BRAF V600E, which re‑engage MAPK despite RET blockade.[3,9–11,15]

3. **PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway (KEGG hsa04151)** – RET Y1062 recruits adaptors that activate PI3K/AKT, supporting growth and anti‑apoptotic signaling.[16] Pralsetinib indirectly downregulates this pathway via RET inhibition, contributing to tumor regression in ARROW.[9,10] Resistance can arise through PI3K/AKT reactivation (e.g., via MET or other RTKs) or mTOR-driven escape, sometimes targeted experimentally with combinations such as vandetanib plus everolimus.[11,15]

4. **JAK–STAT signaling (KEGG hsa04630)** – RET phosphorylates docking sites (e.g., Y752, Y928) that activate STAT3 and related JAK–STAT components, promoting proliferation and immune modulation.[15,16] RET inhibition by pralsetinib is expected to downregulate this axis in responsive NSCLC, whereas JAK–STAT reactivation via alternative cytokine/RTK inputs may support resistance and immune evasion.[3,9,13,15]

5. **PKC / PLCγ and PKA-related signaling (part of KEGG hsa04010)** – RET Y1015 recruits PLCγ, activating PKC, while other sites can engage PKA pathways, enhancing migration and survival.[14,16] Pralsetinib suppresses these RET-dependent inputs in sensitive disease; persistent PKC/PKA activity via other RTKs could contribute to residual signaling under drug pressure.[14–16]

6. **Cell cycle regulation (CDK–RB–E2F modules; KEGG hsa04110)** – RET-driven MAPK and PI3K cascades converge on cyclins/CDKs and RB–E2F to drive G1–S progression in RET fusion NSCLC.[3,9,11,16] Pralsetinib reduces upstream mitogenic signaling, functionally enforcing cell-cycle arrest and underpinning durable responses in ARROW.[9,10] Bypass activation of MAPK/PI3K or co-alterations in genes such as SMARCA4 can maintain cell-cycle progression and are linked to primary or acquired resistance.[9–11]  



## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

Pralsetinib is approved for metastatic RET fusion-positive NSCLC, a population dominated by lung adenocarcinoma with enrichment in younger, never- or light-smokers.[7,8] In the phase I/II ARROW trial, pralsetinib produced objective response rates (ORR) of 65% in 116 previously treated and 73% in 26 treatment-naïve RET fusion-positive NSCLC, indicating substantial efficacy in the prototypical RET-rearranged adenocarcinoma setting.[8] Real‑world datasets and the RETgistry consortium, which included 94 lung cancer patients (mostly NSCLC) treated with selpercatinib or pralsetinib, report median progression-free survival around 13 months and confirm benefit across common fusion partners, particularly KIF5B‑RET and CCDC6‑RET.[1,4]

Non-adenocarcinoma RET‑altered NSCLC subtypes are rare. RET rearrangements occur mainly in adenocarcinoma and are infrequent in squamous histology; available series do not provide pralsetinib-specific efficacy signals in squamous or other rare histologies.[7,8] Across RET fusion-positive NSCLC, resistance to pralsetinib appears largely histology-agnostic and is dominated by off-target mechanisms such as MET amplification and other MAPK/PI3K-pathway alterations, whereas on-target secondary RET mutations are less common.[1,2,4,8] Case-based and registry data also describe small-cell transformation after pralsetinib in RET‑altered NSCLC, underscoring the need for post‑progression biopsy to detect histologic shift and guide subsequent therapy.[1,4]  



## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Pralsetinib has no absolute contraindications reported in lung cancer literature, but its use is constrained by clinically significant toxicities. Interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis and infectious pneumonia are key risks; opportunistic infections (bacterial, viral, fungal, atypical) can occur within the first few months and may be life‑threatening if unrecognized.[6,9] A dedicated multicenter series and a case report of pralsetinib‑related pneumonitis emphasize the need for early evaluation of new respiratory symptoms and prompt drug interruption plus anti‑infective therapy; rechallenge is feasible in selected patients after full resolution.[7,9]

Hypertension, neutropenia, anemia, and broader myelosuppression are among the most frequent grade ≥3 adverse events in ARROW and other NSCLC cohorts, requiring close blood pressure and blood count monitoring with dose interruptions or reductions as needed.[4,8,9] Hepatotoxicity and hemorrhagic events are also highlighted in clinical and review data, with recommendations to monitor liver function and bleeding signs and to interrupt or discontinue treatment for severe events.[4,10]

Real‑world pharmacovigilance (FAERS) identifies additional cardiovascular and thromboembolic signals (e.g., troponin elevation, myocardial necrosis markers, pulmonary embolism) and neurologic effects, most often emerging within the first month, supporting intensified early surveillance.[6] Pralsetinib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4, and reviews recommend careful management of strong CYP3A modulators and polypharmacy.[8,10] NCCN‑aligned reviews and case experience underscore standard tyrosine kinase inhibitor precautions: hold therapy around major surgery for wound‑healing concerns, avoid use in pregnancy and breastfeeding, and maintain vigilance for serious infections when combining with other systemic agents.[5,8–10]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

The phase I/II ARROW trial (NCT03037385) evaluated pralsetinib 400 mg once daily in metastatic RET fusion-positive NSCLC, showing ORRs of 61% in platinum-pretreated and 70–73% in treatment-naïve patients, with durable responses and manageable toxicity including neutropenia, hypertension, and anemia.[1,3,5–8] Updated analyses reported median PFS of 13.0 months (treatment-naïve) and 16.5–17.1 months (pretreated).[3,6] The randomized phase III AcceleRET-Lung trial (NCT04222972) is comparing first-line pralsetinib to platinum-based chemotherapy ± pembrolizumab in RET-rearranged metastatic NSCLC, with PFS as the primary endpoint and completion expected in 2026.[1,3,6,7]
