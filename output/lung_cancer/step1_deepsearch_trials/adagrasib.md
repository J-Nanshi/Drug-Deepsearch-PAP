## 1. Drug Summary

Adagrasib (MRTX849; Krazati) is an oral, covalent, mutant‑selective KRAS G12C inhibitor developed for precision treatment of KRAS G12C‑mutated non‑small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). It irreversibly and selectively binds the cysteine at codon 12 in KRAS G12C within the switch‑II pocket, locking KRAS in its inactive GDP‑bound “OFF” state and thereby suppressing downstream RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK and PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling that drive proliferation and survival in KRAS‑mutant lung adenocarcinoma.[1][4][8] Adagrasib was optimized for a long half‑life (~24 hours), extensive tissue distribution, and central nervous system (CNS) penetration, supporting continuous target coverage and activity against brain metastases.[6]

The U.S. FDA granted accelerated approval to adagrasib (Krazati) in December 2022 for adult patients with KRAS G12C‑mutated locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC, as determined by an FDA‑approved test, who have received at least one prior systemic therapy.[3][9] This approval was based on the phase I/II KRYSTAL‑1 trial (NCT03785249), in which previously treated KRAS G12C‑mutated NSCLC achieved an objective response rate of 42.9% with a median duration of response of 8.5 months and median progression‑free survival (PFS) of 6.5 months on adagrasib 600 mg twice daily.[5][10] In phase 2/3 settings, adagrasib has shown clinically meaningful systemic and intracranial responses, including an intracranial objective response rate of approximately one‑third in patients with untreated brain metastases, underscoring its relevance for KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC with CNS involvement.[2][6]

## 2. Identifiers & Synonyms

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Lung Cancer Context)

Adagrasib (MRTX849) is a mutant‑selective, covalent inhibitor of KRAS G12C that targets the inactive, GDP‑bound “OFF” state of the protein. KRAS G12C arises from a glycine‑to‑cysteine substitution at codon 12, which impairs GAP‑mediated GTP hydrolysis and drives accumulation of active KRAS‑GTP, promoting downstream MAPK (RAF–MEK–ERK) and PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling in NSCLC, particularly lung adenocarcinoma where KRAS mutations occur in ~25–30% of cases and G12C accounts for ~39–40% of KRAS mutations.[1][3][4][8]  

KRAS G12C inhibitors, including adagrasib, bind covalently within the switch‑II pocket (SIIP) of GDP‑bound KRAS G12C, forming a covalent bond with Cys12 and key non‑covalent interactions (including with H95), thereby locking KRAS in its inactive conformation and preventing engagement of effector pathways.[1][2][6][8] This OFF‑state trapping suppresses RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK signaling and, to a lesser extent, PI3K–AKT signaling, leading to reduced proliferation and tumor regression in KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC models and patients.[1][6][7][8]  

In KRYSTAL‑1 and other early‑phase trials, adagrasib produced objective responses in ~45% of advanced KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC patients, confirming on‑target clinical activity.[5][8] However, co‑mutations (e.g., STK11, KEAP1, SMARCA4) and histologic plasticity (adenocarcinoma to squamous transformation) modulate dependency on KRAS and its downstream pathways, contributing to variable sensitivity and shaping subtype‑specific biology within KRAS G12C‑mutant lung adenocarcinoma.[1][6][8]  

Despite potent initial pathway suppression, adaptive and acquired resistance commonly converge on reactivation of RAS–MAPK and/or PI3K signaling via secondary KRAS mutations in the switch‑II pocket (e.g., R68S, H95D/Q/R, Y96C/D), amplification of KRAS G12C, alternative RAS isoform activation, RTK upregulation, or downstream alterations (e.g., in BRAF, MAP2K1, MET, NF1, PTEN), underscoring that adagrasib’s efficacy is tightly linked to sustained blockade of these core signaling axes in KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC.[1][5][7][8]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Adagrasib is a covalent KRAS G12C inhibitor that locks KRAS in the inactive GDP‑bound state and suppresses downstream MAPK/ERK and PI3K/AKT signaling, leading to reduced pERK in tumor biopsies and tumor regression in KRAS G12C–mutant NSCLC models.[2,5–7] However, feedback and bypass signaling rapidly re-engage multiple pathways and shape sensitivity or resistance.

Key pathways and patterns described for KRAS G12C inhibitors (including adagrasib) in lung cancer and mixed solid-tumor cohorts include:

- **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway (e.g., KEGG hsa04010):** Primary oncogenic axis; effectively downregulated by adagrasib initially, but MAPK/ERK activity often rebounds via RTK-driven reactivation and secondary KRAS/RAF/MAPK alterations, mediating acquired resistance.[2,3,5–7]  
- **PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway (e.g., KEGG hsa04151):** KRAS-dependent PI3K activation contributes to survival signaling; dual PI3K/mTOR and MEK inhibition, or co‑targeting mTOR with adagrasib, enhances antitumor activity and can overcome adaptive resistance in KRAS‑mutant NSCLC models.[1,3,4,6]  
- **RTK–SHP2–SOS1 signaling (multiple RTKs including EGFR/ERBB, MET):** Upstream RTK/SHP2/SOS1 activation restores RAS‑GTP and ERK signaling after KRAS G12C blockade; SHP2 or EGFR/HER family inhibition combined with adagrasib shows synergistic suppression of MAPK signaling and tumor growth, and clinical trials (e.g., adagrasib + TNO155, VS‑6766) are ongoing in KRAS G12C NSCLC.[1–4,6,7]  
- **MET/HGF axis:** MET amplification and HGF upregulation drive bypass RAS–ERK signaling and EMT-associated resistance to KRAS G12C inhibitors; dual MET and KRAS G12C inhibition reduces tumor growth in resistant models and is particularly relevant in NSCLC with MET amplification.[1,2,3,7]  

Overall, adagrasib sensitivity is linked to durable suppression of MAPK and PI3K signaling, whereas resistance frequently involves upregulation of RTK–SHP2–SOS1, MET, and downstream MAPK/PI3K nodes or secondary KRAS/MAPK-pathway mutations.[1–7]

## 6. Lung Cancer Subtype Evidence

Adagrasib is primarily used in advanced KRAS G12C-mutant lung adenocarcinoma, where KRYSTAL-1 and KRYSTAL-12 have shown objective response rates around 40–43% and median progression-free survival (PFS) 5.5–6.9 months, confirming meaningful activity but with heterogeneous durability across patients.[1,9,13] Histologic plasticity contributes to resistance: in KRYSTAL-1, two KRAS G12C lung adenocarcinomas that progressed on adagrasib transformed to squamous-cell carcinoma without new driver mutations, and baseline enrichment of squamous transcriptional signatures was linked to shorter benefit.[3,8,18]

Co-mutation-defined subtypes show distinct adagrasib outcomes. Large retrospective and KRYSTAL-1–based analyses indicate that KEAP1, SMARCA4 and CDKN2A co-alterations identify a poor-prognosis subgroup with shorter PFS and overall survival (OS) on KRAS G12C inhibitors, including adagrasib, whereas STK11 mutation alone does not clearly impair efficacy.[10,11,13] In contrast, STK11/KEAP1 double-mutant tumors exhibit particularly aggressive biology, high NRF2 activity, and inferior survival on adagrasib monotherapy, though preclinical work suggests that adding mTOR inhibition can partially restore sensitivity in this subset.[11,14]

Central nervous system (CNS)-metastatic KRAS G12C NSCLC represents another clinically important subtype. In the KRYSTAL-1 phase Ib CNS cohort (NCT03785249), adagrasib 600 mg twice daily produced an intracranial objective response rate of 42%, disease control rate of 90%, intracranial PFS of 5.4 months, and OS of 11.4 months in patients with untreated brain metastases, with CNS toxicity largely limited to low-grade dysgeusia and dizziness.[2] These data, together with broader KRYSTAL-1 experience, support adagrasib as a systemic and intracranial option for KRAS G12C NSCLC with CNS involvement, although resistance—often via RAS–MAPK reactivation or co-mutation–driven programs—still emerges across metastatic sites.[3,8,9,17,21]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Adagrasib (KRAZATI) has no formal contraindications in the US label; risk management relies on warnings/precautions, dose modifications, and avoidance of high‑risk concomitant drugs.[1,3] Gastrointestinal toxicity (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) is very common and often early; the label recommends close monitoring and aggressive supportive care, with withholding and resuming at reduced dose for grade ≥3 events despite optimal anti‑emetic/anti‑diarrheal therapy.[1,3] Severe GI events (including bleeding, obstruction, or intractable symptoms) are uncommon but recognized in clinical experience and may necessitate permanent discontinuation.[9]

QTc prolongation is a key practical contraindication to certain co‑medications and to use in patients with uncorrected baseline risk. The label advises avoiding other QT‑prolonging drugs, correcting hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia, and monitoring ECGs/electrolytes in at‑risk patients; QTc ≥500 ms or ≥60 ms increase from baseline requires interruption and dose reduction, while torsade de pointes or other serious arrhythmias mandate permanent discontinuation.[1,3] Because adagrasib is both a CYP3A4 substrate and perpetrator, strong CYP3A4 inducers/inhibitors and sensitive CYP3A4, CYP2C9, CYP2D6, and P‑gp substrates with narrow therapeutic index should generally be avoided or substituted.[1,3,9]

Hepatotoxicity is frequent (aminotransferase elevations in ~30–40%, ≥5×ULN in 5–7%) and occasionally severe, with a median onset around 3 weeks; early discontinuation for AST/ALT rise occurred in ~8% of trial patients.[1,9,10] The FDA label recommends liver tests at baseline and monthly for 3 months, then as clinically indicated, with stepwise dose reduction, interruption, or permanent discontinuation for grade 2–4 transaminase elevations or AST/ALT >3×ULN with bilirubin >2×ULN.[1,3,10] LiverTox highlights possible immune‑mediated patterns and greater risk when adagrasib follows recent checkpoint inhibitor therapy, supporting heightened vigilance and prompt discontinuation in symptomatic or jaundiced patients.[9]

Interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis is uncommon but potentially fatal; the label directs clinicians to withhold adagrasib for any suspected ILD/pneumonitis and to permanently discontinue if confirmed or if no alternative cause is identified.[1,3] In NSCLC practice, patients with prior treatment‑related ILD, radiation pneumonitis, or baseline significant pulmonary compromise warrant particular caution, paralleling exclusion criteria used for combination trials and reinforcing a low threshold for drug interruption and diagnostic work‑up.[15,16]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

### KRYSTAL‑1 – Phase I/II, Multi‑Cohort (NCT03785249)

KRYSTAL‑1 is an ongoing, multicenter phase I/II study of adagrasib (MRTX849) 600 mg twice daily in advanced solid tumors with KRAS G12C, including previously treated NSCLC.[1,7,9] In the pivotal phase II NSCLC cohort (116 patients; prior platinum chemotherapy and immune checkpoint inhibitor), the confirmed ORR was 42.9%, median duration of response (DOR) 8.5 months, median PFS 6.5 months, and median OS 12.6 months.[1,2,6,9,12,16] Responses were observed across PD‑L1 subgroups and in patients with common co‑mutations (STK11, KEAP1, TP53, CDKN2A).[1,6,9] Safety was dominated by gastrointestinal AEs (diarrhea, nausea, vomiting) and fatigue; grade ≥3 treatment‑related AEs occurred in ~45%, with discontinuation in ~7%.[1,6,16]

A dedicated phase Ib cohort in KRAS G12C‑mutated NSCLC with untreated, asymptomatic CNS metastases (25 patients) showed intracranial ORR 42%, intracranial disease control rate 90%, median intracranial PFS 5.4 months, and median OS 11.4 months, with CNS‑specific TRAEs mainly dysgeusia and dizziness.[4,5,9] These data established clinically meaningful CNS penetration and underpinned accelerated FDA approval of adagrasib for previously treated KRAS G12C‑mutated NSCLC.[3,6,9,19]

### KRYSTAL‑12 – Phase III Adagrasib vs Docetaxel (NCT04685135)

KRYSTAL‑12 is a randomized, open‑label phase III trial comparing adagrasib 600 mg BID with docetaxel 75 mg/m² q3w in unresectable, locally advanced or metastatic KRAS G12C‑mutated NSCLC after prior platinum chemotherapy and anti‑PD‑1/PD‑L1 therapy.[13,14] In 453 patients randomized 2:1 (301 adagrasib; 152 docetaxel), median PFS by blinded independent central review was 5.5 vs 3.8 months (HR 0.58, 95% CI 0.45–0.76; p<0.0001), with ORR 31.9% vs 9.2%.[12,14,16] Median DOR with adagrasib was ~8.3 months.[6,12] Among all patients with baseline CNS metastases, intracranial ORR was 24% with adagrasib vs 11% with docetaxel; in the CNS‑evaluable subset, intracranial ORR reached 40% vs 11%.[9,12] Grade ≥3 treatment‑related AEs occurred in 47% (adagrasib) and 46% (docetaxel), without new safety signals.[12,14,16]

### Early‑Phase Dose‑Finding and First‑Line Combination Development

The first‑in‑human KRYSTAL‑1 phase I/Ib dose‑finding segment established 600 mg BID as the recommended phase II dose based on pharmacokinetics, tolerability, and preliminary activity; in 15 evaluable NSCLC patients at this dose, ORR was 53.3% and median PFS 11.1 months.[7,8] Building on these data, KRYSTAL‑7 (NCT04613596) is a phase II trial of first‑line adagrasib plus pembrolizumab in advanced KRAS G12C‑mutant NSCLC; early reports show ORR ~63% and disease control rate 84% in PD‑L1 TPS ≥50% patients, with a manageable but higher‑grade toxicity profile, supporting ongoing phase III evaluation of this combination.[6,9,11]
