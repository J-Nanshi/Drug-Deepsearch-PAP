## Drug Summary

Trastuzumab is a humanized monoclonal IgG1 antibody targeting the HER2/ERBB2 receptor, which is amplified or overexpressed in ~15–20% of invasive breast cancers and historically conferred a high risk of relapse and death.[6][16] It was first approved in 1998 for metastatic HER2‑positive breast cancer in combination with or following chemotherapy and rapidly became the backbone of HER2‑directed therapy.[2] In early‑stage disease, four pivotal adjuvant trials (HERA, NSABP B‑31, NCCTG N9831, BCIRG‑006) established 1 year of trastuzumab plus chemotherapy as standard of care, roughly halving 3‑year recurrence risk and reducing mortality by about one third in HER2‑positive early breast cancer.[6][7][8]

Joint analyses of NSABP B‑31 and NCCTG N9831 showed that adding trastuzumab to anthracycline/taxane‑based adjuvant chemotherapy improved 10‑year disease‑free survival from 62.2% to 73.7% and overall survival from 75.2% to 84% (hazard ratio [HR] for death 0.63).[4] Benefits were consistent across nodal and hormone‑receptor subgroups, with similar long‑term recurrence‑free survival gains in both HR‑positive and HR‑negative HER2‑positive tumors.[3][6] These data underpin its use from (neo)adjuvant through metastatic settings, often in combination with taxanes and, in metastatic disease, with additional HER2‑targeted agents.[2][9]

Mechanistically, trastuzumab binds subdomain IV of the HER2 extracellular domain, blocking HER2-driven signaling, preventing proteolytic shedding of the ectodomain, and downregulating proliferative PI3K/AKT and MAPK pathways.[9] Its Fc region engages Fcγ receptor–bearing effector cells, mediating antibody‑dependent cellular cytotoxicity and phagocytosis, which are central to its antitumor activity in HER2‑overexpressing breast cancer.[9][12] In precision oncology, trastuzumab exemplifies biomarker‑guided therapy, co‑developed with HER2 testing to select patients most likely to benefit and forming the platform on which dual‑HER2 blockade and HER2‑directed antibody–drug conjugates have been built.[2][9]

## Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Trastuzumab is a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds the extracellular domain IV of HER2/ERBB2 with high affinity, blocking receptor activation and cleavage and thereby inhibiting downstream PI3K/AKT and MAPK signaling, leading to cell‑cycle arrest and reduced proliferation in HER2‑overexpressing breast cancer cells.[4][5][7] Binding promotes HER2 internalization and degradation via c‑Cbl–mediated ubiquitination, functionally downregulating receptor density at the cell surface.[7]  

Through its Fc region, trastuzumab engages activating Fcγ receptors (notably FcγRIIIa/CD16A) on NK cells and other innate immune cells, triggering antibody‑dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) and antibody‑dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP), which preferentially lyse HER2‑overexpressing tumor cells.[1][4][6–8] These mechanisms are most critical in HER2‑addicted tumors (often HER2‑enriched intrinsic subtype), where signaling blockade and immune effector recruitment are maximally effective; in luminal/HER2‑positive disease, parallel hormone‑receptor pathways can attenuate dependence on HER2, reducing relative sensitivity to trastuzumab even when HER2 is amplified.[4][5]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

- **HER2–PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling (KEGG hsa04151)**  
  HER2 activates PI3K–AKT–mTOR to drive proliferation and survival; hyperactivation via *PIK3CA* mutation, PTEN loss, or SRC enhances this axis and is a central mechanism of trastuzumab resistance in HER2‑positive breast cancer.[1,7,8] In resistant settings, AKT signaling can remain active despite HER2 blockade, supporting continued growth and survival.[1,7]

- **HER2–SRC–STAT3 / IL‑6–JAK–STAT3 axis (KEGG hsa04630; Reactome JAK–STAT R-HSA-6785807)**  
  Trastuzumab-resistant HER2‑positive tumors frequently show constitutive STAT3 activation driven by IL‑6, LIFR, or SRC, which upregulates anti‑apoptotic, cell‑cycle, and pro‑angiogenic genes and correlates with poor response to trastuzumab and T‑DM1.[3,5,6–8] IL‑6/STAT3 forms a positive feedback loop, expands cancer stem‑like cells, and is tightly linked to PTEN loss and stromal reactivation in primary HER2‑positive breast cancer.[3,6,7]

- **RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAPK) pathway (KEGG hsa04010)**  
  HER family and SRC signaling activate MAPK, promoting proliferation and survival; SRC‑driven ERK activation contributes to trastuzumab resistance by sustaining downstream mitogenic signaling.[7,8] MAPK is also engaged downstream of IL‑6/JAK/STAT3 and other cytokines, integrating growth factor and inflammatory inputs in resistant HER2‑positive disease.[4,6]

- **Notch–Jagged–STAT3 cancer stem cell pathway (KEGG hsa04330)**  
  In HER2‑positive breast cancer, trastuzumab downregulation of membrane HER2 increases Jagged‑1 availability and Notch activation, which supports cancer stem cell survival and renewal.[2,7] Notch signaling feeds into STAT3 and IL‑6 loops, driving EMT, stemness, and primary or acquired trastuzumab resistance.[1–3,7]

- **Cell‑cycle regulation: cyclin D/CDK–Myc–E2F programs (MSigDB HALLMARK_E2F_TARGETS)**  
  STAT3 and SRC/PI3K signaling upregulate cyclin D1, c‑Myc, CDC2 and related genes, promoting G1–S transition and mitosis in HER2‑positive breast cancer.[3,7,8] Trastuzumab resistance is associated with persistent expression of these cell‑cycle drivers despite HER2 blockade, supporting unchecked proliferation.[3,7,8]

- **Apoptosis and survival signaling (KEGG hsa04210)**  
  IL‑6/STAT3 and SRC/PI3K pathways increase anti‑apoptotic proteins (BCL‑xL, MCL‑1, survivin) and reduce caspase‑mediated death, conferring broad chemoresistance in breast cancer, including to trastuzumab‑based regimens.[3,4,6–8] STAT3 inhibition restores apoptosis and re‑sensitizes resistant HER2‑positive cells to T‑DM1 and trastuzumab in preclinical models.[3,5,6,8]

- **TNFα–NF‑κB–MUC4 signaling (KEGG hsa04668)**  
  TNFα‑driven NF‑κB upregulates MUC4, which sterically hinders trastuzumab–HER2 binding and reduces ADCC, mediating primary and acquired resistance in HER2‑positive breast and gastric cancer.[7] Targeting TNFα or MUC4 can restore trastuzumab sensitivity and improve immune‑mediated killing in resistant tumors.[7]

- **Hippo–YAP1 pathway (KEGG hsa04390)**  
  Inactivation of Hippo signaling with nuclear YAP1–TEAD activation promotes proliferation and has been implicated as a driver of acquired trastuzumab resistance in HER2‑positive breast cancer.[7] YAP1 overactivity is proposed as a biomarker of resistance and a potential target to enhance trastuzumab efficacy.[7]

## Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HR+/HER2– breast cancer, trastuzumab is not indicated because its benefit is tightly linked to HER2 overexpression or gene amplification, whereas most luminal tumors are HER2‑negative by ASCO/CAP criteria.[1][4] Large adjuvant trials and meta‑analyses show trastuzumab’s effect is confined to HER2‑positive disease; HR status modifies prognosis but not the requirement for HER2 amplification.[4][6][7] Consequently, in HR+/HER2– tumors, precision oncology directs treatment toward endocrine and other pathway‑targeted agents rather than HER2 blockade.

In HER2‑positive disease, trastuzumab plus chemotherapy substantially reduces recurrence and mortality and improves overall survival in both early and metastatic settings, with benefit independent of nodal status and tumor size down to at least T1b–c.[3][4][6][7] Only tumors with clear HER2 overexpression/amplification (IHC 3+ or ISH‑positive) derive this benefit; HER2‑low (IHC 1+/2+ ISH‑negative) tumors do not benefit from adjuvant trastuzumab in NSABP B‑47, underscoring HER2 positivity as the key predictive biomarker.[1][6][23] Within HER2‑positive cancers, activation of the PI3K/AKT pathway via PTEN loss or PIK3CA mutations is associated with lower pathologic complete response rates and shorter survival on trastuzumab‑based regimens, highlighting a molecularly defined resistant subset.[10][13–15]

Triple‑negative breast cancer, defined by absent ER, PR, and HER2 expression, lacks the target for trastuzumab and therefore does not benefit from trastuzumab‑based therapy.[19–21][24] TNBC management instead relies on chemotherapy, PARP inhibition for gBRCA‑mutated disease, ADCs, and immunotherapy, with ongoing efforts to exploit other actionable pathways.[18–20][24] Although a minority of TNBCs show HER2‑low expression, phase III data (NSABP B‑47) do not support trastuzumab in HER2‑low disease, so HER2‑directed therapy is not standard in TNBC outside trials.[17][23]

In gBRCA‑mutated breast cancer, the dominant precision‑oncology strategy is PARP inhibition and platinum‑based chemotherapy, particularly in HER2‑negative tumors that are frequently triple‑negative.[18][20][24] BRCA1/2 status alone does not predict trastuzumab benefit; HER2 amplification remains the decisive biomarker, and gBRCA‑mutated tumors that are HER2– are managed according to TNBC or HR+ HER2– paradigms rather than with trastuzumab.[19–21][24]

## Contraindications and Safety

Trastuzumab has no formal contraindications in the U.S. label, but carries a boxed warning for cardiomyopathy, infusion reactions, embryo‑fetal toxicity, and pulmonary toxicity.[4,8] Cardiotoxicity can present as asymptomatic LVEF decline or overt CHF, with highest incidence when given concurrently with anthracyclines; cardiac function must be evaluated before and regularly during treatment, and therapy discontinued (adjuvant) or withheld (metastatic) for clinically significant LVEF decline.[4,7,8]

Serious infusion reactions (rigors, hypotension, dyspnea) and anaphylaxis are uncommon but can be fatal; they occur almost exclusively during or shortly after infusion and mandate interruption or permanent discontinuation for severe reactions.[4,6,8,13] Pulmonary toxicity—including interstitial pneumonitis, pulmonary fibrosis, ARDS, and fatal events—is rare (~0.5% in large adjuvant trials) but life‑threatening; symptoms (new dyspnea, cough, hypoxia) require prompt evaluation, drug discontinuation, and corticosteroid treatment when drug‑induced pneumonitis is suspected.[2,4,8,10,12]

Trastuzumab exposure during pregnancy can cause oligohydramnios, pulmonary hypoplasia, skeletal abnormalities, and neonatal death; pregnancy status must be verified before starting therapy, and effective contraception maintained during treatment and for at least 7 months after the last dose.[4,6–8] Overall, guideline‑concordant care requires baseline and serial LVEF assessment, vigilant monitoring during infusions, and a low threshold to investigate and manage respiratory symptoms.

## Key Clinical Trials

The HERA trial (NCT00045032) showed that 1 year of adjuvant trastuzumab after standard chemotherapy in node‑positive or high‑risk node‑negative HER2‑positive early breast cancer improved 10‑year disease‑free survival by 6–8% and overall survival by 6.5–6.6% versus observation, establishing 12‑month trastuzumab as standard duration.[3] The joint NSABP B‑31 (NCT00004067) and NCCTG N9831 (NCT00005970) analysis demonstrated that adding trastuzumab to paclitaxel after anthracyclines reduced the relative risk of a disease‑free survival event by 40% and death by 37% compared with chemotherapy alone.[3] In BCIRG‑006 (NCT00021255), incorporating trastuzumab into either anthracycline‑taxane or docetaxel–carboplatin regimens significantly improved disease‑free and overall survival versus anthracycline‑taxane alone, confirming durable reductions in recurrence and mortality.[10]
