## 1. Drug Summary

Sacituzumab govitecan (SG; Trodelvy) is a Trop‑2–directed antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) approved in multiple countries for adults with unresectable locally advanced or metastatic triple‑negative breast cancer (mTNBC) after at least two prior systemic therapies (including ≥1 for metastatic disease) and for hormone receptor–positive/HER2‑negative (HR+/HER2–) metastatic breast cancer after endocrine therapy and at least two prior systemic regimens in the metastatic setting.[1],[4],[7],[8] These indications are supported by the phase III ASCENT trial in pretreated mTNBC (NCT02574455) and the phase III TROPiCS‑02 trial in endocrine‑resistant HR+/HER2– disease (NCT03901339).[1],[3],[7],[8]

SG consists of a humanized anti–Trop‑2 IgG1 (hRS7) conjugated via a cleavable CL2A linker to SN‑38, the active metabolite of irinotecan and a topoisomerase I inhibitor, at a high drug‑to‑antibody ratio of ~7.5–8:1.[4],[5],[11],[14],[15] After binding Trop‑2—overexpressed in >90% of breast cancers and associated with aggressive biology—SG is internalized and/or releases SN‑38 in the tumor microenvironment, inducing DNA damage–mediated apoptosis with a bystander effect on neighboring Trop‑2–low/negative cells.[5],[11],[12],[14],[15] Compared with irinotecan, SG delivers up to ~20–136‑fold higher intratumoral SN‑38 exposure while maintaining manageable systemic toxicity.[11],[15]

Within precision oncology, SG exemplifies antigen‑directed chemotherapy delivery that is agnostic to HER2 status but leverages Trop‑2 biology across breast cancer subtypes. In ASCENT and TROPiCS‑02, SG significantly improved progression‑free and overall survival versus physician’s‑choice single‑agent chemotherapy, with consistent benefit across Trop‑2 expression strata, establishing SG as a key later‑line targeted ADC for both mTNBC and endocrine‑resistant HR+/HER2– metastatic breast cancer.[1],[3],[5],[7],[8],[14]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Sacituzumab govitecan (SG) is a TROP2‑directed antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) that couples the humanized anti‑TROP2 antibody hRS7 to the topoisomerase I (TOP1) inhibitor SN‑38 via a hydrolysable CL2A linker, with a high drug‑to‑antibody ratio of approximately seven SN‑38 molecules per antibody.[4] TROP2 (encoded by *TACSTD2*) is a 36‑kDa transmembrane glycoprotein involved in intracellular signaling and is broadly overexpressed in epithelial cancers, including breast cancer, where it is associated with aggressive biology.[1,4] In metastatic triple‑negative breast cancer (mTNBC), TROP2 expression and focal *TACSTD2* amplification correlate with deep and prolonged responses to SG, whereas complete absence of TROP2 is associated with primary resistance.[1,2]

After intravenous administration, SG binds cell‑surface TROP2, is internalized, and undergoes intracellular cleavage of the CL2A linker, releasing SN‑38; the same hydrolysable linker also allows extracellular SN‑38 liberation in the tumor microenvironment, mediating a bystander effect on neighboring TROP2‑low or TROP2‑negative cells.[4] SN‑38 stabilizes the TOP1–DNA cleavage complex, leading to replication‑associated double‑strand breaks, replication stress, and apoptosis; in breast cancer, acquired resistance can emerge through *TOP1* E418K and frameshift mutations that impair SN‑38 binding, or *TACSTD2/TROP2* T256R mutations that disrupt TROP2 membrane localization and hRS7 binding, illustrating that both antigen and payload target integrity are critical for SG activity.[1,2] Because many TNBCs and a subset of HR‑positive/HER2‑negative breast cancers exhibit homologous recombination deficiency, they are particularly vulnerable to TOP1‑mediated DNA damage, and sequential combination strategies with PARP inhibitors such as talazoparib further exploit this vulnerability by prolonging TOP1 cleavage complex persistence and enhancing DNA damage and apoptosis in mTNBC models and patients.[5–8]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), sacituzumab govitecan (SG) is an established later-line standard. In the phase I/II IMMU‑132‑01 basket study, heavily pretreated metastatic TNBC achieved an objective response rate (ORR) of 33.3%, median progression-free survival (PFS) 5.5 months, and overall survival (OS) 13.0 months, which led to accelerated approval and was confirmed in the phase III ASCENT trial with substantial PFS/OS gains over physician’s-choice chemotherapy.[7][13][23] Benefit in ASCENT was consistent across prespecified subgroups and evident even in patients with low Trop‑2 expression or germline *BRCA1/2* mutations, although numbers in these biomarker-defined subsets were small.[20][23]

For HR+/HER2– metastatic breast cancer, SG has demonstrated clinically meaningful activity in a setting of endocrine and chemotherapy resistance. In IMMU‑132‑01, the HR+/HER2– cohort showed ORR 31.5%, median PFS 5.5 months, and median OS 12 months despite ≥2 prior metastatic regimens.[7] The phase III TROPiCS‑02 trial (NCT03901339) then showed superior PFS (hazard ratio [HR] 0.66) and OS (14.4 vs 11.2 months; HR 0.79) versus single‑agent chemotherapy, with improved quality of life and consistent benefit irrespective of Trop‑2 expression level.[9][12][18] The Asian EVER‑132‑002 trial (NCT04639986) confirmed PFS and OS benefit in HR+/HER2– patients, supporting generalizability across populations.[15]

In HER2‑positive disease, HER2‑targeted antibody–drug conjugates dominate, and current Trop‑2–directed SG development focuses on HER2‑negative settings; available reviews discuss SG for TNBC and HR+/HER2– disease rather than HER2‑amplified tumors.[3][13][19] Across both TNBC and HR+/HER2– subtypes, exploratory analyses from ASCENT and TROPiCS‑02 indicate that SG efficacy does not require high Trop‑2 expression and appears largely independent of germline *BRCA* status, positioning SG as a broadly applicable, later‑line ADC option once standard targeted and endocrine therapies have been exhausted.[18][20][23]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Sacituzumab govitecan (SG, Trodelvy) carries a boxed warning for severe or life‑threatening neutropenia and diarrhea.[4],[5],[14] In ASCENT and TROPiCS‑02, any‑grade neutropenia occurred in ~60–63% and grade ≥3 neutropenia in ~48–51%, with febrile neutropenia in ~5–6%; diarrhea occurred in ~56–62% (grade ≥3 ~9–10%).[7],[13],[14] FDA labeling mandates withholding SG for ANC <1500/mm³ or neutropenic fever, periodic CBC monitoring, and considering G‑CSF for secondary prophylaxis, with stepwise 25–50% dose reductions for recurrent severe neutropenia or uncontrolled non‑hematologic toxicity.[4],[5] The PRIMED phase II trial (NCT05520723) showed that primary G‑CSF plus loperamide from cycle 1 reduced early grade ≥3 neutropenia to 16% and eliminated febrile neutropenia, supporting proactive prophylaxis in high‑risk HER2‑negative advanced breast cancer.[3],[6]

Severe diarrhea is the second boxed‑warning toxicity. Labels recommend atropine for early cholinergic diarrhea when not contraindicated, prompt evaluation for infection at onset of late diarrhea, aggressive loperamide‑based treatment, fluid/electrolyte replacement, and holding SG until recovery to ≤grade 1, with dose reduction for recurrent or uncontrolled events.[4],[5],[14] Patients should be instructed to report fever, signs of infection, or dehydration urgently.[14],[18]

Patients with reduced UGT1A1 activity—especially *UGT1A1* *28/*28 or *6*/*6—have substantially higher rates of severe neutropenia, anemia, and diarrhea. In pooled phase III data, grade ≥3 neutropenia reached 61% and febrile neutropenia 12% in *28/*28 carriers versus 49% and 4% in wild type; meta‑analysis estimates an odds ratio ~7 for combined severe toxicities and increased need for dose reductions and interruptions.[7],[13] FDA and MHRA labels advise close monitoring and early dose modification in suspected poor metabolizers but stop short of mandating pre‑treatment genotyping, whereas pharmacogenomic analyses argue that pre‑therapeutic *UGT1A1* testing could support individualized starting doses and intensified prophylaxis.[7],[13],[16]

Severe hypersensitivity or anaphylactic reaction to SG is a formal contraindication.[4],[5] Premedication with antipyretics and H1/H2 antagonists and post‑infusion observation are recommended, with permanent discontinuation after life‑threatening reactions.[4],[18] Hepatic safety is generally favorable: transient aminotransferase elevations occur in up to one‑third of patients, but clinically apparent jaundicing liver injury has not been convincingly documented; nonetheless, caution and enhanced monitoring are advised in patients with significant baseline hepatic impairment.[1] Embryo‑fetal toxicity is expected from the SN‑38 payload; women of reproductive potential should use effective contraception during treatment and for six months afterward, and men with female partners for three months; breastfeeding is not recommended.[4],[5],[14]
