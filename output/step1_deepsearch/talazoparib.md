## 1. Drug Summary

Talazoparib is a highly potent, orally available inhibitor of poly(ADP‑ribose) polymerases PARP1 and PARP2, approved in 2018 by the US FDA for adults with HER2‑negative locally advanced or metastatic breast cancer harboring deleterious or suspected deleterious germline BRCA mutations.[9] In metastatic breast cancer, talazoparib is positioned as a precision therapy for germline BRCA1/2‑mutated, HER2‑negative disease after prior chemotherapy, offering an alternative to single‑agent cytotoxic regimens. In the phase III EMBRACA trial (NCT01945775), talazoparib significantly prolonged progression‑free survival versus physician’s choice chemotherapy (8.6 vs 5.6 months) in this population and improved objective response rates, establishing it as a standard targeted option in gBRCA‑mutated HER2‑negative metastatic breast cancer.[7,9]

Mechanistically, talazoparib exemplifies the PARP‑inhibitor class by blocking PARP1/2 catalytic activity and exerting very strong “PARP‑trapping,” stabilizing PARP–DNA complexes at sites of single‑strand breaks.[1,4,7,9] PARP inhibition prevents efficient base‑excision repair, so unrepaired single‑strand lesions are converted into replication‑associated double‑strand breaks. In tumors with homologous recombination repair deficiency—classically due to BRCA1/2 loss—these double‑strand breaks cannot be accurately repaired, leading to replication fork collapse, genomic instability, and cell death via synthetic lethality.[1,5,7,9] Talazoparib has 50–100‑fold higher PARP‑trapping potency than several other PARP inhibitors, which correlates with strong antitumor activity but also a higher risk of myelosuppression.[1,4,8,9] As a result, talazoparib is a paradigmatic biomarker‑driven agent in precision oncology, exploiting BRCA‑associated homologous recombination deficiency in HER2‑negative breast cancer.

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Talazoparib is a highly potent, selective oral inhibitor of PARP1 and PARP2 that both catalytically inhibits PARP enzymatic activity and strongly traps PARP–DNA complexes at sites of damage.[9],[16],[22] PARP1/2 normally detect single‑strand DNA breaks (SSBs) and catalyze PARylation to recruit base‑excision repair factors; inhibition by talazoparib prevents PARylation, leading to persistence of SSBs that are converted into replication‑associated double‑strand breaks (DSBs) during S phase.[3],[7],[9],[22] Because DSB repair then depends on homologous recombination (HR), tumors with HR deficiency—classically due to germline *BRCA1/2* mutations—are exquisitely sensitive, a synthetic‑lethal interaction that underpins talazoparib’s efficacy in HER2‑negative, g*BRCA*‑mutated breast cancer.[2],[7],[9],[11],[14],[16],[22]

Compared with other PARP inhibitors, talazoparib is distinguished by exceptionally strong PARP‑trapping potency—approximately 100‑fold higher than niraparib and greater than olaparib or rucaparib—making trapped PARP–DNA complexes potent roadblocks to replication forks and amplifying DSB formation and cytotoxicity.[1],[3],[6],[9],[11],[16],[22] This high trapping efficiency is a major driver of its superior in‑vitro antitumor activity (20–200‑fold more active than several other PARP inhibitors) but also contributes to a toxicity profile with prominent myelosuppression, resembling cytotoxic chemotherapy more than weaker trappers.[1],[6],[11],[16],[22]

In breast cancer, talazoparib’s synthetic‑lethal mechanism is most clearly validated in HER2‑negative, germline *BRCA1/2*‑mutated disease, where phase III data (EMBRACA) showed significantly improved progression‑free survival versus physician’s‑choice chemotherapy, across both triple‑negative and hormone‑receptor–positive subgroups.[9],[11],[14],[16] HR deficiency, however, extends beyond *BRCA*; genomic analyses demonstrate HRD signatures in ~20% of breast cancers, including *PALB2*, *RAD51* paralog and other HRR gene alterations, and a subset of *BRCA*‑wild‑type tumors with “BRCAness,” supporting sensitivity to PARP inhibition in broader molecular subsets.[10],[11],[14] In triple‑negative breast cancer (TNBC), where germline *BRCA1* mutations are enriched and HRD is common, talazoparib monotherapy has produced high pathologic complete response rates in the neoadjuvant setting and meaningful activity in metastatic disease.[9],[11],[16] In HR‑positive/HER2‑negative tumors, g*BRCA2* and other HRR alterations similarly create dependence on PARP‑mediated SSB repair, explaining benefit from talazoparib despite concurrent endocrine‑driven biology.[9],[11],[14]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In gBRCA‑mutated HR+/HER2– advanced breast cancer, talazoparib is approved as monotherapy for HER2‑negative, locally advanced/metastatic disease after prior chemotherapy, with PARP inhibitors recommended after failure of endocrine‑based regimens.[9][11][16] In EMBRACA, progression‑free survival (PFS) and objective response rate (ORR) benefits over physician’s‑choice chemotherapy were consistent across hormone‑receptor subgroups, indicating maintained efficacy in HR+ tumors despite lack of overall survival (OS) advantage.[9][21] Guidelines position PARP inhibitors as a key targeted option in HR+/HER2– gBRCA disease once endocrine sensitivity is exhausted.[9][11]

Talazoparib is not approved in HER2‑positive breast cancer, and current evidence is limited to broader PARP‑inhibitor concepts rather than subtype‑specific talazoparib data; HER2+ disease remains managed with HER2‑targeted agents and chemotherapy.[4][11][14]

In gBRCA‑mutated TNBC, talazoparib shows particularly strong activity. In EMBRACA, TNBC subgroups derived similar relative PFS and ORR gains versus chemotherapy as the overall population, with hazard ratios favoring talazoparib and high response rates.[9][21] TNBC is enriched for gBRCA1 mutations and homologous‑recombination deficiency, and PARP inhibition is now a guideline‑endorsed option for gBRCA‑mutated metastatic TNBC alongside platinum and immunotherapy in appropriate biomarker‑defined settings.[8][9][11]

Across HER2‑negative subtypes, benefit from talazoparib is concentrated in germline BRCA‑mutated tumors. Exploratory data suggest PARP inhibitors, including talazoparib, may have activity in tumors with non‑BRCA homologous‑recombination repair gene alterations, but these “beyond‑BRCA” applications remain investigational and are not yet integrated into routine subtype‑specific practice.[9][10][19][21]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Talazoparib has no formal contraindications in the current FDA labeling; risk management relies on boxed/major warnings and patient‑specific factors.[2],[4],[11] The key serious risks are myelodysplastic syndrome/acute myeloid leukemia (MDS/AML), myelosuppression, renal impairment–related overexposure, and embryo‑fetal toxicity. MDS/AML has been reported in <1% of solid‑tumor patients treated with talazoparib, including fatal cases; treatment should not start until recovery from prior chemotherapy‑induced cytopenias, and must be permanently discontinued if MDS/AML is confirmed.[2],[4],[11],[12]

Myelosuppression (anemia, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia) is the dominant on‑treatment toxicity. In EMBRACA, 68.2% of talazoparib‑treated patients had hematologic AEs, with grade 3–4 anemia in 39.2%; events clustered in the first 3–4 months and were usually reversible with dose interruptions, reductions, and transfusional or growth‑factor support, with <2% discontinuing for hematologic toxicity.[9],[10],[13] FDA labeling mandates baseline and monthly complete blood counts, with treatment holds for hemoglobin <8 g/dL, platelets <50,000/µL, or neutrophils <1,000/µL and resumption at reduced dose once thresholds recover.[1],[2],[4],[11]

Talazoparib is primarily renally eliminated; moderate (creatinine clearance 30–59 mL/min) and severe (15–29 mL/min) renal impairment require starting‑dose reductions and closer safety monitoring.[1],[2],[4],[11] Hepatic impairment has minimal impact on pharmacokinetics, but transient aminotransferase elevations occur in about one‑third of patients and warrant interruption if >5× upper limit of normal or if accompanied by symptoms or jaundice.[1],[2],[11],[12]

Talazoparib can cause embryo‑fetal harm; women of reproductive potential must use effective contraception during therapy and for at least 7 months after the last dose, and men with partners of reproductive potential for at least 4 months.[1],[2],[4],[11],[12] Breastfeeding is contraindicated during treatment.[1],[2],[4],[11] Co‑administration with strong P‑gp inhibitors requires talazoparib dose reduction and enhanced toxicity monitoring; BCRP inhibitors warrant vigilance for increased adverse reactions.[1],[2],[4],[11]
