## 1. Drug Summary

Exemestane is an oral aromatase inhibitor used as endocrine therapy for estrogen receptor–positive (ER+) breast cancer, particularly in postmenopausal women.[3] Within endocrine therapy, aromatase inhibitors such as anastrozole, exemestane, and letrozole reduce estrogen synthesis and thereby inhibit growth of hormone‑dependent (luminal A/B) breast cancers that rely on estrogen receptor signaling.[3] 

Pharmacologically, exemestane is a steroidal aromatase inhibitor that suppresses conversion of androgens to estrogens, leading to profound estrogen deprivation in postmenopausal patients; it is commonly used as part of adjuvant or advanced‑disease endocrine strategies in HR‑positive/HER2‑negative breast cancer, often in combination or sequence with other endocrine agents and targeted therapies.[3,5] In the context of modern treatment, exemestane also serves as the endocrine backbone in combinations with targeted agents such as everolimus, although real‑world data suggest that everolimus plus exemestane may be suboptimal after prior CDK4/6 inhibitor exposure in HR‑positive/HER2‑negative disease.[5]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Exemestane is a third‑generation, selective, irreversible steroidal aromatase inhibitor (“aromatase inactivator”) developed for endocrine treatment of postmenopausal breast cancer.[2,3,8] Aromatase is the rate‑limiting cytochrome P450 enzyme that catalyzes the final step of estrogen biosynthesis from androgens; in postmenopausal women, this occurs predominantly in peripheral tissues (e.g., adipose) and within breast tumors, where locally produced estrogens drive growth of hormone‑dependent cancers.[1,2,5,8] Exemestane is structurally related to androgens and behaves as a pseudo‑substrate: it is bound and processed by aromatase to a reactive intermediate that covalently and time‑dependently inactivates the enzyme, producing sustained, mechanism‑based (“suicide”) inhibition rather than simple competitive, reversible blockade.[5,8]

This irreversible inactivation leads to profound and prolonged suppression of circulating estradiol levels in postmenopausal women with ER‑positive breast cancer, with clinical studies confirming marked reductions in blood estrogen concentrations.[2,7,8] By depleting estrogen, exemestane functionally starves ER‑positive/HR‑positive tumor cells of the ligand required for estrogen‑receptor–mediated transcription, thereby inhibiting proliferation and promoting tumor regression or stabilization in both advanced and early‑stage disease.[1–3,7,10] Because its steroidal, covalent binding site differs from that of non‑steroidal aromatase inhibitors, partial non–cross‑resistance is observed: patients progressing on anastrozole or letrozole may still respond to exemestane, and vice versa, supporting a distinct, enzyme‑level mechanism that can be exploited sequentially in ER+/HR+ breast cancer.[1,5–7]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence (Exemestane)

In HR+/HER2– disease, exemestane functions as a key aromatase inhibitor backbone within endocrine therapy. In the adjuvant setting, combining exemestane with ovarian function suppression (OFS) in premenopausal women improves 12‑year disease‑free survival versus tamoxifen plus OFS, with the greatest absolute benefit in high‑risk, chemotherapy‑treated patients.[3] In metastatic HR+/HER2– breast cancer, exemestane is typically partnered with targeted agents (e.g. everolimus or CDK4/6 inhibitors) after progression on prior endocrine lines, reflecting its continued utility but also the central problem of endocrine resistance.[1,5,14] Biomarker‑driven sequencing (e.g. PIK3CA, ESR1, gBRCA) increasingly determines whether exemestane‑based combinations are used versus switching to SERDs, PI3K/AKT/mTOR or PARP inhibitors.[1,7,12,14]

For HER2‑positive tumors, endocrine therapy (including AIs such as exemestane) is mainly relevant in HR+/HER2+ (“luminal B HER2+”) disease, but evidence is dominated by combinations of CDK4/6 and HER2‑targeted agents rather than specific exemestane‑containing regimens.[2,4,5,11] HER2‑driven signaling and frequent PI3K/AKT/mTOR activation reduce reliance on aromatase inhibition alone, so chemotherapy plus anti‑HER2 therapy remains standard, with endocrine ± CDK4/6 inhibitors reserved for more indolent or maintenance settings.[2,5,11] Consequently, exemestane is generally a secondary partner rather than a primary driver of disease control in this subtype.

In triple‑negative breast cancer (TNBC), which by definition lacks ER and PR expression, exemestane has no therapeutic role. TNBC biology is dominated by defects in DNA‑damage repair, TP53 alterations, and immune‑inflammatory pathways, and current precision strategies prioritize chemotherapy, immune checkpoint inhibitors, PARP inhibitors, and antibody–drug conjugates rather than endocrine agents.[5,7,11,13] Any apparent “response” to exemestane in this context would usually reflect misclassification or receptor conversion rather than true TNBC sensitivity.

In gBRCA‑mutated, HER2‑negative advanced breast cancer, PARP inhibitors (olaparib, talazoparib) are now preferred over chemotherapy because they improve progression‑free survival and quality of life in both HR+ and TNBC cohorts.[7,13,14] Many gBRCA‑mutated tumors are HR+, so patients often receive standard AI‑based adjuvant or first‑line metastatic endocrine therapy (including exemestane) before or between PARP inhibitor lines, but there is no evidence that BRCA status specifically enhances exemestane sensitivity.[7,12–14] Instead, BRCA‑directed strategies (PARP inhibition, platinum chemotherapy) and, in later lines, ADCs and PI3K/AKT/mTOR inhibitors now shape sequencing, with exemestane occupying a conventional endocrine slot rather than a BRCA‑tailored intervention.[7,11–14]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Exemestane is indicated for estrogen receptor–positive breast cancer in postmenopausal women; pivotal adjuvant and metastatic trials required natural or induced menopause, with premenopausal women only treated when rendered functionally menopausal by ovarian suppression.[9][12][18] In prevention and high‑risk settings, use has likewise been restricted to postmenopausal women, underscoring menopausal status as a key eligibility criterion.[9][13][18] Exemestane is generally well tolerated, with adverse events dominated by menopausal-type symptoms (hot flashes, arthralgia, fatigue) and musculoskeletal complaints.[10][13]

Bone health is the central long‑term safety concern. MAP.3 and its bone substudy showed that 2 years of exemestane 25 mg daily in healthy postmenopausal women accelerated age‑related bone loss, with significantly greater declines in volumetric and areal bone mineral density at distal radius, tibia, spine, hip, and femoral neck versus placebo despite calcium/vitamin D supplementation.[11] Network meta‑analysis and large adjuvant trials suggest steroidal exemestane may be associated with somewhat lower rates of fractures, osteoporosis, and bone mineral density reduction than nonsteroidal aromatase inhibitors, but clinically relevant bone loss and fractures still occur.[9][14][15] Expert and trial authors recommend baseline fracture risk assessment, periodic bone monitoring, and routine calcium/vitamin D; bone‑directed therapy should be considered in women with low BMD or multiple risk factors.[11][14][18]

Cardiovascular and metabolic effects require vigilance. Comparative endocrine therapy data indicate that, versus tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors (including exemestane) are associated with a higher incidence of fractures and a potentially higher rate of cardiovascular events, whereas tamoxifen confers cardioprotective and lipid‑lowering effects.[9][16] MA.27 reported broadly similar overall cardiovascular event rates between exemestane and anastrozole, with fewer hypertriglyceridemia and hypercholesterolemia events on exemestane but rare atrial fibrillation more frequent on anastrozole.[14] Contemporary cardio‑oncology reviews emphasize that profound estrogen deprivation with aromatase inhibition can increase long‑term cardiovascular risk, particularly in women with pre‑existing cardiovascular disease, warranting structured assessment and management of lipids, blood pressure, and global risk during prolonged therapy.[17][18]

Pregnancy and lactation are contraindicated contexts for aromatase inhibition; trials of exemestane in adjuvant, metastatic, and prevention settings have been restricted to postmenopausal women, and profound estrogen suppression poses theoretical teratogenic and fetal developmental risks.[9][10][12][13] Across settings, safe use of exemestane in breast oncology hinges on: (1) restricting treatment to appropriately postmenopausal (or ovarian‑suppressed) patients; (2) proactive bone health management; and (3) systematic monitoring and optimization of cardiovascular and metabolic risk factors to support long‑term adherence and minimize toxicity.[9–11][14–18]
