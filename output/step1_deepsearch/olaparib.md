## 1. Drug Summary

Olaparib is an oral poly(ADP‑ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitor (PARP1/2) and was the first-in-class PARP inhibitor approved in the USA for patients with deleterious or suspected deleterious germline BRCA‑mutated (gBRCAm), HER2‑negative metastatic breast cancer who have received prior chemotherapy in the neoadjuvant, adjuvant, or metastatic setting; HR‑positive patients must also have received, or be inappropriate for, endocrine therapy.[1] It is now additionally licensed (e.g., following OlympiA) as 1‑year adjuvant therapy for high‑risk, HER2‑negative early breast cancer with germline BRCA1/2 mutations after completion of local therapy and (neo)adjuvant chemotherapy.[3],[10]

Mechanistically, olaparib blocks PARP‑mediated base‑excision repair of single‑strand DNA breaks and traps PARP1/2 on DNA, leading to replication fork collapse and double‑strand breaks.[2],[3] In tumors with homologous recombination repair deficiency—classically due to BRCA1/2 loss—these double‑strand breaks cannot be accurately repaired, creating a synthetic lethal interaction that selectively kills HR‑deficient cancer cells while sparing HR‑proficient normal tissues.[2],[3],[8] This dependence on DNA repair genotype underpins olaparib’s role as a precision oncology agent: treatment is restricted to HER2‑negative breast cancers with germline BRCA1/2 mutations, and broader homologous recombination deficiency (“BRCAness”) is being explored in trials.[3],[7],[12]

In the phase III OlympiAD trial, olaparib monotherapy improved progression‑free survival versus physician’s‑choice single‑agent chemotherapy in gBRCAm, HER2‑negative metastatic breast cancer, with a 2.8‑month longer median PFS and a 42% reduction in risk of progression.[3],[8],[12] The phase III OlympiA trial (NCT02032823) showed that 1 year of adjuvant olaparib significantly improved 3‑year invasive disease‑free survival (85.9% vs 77.1%) and distant recurrence‑free survival in high‑risk, HER2‑negative early breast cancer with germline BRCA mutations, establishing adjuvant PARP inhibition as a new standard and reinforcing the need for systematic germline BRCA testing in eligible patients.[3],[8],[9],[10],[12]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Olaparib is an oral inhibitor of PARP1 and PARP2 that competes with NAD⁺ at the catalytic site, blocking poly(ADP‑ribosyl)ation and thereby impairing base excision repair (BER) of single‑strand breaks (SSBs).[2,3,4,7,9,12] Inhibition of PARP1/2 prevents auto‑PARylation and release of PARP from DNA, so PARP–DNA complexes remain bound (“PARP trapping”), which stalls replication forks, promotes fork collapse and markedly increases replication‑associated double‑strand breaks (DSBs).[3,7,8,10,12] In HR‑proficient cells, these DSBs are largely repaired by high‑fidelity homologous recombination (HR), coordinated by BRCA1 as a scaffold and BRCA2‑mediated loading of RAD51 onto resected DNA ends.[4,7,9]

In BRCA1/2‑deficient breast cancers, HR is compromised, so DSBs arising from unrepaired SSBs and trapped PARP complexes cannot be accurately repaired and are shunted to error‑prone non‑homologous end joining (NHEJ), driving chromosomal aberrations and cell death— the canonical synthetic lethal interaction between PARP loss and HR deficiency.[1,3,4,5,7,8,9,12] This synthetic lethality is most clearly demonstrated in BRCA1/2‑mutant breast cancer models, where homozygous BRCA1/2 loss renders cells “extremely sensitive” to PARP inhibition, while PARP1‑knockout normal cells survive via compensatory HR.[1,4,5,12] Clinically, tumors with germline BRCA1/2 mutations (gBRCA) and broader HR‑deficient (“BRCAness”) phenotypes—through alterations in PALB2, RAD51C/D, PTEN or BRCA1 promoter methylation—show enriched sensitivity to olaparib, although not all HR‑deficient tumors respond and resistance frequently emerges.[1,3,5–7,9,11,12]

In HER2‑negative breast cancer, this mechanism underlies the selective activity of olaparib across both triple‑negative and HR‑positive gBRCA‑mutated subtypes: preclinical work shows basal/TNBC and BRCA1‑mutant lines share BER defects and heightened PARP dependence, while clinical trials (e.g., OlympiAD) demonstrate benefit irrespective of hormone‑receptor status so long as gBRCA mutations are present.[1,6,7,8] At a systems level, PARP inhibition in HR‑deficient breast cancer produces replication‑fork collapse, accumulation of single‑stranded DNA gaps, and catastrophic genomic instability, whereas normal HR‑competent tissues largely tolerate PARP blockade—creating the therapeutic window that underpins olaparib’s use in precision oncology.[3,7,9,12]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HER2‑negative, hormone‑receptor–positive metastatic breast cancer with germline *BRCA1/2* mutations, OlympiAD (NCT02000622) demonstrated improved progression‑free survival and higher objective response rates with olaparib versus single‑agent chemotherapy, while overall survival (OS) differences by HR status were modest (HR for OS 0.86 for HR+ vs 0.93 for TNBC).[2],[4] Subgroup analyses suggested that the main determinant of benefit was treatment line rather than hormone‑receptor status, with greater OS benefit when olaparib was used as first‑line therapy for metastatic disease.[2],[4] In early HER2‑negative gBRCA‑mutated disease, adjuvant olaparib improved 3‑year invasive disease‑free survival, with a numerically smaller but still meaningful effect in HR+ tumors compared with TNBC, supporting biomarker‑driven use across luminal subtypes.[3]

In triple‑negative breast cancer, olaparib shows its largest relative benefit. In OlympiAD, TNBC patients derived similar OS benefit to HR+ patients (HR 0.93 vs 0.86), but earlier use again favored better outcomes.[2],[4] In the early‑stage setting, most patients in the OlympiA trial had TNBC (82.2%), and subgroup analyses indicated greater adjuvant benefit in TNBC than HR+ disease, aligning with the strong dependency of BRCA1‑mutant TNBC on homologous recombination deficiency.[3]

For HER2‑positive breast cancer, current targeted strategies focus on anti‑HER2 antibodies, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, and antibody–drug conjugates; PARP inhibitors, including olaparib, are not established in routine HER2‑amplified disease, and available data are largely exploratory.[1],[3] Across HER2‑negative subtypes, germline *BRCA1/2* mutation remains the key biomarker guiding olaparib use, with the magnitude and prioritization of benefit greatest in gBRCA‑mutated TNBC and clinically relevant but more context‑dependent in HR+/HER2‑ disease.[2]  



## 7. Contraindications and Safety

The EMA product information for Lynparza (olaparib) does not list many absolute contraindications beyond hypersensitivity to olaparib or any excipient; however, it emphasises that treatment must be initiated and supervised by physicians experienced in anticancer therapy, and that patients must have confirmed BRCA1/2 mutation status for breast cancer indications before starting therapy.[3] The label also stresses the need for validated testing and appropriate genetic counselling, underscoring that use outside these defined molecular indications is not supported.[3]

The EU Risk Management Plan (RMP) for olaparib identifies myelodysplastic syndrome/acute myeloid leukaemia (MDS/AML) as an important identified risk, with specific pharmacovigilance activities and risk‑minimisation measures.[4] The RMP further notes second primary malignancies as important potential risks, consistent with class effects of PARP inhibitors and the heavily pre‑treated populations in which they are used.[4]

Bone‑marrow toxicity (anaemia, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia) is a central safety concern in the RMP, with routine monitoring and dose modification recommended as key risk‑minimisation tools.[4] The RMP also highlights missing information in under‑studied populations (e.g., severe renal impairment, some combinations), supporting cautious use and close monitoring when data are limited.[4] While pregnancy‑specific text is truncated in the available extract, olaparib is used only in adults with cancer, and the RMP framework implies standard oncologic precautions regarding embryo–fetal risk.[3][4]
