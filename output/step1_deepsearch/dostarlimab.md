## 1. Drug Summary

Dostarlimab is a humanized IgG4 monoclonal antibody targeting programmed death‑1 (PD‑1), one of the key inhibitory immune checkpoints that down‑regulate effector T‑cell function in the tumor microenvironment.[4,8] By blocking PD‑1 engagement with its ligands PD‑L1 and PD‑L2, dostarlimab restores cytotoxic T‑cell activity and can induce durable antitumor responses in immunogenic tumors characterized by high neoantigen load.[4,11] It belongs to the same functional class as pembrolizumab and nivolumab, which have established PD‑1 blockade as a central strategy in solid tumors.[3,4,8]

Regulatorily, dostarlimab has been approved for recurrent or advanced endometrial cancer with deficient mismatch repair (dMMR) and, in the broader PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitor class, is part of the group of immune checkpoint inhibitors that underpin tumor‑agnostic approvals for MSI‑H/dMMR solid tumors.[4,7,14] In endometrial cancer, phase II/III data (e.g., GARNET and first‑line chemo‑IO combinations) show objective response rates around 40–45% and have established dMMR/MSI‑H as a highly actionable biomarker for PD‑1 blockade in this setting.[4,14]

In breast cancer, dostarlimab is not yet approved. Its development is informed by several precision‑oncology observations: (1) MSI‑H/dMMR is very rare in breast cancer overall and in TNBC (≈0–1%, with 0–0.9% MSI‑H in large TNBC cohorts), but such tumors are expected to be highly sensitive to PD‑1 blockade by analogy with other dMMR/MSI‑H solid tumors.[9,11,12] (2) A minority of metastatic breast cancers across subtypes show TMB ≥10 mut/Mb (≈8–12%), a level associated with improved response to PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors and now recognized as a pan‑tumor immunotherapy biomarker.[10,11,14] (3) TNBC and HER2‑positive disease are relatively TIL‑rich and more immunogenic than HR‑positive/HER2‑negative tumors, and multiple PD‑1/PD‑L1 agents have already demonstrated benefit in PD‑L1‑positive TNBC, supporting the class mechanism in this subtype.[3,10]

Dostarlimab’s most active investigational niches in breast cancer are therefore biomarker‑defined: dMMR/MSI‑H tumors, TMB‑high tumors, and homologous‑recombination‑deficient (often gBRCA‑mutated) HER2‑negative tumors in which PARP inhibitor–PD‑1 combinations may be synergistic.[2,5,10,11] Given the extremely low prevalence of dMMR/MSI‑H in breast cancer, routine MSI/TMB testing using PCR or next‑generation sequencing—aligned with ESMO recommendations—is essential to identify the small subset of patients likely to derive substantial benefit from off‑label or trial‑based dostarlimab therapy.[9,11,13,14]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Dostarlimab (TSR‑042) is a humanized IgG4 monoclonal antibody that binds PD‑1 with high affinity (KD ~300 pM), blocking its interaction with both PD‑L1 and PD‑L2 on tumor and immune cells.[9] PD‑1 engagement normally leads to phosphorylation of its ITSM motif, recruitment of SHP2, and downstream dephosphorylation of TCR‑associated signaling molecules, resulting in reduced T‑cell proliferation, cytokine production, and progressive T‑cell exhaustion and apoptosis.[8,9,14] By preventing PD‑1/PD‑L1 and PD‑1/PD‑L2 binding, dostarlimab disrupts this inhibitory axis, restoring antigen‑specific T‑cell activation with increased IL‑2 and IFN‑γ production and enhanced cytotoxic activity.[9,14] The IgG4 backbone minimizes Fc‑mediated effector functions, reducing depletion of PD‑1–expressing effector T cells and favoring durable immune re‑engagement.[9]

In humanized mouse xenograft models of human breast cancer, dostarlimab significantly reduced tumor growth and increased intratumoral CD8+ T‑cell infiltration while decreasing regulatory T cells, supporting a direct antitumor immune effect in breast tissue.[9] More broadly in breast cancer, the PD‑1/PD‑L1 axis is a key immune‑evasion mechanism: PD‑L1 expression on tumor and immune cells is induced by IFN‑γ–driven JAK/STAT signaling and by oncogenic pathways, promoting CD8+ T‑cell exhaustion and immune escape.[5,14,15] TNBC and some HER2‑positive tumors are relatively more immunogenic, with higher tumor‑infiltrating lymphocytes and PD‑L1 expression, and show higher response rates to PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade than luminal subtypes.[1,4,6,7]

Molecularly defined “hot” contexts further enhance sensitivity to PD‑1 blockade. MSI‑H/dMMR tumors accumulate frameshift mutations and neoantigens, leading to dense T‑cell infiltration and high PD‑1/PD‑L1 expression; anti‑PD‑1 antibodies effectively reactivate these neoantigen‑specific T cells.[10,15] In the tumor‑agnostic phase 1 GARNET trial, dostarlimab produced rapid, robust, and durable responses across dMMR/MSI‑H solid tumors, with benefit independent of tissue of origin, and biomarker analyses indicated that high tumor mutational burden (TMB) and PD‑L1 expression may further enrich for responders beyond dMMR/MSI‑H alone.[10,15] Although breast cancer–specific clinical data for dostarlimab are limited, the same biochemical mechanism applies: its antitumor impact is expected to be greatest in breast cancers that are dMMR/MSI‑H and/or TMB‑high, and in PD‑L1–expressing, T‑cell–inflamed subtypes such as a proportion of TNBC and HER2‑positive disease, where PD‑1–mediated suppression is a dominant adaptive resistance pathway.[1,4–7,10,15]

## 5. Pathways (Brief Overview)

Key immune and DNA‑repair–related pathways modulated downstream of PD‑1 blockade (including dostarlimab, as a PD‑1–targeting antibody) in human breast cancer and other solid tumors are summarized below with representative pathway identifiers and qualitative regulation:

- **T‑cell receptor (TCR) signaling and CD28 costimulation** – PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade restores TCR/CD28‑driven activation, proliferation, and cytotoxic function of CD8⁺ T cells and Th1 cells (Up, Sensitive). KEGG: hsa04660; Reactome: R‑HSA‑202403; MSigDB: HALLMARK_TCR_SIGNALING.[1],[2],[5],[10],[13]

- **Interferon‑γ (IFN‑γ) and cGAS–STING/type I interferon response** – DDR defects or DNA‑damaging therapies activate cGAS–STING, STAT1/STAT4 and IRF1, increasing type I IFNs and IFN‑γ‑driven Th1/CTL programs that favor PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitor response (Up, Sensitive). KEGG: hsa04630; Reactome: R‑HSA‑909733; MSigDB: HALLMARK_INTERFERON_GAMMA_RESPONSE.[1],[5],[10],[11],[13],[17]

- **Antigen processing and presentation (MHC class I pathway)** – PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade and DDR‑targeting combinations enhance antigen presentation machinery (e.g., HLA class I/II, CD40, CD80) and cross‑priming of CD8⁺ T cells (Up, Sensitive). KEGG: hsa04612; Reactome: R‑HSA‑983169; MSigDB: HALLMARK_ALLOGRAFT_REJECTION.[1],[5],[10],[13]

- **PD‑1/PD‑L1 immune checkpoint signaling** – Dostarlimab sterically blocks PD‑1–PD‑L1/PD‑L2 binding, abrogating inhibitory signaling; however, PD‑L1 upregulation (e.g., via ATR/CHK1 or CMTM6‑mediated stabilization) and tumor‑intrinsic PD‑1/PD‑L1 can mediate resistance (Down functional signaling with blockade; pathway dependence = Sensitive; adaptive upregulation = Resistant). KEGG: hsa05235; Reactome: R‑HSA‑540977; MSigDB: HALLMARK_PD1_SIGNALING.[2],[3],[4],[7],[10],[11],[13]

- **Regulatory T cell (Treg), TAM and MDSC‑mediated immunosuppression** – HR⁺/HER2− and metastatic TNBC often show high Treg/TAM/MDSC infiltration and immunosuppressive cytokines (e.g., IL‑1β, CCL2), limiting ICI efficacy; Notch‑driven TAM programs and IL‑1β/CCL2 signaling are key resistance axes (Up, Resistant). KEGG: hsa04659; Reactome: R‑HSA‑449147; MSigDB: HALLMARK_IL6_JAK_STAT3_SIGNALING.[1],[5],[8],[9]

- **DNA damage response (DDR), MMR and homologous recombination (HR)/PARP‑related repair** – dMMR/MSI‑H and HR defects (e.g., BRCA1/2 loss) increase neoantigens, cGAS–STING activation and PD‑L1 expression, generally enhancing sensitivity to PD‑1 blockade and to PARP‑inhibitor–ICI combinations; intact or compensatory DDR can support immune evasion (Defective DDR: Sensitive; intact/compensatory: Resistant). KEGG: hsa03440, hsa03430; Reactome: R‑HSA‑5693532; MSigDB: HALLMARK_DNA_REPAIR.[10],[11],[12],[13],[17]

- **Hypoxia/HIF‑1α, EMT and pro‑metastatic signaling (e.g., Hippo, PI3K–AKT–mTOR, NF‑κB, MAPK)** – PD‑1/PD‑L1 or PD‑L1 blockade in breast models downregulates hypoxia, EMT, Hippo, NF‑κB, PI3K–AKT–mTOR and MAPK programs linked to proliferation, invasion and immune escape; persistent activation of these pathways is associated with resistance (Down with effective blockade = Sensitive; Up = Resistant). KEGG: hsa04066, hsa04151, hsa04010; Reactome: R‑HSA‑2132295; MSigDB: HALLMARK_HYPOXIA, HALLMARK_EPITHELIAL_MESENCHYMAL_TRANSITION.[1],[4],[5],[7],[10]

- **Alternative immune checkpoints and juxtacrine PD‑L1 regulation (CTLA‑4, LAG‑3, TIM‑3, TIGIT, EphA10, Notch)** – PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade in breast explants upregulates multiple alternative checkpoints (CTLA‑4, LAG‑3, TIM‑3, TIGIT) and Notch/EphA10‑driven juxtacrine signaling that sustains PD‑L1 expression, promoting adaptive resistance (Up, Resistant). KEGG: hsa04660 (co‑inhibitory receptors); Reactome: R‑HSA‑388841 (CTLA‑4), R‑HSA‑9013509 (Notch); MSigDB: HALLMARK_NOTCH_SIGNALING.[1],[5],[6],[7],[8]



## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

Across breast cancer subtypes, the current role of dostarlimab is tumour‑agnostic and restricted to the very small fraction of cases that are dMMR/MSI‑H or otherwise enrolled in basket studies of advanced solid tumours. In the GARNET trial, dostarlimab produced a 41.6% ORR across 209 pretreated MSI‑H/dMMR solid tumours, supporting FDA tumour‑agnostic approval, but breast cancer represented only a minor component and no robust subtype‑specific conclusions can be drawn.[1,7] Preclinical work showed growth inhibition in the MDA‑MB‑436 breast cancer model, indicating biological activity but not defining clinical utility.[4]

In HR+/HER2– disease, ICIs overall have shown modest and inconsistent benefit, reflecting low TMB, sparse TILs, and an immunosuppressive microenvironment.[9,11,12,14,16] Emerging neoadjuvant data with other PD‑1 agents (e.g., nivolumab, pembrolizumab) suggest benefit mainly in biomarker‑selected, immune‑enriched HR+ subsets, but no clinical trials have specifically tested dostarlimab in this subtype.[9,11,12]

For HER2‑positive breast cancer, higher baseline TILs and mutational burden support immunogenicity, and multiple ICI‑based combinations are under evaluation, yet available clinical data again involve other PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors rather than dostarlimab.[10,14,15,16] In TNBC, PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors are established in PD‑L1–positive disease, and PARP–ICI combinations show promising activity, particularly in gBRCA‑mutated or HRD‑positive tumours, but these data come from agents such as pembrolizumab, durvalumab, and avelumab.[17,20,22] gBRCA‑mutated and broader HRD TNBC are immunogenically attractive and heavily studied with PARP–ICI combinations; however, current evidence for niraparib–dostarlimab is confined to non‑breast solid tumours, and breast‑specific cohorts remain under early investigation.[18,20,23] Overall, for all breast cancer subtypes, dostarlimab should presently be regarded as an option only for the rare MSI‑H/dMMR (or potentially POLE‑altered, TMB‑high) tumours rather than as a subtype‑wide therapy.[1,3,7,16]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Dostarlimab has no formal contraindications on the FDA label; like other PD‑1/PD‑L1 inhibitors, use is limited mainly by immune‑mediated toxicities and organ dysfunction rather than absolute exclusion criteria.[8],[13] Immune‑mediated events include pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis, endocrinopathies (especially hypo‑/hyperthyroidism), nephritis with renal dysfunction, and serious skin reactions; these can be severe or fatal and require prompt recognition, corticosteroids, and treatment interruption or permanent discontinuation depending on grade.[8],[13],[20] In GARNET and pooled dMMR solid‑tumor data, the most common adverse reactions (≥20%) were fatigue/asthenia, anemia, diarrhea, nausea and transaminase elevation, consistent with class effects.[10],[13]

For breast cancer patients, key cautionary situations mirror those for other PD‑1 agents. Pre‑existing autoimmune disease or prior serious immune‑related adverse events (irAEs) warrant individualized risk–benefit assessment and close monitoring, rather than automatic exclusion.[12],[20] Baseline pulmonary compromise or prior thoracic radiotherapy increase pneumonitis risk; chronic liver disease or baseline transaminase elevation increase risk and consequences of immune hepatitis.[6],[8],[12],[20] Endocrine irAEs (thyroid dysfunction, hypophysitis, adrenal insufficiency, diabetes) are often permanent and particularly relevant in heavily pre‑treated breast cancer patients who may already have treatment‑related endocrinopathies.[10],[12],[20]

Combination regimens with carboplatin–paclitaxel (as in endometrial cancer) increase overall toxicity, with rash, diarrhea, hypothyroidism and hypertension ≥20%; myelosuppression and neuropathy are largely chemotherapy‑driven.[8],[9] Early‑phase data combining dostarlimab with PARP inhibitors and/or chemotherapy in advanced solid tumors show no new safety signals but higher rates of hematologic toxicity, requiring careful coordination of dosing, growth‑factor support, and organ‑function monitoring.[9],[12],[18] In practice, breast cancer patients receiving dostarlimab (e.g., in trials with PARP inhibitors or ADCs) should undergo thorough baseline assessment (autoimmune history, lung and liver function, endocrine status), receive clear education on early irAE symptoms, and be managed according to established PD‑1 toxicity algorithms, with low thresholds to withhold therapy and initiate steroids for grade ≥2 events.[8],[12],[20],[21]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Dostarlimab has not been evaluated in breast‑cancer–only trials; available evidence comes from the tumor‑agnostic dMMR/MSI‑H solid‑tumor program and rectal cancer studies that establish its activity in biomarker‑defined settings that can include rare dMMR breast cancers.

**GARNET (NCT02715284, phase I, multicohort)**  
Nonrandomized, multicenter, open‑label study of dostarlimab monotherapy in advanced solid tumors with biomarker‑defined expansion cohorts, including dMMR/MSI‑H non‑endometrial solid tumors.[12,14] The recommended therapeutic dose is 500 mg IV every 3 weeks for 4 cycles followed by 1000 mg IV every 6 weeks until progression or toxicity.[12,14] In the pooled dMMR solid‑tumor efficacy population (209 patients), objective response rate (ORR) was 41.6% (95% CI 34.9–48.6), with 9.1% complete responses; median duration of response was 34.7 months and median overall survival was not reached at 27.7‑month median follow‑up.[6,8,14] Safety was manageable, with immune‑related events (e.g., hypothyroidism, transaminase elevations, arthralgia) generally low grade; these data underpinned FDA’s tissue‑agnostic approval for dMMR recurrent/advanced solid tumors, a category that includes dMMR breast cancers.[3,8,14]

**Locally Advanced dMMR Rectal Cancer (NCT04165772, phase II, single‑arm)**  
Prospective trial in dMMR locally advanced rectal cancer testing neoadjuvant dostarlimab 500 mg IV every 3 weeks for 9 cycles (≈6 months) with planned chemoradiation and surgery only if residual disease.[7,12,16] In the initial report, all 12 evaluable patients achieved a clinical complete response (100%; 95% CI 74–100) and none required chemoradiation or surgery at a median 12‑month follow‑up; no grade ≥3 adverse events were observed, with mostly grade 1–2 rash, pruritus, and fatigue.[7,12] Updated phase II data (14 patients) confirmed a 100% clinical complete response rate with no additional therapy and no recurrences during 6–25 months of follow‑up, reinforcing the curative‑intent potential of PD‑1 blockade in dMMR tumors.[7,16]

**Tumor‑Agnostic dMMR/POLE‑Altered Solid‑Tumor Cohorts (GARNET prespecified analysis)**  
An interim analysis of 327 patients with dMMR/MSI‑H or POLE‑altered advanced solid tumors (including colorectal, endometrial, ovarian, gastrointestinal, pancreatic, and other sites) treated at the standard GARNET dose showed rapid, robust, and durable activity.[14,16] In dMMR tumors, ORR was approximately 40–45%, median progression‑free survival exceeded 1 year in several tumor‑type subsets, and median overall survival was not reached; 1‑ and 2‑year progression‑free or response‑maintenance rates were high, indicating long‑term disease control in a substantial fraction of patients.[14,16] These pan‑cancer data form the principal clinical basis for using dostarlimab under the FDA’s dMMR solid‑tumor indication in rare dMMR breast cancers identified by comprehensive genomic profiling.[2,8,12,14]
