## Drug Summary

Selpercatinib (LOXO‑292; Retevmo) is an oral, ATP‑competitive, highly selective RET tyrosine kinase inhibitor developed to potently inhibit wild‑type RET and a broad spectrum of activating RET fusions and mutations, including gatekeeper V804L/M, with >100‑fold selectivity over VEGFR2 and minimal off‑target kinase activity.[5][6] By blocking RET autophosphorylation, selpercatinib suppresses canonical downstream pathways such as RAS–MAPK, PI3K–AKT, JAK–STAT, and related signaling that drive proliferation and survival in RET‑addicted tumors.[2][6] This selectivity translates into high response rates and durable disease control in RET‑altered cancers, including robust intracranial activity in NSCLC.[2][4–6]

Regulatory approvals are anchored in the phase I/II LIBRETTO‑001 trial (NCT03157128), where selpercatinib produced objective responses in RET fusion‑positive NSCLC and thyroid cancers and in a basket of other RET fusion‑positive solid tumors.[3–5] On this basis, the FDA granted a tumor‑agnostic accelerated approval in 2022 for adults with locally advanced or metastatic RET fusion‑positive solid tumors that have progressed after prior systemic therapy or lack satisfactory alternatives; responding histologies included breast cancer.[3] Although RET alterations are rare in breast cancer, preclinical and early clinical evidence—such as a complete response reported in a metastatic breast cancer patient within LIBRETTO‑001—support RET fusions/overexpression as an actionable, genomically defined vulnerability in a small subset of hormone receptor–positive disease, positioning selpercatinib as a precision oncology option when RET activation is demonstrable.[6]

## Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Selpercatinib (LOXO‑292) is a highly selective, oral RET tyrosine kinase inhibitor that potently inhibits wild‑type RET and diverse oncogenic RET fusion and mutant isoforms while largely sparing non‑RET kinases.[4] RET activation through gene fusions or activating mutations generates constitutively active kinases that drive tumorigenesis via sustained signaling through RAS/MAPK and PI3K/AKT pathways, among others, functioning as bona fide oncogenic drivers across multiple solid tumors, including rare breast cancers with RET alterations.[3,4,7] In breast cancer, these RET‑driven signaling circuits are expected to maintain proliferation and survival programs analogous to those seen in RET‑rearranged lung and thyroid cancers.[3,7] By occupying the RET ATP‑binding site with an unconventional binding mode that wraps around the gatekeeper region, selpercatinib effectively suppresses RET autophosphorylation and downstream signaling, including ERK activation, leading to growth inhibition and apoptosis in RET‑dependent tumor cells, but remains vulnerable to acquired RET solvent‑front G810 mutations and bypass MET activation.[1,2,4,6–8]

## Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

Across breast cancer, RET fusions are very rare (<1% overall) but clearly oncogenic, with transforming capacity shown in preclinical models via MAPK and PI3K pathway activation.[9][21] In LIBRETTO‑001’s tumour‑agnostic cohort, two patients with RET fusion–positive breast cancer were included among 45 non‑lung/non‑thyroid solid tumours; overall objective response rate was 43.9% with durable benefit, supporting RET dependence irrespective of tissue of origin.[2][18][21]

In HR+/HER2– disease, most reported RET‑altered cases are HER2‑negative with variable ER expression, and one documented ER+/HER2– breast cancer with RET fusion achieved a marked response to selpercatinib, reinforcing a strong biologic rationale for testing endocrine‑refractory HR+/HER2– tumors for RET fusions.[21] For HER2+ tumors, RET alterations are uncommon; an NCOA4–RET fusion has been described as a bypass resistance mechanism to dual HER2 blockade plus aromatase inhibition, suggesting selpercatinib could be considered in highly selected HER2+ cases with acquired RET fusions after standard HER2‑targeted therapy failure.[21]

In TNBC, RET fusions remain exceedingly rare, but pan‑cancer analyses and the tumour‑agnostic LIBRETTO‑001 data imply that TNBC harboring RET fusions should be sensitive to selpercatinib, a particularly relevant option where other targeted drivers are scarce.[2][9][21] Germline BRCA‑mutated breast cancers show no specific interaction with RET biology; available data indicate that RET status, not BRCA status, should guide selpercatinib use, while BRCA guides PARP inhibitor eligibility.[14][15][21]

## Contraindications and Safety

Selpercatinib’s major dose‑limiting toxicities are hypertension and liver enzyme elevations. In LIBRETTO‑321, hypertension was the most common grade ≥3 treatment‑emergent adverse event (≈20%), while most other toxicities were grade 1–2 and manageable with dose modification or supportive care.[6] Longer‑term follow‑up from the same trial confirms a consistent safety profile over >3 years, with key toxicities (hypertension, transaminase elevations, hematologic events) described as clinically monitorable, manageable, and reversible, and with low drug‑related discontinuation and no treatment‑related deaths.[5,8] These data support routine baseline and periodic blood pressure and liver function monitoring in breast cancer patients, especially those with prior anthracycline, HER2‑targeted, or CDK4/6 inhibitor exposure.

QT interval prolongation is a recognized risk with selpercatinib and warrants additional ECG and electrolyte monitoring.[3] This is particularly relevant in metastatic breast cancer, where polypharmacy and use of QT‑prolonging agents are common. Large claims‑based analyses in HR+/HER2− metastatic breast cancer show that over half of patients receive at least one QTc‑prolonging medication and that exposure persists for more than half of treatment time, underscoring the need for systematic review of concomitant drugs and mitigation of Torsades de Pointes risk.[1,2,7] In practice, avoiding or substituting other QT‑prolonging agents and correcting modifiable risk factors (e.g., diuretics causing electrolyte loss) are critical when adding selpercatinib.

From a practical “contraindication‑like” perspective in breast oncology, high‑risk cardiovascular comorbidity and complex QT‑active polypharmacy merit particular caution. More than 60% of patients with newly diagnosed HR+/HER2− metastatic disease have at least one cardiovascular comorbidity, and a substantial fraction receive one or more QT‑prolonging medications, which should inform treatment decisions and intensity of cardio‑oncology monitoring when introducing additional QT‑active targeted agents.[7] Although formal regulatory contraindications are limited, pregnancy and lactation are generally avoided with RET inhibitors, and strong CYP3A4 interactions and uncontrolled hypertension should be considered practical reasons to defer or modify selpercatinib therapy in breast cancer patients.[3,5]

## Key Clinical Trials

The pivotal phase I/II basket trial LIBRETTO‑001 (LOXO‑RET‑17001; NCT03157128) enrolled patients with advanced RET‑altered solid tumors; in 41 evaluable RET fusion–positive non‑lung/non‑thyroid cancers, including breast cancer, selpercatinib achieved an overall response rate (ORR) of 44% with median duration of response 24.5 months.[5],[11],[13],[15] Within LIBRETTO‑001, a CCDC6–RET fusion–positive metastatic breast cancer case had a rapid partial response by day 21 and a confirmed complete response by day 231, remaining on selpercatinib 160 mg twice daily with ongoing remission beyond 300 days.[6] These data underpinned the FDA’s 2022 tissue‑agnostic accelerated approval of selpercatinib for locally advanced or metastatic RET fusion–positive solid tumors.[11],[8],[9],[12],[13],[15]
