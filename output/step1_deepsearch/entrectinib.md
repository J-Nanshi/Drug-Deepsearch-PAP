## Drug Summary

Entrectinib (Rozlytrek) is an orally active, CNS‑penetrant small‑molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitor that selectively targets TRKA/B/C (encoded by *NTRK1/2/3*), ROS1, and ALK through ATP‑competitive inhibition at low‑nanomolar concentrations.[10,17,20,22] It was rationally designed to achieve high systemic and intracranial exposure, with weak interaction with P‑glycoprotein and favorable brain‑to‑plasma and CSF‑to‑plasma ratios, distinguishing it from earlier TKIs such as crizotinib and larotrectinib.[10,17,20] Preclinical models show potent antitumor activity and tumor regression in xenografts and intracranial models driven by *NTRK*, *ROS1*, or *ALK* fusions.[5,17,20]

Regulatory approvals are tumor‑agnostic: the US FDA and EMA authorize entrectinib for adults and children (age thresholds differ by region) with unresectable or metastatic solid tumors harboring an *NTRK* gene fusion (without known resistance mutations) that have progressed after treatment or lack satisfactory alternatives, as well as for ROS1‑positive advanced NSCLC.[9,12,13,16,18,20,22] Integrated analyses of three phase I/II basket trials—ALKA‑372‑001, STARTRK‑1, and STARTRK‑2 (including NCT02097810 and NCT02568267)—in *NTRK* fusion‑positive solid tumors reported an objective response rate of ~57% with durable responses and substantial intracranial activity across multiple histologies.[1,10,17,20,22]

In breast oncology, *NTRK* fusions are rare overall (<1% of unselected breast cancers) but highly enriched in secretory breast carcinoma, where *ETV6–NTRK3* fusions occur in >90% of cases and define this rare subtype.[2,5,18] Secretory breast carcinomas are often triple‑negative but clinically indolent; for patients with advanced or metastatic, unresectable disease, TRK inhibitors such as entrectinib provide a rational, histology‑independent option once an *NTRK* fusion is confirmed by an approved assay (e.g., FoundationOne CDx or FoundationOne Liquid CDx).[2,9,14,15,18,20,22] Case‑level evidence in *NTRK*‑fusion–positive breast cancer, including triple‑negative disease, supports clinically meaningful responses to TRK inhibition, and contemporary breast and TNBC reviews now list entrectinib among targeted options for metastatic *NTRK*‑fusion–positive tumors that have exhausted standard therapies.[2,18,22,23]

**Key overview/regulatory sources:** integrated phase I/II analysis of entrectinib in *NTRK*‑fusion solid tumors (ALKA‑372‑001, STARTRK‑1, STARTRK‑2; NCT02097810, NCT02568267)[1]; Adis drug evaluation of entrectinib in *NTRK*+ solid tumors and ROS1+ NSCLC[10,20]; FDA and EMA product information and companion‑diagnostic documentation for Rozlytrek and *NTRK*/*ROS1* fusion testing.[9,12,13,14,15,16]

## Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

No citable source URLs are available in the provided Source material, so detailed mechanistic statements cannot be formally referenced here.

Entrectinib is an ATP-competitive inhibitor of the tyrosine kinases TRKA, TRKB, TRKC (encoded by NTRK1/2/3), ROS1, and ALK. In NTRK fusion–positive tumors such as secretory breast carcinoma harboring ETV6–NTRK3 and other NTRK fusions, chimeric TRK fusion proteins are constitutively active, driving downstream signaling through canonical oncogenic cascades including MAPK, PI3K–AKT, PLCγ, and JAK–STAT. By occupying the kinase ATP-binding site of TRK fusion proteins, entrectinib prevents receptor autophosphorylation and adaptor recruitment, thereby functionally blocking these pathways and reducing proliferation and survival signaling. This targeted mechanism underlies its tissue‑agnostic activity in NTRK fusion–positive solid tumors and is particularly relevant in breast cancer for rare secretory carcinomas and other NTRK fusion–positive cases across HR+/HER2–, HER2+, and triple‑negative subtypes, where the dominant oncogenic driver is the TRK fusion rather than hormone receptor or HER2 signaling.

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

Key signaling networks in ETV6–NTRK3–driven secretory breast carcinoma converge on canonical neurotrophin/TRK cascades. The ETV6–NTRK3 fusion encodes a constitutively active TRKC kinase that drives MAPK/ERK, PI3K–AKT, STAT3, PLCγ and PKC signaling, promoting proliferation and survival; entrectinib, a potent pan‑TRK inhibitor, suppresses these pathways in TRK‑fusion models, establishing them as key sensitivity axes.[1][3][11][14] In Ba/F3 and primary TRK‑fusion tumor models, TRK inhibition leads to coordinated down‑regulation of MAPK, PI3K–AKT, PKC and STAT3 signaling, consistent with TRK being an obligate upstream node.[3]

Across solid tumors with NTRK fusions, constitutively active TRK fusion proteins signal through Ras/Raf/MEK/ERK, PI3K/AKT/mTOR and PLCγ pathways, and blockade by entrectinib yields high response rates in histology‑agnostic trials, supporting these cascades as dominant determinants of drug sensitivity.[3][11][14] Adaptive EGFR and other RTK signaling can re‑engage MAPK and PI3K pathways after TRK inhibition, providing bypass survival routes and a mechanistic basis for acquired resistance that is likely relevant to NTRK‑fusion breast cancer.[3][15][16] Collectively, TRK–neurotrophin, MAPK/ERK, PI3K–AKT/mTOR, STAT3 and PLCγ/PKC signaling represent the principal modulated networks governing entrectinib sensitivity and resistance in NTRK fusion–positive breast carcinoma.[1][3][11][14–16]

## Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HR+/HER2– metastatic breast cancer, entrectinib is relevant when tumors harbor *NTRK* fusions, most often in secretory carcinoma, which can show weak ER expression while remaining HER2–.[6],[7] In the integrated phase I/II *NTRK* fusion solid‑tumor analyses, breast cancer was a small subset (7 patients) but showed a high objective response rate (~71%) and median duration of response (≈12.9 months), indicating substantial benefit when *NTRK* is the main oncogenic driver.[2],[4] Given its tumor‑agnostic approval and lack of endocrine pathway targeting, entrectinib is best positioned as a precision option layered onto standard HR‑directed therapy once an *NTRK* fusion is identified.[4]

For HER2+ breast cancer, entrectinib has no role in HER2‑driven disease itself, but rare tumors may be both HER2+ and *NTRK* fusion–positive or have secretory histology.[6],[8] In such double‑driver settings, entrectinib would generally be reserved for disease that has exhausted anti‑HER2 standards, provided molecular work‑up confirms that the *NTRK* fusion remains a relevant driver.[4],[8] Its CNS penetration and intracranial responses in *NTRK*‑fusion solid tumors support use in HER2+/*NTRK*+ cases with brain metastases.[2],[3],[4]

In triple‑negative breast cancer (TNBC), secretory carcinoma represents a distinctive TRK‑addicted subset, usually triple‑negative with canonical *ETV6–NTRK3* fusion and diffuse pan‑TRK expression.[6],[7] Secretory TNBC is often indolent but can be chemoresistant and develop CNS metastases, as illustrated by a fatal case with multiple brain lesions despite intensive chemotherapy.[7] For advanced or metastatic secretory TNBC, entrectinib is a highly rational targeted therapy, with basket‑trial and reimbursement‑review data showing robust systemic and intracranial responses across *NTRK*‑rearranged tumors, including breast cancer.[2],[3],[4],[7]

For gBRCA‑mutated breast cancer, entrectinib is not directed at homologous‑recombination deficiency and its efficacy is independent of BRCA status.[6],[8] PARP inhibitors and platinum chemotherapy remain the primary targeted options, while entrectinib is reserved for the rare coexistence of an actionable *NTRK* fusion in gBRCA‑mutated, HER2‑negative disease.[4],[6],[8] Across subtypes, the main precision‑oncology role of entrectinib in breast cancer is therefore confined to *NTRK* fusion–positive (often secretory) tumors, including those with CNS metastases, where durable systemic and intracranial control has been demonstrated.[2],[3],[4]

## Contraindications and Safety

Entrectinib (ROZLYTREK) has no formal contraindications in the US label, but its use is limited by several serious warnings and precautions that are highly relevant for breast cancer patients with NTRK fusions.[1,7] Cardiac risks include congestive heart failure (CHF) and QTc prolongation; labels recommend baseline assessment of left ventricular ejection fraction, QT interval, and electrolytes, with treatment interruption, dose reduction, or discontinuation for new or worsening CHF or significant QTc prolongation.[1,7,20]

Central nervous system effects (cognitive impairment, mood disorders, dizziness, sleep disturbances) are frequent and particularly important in patients with brain metastases or on psychoactive drugs; management is via dose interruption or reduction according to severity.[1,7,14,20] Skeletal fractures are a distinct safety signal and warrant prompt evaluation of bone pain or trauma—critical in breast cancer populations with bone metastases or pre‑existing osteoporosis.[1,7,14] Hepatotoxicity is common at the laboratory level; regulatory guidance and LiverTox recommend ALT/AST monitoring every 2 weeks for the first month, then monthly, with dose modification or discontinuation for significant elevations.[1,6,11,22]

Hyperuricemia requires baseline and periodic uric acid monitoring and may necessitate urate‑lowering therapy and temporary interruption.[1,7,11] Vision disorders and embryo–fetal toxicity mandate withholding therapy for new or functionally significant visual changes and strict contraception and avoidance of breastfeeding.[1,7,11,18] Because entrectinib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4, strong or moderate CYP3A inhibitors/inducers and QT‑prolonging drugs should be avoided or require dose adjustment and close ECG monitoring, especially in heavily pretreated breast cancer patients receiving polypharmacy.[1,7,14,20]

## Key Clinical Trials

The tumor-agnostic efficacy of entrectinib in NTRK fusion–positive solid tumors, including breast cancer and patients with baseline CNS metastases, has been established primarily through the integrated phase I/II analysis of ALKA‑372‑001 (EudraCT 2012‑000148‑88), STARTRK‑1 (NCT02097810), and STARTRK‑2 (NCT02568267), which demonstrated high objective response rates and durable responses, supporting meaningful systemic and intracranial activity across histologies. The pooled dataset forming the basis of the tumor‑agnostic regulatory approval included breast cancer among the more common NTRK‑driven tumor types and showed that a substantial proportion of responses were maintained for ≥12 months, consistent with durable disease control and CNS‑penetrant activity.
