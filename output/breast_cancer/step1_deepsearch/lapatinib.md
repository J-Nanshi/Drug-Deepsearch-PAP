## Drug Summary

Lapatinib is an oral, small‑molecule, reversible tyrosine kinase inhibitor that targets the cytoplasmic ATP‑binding site of HER2 (ErbB2) and EGFR (HER1), suppressing receptor autophosphorylation and downstream PI3K/AKT and ERK1/2 signaling, thereby inhibiting proliferation and promoting apoptosis in HER2‑driven breast cancer cells.[1] It was approved by the US FDA in 2007 for the treatment of HER2‑positive metastatic breast cancer (MBC), particularly in patients previously exposed to anthracyclines, taxanes, and trastuzumab or who have developed resistance to trastuzumab.[1] Lapatinib is typically used in combination with capecitabine or endocrine therapy in the metastatic setting and is positioned as a later‑line HER2‑targeted tyrosine kinase inhibitor rather than a first‑line agent.[1,7]

Within contemporary precision oncology, HER2‑positive MBC is managed with monoclonal antibodies (trastuzumab, pertuzumab, margetuximab), antibody–drug conjugates (T‑DM1, trastuzumab deruxtecan), and TKIs (tucatinib, lapatinib, neratinib), which have collectively transformed outcomes in this subtype.[1,7] Phase III data such as MA.31 (NCT00667251) showed that first‑line taxane–lapatinib was inferior to taxane–trastuzumab in progression‑free survival and toxicity, supporting trastuzumab‑based regimens as preferred initial therapy.[1] Lapatinib retains a role as an oral HER2/EGFR TKI option for HER2‑positive MBC—including patients with brain metastases—when newer agents (tucatinib, trastuzumab deruxtecan, T‑DM1) are unavailable, contraindicated, or exhausted, and is used in combination strategies (e.g. with aromatase inhibitors or trastuzumab) to provide dual HER2 blockade in selected hormone receptor–positive/HER2‑positive disease.[1,3,7]

## Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Lapatinib is an oral 4‑anilinoquinazoline tyrosine kinase inhibitor that binds reversibly to the intracellular ATP‑binding site of EGFR (HER1) and HER2, competitively blocking ATP use and preventing receptor autophosphorylation and substrate phosphorylation.[4,16] By inhibiting HER1/HER2 kinase activity, lapatinib suppresses downstream mitogenic and survival signaling, particularly the PI3K/AKT and RAS/RAF/MEK/MAPK (ERK1/2) cascades that are central to HER2‑driven breast cancer proliferation and anti‑apoptotic signaling.[4,7,9,16] In HER2‑overexpressing models, lapatinib’s potency (IC50 <0.2 μM for both EGFR and HER2) and slow dissociation from the kinase domains translate into prolonged downregulation of receptor phosphorylation and growth inhibition, with tumor cells undergoing apoptosis or growth arrest depending on context.[4,16]

In HER2‑amplified breast cancer, HER2 forms ligand‑independent homo‑ and heterodimers (notably HER2/HER3 and EGFR/HER2) that strongly activate PI3K/AKT for survival and MAPK/ERK and PLCγ pathways for proliferation and invasion.[1,7,16] Lapatinib interrupts this signaling node by blocking the HER2 and EGFR kinase cores, thereby reducing activation of AKT and ERK and downstream effectors such as cyclin D1, while promoting pro‑apoptotic programs.[1,4,7,16] Preclinical data show that lapatinib can still downregulate AKT signaling and inhibit proliferation in trastuzumab‑refractory HER2‑positive models with PI3K pathway activation (PTEN loss, PIK3CA mutations) or p95HER2 expression, indicating that direct kinase blockade can bypass some upstream antibody‑resistance mechanisms.[1]

In EGFR/HER2‑coexpressing or EGFR‑overexpressing breast cancers, lapatinib biochemically inhibits EGFR as well as HER2 and thus convergent PI3K/AKT and MAPK signaling.[4,8,16] However, clinical benefit in HER2‑non‑amplified disease has been limited, and resistance frequently involves maintenance or restoration of downstream AKT and ERK activity through alternative routes (e.g., Src‑driven EGFR signaling, Rac activation, or PI3K/AKT reactivation), despite persistent HER2 kinase inhibition.[5,7,8] Consequently, the clinically relevant antitumor activity of lapatinib in breast cancer is largely confined to HER2‑addicted subtypes, where blockade of HER2/EGFR‑initiated PI3K/AKT and MAPK pathways effectively disrupts the dominant oncogenic driver.[1,4,7–9,16]

## Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HER2‑positive metastatic breast cancer, lapatinib is most active and is approved with capecitabine after prior anthracycline, taxane and trastuzumab, improving time to progression versus capecitabine alone but without a clear overall‑survival gain.[16] As first‑line therapy, lapatinib‑based regimens are inferior to trastuzumab‑based taxane combinations, so lapatinib is generally positioned later or in dual HER2 blockade with trastuzumab, where it can enhance progression‑free survival and clinical benefit in trastuzumab‑pretreated disease.[2][3][18] Resistance is common and involves activation of HER family members (especially HER3), PI3K/AKT and MAPK signaling, and alternative RTKs, underscoring the need for combination strategies and biomarker‑guided use.[2][9]

In HR+/HER2+ (“triple‑positive”) disease, lapatinib plus an aromatase inhibitor or letrozole significantly prolongs progression‑free survival and increases clinical benefit compared with endocrine therapy alone, supporting dual ER–HER2 targeting in this subgroup.[2][3][16] The phase III ALTERNATIVE trial showed that adding lapatinib to trastuzumab plus aromatase inhibitor further extended PFS versus trastuzumab plus aromatase inhibitor, highlighting endocrine escape and HER2–ER crosstalk as key resistance mechanisms and providing a rationale for multi‑target blockade in carefully selected, less rapidly progressive HR+/HER2+ metastatic breast cancer.[1][2]

For HER2‑negative subtypes, including HR+/HER2– and triple‑negative breast cancer, lapatinib has shown minimal efficacy. A randomized phase II trial of letrozole plus lapatinib versus letrozole plus placebo in postmenopausal HR+/HER2‑ tumors produced no pathologic complete responses and only a modest increase in clinical response rates, and lapatinib monotherapy has not demonstrated meaningful benefit in unselected HER2‑negative metastatic disease.[2] TNBC and gBRCA‑mutated breast cancers, which lack HER2 overexpression, remain largely insensitive to HER2‑directed TKIs, and current evidence does not support lapatinib use outside HER2‑positive, biomarker‑defined settings.[2][8][16]

## Contraindications and Safety

Lapatinib (TYKERB) carries a boxed warning for hepatotoxicity; severe liver injury and fatal cases have occurred in trials and post‑marketing, with uncertain causality.[1],[4] It is contraindicated in patients with known severe hypersensitivity (e.g., anaphylaxis) to lapatinib or any component.[1],[7] Baseline and regular liver function monitoring (about every 4–6 weeks) is required, with permanent discontinuation and no re‑challenge if severe liver test abnormalities or clinical hepatotoxicity develop.[1],[3],[4]

Cardiac dysfunction is a key risk: decreases in left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) occur in ~1% of patients, usually asymptomatic but occasionally clinically significant.[1],[4],[8] Normal LVEF should be documented before treatment and periodically during therapy; TYKERB should be interrupted or discontinued for Grade ≥2 LVEF decline or LVEF below institutional lower limit, with cautious dose‑reduced re‑initiation only after recovery and if asymptomatic.[1],[2],[8] Lapatinib can prolong the QT interval, with a concentration–QTc relationship observed; ECG and electrolytes should be monitored, and use is cautioned in patients with baseline QT prolongation, significant cardiac disease, or concomitant QT‑prolonging drugs.[1],[4]

Diarrhea is very common and can be severe, leading to dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and treatment interruption; Grade 3 or complicated Grade 1–2 diarrhea requires treatment interruption, aggressive antidiarrheal therapy and fluid/electrolyte replacement, with dose reduction on re‑start; Grade 4 diarrhea mandates permanent discontinuation.[1],[5],[7] Interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis and severe cutaneous reactions (including life‑threatening events) have been reported; TYKERB should be permanently discontinued if severe pulmonary symptoms or severe skin reactions occur.[1],[3],[4]

Lapatinib can cause embryo‑fetal harm, with fetal death and malformations seen in animals at exposures near or modestly above human clinical exposure; use in pregnancy and lactation should be avoided.[1],[4] Females of reproductive potential require pregnancy testing before initiation and effective contraception during treatment and for at least 1 week after the last dose; males with partners of reproductive potential should also use effective contraception for 1 week after the last dose.[1],[7] Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors or inducers and grapefruit should generally be avoided or require substantial dose adjustment with careful toxicity monitoring, as lapatinib exposure can be markedly increased or decreased.[1],[5],[7]
