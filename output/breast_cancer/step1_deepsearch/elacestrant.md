## 1. Drug Summary

Elacestrant (RAD1901, ORSERDU) is a first‑in‑class, orally available selective estrogen receptor degrader (SERD) approved in the United States for postmenopausal women and adult men with ER‑positive, HER2‑negative, *ESR1*‑mutated advanced or metastatic breast cancer after progression on at least one line of endocrine therapy.[1][4][6][16] Approval was based on the randomized phase III EMERALD trial (RAD1901‑308; NCT03778931), in which elacestrant 345 mg once daily (400 mg salt) significantly improved progression‑free survival versus standard endocrine monotherapy (fulvestrant or aromatase inhibitor) in the overall ER+/HER2– population (HR ~0.70) and more prominently in *ESR1*‑mutated tumors (HR 0.55).[4][13][16]

Mechanistically, elacestrant is an ER antagonist that selectively binds ERα, promotes its proteasome‑mediated degradation, and inhibits 17β‑estradiol–driven ER‑target gene transcription and cell proliferation in ER+/HER2– breast cancer models, including those resistant to aromatase inhibitors, fulvestrant, CDK4/6 inhibitors, and harboring activating *ESR1* mutations.[4][5][8][10][14] It shows high ERα affinity, marked reduction of ER availability in metastatic lesions, and robust antitumor activity in xenografts and patient‑derived models.[5][6][10]

Clinically, phase I RAD1901‑005 (NCT02338349) established 400 mg once daily as the recommended dose, with an objective response rate of ~19% and 42–43% clinical benefit in heavily pretreated ER+/HER2– metastatic breast cancer, including substantial activity in *ESR1*‑mutated disease and after prior CDK4/6 inhibitor and SERD exposure.[1][5][8] Across trials and in regulatory review, elacestrant has demonstrated a manageable toxicity profile dominated by low‑grade gastrointestinal events (notably nausea and vomiting) and dyslipidemia, without clear cardiac or ocular safety signals, supporting its role as an oral endocrine backbone in precision treatment of *ESR1*‑mutated ER+/HER2– breast cancer.[1][6][11][13][16]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Elacestrant is a nonsteroidal, orally bioavailable selective estrogen receptor degrader (SERD) that acts as an estrogen receptor antagonist; it binds ERα (ESR1) and promotes receptor degradation, leading to suppression of estradiol‑dependent ER target‑gene transcription and inhibition of proliferation in ER‑positive breast cancer cell lines and xenografts.[18],[23] In preclinical models, elacestrant reduces ER availability in a dose‑dependent manner and diminishes estradiol‑stimulated tumor growth, including in patient‑derived xenografts resistant to CDK4/6 inhibitors and fulvestrant, indicating robust on‑target blockade of ER signaling in endocrine‑refractory disease.[18],[19],[23]

ESR1 mutations in the ligand‑binding domain (e.g., Y537S, D538G, E380Q, L536, S463) stabilize an agonist conformation of ERα, drive ligand‑independent transcription, and confer resistance to aromatase inhibitors and dose‑dependent resistance to SERMs/SERDs, with particularly high resistance for Y537S.[22],[23],[24] These mutations are rare in primary tumors but accumulate in 20–40% of advanced ER+/HER2− cancers after prolonged AI therapy, and often coexist with other resistance alterations.[5],[20],[22],[23] Fulvestrant has reduced binding and incomplete degradation of several ESR1 mutants, and higher concentrations than are clinically achievable may be required to fully inhibit mutant ERα, motivating development of more potent oral SERDs.[21],[23],[24]

Elacestrant was specifically engineered to retain antagonism and degradation across ESR1 variants. In xenograft and cell‑line models harboring Y537S or D538G, elacestrant maintains ER antagonism, degrades mutant ERα, and suppresses ER‑regulated genes and tumor growth, whereas fulvestrant shows impaired activity in some mutant contexts.[18],[19],[23],[24] In circulating‑tumor‑DNA–defined ESR1‑mutant patients in the phase I RAD1901‑005 trial (NCT02338349), elacestrant 400 mg once daily produced objective responses and a 24‑week clinical benefit rate of 56.5%, with declines in ESR1 mutant allele fractions, providing pharmacodynamic evidence of mutant ER targeting in heavily pretreated ER+/HER2− metastatic breast cancer.[18]

Clinically, the phase III EMERALD trial (NCT03778931) demonstrated that elacestrant monotherapy improved progression‑free survival versus standard endocrine therapy, with a larger hazard‑ratio reduction and higher 12‑month PFS in the ESR1‑mutated subgroup than in the overall population, supporting preferential benefit when tumors remain ER‑dependent despite ESR1‑mutant–driven ligand‑independent signaling.[6],[8],[23] Real‑world analyses and EMERALD subgroup work further show that elacestrant retains activity after prior fulvestrant and across diverse ESR1 alleles (including Y537S and D538G), consistent with a mechanism of more complete and mutation‑agnostic ERα antagonism and degradation in ESR1‑mutant HR+/HER2− metastatic breast cancer.[17],[19],[23]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HR+/HER2– metastatic breast cancer, elacestrant shows its clearest benefit in *ESR1*-mutated tumors after prior endocrine therapy plus a CDK4/6 inhibitor. In EMERALD, elacestrant significantly improved progression‑free survival (PFS) versus standard endocrine monotherapy, with the effect strongly enriched in *ESR1*-mutant disease and particularly in patients whose prior ET+CDK4/6i duration was ≥12 months, where median PFS reached 8.6 months versus 1.9 months with standard care.[3,5,6] These data support a precision-oncology role for elacestrant as biomarker-driven endocrine monotherapy that can defer chemotherapy or more toxic combinations.[3,5,6]

Within HR+/HER2– disease, *ESR1* mutations define the key target population. EMERALD subgroup analyses show consistent PFS benefit across *ESR1* hotspot variants (D538G, Y537S/N), across co‑alterations (*PIK3CA*, *TP53*), and in HER2‑low tumors, without clear loss of efficacy in visceral or high‑burden disease.[3,5,8] The NICE appraisal and expert commentaries therefore emphasize routine *ESR1* testing (preferably via ctDNA) at progression after ET+CDK4/6i to select patients most likely to benefit.[4,6] Early‑stage, ER+/HER2– tumors in the ELIPSE window trial also showed marked Ki67 suppression and a shift toward a more endocrine‑sensitive phenotype, indicating biologic activity in luminal disease but without clinical outcome data yet.[1,5]

For HER2‑positive, triple‑negative, or gBRCA‑mutated breast cancer, there are no dedicated clinical data supporting elacestrant outside the HR+/HER2–, *ESR1*-mutated setting.[3–6] HER2‑low but HER2‑negative, HR+ tumors appear to remain ER‑driven, as EMERALD subgroup analyses showed similar elacestrant benefit in HER2‑low versus other HR+/HER2– tumors, but this does not extend to HER2‑amplified disease.[3] In TNBC and gBRCA‑mutated tumors, current evidence and treatment algorithms prioritize chemotherapy, PARP inhibitors, and HER2‑targeted or antibody–drug conjugate therapies; elacestrant is not integrated into these subtype‑specific pathways at present.[4,6]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

No specific source URLs were provided in the source material for this section, so detailed label‑ and trial‑based numeric data cannot be cited. The following is therefore limited to a high‑level, non‑quantitative synthesis consistent with the user’s description but without source‑linked figures or identifiers.

Elacestrant (Orserdu) does not have an absolute contraindication listed in major regulatory summaries; instead, use is guided by warnings and precautions, dose‑modification rules, and organ‑function and drug–drug interaction considerations. The most consistently emphasized risks are gastrointestinal intolerance (notably nausea and vomiting), dyslipidemia, and embryo‑fetal toxicity. Pregnancy and breastfeeding are discouraged, and effective contraception is required during and shortly after treatment because of teratogenicity signals from non‑clinical studies.

Gastrointestinal toxicity is the main dose‑limiting clinical issue, typically presenting as low‑grade nausea, vomiting, decreased appetite, and dyspepsia, with a minority of patients requiring dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation. Lipid abnormalities (hypercholesterolemia and hypertriglyceridemia) are common enough that baseline and periodic lipid monitoring are recommended, with consideration of lipid‑lowering therapy when appropriate. 

Hepatic function and drug interactions are key safety determinants: elacestrant exposure increases with worsening hepatic impairment, leading to recommended dose reduction in moderate impairment and avoidance in severe impairment. As a CYP3A substrate and transporter inhibitor, it requires careful review of concomitant medications, especially strong CYP3A modulators and sensitive P‑gp/BCRP substrates. No consistent signal of clinically meaningful QTc prolongation, myelosuppression, or specific cardiac/ocular toxicity has emerged from the available trial and early post‑marketing data, but early treatment weeks warrant closer monitoring, particularly in older, comorbid, or heavily pretreated patients.
