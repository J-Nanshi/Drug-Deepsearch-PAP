## 1. Drug Summary

Alpelisib is an oral, α‑isoform–selective class I PI3K inhibitor (p110α) developed by Novartis and marketed as Piqray for oncology indications and Vijoice for PIK3CA‑related overgrowth spectrum.[3],[7],[19] In breast cancer, it is FDA‑approved in combination with the ER antagonist fulvestrant for postmenopausal women and men with hormone receptor–positive (HR+), HER2‑negative, *PIK3CA*‑mutated advanced or metastatic disease whose cancer has progressed on or after endocrine therapy.[6],[7],[17],[18] The recommended breast cancer dose is 300 mg orally once daily with food, together with standard‑dose fulvestrant.[7],[17]

Mechanistically, alpelisib selectively inhibits the p110α catalytic subunit encoded by *PIK3CA*, a key driver of PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling frequently activated by *PIK3CA* mutations or PTEN loss in HR+/HER2– breast cancer.[13],[15],[18] Inhibition of PI3Kα reduces downstream AKT/mTOR signaling, thereby suppressing tumor cell growth and survival and helping to overcome endocrine resistance linked to PI3K pathway hyperactivation.[2],[13],[18] In the phase III SOLAR‑1 trial, alpelisib plus fulvestrant significantly prolonged progression‑free survival versus fulvestrant alone in *PIK3CA*‑mutated HR+/HER2– advanced breast cancer (11.0 vs 5.7 months; HR 0.65), with a numerically longer overall survival (39.3 vs 31.4 months) that did not reach statistical significance, establishing *PIK3CA* mutation as a predictive biomarker and positioning alpelisib as a precision‑oncology therapy for this molecularly defined subgroup.[4],[6],[13],[17],[18]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Alpelisib is an oral, PI3Kα‑specific small‑molecule kinase inhibitor that targets the p110α catalytic subunit encoded by *PIK3CA*, a key oncogene in epithelial cancers including breast cancer.[7,16] Class I PI3K is a p85–p110 heterodimer; upon activation by receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) or G‑protein–coupled receptors, p110α converts phosphatidylinositol‑4,5‑bisphosphate (PIP2) to phosphatidylinositol‑3,4,5‑triphosphate (PIP3), which recruits and activates AKT and downstream mTOR complexes, driving protein synthesis, cell growth, survival, and metabolism.[5,6,8,16] The tumor suppressor PTEN counteracts this by dephosphorylating PIP3 back to PIP2, and PTEN loss further augments PI3K pathway output.[5,6]

Oncogenic *PIK3CA* mutations cluster in the helical (E542K, E545K) and kinase (H1047R) domains of p110α, leading to constitutive PI3K activity and persistent AKT/mTOR signaling independent of upstream receptor input.[2,4,5] These driver mutations are particularly enriched in hormone receptor–positive (HR+) breast cancer, where 35–40% of HR+ and 25–30% of HER2‑positive tumors harbor *PIK3CA* alterations, and multi‑mutant *PIK3CA* in cis confers heightened PI3K dependency and sensitivity to p110α inhibition.[1] In HR+/HER2– metastatic breast cancer, *PIK3CA* mutations are frequent and define the population in which PI3K pathway inhibition has translated into a standard of care.[2,5,16]

By selectively inhibiting PI3Kα, alpelisib reduces PIP3 levels, thereby decreasing AKT phosphorylation and downstream mTOR signaling, which impairs tumor cell proliferation and survival.[5,9–11,16] The FDA label describes alpelisib as a kinase inhibitor whose mechanism of action is mediated through inhibition of the PI3K/AKT pathway, with on‑target metabolic effects such as hyperglycemia reflecting blockade of PI3K‑dependent insulin signaling.[9–11,16] Inhibition of PI3Kα in insulin‑responsive tissues diminishes AKT activation, GLUT4 translocation, and the suppression of gluconeogenesis and glycogen synthase kinase‑3, mechanistically explaining the clinically observed hyperglycemia and occasional ketoacidosis.[16]

Clinically, alpelisib is approved in combination with fulvestrant for adults with HR‑positive, HER2‑negative, *PIK3CA*‑mutated advanced or metastatic breast cancer after progression on endocrine therapy.[9–11,14,16] In this luminal subtype, endocrine therapy and PI3K signaling form parallel survival pathways; genomic activation of PI3K via *PIK3CA* mutation is a central mechanism of endocrine resistance.[5,16] Phase III data (SOLAR‑1) showed that selective PI3Kα inhibition with alpelisib plus fulvestrant significantly improved progression‑free outcomes in *PIK3CA*‑mutated HR+/HER2– disease compared with fulvestrant alone, and benefit was largely confined to the *PIK3CA*‑altered cohort, supporting *PIK3CA* mutation as a predictive biomarker of response.[5,11,16] These results underlie the precision‑oncology paradigm in which pharmacologic suppression of PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling restores endocrine sensitivity and achieves meaningful disease control in *PIK3CA*‑mutated HR+/HER2– breast cancer.

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HR+/HER2– metastatic breast cancer, alpelisib plus fulvestrant significantly improves PFS over fulvestrant alone in PIK3CA‑mutated tumors (11.0 vs 5.7 months; HR 0.65) in SOLAR‑1, leading to regulatory approval and establishing PIK3CA mutation as a key predictive biomarker.[2,4,7,8,9,12] BYLieve and real‑world series show maintained biological activity after prior CDK4/6 inhibitors but with modest response rates and frequent dose interruptions or discontinuations due to toxicity, especially hyperglycemia and rash.[8,9,12] Overall survival benefit remains numerically favorable but not definitively proven, and cost‑effectiveness and tolerability constrain broad use in later‑line settings.[4,8,12]

In HER2‑positive disease, PIK3CA mutations are common and associated with resistance to HER2‑targeted therapy, providing a strong biologic rationale for PI3Kα inhibition.[9,10] A phase I trial of alpelisib with trastuzumab and the HER3 antibody LJM716 in PIK3CA‑mutant HER2+ metastatic breast cancer showed limited objective responses but durable stable disease in some patients, with substantial gastrointestinal and metabolic toxicity that curtailed further development of this triplet.[10] Early T‑DM1–alpelisib combinations have reported encouraging response rates, but data remain phase I and non‑comparative, so the clinical role of alpelisib in HER2+ disease is still investigational.[9,10]

For triple‑negative breast cancer, PIK3CA mutations occur in a minority of cases; in a phase I/II study of alpelisib plus nab‑paclitaxel in HER2‑negative metastatic disease, activity was seen in both HR+ and TNBC, with higher clinical benefit and longer PFS in PIK3CA‑mutated tumors and one long‑lasting response in PIK3CA‑mutant TNBC.[7] These findings support PI3K pathway dependence in a biomarker‑defined TNBC subset, but alpelisib is not yet standard outside trials, and ongoing phase II/III studies (e.g., EPIK‑B3, NCT04251533) are needed to define benefit.[7]

In gBRCA‑mutated breast cancer, treatment paradigms are dominated by PARP inhibitors, which improve PFS in gBRCA‑mutated HER2‑negative disease.[1,3] Current biomarker‑driven algorithms view PIK3CA and gBRCA testing as parallel pathways, and available guidelines and reviews do not demonstrate any specific incremental benefit of alpelisib beyond its role in PIK3CA‑mutant HR+/HER2– tumors.[1,3,8]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Alpelisib has no absolute contraindications beyond hypersensitivity to the drug or its excipients in current guidance, but its use is constrained by a characteristic toxicity profile dominated by metabolic and dermatologic adverse events. Expert consensus and guidelines position alpelisib as a preferred second‑line option in HR+/HER2–, *PIK3CA*‑mutated advanced breast cancer, with the caveat that patients should have acceptable baseline fasting plasma glucose and HbA1c and no uncontrolled diabetes, given the high risk of on‑target hyperglycemia.[3,8] Pregnancy and lactation are contraindicated: PI3K pathway inhibitors including alpelisib are considered potentially teratogenic, and effective contraception is recommended for all patients of childbearing potential during treatment and for 1 week after the last dose; breastfeeding should likewise be avoided during treatment and for 1 week after discontinuation.[3]

Hyperglycemia is the most frequent and clinically important toxicity, reflecting PI3Kα‑mediated insulin resistance. In SOLAR‑1, alpelisib plus fulvestrant significantly improved PFS and OS but at the cost of substantial rates of hyperglycemia, necessitating proactive metabolic monitoring and management.[3,8] Consensus recommendations for PAM‑pathway inhibitors emphasize metformin as first‑line therapy for treatment‑emergent hyperglycemia, early endocrinology involvement when glucose remains poorly controlled, and dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation for persistent high‑grade events.[3] Because PAM‑pathway inhibition reduces insulin sensitivity, insulin doses may need to be increased in patients with pre‑existing diabetes.[3]

Rash and diarrhea are also common, mechanism‑based toxicities with important implications for adherence. Review of clinical experience with alpelisib highlights hyperglycemia, rash, and diarrhea as the principal adverse events, generally manageable with “intensive monitoring and timely intervention,” allowing most patients to remain on treatment.[8] For PAM‑pathway inhibitors as a class, expert consensus recommends prophylactic nonsedating antihistamines (e.g., cetirizine or loratadine 10 mg daily) starting at initiation to reduce incidence and severity of rash, together with skin‑care measures (mild soaps, fragrance‑free detergents, emollients, sunscreen).[3] Diarrhea should be managed with loperamide, aggressive oral hydration, and appropriate dose modifications; recurrent events may warrant secondary loperamide prophylaxis.[3]

Overall, safe use of alpelisib in breast cancer requires careful patient selection, baseline assessment of metabolic comorbidities, and structured monitoring algorithms for glucose, skin, and gastrointestinal toxicity. International consensus and guideline‑aligned reviews stress that with appropriate toxicity prevention and early intervention—particularly for hyperglycemia, rash, and diarrhea—alpelisib can be delivered safely and effectively to eligible HR+/HER2–/*PIK3CA*‑mutated patients.[3,4,8]

**Precision‑oncology perspective:** Across phase I–III studies, alpelisib’s clinical benefit is tightly linked to PI3K‑pathway activation—predominantly PIK3CA mutations and, in TNBC, PTEN loss—with negligible benefit in PIK3CA‑wild‑type cohorts. SOLAR‑1 and BYLieve establish alpelisib + endocrine therapy as a genomically selected option in HR+/HER2– disease, while EPIK‑B3 and ALPHABET extend this biomarker‑driven strategy into TNBC and HER2+ settings. ctDNA‑based PIK3CA testing and dynamic ctDNA profiling (e.g., emerging PTEN loss at progression) are increasingly integrated into trial design, refining selection and resistance monitoring for PI3Kα‑targeted therapy [2,7,9–11,14,15].
