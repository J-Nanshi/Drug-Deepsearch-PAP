## 1. Drug Summary

Pembrolizumab is a humanized IgG4 monoclonal antibody targeting the programmed cell death‑1 (PD‑1) receptor on activated T cells, blocking its interaction with PD‑L1 and PD‑L2 and thereby releasing inhibitory signaling that constrains effector T‑cell function and antitumor immunity.[2][3] By restoring cytotoxic T‑cell activity within the tumor microenvironment, pembrolizumab exemplifies PD‑1 immune checkpoint inhibition as a precision immunotherapy across multiple solid tumors.

In breast cancer, the clearest and most practice‑changing role of pembrolizumab is in triple‑negative breast cancer (TNBC). For metastatic TNBC, pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy is an approved first‑line standard for PD‑L1–positive disease (CPS ≥10), based on KEYNOTE‑355 (NCT02819518), which showed clinically meaningful improvements in progression‑free and overall survival compared with chemotherapy alone.[1][3][4][6] In early high‑risk stage II–III TNBC, pembrolizumab added to neoadjuvant chemotherapy followed by adjuvant pembrolizumab (KEYNOTE‑522, NCT03036488) significantly increased pathologic complete response and improved event‑free and overall survival, leading to regulatory approval irrespective of PD‑L1 status.[2][5][6]

Beyond TNBC, pembrolizumab and other ICIs are being actively explored in HR+/HER2− and HER2‑positive breast cancer. Current data show more modest and heterogeneous activity in these less immunogenic subtypes, with potential benefit emerging only in selected biomarker‑enriched or combination‑therapy contexts.[6][8] Overall, pembrolizumab is now integrated as a standard component of chemo‑immunotherapy for PD‑L1–positive metastatic TNBC and high‑risk early‑stage TNBC, while its role in other breast cancer subtypes remains investigational.

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Pembrolizumab is a highly selective humanized IgG4 monoclonal antibody that binds PD‑1 on activated T cells, B cells, and other immune cells, preventing engagement with its ligands PD‑L1 and PD‑L2.[3,5,8] PD‑1’s cytoplasmic tail contains an ITIM and an ITSM; ligand binding induces phosphorylation of these motifs and recruitment of SHP2, which dephosphorylates proximal T‑cell receptor (TCR) and CD28 signaling components (e.g., ZAP‑70, PI3K), thereby suppressing proliferation, cytokine production, and cytotoxic function.[1,3,5,8] Structural work shows that full SHP2 activation requires bidentate binding of phosphorylated ITIM and ITSM to the N‑SH2 and C‑SH2 domains of SHP2, respectively, locking SHP2 in an open, active conformation that enforces T‑cell inhibition.[1] By blocking PD‑1–PD‑L1/PD‑L2 ligation, pembrolizumab prevents ITIM/ITSM phosphorylation and SHP2 recruitment, restores PI3K–AKT and RAS–ERK signaling in T cells, and re‑establishes effector cytokine secretion and cytotoxicity against breast tumor cells.[1,5,8]

In breast tumors, PD‑1 is highly upregulated on tumor‑infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), which display an exhausted phenotype with impaired TCR signaling, reduced IL‑2/IFN‑γ production, and increased SHP2 expression, while PD‑L1 is frequently expressed on tumor and myeloid cells; this PD‑1/SHP2 axis correlates with higher grade, nodal involvement, and metastasis in breast cancer.[3] Triple‑negative breast cancer (TNBC) exhibits relatively high TIL density, tumor mutational burden, and PD‑L1 expression, and pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy has become standard in high‑risk early‑stage and PD‑L1‑positive metastatic TNBC, where PD‑1 blockade can effectively reinvigorate pre‑existing antitumor T cells.[10,11,14,18,21] HER2‑positive disease also shows enriched TILs and PD‑L1 compared with HR+/HER2– tumors, and early trials of pembrolizumab with HER2‑targeted therapy demonstrate activity mainly in PD‑L1‑positive subsets, suggesting that PD‑1 blockade can augment HER2‑directed immunity but with more modest impact than in TNBC.[18,21] HR+/HER2– breast cancers are less immunogenic, with lower TIL and PD‑L1 levels; in this context, pembrolizumab still acts by relieving PD‑1–mediated suppression but typically requires combination with chemotherapy, CDK4/6 inhibition, or endocrine therapy to increase antigen release and inflame the microenvironment, and current data show only modest, biomarker‑enriched benefit.[17,18,21,22]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HR+/HER2− disease, pembrolizumab is not approved and metastatic trials have generally shown modest activity, with small single‑arm or randomized studies of pembrolizumab combinations yielding limited progression‑free survival gains and no clear overall‑survival advantage in unselected populations.[3,4,17] In contrast, neoadjuvant chemo‑pembrolizumab improves pCR in high‑risk early ER+/HER2− tumors: I‑SPY2 nearly doubled estimated pCR (30% vs 13%), and KEYNOTE‑756 demonstrated pCR 24.3% vs 15.6%, with numerically greater benefit in PD‑L1‑high and ER‑low (≤10%) subsets.[2,4,7,8] Across studies, benefit appears enriched in immune‑inflamed luminal tumors characterized by higher PD‑L1 CPS, stromal TILs and T‑cell–inflamed gene signatures, whereas the broader HR+/HER2− population remains largely immunotherapy‑insensitive.[1,2,4,17]

For HER2‑positive breast cancer, evidence for pembrolizumab is limited to small early‑phase or basket experiences; no phase III data demonstrate added benefit, and current systematic reviews emphasize that checkpoint inhibitors are not standard of care in this subtype.[1,3,17] HER2‑enriched tumors often show higher TILs than luminal cancers, but predictive value of PD‑L1 or TILs for pembrolizumab benefit is unproven, so use remains investigational.[1,17]

In TNBC, pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy is established in both early and metastatic settings. KEYNOTE‑522 showed that adding neoadjuvant and adjuvant pembrolizumab to anthracycline‑ and taxane‑based chemotherapy increased pCR (≈65% vs 51%) and improved 3‑year EFS (HR ~0.63), regardless of PD‑L1 status, defining a new standard for high‑risk stage II–III TNBC.[3,5,6,10] In first‑line metastatic TNBC, KEYNOTE‑355 demonstrated clinically meaningful PFS and OS gains confined to PD‑L1 CPS ≥10 tumors (median OS 23.0 vs 16.1 months; HR 0.73), leading to regulatory approval; CPS ≥10 is now the key biomarker for patient selection.[1,3,5,6,9,10,13,14] Exploratory work from KEYNOTE‑086 and KEYNOTE‑119 further links higher PD‑L1, TILs, T‑cell–inflamed gene expression and TMB with greater pembrolizumab benefit, especially when used earlier and in combination with chemotherapy.[1,11,15,16]

Across subtypes, gBRCA/HRD status is associated with a more immunostimulatory phenotype and heightened sensitivity to platinum and PARP inhibition, but does not consistently predict pembrolizumab benefit on its own.[1,11,17] In TNBC, PARP inhibitor–pembrolizumab combinations such as niraparib–pembrolizumab have yielded the highest response rates in gBRCA‑mutated cohorts (ORR ~67%), suggesting synergy between DNA damage and PD‑1 blockade.[5,11,12,17] In HR+/HER2− disease, PARP–ICI strategies and broader HRD‑directed immunotherapy remain exploratory, and current evidence supports using BRCA/HRD primarily to guide DNA‑damaging and PARP‑inhibitor therapy rather than pembrolizumab selection.[1,3,11,17]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Pembrolizumab has no absolute disease-based contraindications in the EMA product information, but its use is restricted to specialist oncology settings, with mandatory PD‑L1 testing for triple‑negative breast cancer (TNBC) indications and treatment only in patients meeting the approved TNBC criteria.[3],[4] Key safety governance measures include treatment under experienced physicians, use of patient alert cards, and clear instructions to delay or discontinue dosing when significant immune‑related toxicity occurs.[3],[4],[7]

Immune‑related adverse events (irAEs) arise from PD‑1 blockade breaking immune tolerance and can affect any organ system; cutaneous and endocrine events are most common, while cardiac and neurologic irAEs are less frequent but potentially severe.[6] EMA notes that side effects are generally manageable and similar to other cancer therapies, but highlights immune‑related toxicities as a central risk, requiring dose interruption or permanent discontinuation when severe.[4],[7] Management typically relies on corticosteroids and other immunosuppressants, which may attenuate antitumor immunity and therefore must be balanced against oncologic benefit.[6]

From a precision oncology perspective, patient selection for breast cancer—particularly TNBC—must integrate PD‑L1 status, overall performance status, comorbid autoimmune disease, and ability to tolerate overlapping chemotherapy and immune‑mediated toxicities.[3],[4],[6] EMA and expert reviews emphasise early recognition, grading, and multidisciplinary management of irAEs, as most are reversible with timely intervention; biomarker‑based approaches (e.g., PD‑L1, MSI‑H/dMMR, TMB) are used for response selection and are under study to better predict irAE risk and refine individualised risk–benefit assessment.[4],[6]
