## Drug Summary

Capivasertib (AZD5363; Truqap) is an oral, ATP-competitive, small‑molecule inhibitor of all three AKT isoforms (AKT1/2/3), a central kinase node in the PI3K–AKT–mTOR signaling cascade that regulates tumor cell proliferation, survival, metabolism, and endocrine resistance.[1–3,11,12] By binding the AKT catalytic site, capivasertib blocks phosphorylation of downstream AKT substrates and induces antiproliferative and pro‑apoptotic effects in breast cancer models, with enhanced activity in tumors harboring activating *PIK3CA* or *AKT1* mutations or *PTEN* loss.[3,8,11,12]

Regulatory approvals in the United States, Japan, and other regions specify capivasertib in combination with fulvestrant for adults with hormone receptor–positive, HER2‑negative (HR+/HER2–) locally advanced or metastatic breast cancer with one or more *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN* alterations, following progression on at least one prior endocrine‑based regimen in the metastatic setting or recurrence within 12 months of completing adjuvant therapy.[2,3,7–9,11,14] This biomarker‑defined indication reflects that ~30–50% of HR+/HER2– tumors harbor PI3K–AKT–PTEN‑axis alterations and that clinical benefit in CAPItello‑291 (NCT04305496) was concentrated in the alteration‑positive subgroup.[2,3,7,9,11]

Within the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway, PI3K activation and PTEN loss increase PIP3, recruiting and activating AKT, which in turn drives mTORC1‑dependent protein synthesis, cell‑cycle progression, and anti‑apoptotic signaling.[1–3] Capivasertib acts downstream of PI3K and PTEN to interrupt this hyperactivated signaling, thereby targeting a pathway mutated in more than half of breast cancers and in ~30–50% of HR+/HER2– disease.[1–3] In the phase III CAPItello‑291 trial (NCT04305496), adding intermittent capivasertib 400 mg twice daily (4 days on/3 days off) to fulvestrant significantly improved progression‑free survival versus placebo–fulvestrant in HR+/HER2– advanced/metastatic breast cancer that had progressed on aromatase inhibitor therapy, with a hazard ratio of 0.60 in the overall population and 0.50 in the *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN*‑altered subgroup, supporting its role as a first‑in‑class AKT‑targeted therapy in this setting.[3,4,7,9–11,12,14]

## Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Capivasertib (Truqap) is an orally bioavailable, ATP‑competitive inhibitor of all three AKT isoforms (AKT1/2/3), the central kinases in the PI3K–AKT–mTOR pathway that regulates tumor cell metabolism, proliferation, and survival.[1–3,4,6,8,10] Biochemically, capivasertib binds the AKT catalytic site, blocking phosphorylation of canonical AKT substrates including PRAS40 and GSK3β and downstream mTORC1 effectors such as S6 and 4E‑BP1, leading to inhibition of protein synthesis, cell‑cycle progression, and survival signaling.[4,6,8] In large in‑vitro panels, it inhibits proliferation of many solid‑tumor cell lines, with particular sensitivity in breast cancer and enhanced activity in models harboring activating *PIK3CA* or *AKT1* mutations or *PTEN* loss.[1–3,4,6,8,10] In ER‑positive and HER2‑amplified breast cancer xenografts, capivasertib monotherapy and combinations with anti‑HER2 agents or endocrine therapy suppress tumor growth, consistent with on‑target pathway blockade in vivo.[1,4,8]

In HR+/HER2– breast cancer, the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway is hyperactivated in ~30–50% of tumors through *PIK3CA* mutations, *AKT1* activating mutations, and *PTEN* inactivating alterations, and this activation is a key driver of endocrine resistance.[2,3,4,7,10,14] AKT signaling supports estrogen‑independent ER activity and cooperates with ER‑driven transcription; its activation is also linked to resistance to anti‑HER2 therapy, chemotherapy, and CDK4/6 inhibitors via PTEN loss and other upstream events.[4,8,14] By inhibiting AKT1/2/3, capivasertib reduces phosphorylation of ER‑cooperating survival pathways and mTORC1‑driven translation, thereby attenuating estrogen‑independent growth signals and restoring sensitivity to ER antagonism.[1–4,6,8,10,14] In ER‑positive, endocrine‑resistant breast cancer models, capivasertib combined with fulvestrant or other endocrine agents produces deeper suppression of ER‑regulated transcription and more durable tumor control than either agent alone, particularly in *PIK3CA*‑mutant or *PTEN*‑deficient settings.[1,4,8]

Clinically, these mechanistic effects translate into preferential benefit in tumors with genetically “hard‑wired” PI3K‑pathway activation. In the phase III CAPItello‑291 trial (NCT04305496), adding capivasertib to fulvestrant significantly improved progression‑free survival (PFS) in HR+/HER2– advanced breast cancer overall (HR 0.60) and with a larger effect in the *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN*‑altered population (PFS 7.3 vs 3.1 months; HR 0.50).[1–3,5,7,10] FDA and subsequent analyses concluded that the benefit–risk profile is clearly favorable in biomarker‑positive disease but not in biomarker‑negative tumors, aligning with the concept that capivasertib’s primary antitumor activity derives from direct inhibition of the genetically activated AKT node.[2,3,7,10] Nevertheless, some activity in biomarker‑negative subgroups and in preclinical models lacking canonical *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN* alterations suggests that capivasertib can also counteract non‑genetic or upstream PI3K/AKT activation that emerges under endocrine pressure, likely by shutting down convergent survival signaling at AKT.[3–5,7,10,14]

At the resistance interface, multiple adaptive mechanisms can attenuate capivasertib efficacy. Proteomic analyses of *PIK3CA*‑mutated tumors and breast cancer cell lines indicate that high baseline mTORC1‑driven protein translation, reflected by upregulation of ribosomal and translation‑initiation proteins, correlates with intrinsic resistance to capivasertib monotherapy despite similar AKT1/2 levels, implicating downstream mTORC1 activation as a key bypass pathway.[6] Preclinical work also shows that AKT blockade induces feedback upregulation and phosphorylation of upstream receptor tyrosine kinases, particularly HER3 (and to a lesser extent HER2), and that elevated SGK1—a kinase regulated by PI3K/mTORC2—can substitute for AKT to maintain survival, both of which undermine sustained pathway suppression.[4,6] Conversely, tumors with *PIK3CA* or *AKT1* mutations in the absence of mTORC1‑activating lesions (*MTOR*, *TSC2*) and without RAS pathway activation appear more sensitive to capivasertib, underscoring that its most robust effects occur where AKT is the dominant PI3K effector and downstream escape routes are limited.[4,6,8,10,14]

## Pathways (Brief Overview)

Capivasertib is a selective ATP-competitive inhibitor of AKT; thus, its dominant pharmacodynamic effects in breast cancer occur through inhibition of the PI3K/AKT/mTOR axis, with downstream modulation of cell‑cycle progression, apoptosis, autophagy, and survival signaling. The PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway is one of the most deregulated oncogenic cascades in breast cancer, frequently driven by *PIK3CA*, *AKT1* (including PH‑domain E17K mutations), and *PTEN* alterations, and acts as a master regulator of proliferation, metabolism, and resistance to therapy.[1,4] Inhibition of AKT reduces phosphorylation of mTORC1 effectors such as 4E‑BP1 and S6K, thereby constraining protein synthesis and cell‑cycle transit, while relieving AKT‑mediated suppression of pro‑apoptotic proteins (e.g., Bad, caspase‑9) and promoting mitochondrial apoptosis.[2,3,5]

Experimental work in human breast cancer models targeting PI3K/AKT/mTOR shows that pathway suppression consistently decreases p‑AKT and p‑mTOR, induces S‑phase or G0/G1 arrest, and activates intrinsic apoptosis and/or autophagy, underscoring how AKT blockade can reprogram cell‑fate decisions.[2,3] Crosstalk between PI3K/AKT/mTOR and MAPK/ERK enables compensatory activation of RAF–MEK–ERK when PI3K–mTOR is inhibited, a key mechanism of adaptive resistance; dual targeting of these kinase networks is therefore a rational strategy in tumors with strong RTK/RAS input.[1,5] In breast cancer stem‑like populations, PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling supports self‑renewal and tumorigenicity, so AKT inhibition by capivasertib is expected to impact not only bulk tumor cells but also CSC-associated pathways such as Wnt/β‑catenin, STAT3, and TGF‑β that intersect with PI3K/AKT/mTOR.[1]

## Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

Across HR+/HER2– disease, multiple phase II–III datasets (FAKTION, CAPItello‑291) show that adding capivasertib to fulvestrant improves progression‑free survival versus fulvestrant alone in aromatase‑inhibitor–resistant advanced breast cancer, including after prior CDK4/6 inhibition.[1,5–7,13,14] Benefit is consistently greater in tumors harboring *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN* alterations, which drove regulatory approvals by FDA and other agencies, although some activity is still observed in biomarker‑negative or unknown tumors.[5–9,11,14]

For HER2+ breast cancer, available reviews and trial summaries focus on HR+/HER2– or HER2‑low populations; they do not report dedicated efficacy cohorts of HER2‑amplified disease treated with capivasertib, so any subtype‑specific effect in HER2‑driven tumors remains uncharacterized.[6,11,14]

In triple‑negative breast cancer, the phase II PAKT trial reported progression‑free and overall survival gains when capivasertib was combined with paclitaxel in metastatic disease, with benefit enriched in *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN*‑altered tumors, leading to the ongoing phase III CAPItello‑290 without mandatory biomarker selection.[6,11,14] These data support a biomarker‑driven role for AKT inhibition in TNBC but remain earlier‑phase and less definitive than in HR+/HER2– disease.

For gBRCA‑mutated breast cancer, current clinical evidence centers on PARP inhibitors; capivasertib‑specific outcomes in this genetically defined subgroup are not reported, and its potential role remains speculative within broader discussions of PI3K/AKT pathway targeting.[6,11,14]

## Contraindications and Safety

Capivasertib (TRUQAP) has a single labeled contraindication: severe hypersensitivity to capivasertib or any tablet component.[1,10] No boxed warning is present on the FDA label; key risks are captured under Warnings and Precautions for hyperglycemia, diarrhea, cutaneous adverse reactions, and embryo‑fetal toxicity.[1,3,10,21]

Metabolic toxicity is central. Hyperglycemia occurred in ~16–18% of patients in CAPItello‑291, with grade ≥3 events in ~2–3%, including rare diabetic ketoacidosis and diabetic metabolic decompensation.[11,17,19,21] The label mandates baseline and periodic fasting glucose and HbA1c, and stepwise interruption, dose reduction, or permanent discontinuation depending on fasting glucose thresholds.[1,3] Case reports describe marked, dosing‑day–restricted hyperglycemia and capivasertib‑induced DKA even in patients without prior diabetes, underscoring the need for early, real‑time glucose monitoring and prompt initiation of antihyperglycemics or insulin in high‑risk patients.[13,18,14]

Gastrointestinal and dermatologic toxicities are frequent and dose‑limiting. Diarrhea occurs in ~60–72% (grade ≥3 ~7–9%) and rash or broader cutaneous reactions in ~38–58% (grade ≥3 ~12–17%), typically within the first 2–3 weeks.[4,11,19,21,23] FDA labeling and CAPItello‑291 management algorithms emphasize early loperamide, hydration, skin care, and graded dose interruption, reduction, or discontinuation for persistent grade ≥2 diarrhea or significant rash.[1,3,19] Compared with PI3K inhibitors such as alpelisib, capivasertib shows a somewhat lower incidence of severe hyperglycemia but still requires vigilant monitoring, particularly in patients with pre‑existing metabolic risk.[15,24]

Hepatic and other systemic safety signals are relatively limited. LiverTox notes transient ALT/AST elevations (ALT >5×ULN in ~3%) without documented jaundiced liver injury; routine LFT monitoring is not mandated, but therapy should be held for marked enzyme elevations or any elevation with jaundice or symptoms.[5,22] Capivasertib is primarily metabolized by CYP3A4; strong or moderate CYP3A inhibitors/inducers should be avoided or require capivasertib dose adjustment to mitigate excess exposure and toxicity.[1,3,5,10] TRUQAP is embryo‑fetotoxic, and patients must avoid pregnancy and breastfeeding, with fulvestrant reproductive precautions also applying.[1,3,5,10,21]

## Key Clinical Trials

Capivasertib (AZD5363) has been evaluated mainly in HR‑positive/HER2‑negative (HR+/HER2–) advanced or metastatic breast cancer in combination with endocrine therapy, particularly fulvestrant.

The randomized phase II FAKTION trial (NCT01992952) enrolled 140 postmenopausal women with ER+/HER2– metastatic or locally advanced inoperable breast cancer relapsing or progressing on aromatase inhibitors. Fulvestrant plus capivasertib (400 mg twice daily, 4 days on/3 days off) improved median progression‑free survival (PFS) to 10.3 vs 4.8 months with fulvestrant plus placebo (HR 0.58, 95% CI 0.39–0.84) and, with longer follow‑up, median overall survival (OS) to 29.3 vs 23.4 months (HR 0.66, 95% CI 0.45–0.97).[1,5] Benefit was greatest in tumors with PI3K/AKT/PTEN pathway alterations (PFS 12.8 vs 4.6 months; OS 38.9 vs 20.0 months).[1]

The pivotal, double‑blind phase III CAPItello‑291 trial (NCT04305496) randomized 708 patients with HR+/HER2– advanced breast cancer progressing on or after aromatase inhibitor therapy (± prior CDK4/6 inhibitor) to capivasertib–fulvestrant or placebo–fulvestrant. In the overall population, median PFS was 7.2 vs 3.6 months (HR 0.60, 95% CI 0.51–0.71; P<0.001); in the AKT‑pathway–altered subgroup, PFS was 7.3 vs 3.1 months (HR 0.50, 95% CI 0.38–0.65; P<0.001).[4] Grade ≥3 rash (12.1%) and diarrhea (9.3%) were the most frequent high‑grade adverse events; treatment discontinuation due to adverse events occurred in 13.0% vs 2.3%.[4]

An extended Chinese cohort within CAPItello‑291 (also NCT04305496; 134 patients) confirmed benefit in an Asian population. Median PFS was 6.9 vs 2.8 months overall (HR 0.51, 95% CI 0.34–0.76), 5.7 vs 1.9 months in *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN*‑altered tumors (HR 0.41, 95% CI 0.19–0.85), and 9.2 vs 2.7 months in non‑altered tumors (HR 0.38, 95% CI 0.21–0.68).[3] Diarrhea (60.6%) and hyperglycemia (57.7%) were the most common adverse events, with discontinuation in 11.3% vs 3.2%.[3]

A pharmacoeconomic analysis using CAPItello‑291 data (NCT04305496) confirmed the magnitude of PFS benefit (7.2 vs 3.6 months; HR 0.60, 95% CI 0.51–0.71; P<0.001) but found capivasertib–fulvestrant not cost‑effective at current US or assumed Chinese pricing.[2] Collectively, FAKTION and CAPItello‑291 established capivasertib plus fulvestrant as an active regimen in AI‑resistant HR+/HER2– advanced breast cancer, particularly in tumors with *PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN* alterations.[1–5]
