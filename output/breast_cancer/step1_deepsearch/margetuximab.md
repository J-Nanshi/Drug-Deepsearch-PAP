## 1. Drug Summary

Margetuximab-cmkb (Margenza) is a mouse–human chimeric, Fc‑engineered IgG1 monoclonal antibody targeting HER2, approved in combination with chemotherapy for adults with advanced or metastatic HER2‑positive breast cancer who have received at least two prior anti‑HER2 regimens, including at least one for metastatic disease.[4–6,8] In the phase III SOPHIA trial (NCT02492711), conducted in heavily pretreated HER2‑positive advanced/metastatic breast cancer, margetuximab plus chemotherapy produced a modest but statistically significant improvement in centrally assessed progression‑free survival versus trastuzumab plus chemotherapy (median 5.8 vs 4.9 months; HR ~0.76), with numerically higher response rates and broadly similar overall survival (~21–22 months) and safety.[1–3,6,7]

Mechanistically, margetuximab binds the same HER2 epitope as trastuzumab and retains comparable Fc‑independent antiproliferative effects, but its Fc region is engineered with five amino‑acid substitutions to increase affinity for the activating Fcγ receptor CD16A and decrease affinity for the inhibitory Fcγ receptor CD32B, thereby enhancing antibody‑dependent cellular cytotoxicity and broader immune activation.[3,6,8–11] This Fc optimization is particularly relevant for the common low‑affinity CD16A‑158F variant, in which exploratory analyses from SOPHIA suggested a possible overall‑survival advantage for margetuximab compared with trastuzumab, while CD16A‑158VV patients may derive more benefit from trastuzumab.[1–3,8,10] Within the contemporary HER2‑positive metastatic breast cancer algorithm, margetuximab is positioned as a later‑line monoclonal‑antibody option after trastuzumab/pertuzumab‑taxane and antibody–drug conjugates (T‑DM1, trastuzumab deruxtecan), offering an Fc‑engineered strategy to re‑engage immune effector mechanisms in trastuzumab‑experienced disease.[6,8,10,11]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Margetuximab is a chimeric IgG1 monoclonal antibody derived from trastuzumab, retaining the same HER2 epitope specificity and Fc‑independent antiproliferative effects while introducing Fc engineering to enhance immune effector engagement.[3][4][5][8][9] Like trastuzumab, it binds to the extracellular domain of HER2 and inhibits HER2‑driven signaling, leading to reduced proliferation and survival of HER2‑overexpressing breast cancer cells.[1][4][9] These Fab‑mediated effects are relevant across HER2‑positive subtypes (HR+/HER2+ and HR–/HER2+), which are primarily driven by HER2 overexpression rather than hormone‑receptor status.[4][12][14]

The defining feature of margetuximab is its Fc optimization: five amino‑acid substitutions in the IgG1 Fc region (L235V, F243L, R292P, Y300L, P396L) increase affinity for the activating Fcγ receptor CD16A (FcγRIIIa) and decrease affinity for the inhibitory Fcγ receptor CD32B (FcγRIIb).[2][4][8] This design enhances antibody‑dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) by NK cells and other CD16A‑expressing effectors, and augments antibody‑dependent cellular phagocytosis and broader innate/adaptive immune responses to HER2.[2][4][7][8] Importantly, the engineered Fc shows elevated binding to both high‑affinity CD16A‑158V and low‑affinity CD16A‑158F allotypes, specifically addressing the reduced clinical benefit historically seen with trastuzumab in CD16A‑158F carriers, who constitute the majority of HER2‑positive patients.[3][4][5][7][8]

Preclinical and early‑phase clinical data demonstrate that margetuximab mediates stronger ADCC than trastuzumab across CD16A genotypes, with a proportionally greater gain in CD16A‑158F carriers and activity extending to trastuzumab‑resistant and lower‑HER2–expressing cells.[3][4][7][8] In the phase 3 SOPHIA trial in ERBB2‑positive advanced breast cancer, exploratory analyses showed that patients carrying at least one CD16A‑158F allele derived greater progression‑free survival benefit from margetuximab plus chemotherapy versus trastuzumab plus chemotherapy, whereas no clear benefit was observed in the smaller CD16A‑158VV subgroup.[3][5] Collectively, margetuximab’s mechanism in HER2‑positive breast cancer couples trastuzumab‑like HER2 signaling blockade with Fc‑engineered enhancement of activating FcγR engagement and ADCC, preferentially improving immune‑mediated tumor cell killing in the large subset of HER2‑positive patients with low‑affinity CD16A polymorphisms.[3][4][5][6][8][9]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

Across intrinsic breast cancer subtypes, clinically relevant data for margetuximab are almost entirely restricted to HER2‑positive disease. All pivotal trials in metastatic breast cancer, including SOPHIA (NCT02492711) and the Chinese phase II bridging study (NCT04262804), required HER2 overexpression/amplification by IHC/ISH; there are no efficacy datasets in HER2‑negative HR+/HER2–, triple‑negative, or gBRCA‑mutated cohorts independent of HER2 status.[8,10,13,17] Preclinical and early clinical work show activity in HER2‑overexpressing tumors and support Fc‑engineered enhancement of ADCC, particularly in patients carrying low‑affinity CD16A‑158F alleles.[2,6,8,11,13,17]

In HER2‑positive metastatic breast cancer—encompassing both HR‑positive/HER2+ (luminal B) and HR‑negative/HER2‑enriched subtypes—margetuximab plus chemotherapy produced modest but statistically significant improvements in progression‑free survival versus trastuzumab plus chemotherapy in heavily pretreated patients, leading to FDA approval as a later‑line option.[8,13,17] Exploratory analyses from SOPHIA and subsequent reports suggest greater benefit in CD16A‑158F carriers, consistent with the Fc‑engineering rationale, but this biomarker is not yet used for patient selection.[8,11,17] Phase I data (NCT01148849) demonstrated single‑agent activity in trastuzumab‑pretreated HER2‑positive breast cancer, confirming on‑target efficacy in this subtype.[2,4,6]

For HER2‑low HR+ or HER2‑low TNBC, available data show preclinical activity and limited clinical exploration, but no meaningful responses in a small phase II IHC2+/ISH‑negative cohort and no established role in breast cancer outside HER2‑positive disease.[10,12] There are no dedicated studies of margetuximab in gBRCA‑mutated breast cancer beyond the subset that is also HER2‑positive; PARP inhibitor–based strategies remain the evidence‑based standard for gBRCA‑mutated, HER2‑negative tumors.[1,5]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Specific regulatory contraindications and boxed warnings for margetuximab are not detailed in the available source; however, as a HER2‑targeted monoclonal antibody, its safety profile aligns with the class effects described for HER2‑directed agents in breast cancer.[1] Class‑related cardiotoxicity (decline in left ventricular ejection fraction, clinical heart failure) is a key concern with HER2‑targeted antibodies, and neoadjuvant/adjunct HER2 blockade trials highlighted asymptomatic LVEF decreases as a characteristic toxicity requiring cardiac monitoring.[1] For margetuximab, this implies baseline and periodic assessment of cardiac function and caution in patients with significant pre‑existing cardiac disease.

The review notes that margetuximab has demonstrated a “favorable safety profile” in combination with chemotherapy, with the most commonly reported adverse effects including fatigue, fever, vomiting, nausea, diarrhea, neutropenia, constipation, abdominal pain, headache, alopecia, palmar–plantar erythrodysesthesia, peripheral neuropathy, cough, reduced appetite, limb pain, and infusion‑related complications.[1] Infusion‑related reactions and other immune‑mediated events are therefore clinically relevant and should be managed with standard infusion‑reaction precautions used for HER2‑directed mAbs.

The article does not provide detailed regulatory warnings on embryo–fetal toxicity or hepatic injury specifically for margetuximab, but, by analogy to HER2‑targeted antibody experience summarized in the review, embryo–fetal risk and the need for pregnancy avoidance and careful toxicity monitoring are expected class considerations.[1] Overall, available data characterize margetuximab’s safety as manageable and consistent with HER2‑antibody–based regimens when used with appropriate monitoring and supportive care.[1]
