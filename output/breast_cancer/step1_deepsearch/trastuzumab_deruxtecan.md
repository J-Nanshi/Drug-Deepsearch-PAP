## 1. Drug Summary

Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd, fam‑trastuzumab deruxtecan‑nxki; Enhertu) is a HER2‑directed antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) comprising a humanized trastuzumab backbone linked via a cleavable tetrapeptide linker to deruxtecan, a potent topoisomerase I inhibitor; approximately eight DXd molecules are attached per antibody, enabling high cytotoxic payload delivery.[1][4][7] After binding HER2 on tumor cells, the ADC is internalized and the linker is cleaved in lysosomes, releasing deruxtecan, which induces DNA damage and apoptotic cell death; a bystander effect extends killing to neighboring HER2‑expressing cells.[1][7][12] This targeted design underpins its role as a precision oncology agent across a spectrum of HER2 expression levels, including HER2‑positive, HER2‑low, and HER2‑ultralow breast cancers.[4][7][8][11][12]

Regulatory approvals in breast cancer now span multiple biomarker‑defined populations. In HER2‑positive unresectable or metastatic disease, Enhertu is approved (regular approval in the U.S. and EU) for adults who have received at least one prior anti‑HER2‑based regimen in the metastatic, neoadjuvant, or adjuvant setting and relapsed within 6 months, based on the phase III DESTINY‑Breast03 trial (NCT03529110), where T‑DXd markedly prolonged progression‑free survival versus T‑DM1 and achieved high objective response rates.[2][9][13] For HER2‑low (IHC 1+ or 2+/ISH‑negative) metastatic breast cancer previously treated with chemotherapy, DESTINY‑Breast04 (NCT03734029) demonstrated superior progression‑free and overall survival versus physician’s‑choice chemotherapy, leading to FDA approval at 5.4 mg/kg every 3 weeks.[7][11] More recently, DESTINY‑Breast06 (NCT04494425) showed that in HR‑positive, HER2‑low or HER2‑ultralow metastatic breast cancer progressing after ≥1 endocrine therapy and no prior metastatic chemotherapy, T‑DXd significantly improved progression‑free survival versus single‑agent taxane or capecitabine, supporting its use earlier in the HR‑positive metastatic setting and extending the label to HER2‑ultralow disease.[4][8]

From a precision‑oncology perspective, T‑DXd has reshaped HER2 classification by demonstrating clinically actionable benefit in tumors with low or ultralow HER2 expression, which were historically managed as HER2‑negative and treated only with endocrine therapy or chemotherapy.[1][7][11][12] Current treatment algorithms position T‑DXd as: (1) preferred second‑line (and in some high‑risk scenarios first‑line or early‑line) therapy in HER2‑positive metastatic breast cancer after trastuzumab‑ and taxane‑based regimens; and (2) a key post‑endocrine option in HR‑positive, HER2‑low or HER2‑ultralow metastatic disease, often as the first cytotoxic‑containing regimen.[2][4][8][9][11][12] Precision use requires rigorous HER2 assessment (including HER2‑low/ultralow categories) with validated IHC assays and careful risk–benefit evaluation given characteristic toxicities—particularly interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis, myelosuppression, and gastrointestinal adverse events highlighted in FDA/EMA labeling and boxed warnings.[3][4][6][8][9][11][12]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd; fam‑trastuzumab deruxtecan‑nxki) is a HER2‑directed antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) in which a humanized anti‑HER2 IgG1 (trastuzumab backbone) is covalently linked to a highly potent topoisomerase I inhibitor payload (DXd, an exatecan derivative) via a cleavable tetrapeptide linker, with a high, homogeneous drug‑to‑antibody ratio of ~8:1.[1–4,13,18,22] After binding to HER2 on breast cancer cells, the ADC–receptor complex is internalized; lysosomal or tumor‑associated proteases then cleave the tetrapeptide linker, releasing membrane‑permeable DXd that enters the nucleus, stabilizes topoisomerase I–DNA complexes, and induces replication‑associated DNA damage and apoptotic cell death.[2,4,5,8,13,18,22] The trastuzumab moiety retains canonical HER2‑blocking and Fc‑mediated immune functions (e.g. antibody‑dependent cell‑mediated cytotoxicity), while DXd provides potent, non–cross‑resistant cytotoxicity distinct from microtubule‑targeting payloads such as DM1 in T‑DM1.[6,8,13]

DXd’s ability to diffuse across cell membranes underpins a “bystander effect,” whereby payload released inside HER2‑expressing cells can distribute into the surrounding tumor microenvironment and kill neighboring cells with low or even minimal HER2 expression.[1–5,8,13,18,22] This contrasts with earlier HER2 ADCs using non‑cleavable linkers and less permeable payloads, which showed limited activity in HER2‑low or heterogeneous tumors.[3,6,8] Preclinical and clinical data demonstrate that this combination of high DAR, cleavable linker, and diffusible payload allows T‑DXd to exert antitumor activity across a continuum of HER2 expression, including tumors classified as HER2‑low (IHC 1+ or 2+/ISH‑negative).[2,4,5,7,8,15,22]

In HER2‑positive metastatic breast cancer, T‑DXd’s mechanism integrates high‑affinity HER2 targeting, internalization, and DXd‑mediated DNA damage, leading to markedly improved progression‑free and overall survival compared with T‑DM1 in DESTINY‑Breast03 (NCT03529110).[4,12,13,17,21] In HER2‑low disease, HER2 serves primarily as a delivery address rather than a dominant oncogenic driver: T‑DXd leverages residual HER2 expression to localize the ADC, while the bystander‑capable DXd payload eliminates both HER2‑low and adjacent HER2‑negative cells, explaining the substantial efficacy seen in DESTINY‑Breast04 (NCT03734029) and DESTINY‑Breast06 (NCT04494425).[2,5,7–9,15,20,22–24] Collectively, these properties allow T‑DXd to bridge traditional HER2‑positive and HER2‑negative categories and underpin its use across HER2‑positive, HER2‑low, and HER2‑ultralow metastatic breast cancer.[2,4,5,7–9,15,18,22–24]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HER2‑positive metastatic breast cancer, trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd) is now a standard second‑line option after trastuzumab and taxane, with DESTINY‑Breast03 showing a marked progression‑free survival (PFS) advantage over trastuzumab emtansine (T‑DM1) (28.8 vs 6.8 months; HR 0.33) and higher response rates (~80% vs 35%), with consistent benefit irrespective of hormone‑receptor status.[3],[8] Patient‑reported outcomes from DESTINY‑Breast03 indicate maintained global quality of life and delayed time to deterioration versus T‑DM1, supporting a favorable efficacy–tolerability balance in HER2‑positive disease.[1],[7] Retrospective and real‑world data further suggest T‑DXd remains active in tumors with heterogeneous or reduced HER2 expression, underscoring its utility across HER2‑high biological spectra.[4],[8]

For HR+/HER2‑negative, HER2‑low disease, phase III data (DESTINY‑Breast04) synthesized in recent reviews demonstrate that T‑DXd significantly improves PFS (≈9.9 vs 5.1 months; HR ~0.50) and overall survival (OS ≈23.4 vs 16.8 months; HR ~0.64) compared with physician’s‑choice chemotherapy, with clear benefit in the HR‑positive subgroup.[6],[7] These findings are biologically supported by the high drug‑to‑antibody ratio and bystander effect, enabling killing of HER2‑low and adjacent HER2‑negative cells in endocrine‑resistant luminal tumors.[6],[7]

In triple‑negative HER2‑low metastatic breast cancer, DESTINY‑Breast04 and narrative reviews show that T‑DXd improves outcomes versus chemotherapy and achieves meaningful objective responses, although absolute benefit is smaller than in HR‑positive disease.[6],[7] The bystander effect and HER2‑independent activity of the topoisomerase‑I payload provide a rationale for efficacy despite low HER2 expression in TNBC, positioning T‑DXd as a key ADC option alongside sacituzumab govitecan in heavily pretreated patients.[6],[7]

Data specific to germline *BRCA*‑mutated breast cancer are sparse in the available literature. Reviews of ADC predictive factors note that homologous‑recombination status has not emerged as a dominant determinant of T‑DXd benefit, whereas HER2 expression level (including HER2‑low) and tumor microenvironment features appear more critical.[6] Consequently, while PARP inhibitors remain preferred for gBRCA‑mutated disease where indicated, current evidence does not suggest that gBRCA status intrinsically limits sensitivity to T‑DXd, and such patients may still derive benefit when their tumors are HER2‑positive or HER2‑low.[6],[7]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Regulatory labeling for trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‑DXd, Enhertu) lists no absolute contraindications, but the drug carries boxed/major warnings for interstitial lung disease (ILD)/pneumonitis, neutropenia, left‑ventricular dysfunction, and embryo‑fetal toxicity, requiring careful selection and monitoring of breast cancer patients.[4],[6] ILD/pneumonitis is the most critical toxicity: the original FDA label warns of fatal ILD and mandates permanent discontinuation for grade ≥2 events, with close monitoring for new/worsening respiratory symptoms and early corticosteroids.[4] Updated FDA and EMA information and breast‑cancer–focused meta‑analyses show ILD incidences around 10–12% in metastatic breast cancer, with higher risk at 6.4 vs 5.4 mg/kg and a small but non‑negligible proportion of grade 5 deaths.[6],[9],[12],[24] Expert and multidisciplinary guidelines emphasize baseline lung assessment, CT‑based surveillance tailored to median time‑to‑onset, prompt drug interruption for any suspected ILD, systemic steroids, and permanent discontinuation for symptomatic ILD.[7],[18],[19],[21],[22]

Myelosuppression is common: across DESTINY‑Breast trials, any‑grade neutropenia and anemia occur in roughly one‑third of patients, with grade ≥3 neutropenia in ~16–19% and clinically relevant thrombocytopenia in a smaller subset.[9],[11],[16],[21] Labels and consensus guidance recommend CBCs prior to each cycle, dose interruption or reduction for grade 3–4 neutropenia or febrile neutropenia, and supportive care (e.g., growth factors as appropriate).[4],[6],[17],[21] Cardiac toxicity is less frequent but important: pooled breast‑cancer data show reduced LVEF in ~2% and QTc prolongation in ~8%, mostly asymptomatic, supporting baseline and periodic echocardiography per anti‑HER2 cardiotoxicity guidance.[6],[9],[11],[12],[21]

Both FDA and EMA highlight embryo‑fetal risk and advise verifying pregnancy status, using effective contraception during and for several months after therapy, and avoiding breastfeeding.[4],[6],[11],[24] T‑DXd is now classified by NCCN‑aligned experts as highly emetogenic; prophylaxis with a 5‑HT3 antagonist plus dexamethasone, with or without an NK1 antagonist, is recommended to maintain adherence.[18],[21] Additional but less frequent concerns include fatigue, alopecia, and rare severe cardiac or pulmonary events; multidisciplinary, guideline‑driven toxicity management is essential to preserve the favorable benefit–risk profile in HER2‑positive and HER2‑low metastatic breast cancer.[11],[16],[18],[21],[24]
