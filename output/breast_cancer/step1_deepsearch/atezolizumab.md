## 1. Drug Summary

Atezolizumab (Tecentriq) is a phage‑derived, humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds programmed death‑ligand 1 (PD‑L1/CD274) and blocks its interaction with PD‑1 and B7.1 on T cells, thereby reversing PD‑1/PD‑L1–mediated T‑cell exhaustion and restoring antitumor immunity.[1] Its Fc region is engineered to minimize antibody‑dependent cell‑mediated cytotoxicity against PD‑L1–expressing immune cells, distinguishing it from some other anti–PD‑L1 antibodies.[1,3] High‑resolution structural studies show atezolizumab Fab engages the front β‑sheet and CC′/FG loops of PD‑L1, directly competing with PD‑1 for binding.[3]

Clinically, atezolizumab is an FDA‑approved anti–PD‑L1 immune checkpoint inhibitor across multiple solid tumors, initially in urothelial carcinoma and non–small‑cell lung cancer, with subsequent expansion to additional malignancies.[1,3] In breast cancer, its relevance is concentrated in triple‑negative breast cancer (TNBC), which is characterized by higher PD‑L1 expression and tumor‑infiltrating lymphocytes than hormone receptor–positive disease.[4,13] The phase III IMpassion130 trial (NCT02425891) established atezolizumab plus nab‑paclitaxel as a first‑line standard for PD‑L1–positive unresectable or metastatic TNBC, demonstrating improved progression‑free survival and a clinically meaningful overall‑survival advantage in the PD‑L1 immune‑cell–positive subgroup, though not in the overall intention‑to‑treat population.[8,12,13]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Atezolizumab is a humanized IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds programmed death‑ligand 1 (PD‑L1) and blocks its interaction with PD‑1 and B7.1 (CD80), thereby releasing inhibitory signals on activated T cells and restoring cytotoxic effector function against tumor cells.[4,16] The PD‑1/PD‑L1 axis normally limits T‑cell proliferation, cytokine secretion, and survival to maintain immune homeostasis; many tumors, including breast cancers, exploit PD‑L1 overexpression to induce T‑cell exhaustion and immune escape.[12,16]

TNBC is the most immunogenic breast subtype, characterized by higher PD‑L1 expression—predominantly on tumor‑infiltrating immune cells—and abundant tumor‑infiltrating lymphocytes, creating a pre‑existing but suppressed antitumor response.[1,4,9,22] In PD‑L1–positive metastatic TNBC, atezolizumab plus nab‑paclitaxel improves progression‑free survival and yields clinically meaningful overall survival gains, with benefit confined to tumors with ≥1% PD‑L1–positive immune cells by SP142 assay, underscoring that its activity depends on PD‑L1–driven immune evasion and a responsive immune microenvironment.[2,6,7,22]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In metastatic TNBC, atezolizumab plus nab‑paclitaxel significantly improved PFS and numerically improved OS in PD‑L1–positive tumors (SP142 IC ≥1%), with ORR ~56% and median OS 25.0 vs 18.0 months versus chemotherapy alone; benefit was minimal in PD‑L1–negative disease, underscoring PD‑L1 IC as the dominant predictive biomarker.[13],[15] Early monotherapy data show modest ORR (~10%) but very durable responses in a subset, again enriched in PD‑L1–positive, immune‑inflamed TNBC.[15]

In early‑stage TNBC, adding atezolizumab to neoadjuvant chemotherapy improved pCR in IMpassion031, whereas the NeoTRIPaPDL1 study did not show a pCR or EFS advantage, suggesting that regimen, timing, and microenvironmental context modulate benefit.[13],[12] For HR+/HER2– and HER2+ breast cancers, current evidence indicates low immunogenicity with infrequent PD‑L1 expression and limited efficacy of ICIs; expert consensus does not support routine immunotherapy, including atezolizumab, outside trials in these subtypes.[4],[12],[14]

Germline BRCA‑mutated TNBC is highly immunogenic, but available data indicate that benefit from PD‑1/PD‑L1 blockade is primarily driven by PD‑L1 positivity, TILs, and TMB rather than BRCA status per se.[9],[11],[15] Overall, atezolizumab’s validated role is confined to PD‑L1–positive TNBC within chemo‑immunotherapy regimens, with efficacy tightly linked to an inflamed tumor immune microenvironment.[3],[10],[13–15]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Intravenous atezolizumab has no formal contraindications in the US or EU labels; however, the subcutaneous TECENTRIQ HYBREZA formulation is contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to hyaluronidase or any of its components.[1,15] In metastatic TNBC, regulatory guidance emphasizes that serious immune‑mediated toxicities rather than classic chemotherapy-type contraindications drive risk–benefit decisions.[4,6,9,10]

Immune‑mediated adverse reactions can involve virtually any organ system and be severe or fatal. Key events include pneumonitis, colitis, hepatitis (with or without liver tumor), endocrinopathies (thyroid dysfunction, hypophysitis, adrenal insufficiency, type 1 diabetes), nephritis, severe cutaneous reactions (SJS/TEN/DRESS), myocarditis/pericarditis, and neurologic syndromes such as myasthenia gravis and Guillain–Barré–like presentations.[1,4,6,7,9,15] Labels recommend baseline and periodic liver enzymes, creatinine, and thyroid function, with CTCAE‑based algorithms: withhold for most grade 2–3 events and permanently discontinue for grade 4 or steroid‑refractory toxicity.[1,4,6,15]

Pregnancy and lactation are major safety concerns. PD‑L1 blockade may promote immune‑mediated fetal rejection; women of childbearing potential should use effective contraception during treatment and for 5 months after the last dose, and breastfeeding should be avoided in the same interval.[1,4,6,15] In TNBC, EMA restricts breast‑cancer use to combination with nab‑paclitaxel (not paclitaxel), reflecting safety and efficacy data from IMpassion130/131 and reinforcing adherence to approved product information.[4,9,10,12,14,16]

Older or comorbid patients, and those with prior serious immune‑mediated events or baseline pulmonary or hepatic compromise, require heightened monitoring and early multidisciplinary involvement, as emphasized in StatPearls and risk‑management reviews.[3,7,9,15]  



## 8. Key Clinical Trials

IMpassion130 (NCT02425891) randomized 902 patients with previously untreated unresectable locally advanced/metastatic TNBC to atezolizumab plus nab‑paclitaxel versus placebo plus nab‑paclitaxel; PD‑L1 was prospectively assessed on tumor‑infiltrating immune cells with the VENTANA SP142 assay, using IC ≥1% as the positive threshold.[1,5,8,10,16] In PD‑L1–positive tumors, atezolizumab improved PFS (7.5 vs 5.0 months; HR 0.62) and produced a clinically meaningful OS gain (25 vs 15.5–18 months; HR ~0.62–0.71).[1,2,5,6,8,16] IMpassion131 (NCT03125902), a similar first‑line trial substituting weekly solvent‑based paclitaxel, did not improve PFS or OS in either the ITT or PD‑L1–positive populations, highlighting chemotherapy backbone and validated PD‑L1 selection as critical determinants of benefit.[1,2,7]

| EMT / invasion–metastasis program | Downregulated by atezolizumab in TNBC cells | Suppression associated with sensitivity; persistent EMT linked to resistance and aggressive disease | In PD‑L1–expressing TNBC cells, atezolizumab reduced expression of EMT and metastasis‑promoting genes and functionally inhibited migration and invasion.[3] Clinically, PD‑L1 expression in TNBC correlates with larger size, nodal involvement and distant metastasis, and is associated with worse DFS, indicating that EMT‑linked, PD‑L1‑high tumors are aggressive but potentially targetable by PD‑L1 blockade.[6,8] Failure to reverse EMT programs may underlie poor outcomes in some PD‑L1–positive cases. | [3,6,8] |
| Hypoxia response pathway (e.g., HIF‑regulated genes) | Downregulated by atezolizumab in TNBC cells | Downregulation may enhance sensitivity; persistent hypoxia signaling may foster resistance | Transcriptomic profiling showed that atezolizumab downregulated hypoxia‑associated genes in MDA‑MB‑231, in parallel with reduced survival and EMT/metastasis signatures.[3] Because hypoxia promotes immunosuppression and PD‑L1 expression, its attenuation may create a more permissive microenvironment for T‑cell activity; continued hypoxia signaling could counteract PD‑L1 blockade. | [3] |
| Apoptosis and DNA‑damage response pathways | Upregulated by atezolizumab in TNBC cells | Upregulation associated with sensitivity | Atezolizumab increased expression of pro‑apoptotic and DNA repair–related genes and induced apoptosis/necrosis in TNBC cell lines.[3] In the clinic, BRCA1/2‑mutated mTNBC (with intrinsic DNA‑repair defects) derived benefit from atezolizumab plus nab‑paclitaxel when tumors were PD‑L1 IC‑positive, similar to BRCA‑wild‑type disease, suggesting that DNA‑repair status does not negate benefit but may interact with immune activation and PARP‑inhibitor strategies.[10,14] | [3,10,14] |
| Tumor‑infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) / CD8+ T‑cell axis | TILs and intratumoral CD8+ cells enriched in TNBC; higher levels associate with better outcomes and atezolizumab benefit when PD‑L1 IC+ | High TILs/CD8+ with PD‑L1 IC+ → sensitive; low or exhausted TILs → primary resistance | TNBC has higher TIL density than other subtypes, and high TILs predict improved prognosis and chemotherapy response.[2,5,7,16] In IMpassion130, CD8+ T cells and stromal TILs were moderately correlated with PD‑L1 IC+, and improved PFS/OS with atezolizumab plus nab‑paclitaxel was seen only in CD8+ or sTIL‑positive tumors that were also PD‑L1 IC+; TILs alone were insufficient without PD‑L1 expression.[10,14] Clinically, high TILs are favorable, but their positive impact is attenuated when tumor PD‑L1 is high, supporting combined use of PD‑L1 blockade in inflamed tumors.[6,8] | [2,5,6–8,10,14,16] |
| Antigen presentation / MHC‑I pathway | Frequently downregulated or defective in breast cancer; not directly restored by atezolizumab | Downregulation mediates primary/acquired resistance; intact pathway supports sensitivity | Breast cancers, including TNBC, often downregulate TAP1/2 and TAPBP and exhibit loss or epigenetic silencing of MHC‑I, impairing peptide loading and presentation to CD8+ T cells.[16] Such defects are associated with poor outcomes and may blunt benefit from PD‑L1 blockade by limiting neoantigen recognition. Although not directly targeted by atezolizumab, restoring antigen‑presentation machinery (e.g., via epigenetic therapies) is a rational strategy to overcome resistance. | [16] |
| IFN‑γ / JAK–STAT signaling | Required for effective anti‑tumor immunity; tumors may acquire JAK/STAT defects under checkpoint pressure | Intact IFN‑γ–JAK–STAT signaling supports sensitivity; pathway loss confers resistance | IFN‑γ signaling is critical for anti‑tumor immunity, and loss‑of‑function mutations in JAK1/2 or STAT1 have been described as mechanisms of acquired resistance to anti‑PD‑L1/anti‑CTLA‑4 in other cancers.[16] Breast tumors can similarly evade IFN‑γ–mediated effects by downregulating this axis, thereby preventing upregulation of antigen presentation and chemokines needed for T‑cell recruitment; such alterations would be expected to limit atezolizumab efficacy despite PD‑L1 expression. | [16] |
| Alternative immune checkpoint pathways (LAG‑3, TIM‑3, TIGIT, VISTA, B7‑H3/B7‑H4) | Upregulated on T cells or tumor cells after PD‑L1 blockade in multiple tumors | Upregulation mediates adaptive resistance | Breast cancer resistance to immunotherapy is linked to compensatory expression of other inhibitory ligands and receptors, including B7‑family members (B7‑H3, B7‑H4) and T‑cell checkpoints LAG‑3, TIM‑3, TIGIT and VISTA.[16] Their induction after anti‑PD‑L1 therapy can re‑establish T‑cell exhaustion and allow tumor escape, explaining incomplete or transient responses to atezolizumab and providing a rationale for combinatorial checkpoint inhibition. | [16] |
| Tumor mutational burden (TMB) / neoantigen load | High TMB more common in TNBC; in IMpassion130, benefit greatest in top TMB quartile | High TMB enriches for sensitivity but is not sufficient alone | TNBC exhibits higher TMB and genomic instability than other breast cancer subtypes, contributing to increased neoantigen load and immunogenicity.[4,5,15] In IMpassion130, PFS benefit from atezolizumab plus nab‑paclitaxel was greatest in the highest TMB quartile and appeared independent of PD‑L1 status, suggesting that high TMB can further sensitize tumors to PD‑L1 blockade by augmenting antigenic diversity, though many high‑TMB tumors still fail to respond due to downstream resistance mechanisms.[2,15] | [2,4,5,15] |
