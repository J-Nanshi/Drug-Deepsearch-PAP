## 1. Drug Summary

Larotrectinib is a highly selective, oral small‑molecule inhibitor of the tropomyosin receptor kinases TRKA, TRKB, and TRKC encoded by NTRK1–3.[9] First‑generation TRK inhibitors such as larotrectinib potently block TRK kinase activity in the low‑nanomolar range and suppress downstream MAPK, PI3K–AKT, PKC, and STAT3 signaling in TRK fusion–driven models, leading to growth inhibition and tumour regression in vitro and in vivo.[9] These agents exemplify a precision‑oncology paradigm in which a single genomic driver (an NTRK fusion) is targeted across diverse histologies.

Larotrectinib received US FDA accelerated, tissue‑agnostic approval in 2018 for adult and paediatric patients with metastatic or unresectable solid tumours harbouring an NTRK gene fusion, without a known resistance mutation, and lacking satisfactory alternative treatments.[3][4] Approval was based on pooled data from three single‑arm trials—adult phase I LOXO‑TRK‑14001 (NCT02122913), paediatric SCOUT (NCT02637687), and basket NAVIGATE (NCT02576431)—in which the first 55 NTRK fusion–positive patients across 12 tumour types achieved an objective response rate (ORR) of 75% (95% CI 61–85%), with durable responses and <5% treatment‑related grade 3–4 adverse events.[3][4][8][9]

NTRK fusions are rare in common breast cancer overall (~0.3% of adult solid tumours), but they occur at very high frequency (up to or >90%) in secretory breast carcinoma, where the canonical ETV6–NTRK3 fusion is a characteristic oncogenic driver.[1][8][9] Case reports describe metastatic triple‑negative or secretory‑type breast cancers with NTRK3 fusions achieving marked radiographic and clinical responses to larotrectinib after failure of multiple prior systemic therapies, supporting its relevance for this molecularly defined subset.[1][6] In this precision‑oncology context, larotrectinib functions as a tumour‑agnostic, biomarker‑driven therapy deployable across breast cancer subtypes when an activating NTRK fusion is present and dominant.

## Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Larotrectinib is an orally bioavailable, first‑generation, ATP‑competitive small‑molecule that potently and selectively inhibits the kinase domains of TRKA, TRKB and TRKC, with in‑vitro IC₅₀ values of 5–11 nM against TRKA/B/C and >100‑fold selectivity versus hundreds of non‑TRK kinases.[2,7,8] By occupying the ATP‑binding pocket of the active TRK conformation, it prevents receptor autophosphorylation and substrate phosphorylation, thereby shutting down neurotrophin‑driven signaling through canonical RAS‑RAF‑MEK‑ERK (MAPK), PI3K‑AKT and PLCγ cascades that mediate proliferation, survival and differentiation.[14]

In breast cancer, the most relevant context is oncogenic *NTRK* gene fusions. These rearrangements juxtapose the 3′ kinase domain of *NTRK1/2/3* to 5′ partners that provide dimerization or oligomerization motifs, generating chimeric TRK oncoproteins with constitutive, ligand‑independent kinase activity.[7,14] Secretory breast carcinoma is paradigmatic: >90% of cases harbor the *ETV6‑NTRK3* fusion, which is essentially pathognomonic and drives tumorigenesis via continuous TRKC signaling.[7,14] More broadly, *NTRK* fusions are rare in common breast cancer subtypes (<1% overall) but, when present, function as dominant lineage‑agnostic drivers.[4,11,12]

Preclinical models bearing TRK fusions such as *TPM3‑NTRK1*, *MPRIP‑NTRK1*, *TRIM24‑NTRK2* and *ETV6‑NTRK3* show that larotrectinib rapidly suppresses TRK phosphorylation and downstream RAF‑MEK‑ERK and PI3K‑AKT signaling, leading to growth arrest and apoptosis in vitro and tumor regression in xenografts.[2,4] Clinically, pooled phase I/II datasets across solid tumors—including breast cancers—demonstrate objective response rates around 70–80% and durable disease control in *NTRK* fusion‑positive patients treated with larotrectinib, supporting true oncogene addiction to TRK signaling.[1,2,6,8,11,13,15] These responses are tumor‑agnostic and observed irrespective of the specific *NTRK* gene or fusion partner, consistent with a shared biochemical mechanism: collapse of TRK‑driven MAPK and PI3K‑AKT output.

In *NTRK*‑rearranged breast tumors, this blockade has several mechanistic consequences. First, it extinguishes the dominant proliferative/survival axis (MAPK, PI3K‑AKT, and related effectors such as STAT3 and PKC) that is activated downstream of the fusion receptor, thereby inducing apoptosis or durable cell‑cycle arrest despite an otherwise aggressive phenotype (e.g., triple‑negative immunoprofile in secretory carcinoma).[11,14,16] Second, it can unmask or select for alternative signaling routes. In TRK‑fusion cancers progressing on first‑generation TRK inhibitors, on‑target resistance mutations (e.g., solvent‑front TRKA G595R or TRKC G623R, gatekeeper and xDFG substitutions) and off‑target events (e.g., *KRAS*/*BRAF* mutations or *MET* amplification) restore MAPK or PI3K‑AKT signaling despite continued TRK inhibition.[3,4,7,11,16,19] Although these resistance data come largely from non‑breast histologies, they imply that *NTRK*‑positive breast cancers treated with larotrectinib are initially TRK‑addicted but can evolve bypass or secondary‑mutation–mediated reactivation of the same downstream pathways.

From a precision‑oncology standpoint, larotrectinib’s mechanism in breast cancer is therefore best understood as highly selective pharmacologic silencing of an aberrant TRK signaling hub created by *NTRK* fusions—most commonly *ETV6‑NTRK3* in secretory breast carcinoma—leading to rapid dismantling of MAPK and PI3K‑AKT oncogenic programs and clinically meaningful, often durable responses in this molecularly defined subset.[1,2,4,8,11,14–16]

## 6. Breast Cancer Subtype Evidence

In HR+/HER2– breast cancer, current evidence emphasizes the dominance of endocrine and CDK4/6‑based strategies, with molecular profiling mainly used to refine risk and reveal additional targets rather than to systematically seek *NTRK* fusions.[4],[5] Available data indicate that *NTRK* fusions occur in only a minute fraction of non‑secretory breast cancers, so larotrectinib is best viewed as a rare “long‑tail” option identified through broad somatic next‑generation sequencing (NGS) in advanced disease rather than subtype‑directed testing.[1],[4]

For HER2+ disease, most precision efforts focus on HER2‑directed and pathway‑adapted regimens, and no dedicated series of HER2+/*NTRK*‑rearranged breast cancers treated with larotrectinib were identified in these sources.[4],[6] Secretory histology—the context in which *ETV6–NTRK3* is pathognomonic—is usually not HER2‑driven, further limiting overlap between canonical HER2+ tumors and *NTRK*‑fusion biology.[2] In practice, *NTRK* testing in HER2+ breast cancer is more likely to be considered at metastatic progression as part of comprehensive genomic profiling rather than at diagnosis.[1],[4]

The clearest human evidence for larotrectinib in breast cancer is in triple‑negative disease with secretory features. A detailed case report of metastatic TNBC with dual *NTRK3* fusions (*ETV6–NTRK3* and *CRTC3–NTRK3*) showed rapid, marked clinical and radiographic response to larotrectinib after multiple prior systemic regimens, illustrating profound drug sensitivity when the fusion is the dominant driver.[1] Large genomic datasets confirm that secretory breast carcinoma accounts for most *NTRK*‑positive breast cancers, while non‑secretory tumors contribute only a very small fraction of *NTRK*‑fusion cases.[1] Given the overall aggressiveness and poor prognosis of TNBC subtypes,[4],[8] these findings strongly support routine somatic NGS (including *NTRK1/2/3*) in advanced or unusual‑histology TNBC to identify candidates for larotrectinib.[1],[4],[8]

For germline *BRCA*‑mutated breast cancer, the sources stress the link between *BRCA1* and TNBC biology but do not describe systematic co‑occurrence of *gBRCA* mutations with *NTRK* fusions or specific larotrectinib outcomes in this subgroup.[4] In the metastatic TNBC larotrectinib case, extensive germline testing for *BRCA1/2* and other susceptibility genes was negative, yet the tumor remained exquisitely sensitive to TRK inhibition, underscoring that *NTRK* fusion–driven responsiveness to larotrectinib is a somatic event independent of hereditary predisposition and must be actively sought through tumor or cfDNA NGS.[1]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

Larotrectinib (VITRAKVI) has no formal contraindications in the current U.S. or EU prescribing information; use is governed by warnings, precautions, and dose‑modification guidance.[1][8][10][11] For adults and children with NTRK fusion–positive breast cancer, the same label restrictions apply, with additional attention to comorbid CNS disease, polypharmacy, and prior hepatic injury.

Central nervous system effects (dizziness, cognitive impairment, mood disorders, sleep disturbances) are a key safety signal. Labels advise patients not to drive or operate hazardous machinery if these occur, and recommend withholding and resuming at reduced dose or permanently discontinuing based on severity.[8][9][10][11] This is particularly relevant in metastatic breast cancer where brain metastases, sedating co‑medications, and falls are common.

Hepatotoxicity is another major concern. ALT/AST, ALP and bilirubin should be checked before treatment, every 2 weeks for the first 1–2 months, then monthly or as clinically indicated.[1][8][9][10][11] Grade ≥3 transaminase elevations require treatment interruption and stepwise dose reduction, and ALT/AST >3× ULN with bilirubin >2× ULN mandates permanent discontinuation.[1][8][9][10] Patients with moderate–severe hepatic impairment (Child‑Pugh B–C) require a 50% starting‑dose reduction.[1][8][10][11]

Embryo‑fetal toxicity is highlighted: larotrectinib can cause fetal harm, so females of reproductive potential need pregnancy testing before initiation and effective contraception during treatment and for at least 1 month (EU) or 1 week (US) after the last dose; breastfeeding is not recommended during and for 1 week after therapy.[1][8][10][11] Males with partners who can become pregnant should use effective contraception for at least 1 month after the final dose in the EU label.[1] Skeletal fractures have been reported; any new bone pain or mobility change should prompt evaluation, which is critical in breast cancer patients with bone metastases.[8][9][10]

Co‑administration with strong/moderate CYP3A4 or P‑gp inducers or strong inhibitors should generally be avoided; if unavoidable, structured dose adjustments and close toxicity monitoring are required.[1][8][9][10][11] Pediatric and adult safety profiles are broadly similar, but children have higher rates of weight gain and neutropenia, warranting growth and blood‑count monitoring.[12]

## 8. Key Clinical Trials

Larotrectinib’s tumour‑agnostic approval is based on three pivotal phase I/II studies conducted under a unified development program: the adult phase I trial LOXO‑TRK‑14001 (NCT02122913), the paediatric phase I/II SCOUT trial (NCT02637687), and the adolescent/adult basket trial NAVIGATE (NCT02576431).[1][7][8][12][18] All three enrolled patients with locally advanced or metastatic solid tumours harbouring *NTRK* fusions, with oral larotrectinib given continuously (typically 100 mg twice daily in adults; 100 mg/m² twice daily in children) until progression or unacceptable toxicity.[5][6][7][12][18] Secretory breast carcinoma is specifically highlighted among tumour types in which *NTRK* fusions are highly prevalent (~90%), and is explicitly included among entities eligible for larotrectinib in SCOUT and NAVIGATE.[6][8][18]

In the initial integrated efficacy analysis of the first 55 TRK fusion–positive patients from NCT02122913, NCT02637687 and NCT02576431, the overall response rate (ORR) was 75% (95% CI 61–85) by independent review, with 13% complete responses; 71% of responses and 55% of patients remained progression‑free at 1 year.[7][8][21] An expanded pooled analysis in 159 patients across ≥17 histologies reported an investigator‑assessed ORR of 79% (95% CI 72–85), including 16% complete responses, median progression‑free survival (PFS) 28.3 months, and median overall survival (OS) 44 months.[5][6][12][20] Within this programme, six patients with TRK fusion–positive breast cancer (three secretory and three non‑secretory) achieved an 83% partial response rate to larotrectinib, mirroring activity seen across other histologies and supporting its use in *NTRK* fusion–positive breast tumours.[4]

SCOUT (NCT02637687) further established larotrectinib as a first‑line option in paediatric and AYA patients with unresectable or metastatic TRK fusion cancers, including secretory breast carcinoma. In treatment‑naïve patients pooled from NCT02122913, NCT02637687 and NCT02576431 (n=101; 14 tumour types), ORR was 77% (95% CI 68–85), median duration of response 59 months, median PFS 61 months, and 5‑year OS 76%.[6] SCOUT also introduced a ‘wait‑and‑see’ strategy allowing elective discontinuation after surgery or durable response; most patients who later relapsed regained response upon larotrectinib re‑challenge, underscoring the depth and reversibility of TRK dependence.[6][17]

Safety across the integrated larotrectinib safety set (260 patients from NCT02122913, NCT02637687 and NCT02576431) has been favourable, with most adverse events grade 1–2 and treatment‑related grade 3–4 events infrequent (e.g. alanine aminotransferase elevation 3%, anaemia 2%); only 2% discontinued due to adverse events, supporting long‑term administration in metastatic settings.[5][12][14][21] A matched real‑world comparison using patients from these three trials demonstrated substantially longer OS (median not reached vs 37.2 months; HR 0.44) and PFS (36.8 vs 5.2 months; HR 0.29) with larotrectinib versus non‑TRK inhibitor standard‑of‑care, reinforcing that single‑arm trial outcomes translate into meaningful survival benefit in routine practice for TRK fusion cancers, including the rare *NTRK*‑rearranged breast cancer subset.[2]

| KRAS / RAS signaling | Downstream of TRK; normally suppressed by effective TRK inhibition. | Tumor immune microenvironment / inflammatory pathways | ETV6‑NTRK3‑driven tumors can modulate immune signaling; TRK inhibition alters cytokine milieu and immune cell infiltration in some models. Inflammatory reprogramming during resistance could create new vulnerabilities or immunosuppressive barriers. | In a selitrectinib‑resistant NTRK1‑fusion sarcoma, RNA‑seq and CyCIF showed enrichment of inflammatory and immune‑related signatures and increased cytotoxic T cells and macrophages compared with earlier, TRK‑inhibitor–sensitive tumors.[10] SBC microenvironment is generally indolent, but secretory TNBC with NTRK3 fusions may share fibrotic, immune‑poor features that predict poor checkpoint inhibitor response; nonetheless, evolving inflammation under KRAS‑driven resistance suggests potential benefit from integrating immunotherapy with TRK/MAPK‑directed regimens.[3,5,6,10,11] | Hemming et al. link KRAS‑mediated resistance to increased inflammatory signatures and altered immune infiltrates, proposing immunotherapy as a rational partner in resistant TRK‑driven tumors.[10] Medford et al. describe a fibrotic microenvironment and poor immunotherapy response in an NTRK3‑fusion TNBC case, underscoring the need for better immune‑modulating strategies.[3] |
| Epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT) / invasion pathways | NTRK fusions, via MAPK/PI3K and possibly STAT5, promote invasive behavior and secretory phenotype; TRK inhibition reduces metastatic potential in fusion‑driven tumors.[5–7,13,17] | EMT and invasion programs can sustain dissemination despite good primary‑tumor control with larotrectinib; alternative drivers (e.g., MET, ALK, RET fusions) in secretory‑like tumors may maintain EMT under TRK blockade, contributing to late relapse or organ‑specific metastasis. | ETV6‑NTRK3 in SBC and salivary secretory carcinomas is associated with distinctive secretory morphology and, in a minority, distant metastases, indicating that the fusion can support invasion in permissive genomic contexts.[5,6] Secretory‑type tumors in other organs (MASC, thyroid, salivary gland) often harbor additional kinase fusions (ALK, MET, RET) that drive aggressive behavior and may provide EMT signals independent of TRK.[6] In pancreatic and other RTK‑driven cancers, vertical MAPK inhibition reduces invasion and metastasis, suggesting that sustained MAPK/PI3K activity—whether TRK‑ or bypass‑driven—underlies EMT in NTRK‑fusion breast cancer and may need dual‑pathway targeting. | Narrative SBC review emphasizes that although prognosis is usually excellent, some SBCs show distant metastasis and death, particularly with larger size, nodal involvement or higher grade, implying EMT/invasion programs beyond TRK alone.[5,6] Pancreatic fusion‑positive tumors with NTRK alterations are treated with larotrectinib/entrectinib in basket trials, and preclinical kinase‑inhibitor work links MAPK suppression to reduced metastasis.[17] |
| Vertical pathway inhibition (TRK plus downstream MAPK/PI3K) | Therapeutic, not native, pathway configuration: simultaneous targeting of TRK and downstream nodes (MEK, ERK, RAF, PI3K/mTOR) to prevent reactivation of core survival cascades.[10,11,13,17,18] | Designed to overcome both on‑target (NTRK mutation) and off‑target (BRAF/KRAS/MET, PI3K) resistance; particularly relevant in aggressive NTRK‑fusion tumors (e.g., TNBC‑like SBC, metastatic TNBC with NTRK3 fusions) where MAPK/PI3K signaling is heavily wired. | In larotrectinib‑resistant CTRC‑NTRK1 pancreatic cancer with acquired BRAF V600E and KRAS G12D, combined RAF+MEK inhibition (without TRK) restored tumor control in preclinical models, highlighting MAPK as a convergent resistance hub.[11,17] RTK‑focused pancreatic and breast cancer reviews advocate “vertical” inhibition—blocking both the driver RTK and downstream kinases—to prevent adaptive MAPK/PI3K reactivation.[17,18,23] For NTRK‑fusion breast cancers, particularly TNBC subtypes enriched for MAPK/PI3K activation, clinical exploration of larotrectinib or next‑generation TRK inhibitors plus MEK/ERK or PI3K/mTOR inhibitors is mechanistically justified, though prospective data are not yet available. | Cocco/Xie and Qin et al. highlight MAPK reactivation via BRAF/KRAS/MET and support combination TRK+MAPK strategies in resistant NTRK‑fusion tumors.[11,13] Kinase‑inhibitor reviews in pancreatic and breast cancer describe vertical pathway inhibition (e.g., EGFR+MEK, HER2+CDK4/6) as a successful paradigm likely translatable to TRK‑addicted breast cancers.[17,18,23] |
