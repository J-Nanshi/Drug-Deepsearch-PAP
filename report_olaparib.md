## 1. Drug Summary

Olaparib (Lynparza; DrugBank DB09074) is an oral small‑molecule poly(ADP‑ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitor approved in multiple indications, including breast cancer.[8][11] In the US label (2022), olaparib is indicated for:  
- Adjuvant treatment of adults with deleterious or suspected deleterious germline BRCA‑mutated (gBRCAm), HER2‑negative high‑risk early breast cancer who have received (neo)adjuvant chemotherapy.[8]  
- Treatment of adults with deleterious or suspected deleterious gBRCAm, HER2‑negative metastatic breast cancer previously treated with chemotherapy in the neoadjuvant, adjuvant, or metastatic setting; hormone‑receptor–positive patients should also have received, or be inappropriate for, endocrine therapy.[8][9]  
The FDA breast cancer indication requires selection based on an FDA‑approved BRCA companion diagnostic.[7][8][10]

### Mechanism of action and synthetic lethality

Olaparib is a potent, selective inhibitor of PARP1 and PARP2, blocking NAD⁺‑dependent PARylation and trapping PARP–DNA complexes at sites of single‑strand breaks.[11][12][14][15] In HR‑proficient cells, unrepaired single‑strand breaks converted to double‑strand breaks during replication can be accurately repaired by BRCA‑dependent homologous recombination (HR).[12][14][15] In tumors with germline BRCA1/2 pathogenic variants or broader homologous recombination repair (HRR) deficiency, HR is impaired; cells become reliant on PARP‑mediated repair and alternative end‑joining pathways. PARP inhibition in this context produces synthetic lethality: persistent replication‑associated double‑strand breaks, error‑prone repair (NHEJ/MMEJ), genomic instability, and cancer cell death, while normal HR‑competent tissues are relatively spared.[12][14][15] PARP trapping is now recognized as a major driver of cytotoxicity in BRCA‑deficient tumors.[12][15]

### Key clinical evidence in breast cancer and precision‑oncology positioning

In metastatic HER2‑negative gBRCAm breast cancer, the phase III OlympiAD trial (NCT02000622) randomized patients to olaparib tablets 300 mg twice daily versus physician’s choice single‑agent chemotherapy. Olaparib improved progression‑free survival and objective response, and final overall‑survival analysis showed median OS 19.3 vs 17.1 months (HR 0.90, 95% CI 0.66–1.23), with a signal for greater benefit when used as first‑line therapy for metastatic disease; toxicity was mainly low‑grade and manageable.[1] These data underpinned the metastatic breast cancer approval reflected in FDA labels.[7][8][9][10]

In high‑risk, HER2‑negative gBRCA1/2‑mutated early breast cancer, the phase III OlympiA trial (n=1836) demonstrated that 1 year of adjuvant olaparib after completion of local therapy and (neo)adjuvant chemotherapy significantly improved invasive disease‑free survival (4‑year IDFS 82.7% vs 75.4%) and distant disease‑free survival (4‑year DDFS 86.5% vs 79.1%), and at the second interim analysis improved overall survival (HR 0.68, 98.5% CI 0.47–0.97).[3] With longer follow‑up (~6 years), company and meeting reports show sustained benefit: hazard ratio for death 0.72 with 6‑year OS 87.5% vs 83.2%, and consistent benefit in both triple‑negative and ER‑positive subgroups, without new safety signals or excess myelodysplastic syndrome/acute myeloid leukemia.[2][5] These findings led to global approvals of adjuvant olaparib in gBRCAm HER2‑negative high‑risk early breast cancer.[5][8][11]

Within precision oncology, olaparib is a paradigmatic biomarker‑driven therapy: treatment is guided by germline BRCA1/2 mutation status in addition to standard ER/PR/HER2 profiling, and increasingly integrated across the disease continuum—from neoadjuvant investigation in BRCA‑mutated, HER2‑negative early disease (e.g., OlympiaN, NCT05498155) to adjuvant high‑risk and metastatic settings.[4][5][8][11][13] Its use exemplifies targeting DNA damage response vulnerabilities (HRD) with a matched PARP inhibitor, with demonstrated efficacy across both triple‑negative and hormone‑receptor–positive HER2‑negative breast cancer subtypes harboring germline BRCA mutations.[1][2][3][5][8][13]

## 3. Mechanism of Action (Breast Cancer Context)

Olaparib is an oral poly(ADP‑ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitor that exploits homologous recombination deficiency (HRD)—most classically due to *BRCA1/2* alterations—to induce synthetic lethality in breast cancer.

### PARP1/2 inhibition and PARP trapping

PARP1 is activated by binding to DNA single‑strand breaks (SSBs), where it converts NAD⁺ into poly(ADP‑ribose) (PAR) chains that recruit repair factors such as XRCC1 and promote efficient SSB repair and replication fork restart.[1] Although PARP1 contributes to SSB repair and can influence base excision repair (BER), it is not essential for BER per se; BER core factors are indispensable whereas PARP1‑null mice are viable, underscoring that PARP1 is an accessory factor in this context.[1]

Olaparib inhibits PARP catalytic activity by competing with NAD⁺ at the active site, thereby blocking PAR synthesis and impairing PAR‑dependent SSB repair and replication‑associated damage responses.[1] Beyond simple catalytic inhibition, a key cytotoxic mechanism is “PARP trapping”: in the presence of PARP inhibitors, PARP1 is stabilized on DNA repair intermediates, particularly during BER/SSB repair, forming PARP–DNA complexes that obstruct replication fork progression.[1] These stalled or collapsed forks generate one‑ended double‑strand breaks (DSBs) during S phase, which require high‑fidelity homologous recombination (HR) for repair.[1]

### Synthetic lethality in BRCA1/2‑mutated and HR‑deficient tumors

BRCA1 and BRCA2 are central HR factors that suppress genomic instability; inherited monoallelic mutations in these genes greatly increase breast cancer risk, and tumors arising in carriers typically lose the remaining wild‑type allele.[1] In *BRCA1/2*‑deficient cells, HR is impaired, so replication‑associated DSBs produced by PARP inhibition and PARP trapping cannot be accurately repaired.[1] The resulting reliance on error‑prone end‑joining pathways leads to lethal chromosomal aberrations when PARP is inhibited, whereas isolated loss of BRCA function or PARP1 activity alone is compatible with cell survival—fulfilling the definition of synthetic lethality.[1]

Preclinical and early clinical studies established that BRCA1/2‑mutant breast tumors are intrinsically sensitive to PARP inhibitors, including olaparib, with relative sparing of normal HR‑proficient tissues.[1,2] Furthermore, HRD can arise in ostensibly *BRCA1/2*‑wild‑type breast cancers (“BRCAness”). For example, loss of the HR regulator Kub5‑Hera/RPRD1B in BRCA‑proficient breast cancer cells reduces CDK1‑mediated BRCA1 phosphorylation and RAD51 recruitment, generating an HRD gene‑expression signature and creating a synthetic lethal interaction with PARP1 inhibition.[2] These data support a broader mechanistic model in which any lesion that compromises BRCA‑dependent HR (germline *BRCA1/2* mutation or acquired HRD) can confer olaparib sensitivity.

### Breast cancer subtype context

Clinically, olaparib’s mechanism translates into selective activity in HER2‑negative breast cancers with germline *BRCA1/2* mutations:

- In the phase III OlympiAD trial (NCT02000622), patients with HER2‑negative metastatic breast cancer and germline *BRCA1/2* mutations were randomized to olaparib versus single‑agent chemotherapy.[7,8] Olaparib improved progression‑free survival and showed a trend toward longer overall survival, with hazard ratios for overall survival favoring olaparib in both triple‑negative and hormone receptor–positive subgroups, although differences were not statistically significant.[7,8,10]
- Subgroup analyses demonstrated similar overall survival patterns in hormone receptor–positive and triple‑negative metastatic disease (HR for olaparib vs chemotherapy 0.86 and 0.93, respectively), consistent with a mechanism driven by HR status rather than hormone receptor or HER2 expression.[7,8,10]

In early‑stage HR‑positive/HER2‑negative disease, the OlympiA trial (referenced in guideline‑type summaries) showed that adjuvant olaparib in patients with germline *BRCA* mutations and high‑risk features improved invasive disease‑free and overall survival; this has led to its use in HR+/HER2− early breast cancer with germline *BRCA* mutation and high recurrence risk.[13,15] Mechanistically, these data reinforce that olaparib targets a BRCA/HRD dependency that cuts across standard receptor‑defined subtypes, with particular enrichment of *BRCA1* alterations in triple‑negative cancers and *BRCA2* alterations in a subset of HR‑positive/HER2‑negative tumors.[1,2,7,13,15]

## 7. Contraindications and Safety

### 7.1 Formal contraindications

- U.S. FDA and NCCN-based materials state that olaparib (Lynparza) has **no formal contraindications** in its prescribing information.[1][4][5][10]  
- NCCN-aligned educational material similarly notes: “There are no contraindications for LYNPARZA.”[10]

### 7.2 Key boxed / warning-level safety risks

#### Myelodysplastic syndrome / acute myeloid leukemia (MDS/AML)

- Incidence with olaparib monotherapy across trials:
  - FDA label safety update: MDS/AML occurred in **<1.5% (28/2351)** of patients receiving olaparib monotherapy; most events were fatal.[2]
  - Earlier tablet and capsule labels also report MDS/AML in **<1.5%** of exposed patients with a majority of fatal outcomes.[4][5]
  - NCCN-aligned summary: MDS/AML occurred in **<1.5%** of patients on olaparib monotherapy; duration of therapy before MDS/AML ranged from **<6 months to >2 years**.[10]
- Predisposing factors:
  - Nearly all affected patients had prior **platinum-based chemotherapy and/or other DNA‑damaging agents (including radiotherapy)**; many had **>1 prior malignancy or bone marrow dysplasia**.[2][5][10]
- Breast cancer–specific context:
  - In OlympiAD (gBRCA‑mutated HER2‑negative metastatic breast cancer), **no MDS/AML cases** were reported in either arm at the time of primary analysis.[6]
  - In OlympiA (gBRCA‑mutated high‑risk early breast cancer), adjuvant olaparib **did not increase serious AEs including leukemia** compared with placebo.[13]
- Monitoring and management (all indications, including breast):
  - Do **not start olaparib until hematologic toxicity from prior chemotherapy has recovered to ≤ Grade 1**.[2][5][10]
  - **CBC at baseline and monthly** thereafter; for prolonged cytopenias, **interrupt olaparib and monitor weekly** until recovery.[2][5][10]
  - If counts fail to recover to ≤ Grade 1 within 4 weeks, **refer to hematology** for bone marrow and cytogenetics; **permanently discontinue olaparib if MDS/AML is confirmed**.[2][5][10]

#### Hematologic toxicity (dose‑limiting)

- Across breast cancer trials and pooled experience, hematologic toxicity is the **principal dose‑limiting toxicity**.
- Common treatment‑emergent events:
  - OlympiAD (metastatic gBRCA HER2‑negative):
    - Any‑grade adverse reactions ≥20%: **anemia 40%, fatigue 37%, nausea 58%, vomiting 30%, neutropenia 27%, leukopenia 25%, diarrhea 21%, headache 20%**.[10]
    - Common lab abnormalities ≥25%: **↓ hemoglobin 82%, ↓ lymphocytes 73%, ↓ leukocytes 71%, ↑ MCV 71%, ↓ ANC 46%, ↓ platelets 33%**.[10]
  - OlympiA (adjuvant high‑risk early breast cancer):
    - Grade ≥3 AEs more frequent with olaparib; most common were **anemia 9%, neutropenia 5%, leukopenia 3%, fatigue 2%**.[13]
    - Any‑grade: **nausea 57%, fatigue 40%, anemia 23%, vomiting 23%, headache 20%**.[13]
- Label‑level hematologic profile:
  - Tablets label (2020): most common adverse reactions ≥10% with single‑agent olaparib include **anemia, neutropenia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia**, along with non‑hematologic toxicities.[1][4]
  - Capsules label: most common lab abnormalities ≥25% include **↓ hemoglobin, ↓ lymphocytes, ↓ leukocytes, ↓ ANC, ↓ platelets, ↑ MCV, ↑ serum creatinine**.[5]
- Management:
  - For clinically significant anemia, neutropenia, or thrombocytopenia: **interrupt therapy and/or reduce dose** per label dosing‑modification guidance.[1][4][5][8][10]
  - NCCN-aligned material notes potentiation and prolongation of **myelosuppression when combined with other myelosuppressive/DNA‑damaging anticancer agents**, so such combinations should be used cautiously.[10]
  - Transfusion support or dose reduction is commonly used in practice for anemia in the adjuvant setting; if recurrent/severe despite optimization, discontinuation may be appropriate.[15]

#### Pneumonitis

- Frequency:
  - FDA labels (tablets and capsules): **pneumonitis occurred in ~0.8%–<1%** of olaparib‑treated patients; some cases were fatal.[1][4][5]
- Management:
  - If new or worsening **dyspnea, cough, fever, or radiologic lung findings** occur, **interrupt olaparib and promptly investigate**.[1][2][4][5][10]
  - **Permanently discontinue** olaparib if pneumonitis is confirmed.[1][4][5][10]
- Breast‑cancer–specific data:
  - No pneumonitis signal was highlighted in OlympiAD or OlympiA reports; overall, safety was described as consistent with the known olaparib profile.[6][8][13][14][15]

#### Venous thromboembolism (VTE)

- Updated FDA tablet label notes **venous thromboembolism, including pulmonary embolism**, particularly in mCRPC (8% incidence), but this warning is general for the product.[1]
- Although the highest risk is described in prostate cancer, breast cancer patients—especially those with additional VTE risk factors—should be monitored for **leg swelling, dyspnea, chest pain, tachypnea, or tachycardia**; treat VTE/PE as clinically appropriate while reassessing continued olaparib use.[1][2]

#### Embryo‑fetal toxicity and reproductive risk

- Mechanism‑based risk:
  - Olaparib can **cause fetal harm**; teratogenicity and embryo‑fetal toxicity were observed in animal studies at exposures below human therapeutic levels.[2][4][5]
- Recommendations:
  - **Pregnancy test** before starting treatment in females of reproductive potential.[2][10]
  - **Effective contraception**:
    - Females: during treatment and for **6 months** after last dose.[2][10]
    - Males with partners of reproductive potential or who are pregnant: during treatment and for **3 months** after last dose; do not donate sperm during this period.[2][10]
  - **Do not breastfeed** during treatment and for **1 month** after the last dose due to potential serious adverse reactions in the infant.[1][4][5][10]

### 7.3 Other common and clinically relevant toxicities

#### Non‑hematologic adverse effects

- Across metastatic and adjuvant breast cancer trials and the label, the most frequent non‑hematologic AEs are:
  - **Gastrointestinal**: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dyspepsia, decreased appetite, abdominal pain, constipation.[1][4][5][8][10][13][15]
  - **Constitutional**: fatigue/asthenia, headache, dizziness.[1][4][5][8][10][13][15]
  - **Infections**: upper respiratory/nasopharyngitis, other respiratory tract infections.[5][8][10]
  - **Dysgeusia / taste changes**.[1][4][5][8]
- OlympiAD (metastatic breast cancer) AE profile (all grades, ≥20%): **anemia 40%, nausea 58%, fatigue 37%, vomiting 30%, neutropenia 27%, respiratory tract infection 27%, leukopenia 25%, diarrhea 21%, headache 20%**.[10]
- OlympiA (adjuvant) any‑grade AEs with olaparib: **nausea 57%, fatigue 40%, anemia 23%, vomiting 23%, headache 20%**; serious AEs and second cancers (including leukemia) were **not increased vs placebo**.[13][15]
- Management:
  - **Take with food or a snack** to mitigate early nausea, as recommended in OlympiA‑focused expert commentary.[15]
  - Use standard antiemetics and symptomatic measures; consider dose interruption/reduction if persistent or severe.[1][4][5][8][10][15]

#### Dermatologic reactions

- Post‑marketing label update lists rare skin/subcutaneous events such as **erythema nodosum, rash, dermatitis**.[2]
- Typically low‑grade; manage with topical/systemic therapy as needed and consider dose modification if severe.[2]

### 7.4 Dose modifications and organ dysfunction

- **Dose modification for toxicity**:
  - For tablets, dose interruptions and stepwise reductions (e.g., from 300 mg BID to 250 mg BID, then 200 mg BID) are recommended for intolerable AEs.[4][8][10][15]
  - In OlympiAD, **9 of 10 patients** remained on olaparib without AE‑related discontinuation; **31%** required dose reduction and **28%** dose interruption for adverse reactions, vs 25% and 35% respectively with physician’s‑choice chemotherapy.[10]
- **Renal impairment**:
  - Mild renal impairment (CLcr 51–80 mL/min): **no dose adjustment**.[1][2][5][10]
  - Moderate renal impairment (CLcr 31–50 mL/min): **reduce tablet dose to 200 mg BID**.[1][2][4][5][10]
  - **No data** in severe renal impairment or end‑stage disease (CLcr ≤30 mL/min); use is not recommended or should be undertaken with extreme caution.[1][2][5][10]
- **Hepatic impairment**:
  - Mild to moderate hepatic impairment (Child‑Pugh A or B): **no starting dose adjustment** required; only modest increases in exposure (~8–15%) were observed.[2][5][10]
  - No data in **severe hepatic impairment (Child‑Pugh C)**; use with caution.[2][5][10]

### 7.5 Drug–drug interactions with safety relevance

- **CYP3A inhibitors**:
  - Strong or moderate CYP3A inhibitors (e.g., itraconazole, clarithromycin, ketoconazole, ciprofloxacin) **increase olaparib exposure** and risk of toxicity; avoid if possible, or **reduce olaparib dose** per label if unavoidable.[1][4][5][8][10]
  - Patients should **avoid grapefruit, grapefruit juice, Seville oranges, Seville orange juice**.[10]
- **CYP3A inducers**:
  - Strong or moderate CYP3A inducers (e.g., phenytoin, rifampicin, carbamazepine, bosentan, efavirenz, modafinil) may **reduce olaparib exposure and efficacy**; avoid co‑administration.[4][5][8][10]
- **Other myelosuppressive/DNA‑damaging agents**:
  - Clinical studies show **potentiation and prolongation of myelosuppressive toxicity** when olaparib is combined with other myelosuppressive anticancer agents, including DNA‑damaging therapies.[10]
  - In breast cancer, this is particularly relevant when sequencing with or following intensive chemotherapy or radiotherapy; careful timing and hematologic monitoring are required.

### 7.6 Breast cancer–specific safety considerations (metastatic vs adjuvant)

- **Metastatic gBRCA HER2‑negative disease (OlympiAD, NCT02000622)**:
  - Overall, olaparib was **better tolerated than physician’s‑choice chemotherapy**, with **fewer grade ≥3 AEs (36.6% vs 50.5%)** and fewer AE‑related discontinuations (4.9% vs 7.7%).[6]
  - Main AEs: **nausea, anemia, fatigue**; fewer negative effects on white blood cells compared with chemotherapy.[6]
  - No MDS/AML cases were observed in either arm at primary analysis.[6]
- **High‑risk early gBRCA HER2‑negative disease (OlympiA, NCT02032823)**:
  - Safety was **consistent with prior olaparib experience**, with manageable toxicity.[13][14][15]
  - Grade ≥3 hematologic AEs (anemia, neutropenia, leukopenia) occurred but were generally manageable with dose modification and supportive care.[13][15]
  - Expert commentary emphasizes the importance of **early nausea management (food with dosing)** and **close CBC monitoring for anemia**, with dose reductions or transfusion support as needed; persistent or severe toxicity in the curative setting may justify discontinuation.[15]
  - No excess signal for **leukemia/MDS or other second malignancies** was observed with adjuvant olaparib at the time of reporting.[13][14][15]

### 7.7 Practical risk–benefit and monitoring summary for precision breast oncology

For gBRCA‑mutated, HER2‑negative breast cancer (metastatic and high‑risk early):

- **Major safety priorities**:
  - Early identification and management of **hematologic toxicity** (anemia, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia) with monthly CBCs (more frequently if cytopenic).
  - Vigilance for **MDS/AML** in heavily pretreated patients (especially with prior platinum and radiotherapy); prompt work‑up of persistent cytopenias.
  - Surveillance for **pneumonitis** and **VTE** symptoms.
  - Strict **pregnancy prevention and lactation avoidance**.
- **Population‑specific nuance**:
  - In **metastatic disease**, the risk of cumulative marrow damage is higher due to prior therapies; MDS/AML risk, though still low, is most relevant in this setting, necessitating long‑term hematologic follow‑up.[2][5][8][10]
  - In **adjuvant high‑risk early disease**, the absolute benefit in invasive disease‑free and overall survival must be weighed against a 1‑year exposure to a drug with predominantly manageable GI and hematologic toxicity; the absence so far of an increased leukemia/MDS signal is reassuring but mandates ongoing surveillance.[13][14][15]
